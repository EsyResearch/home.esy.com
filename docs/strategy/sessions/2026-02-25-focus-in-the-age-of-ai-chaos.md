# Focus in the Age of AI Chaos

**Date:** February 25, 2026
**Type:** Strategy Session — Mindset & Positioning

---

## The Problem

AI is moving so fast that it's hard to stay focused building one thing. Every day there's a new tool (OpenClaw, etc.), a new demo on X, a new "I built this in 20 minutes" thread. The temptation is to try everything, learn everything, be everywhere. This is a real, documented phenomenon — JetBrains research shows 74% of AI-assisted developers don't even notice they're context-switching more frequently. A METR study found developers were 19% slower with AI tools despite feeling 24% faster. The gap is "orchestration overhead."

---

## The Core Insight

Esy is the solution to the problem I'm experiencing. The whole thesis — structured workflows over open-ended prompting, guided process over guesswork, artifacts over conversations — is an answer to AI chaos. I'm building order for people drowning in options. I should apply the same principle to myself.

---

## Why Esy's Position Is Durable

### Abstraction layer advantage
Esy sits above the AI model layer. Templates use Claude today, Nano Banana Pro for infographics — the engines will change. But the workflow structure (Intake → Research → Outline → Draft → Cite & Format → Artifact) is stable. Users don't care which LLM runs underneath. They care that they get a publishable artifact.

### The moat is workflow design, not AI access
Anyone can call Claude's API. What's hard to replicate: the structured intake process, multi-stage orchestration, output formatting, and the educational ecosystem around it.

### "Boring problem solving" wins
Research from 2026 consistently shows: career advancement goes to people who connect AI to real, recurring processes — not people who generate impressive demos. Esy solves boring, real, repeatable problems: "I need a research paper with citations." That's what the market rewards.

## The Other Guy

The person trying every new tool, posting demos, chasing the wave:

- **Months 1-6:** Looks like he's winning. Twitter is popping. "Built this in 20 minutes with [new tool]." Likes, followers, engagement.
- **Months 6-12:** 30 things built, zero shipped. GitHub is a graveyard. Broad surface knowledge, deep mastery of none. Can't explain what he does.
- **Year 2:** Market has matured. Customers want reliability, not demos. They Google "argumentative essay workflow" and find esy.com. They don't find his Twitter thread from 11 months ago.
- **Year 3:** Starting from zero trying to build what Esy already has — except Esy has 2 years of SEO compounding, 100+ templates, a video library, a user base, and a refined pipeline.

**What he builds depreciates** — every demo becomes irrelevant when the next model drops.
**What Esy builds appreciates** — each template, video, and article makes the next one more valuable.

He's trading. I'm compounding.

- Only 3-5% of indie builders reach meaningful sustained revenue

In 2026, execution got easy. Survival didn't. Starting is no longer the filter. **Staying is the filter.**

## The X/Twitter Influencer Reality

When I see someone with 100k+ followers demoing AI tools:

- ~70-80% are monetizing the audience (courses, sponsorships, community), not the software they demo
- ~15-20% have modest product revenue ($1-10k/month), supplemented by audience monetization
- ~5% or less are genuinely building sustainable software businesses

**The tell:** If their pinned tweet links to a course on "how to build AI apps" rather than to their product — the following IS the business. The SaaS is theater. I am their customer, not their peer.

## Can I Build in Public AND Build the Product?

Short answer: not as two separate brands. The cognitive split is real:

- Building for users = empathy-driven, systems-level thinking about their problems
- Building an audience = performance-driven thinking about what gets engagement
- They use the same resource (attention) and pull in opposite directions
- Audience-building always gives faster feedback (likes are instant, revenue takes months), so the gravitational pull is always toward becoming a content creator who used to build products

**The solution:** Don't build a personal brand AND Esy. Make Esy the brand. School videos, research articles, and templates ARE content marketing — they just live at esy.com instead of on personal Twitter. One motion, not two.

## Strategic Filters

### When evaluating a new AI tool, ask:
"Does this make my workflows produce better artifacts?"
- If yes → explore it on a Friday afternoon
- If no → bookmark and move on

### What to pay attention to:
- New AI models that improve writing/research quality
- Advances in citation/source verification
- New output formats users want (video essays? interactive docs?)
- What users are actually asking for

### What to safely ignore:
- New AI chat interfaces (they compete with prompting, not workflows)
- Social AI tools, AI companions
- General-purpose autonomous agents (unless they improve orchestration)
- What X is excited about this week

## What to Do When the Red Dress Walks By

When I feel the pull to chase something new, re-read this and remember:

1. **I don't need perfection. I need cadence.** Volume with increasing quality. The infrastructure is built. Feed the machine.
2. **The rarest skill in 2026 isn't knowing how to use AI. It's the discipline to keep building the same thing when everyone around you is starting something new.**
3. **I'm in a race where 95% of runners drop out.** I don't need to be fast. I need to be the one still running.
4. **The chaos is my market opportunity, not my to-do list.** More chaos = more people who need structured workflows = more potential Esy users.
5. **Ship the next template. Record the next tutorial. Improve the pipeline.** That's the job. It's not glamorous. It's what wins.
