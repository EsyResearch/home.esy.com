# Visual Essay Ideas — Science

> Last updated: February 25, 2026

Ideas for visual essays in the Science category, evaluated for photographic richness, quantitative data availability, and narrative potential.

---

## Paleoanthropology / Human Evolution

Cluster essays expanding the [Seven Million Years](/essays/science/seven-million-years) series. Each candidate is rated on three axes:

- **Photography**: Availability of high-quality specimen, site, and reconstruction images (Wikimedia Commons, museum archives, open-access publications)
- **Data**: Quantitative datasets suitable for interactive visualizations (morphometrics, isotopes, genomics, dating, stratigraphy)
- **Narrative**: Story arc — discovery drama, scientific debate, paradigm shifts, human interest

### Tier 1 — Exceptional candidates

#### 1. Homo naledi (Rising Star Cave, South Africa, 2013–2015)

**Photography**: Exceptional. Over 1,500 fossil specimens from 15+ individuals. The Dinaledi Chamber, the narrow 18cm chute entrance, and the extensive eLife open-access publications include high-resolution specimen photography. National Geographic coverage and a Netflix documentary provide supplementary visual material.

**Data**: The richest single-site hominin collection in Africa. Datasets include:
- Dental morphology and wear patterns across 15+ individuals
- Cranial capacity (~560cc) vs. behavioral complexity metrics
- Body proportion analysis (primitive shoulder/trunk + modern hands/feet)
- U-Th and ESR dating results (~236,000–335,000 years — unexpectedly young)
- 3D surface scans available through MorphoSource

**Narrative**: Lee Berger's unconventional recruitment of "underground astronauts" (small-bodied scientists who could fit through the chute). The deliberate body disposal debate — were they burying their dead with a brain one-third the size of ours? The dating controversy that placed them contemporary with early Homo sapiens. The philosophical question: what behaviors define humanity?

**Potential visualizations**:
- Interactive cave cross-section (3D or layered SVG)
- Anatomical mosaic diagram (primitive vs. modern trait mapping)
- Brain size vs. behavioral complexity scatter plot across Homo species
- Specimen recovery map of the Dinaledi Chamber floor
- Dating method comparison chart (U-Th, ESR, paleomag)
- Body proportion comparison: naledi vs. erectus vs. sapiens

**Cluster parent**: Seven Million Years
**Estimated scope**: 30–35 min, 10–12 visualizations, 20+ sources
**Priority**: Highest — natural next essay after Lucy

---

#### 2. Turkana Boy (KNM-WT 15000, Kenya, 1984)

**Photography**: Excellent. The most complete early Homo skeleton, housed at the National Museums of Kenya. Well-photographed from multiple angles. The Nariokotome site on Lake Turkana's western shore is documented. Cast replicas in museums worldwide.

**Data**: Exceptionally rich for a single specimen:
- Skeletal completeness: ~40% (108 bones)
- Growth plate analysis: dental vs. skeletal age discrepancy (died at ~8–11 years, but was 5'3")
- Adult stature projection: ~6'1" (185cm) — the first tall, linear body plan
- Limb proportions and thermoregulation modeling (Allen's rule)
- Vertebral pathology (possible scoliosis or skeletal dysplasia)
- Endocranial volume: 880cc (projected adult ~909cc)
- Association with Acheulean tool technology

**Narrative**: Kamoya Kimeu (the world's greatest fossil hunter) spotting a fragment of skull on an eroded slope. Richard Leakey's team excavating for five seasons. The boy who would have towered over modern 11-year-olds. The first body built for distance — long legs, narrow hips, barrel chest. The debate over whether he could speak (thoracic vertebral canal narrowing). His possible cause of death (septicemia from a dental abscess?).

**Potential visualizations**:
- Skeletal completeness overlay (recovered bones highlighted on full skeleton)
- Growth curve comparison: Turkana Boy vs. modern human vs. chimpanzee
- Body proportion diagram: limb ratios across hominin species
- Thermoregulation model: body surface area vs. mass in tropical heat
- Stature comparison: Turkana Boy (projected adult) vs. Lucy vs. modern human
- Acheulean tool technology timeline
- Lake Turkana basin stratigraphic column

**Cluster parent**: Seven Million Years
**Estimated scope**: 25–30 min, 8–10 visualizations, 18+ sources
**Priority**: High — strongest single-specimen story after Lucy

---

#### 3. Homo floresiensis ("The Hobbit", Flores, Indonesia, 2003)

**Photography**: Good. The Liang Bua cave is well-documented. The LB1 skull is iconic and widely photographed. Site excavation photography from Australian-Indonesian team publications. Flores island geography is visually striking.

**Data**: Solid across multiple domains:
- Cranial capacity: ~420cc (smaller than chimpanzee, yet associated with stone tools)
- Stature: ~106cm (3'6") — extreme insular dwarfism
- Wrist bone morphology (primitive, not modern human)
- Stone tool assemblages (Oldowan-like, possibly more sophisticated)
- Stegodon (dwarf elephant) butchery evidence
- Dating: ~100,000–50,000 years ago (overlapping with Homo sapiens in the region)
- Comparative island dwarfism data from other mammals (Stegodon, rats)
- Recent Homo luzonensis discovery (Philippines) adds comparative dimension

**Narrative**: The "Lord of the Rings" timing of the discovery. Peter Brown and Mike Morwood's announcement shocking the field. The "hobbit wars" — Teuku Jacob's unauthorized removal of fossils, the pathology vs. new species debate that consumed a decade. The island rule: giant rats, dwarf elephants, and tiny humans coexisting. The question of how they got to Flores (sea crossing or land bridge?). The tantalizing overlap with modern human arrival in the region. Homo luzonensis echoing the same pattern on another island.

**Potential visualizations**:
- Island rule diagram: body size shifts for multiple Flores species
- Brain size vs. tool complexity anomaly chart
- Liang Bua cave stratigraphy
- Southeast Asian island map with hominin sites and sea level changes
- Skull comparison: floresiensis vs. erectus vs. sapiens (scaled)
- Wrist bone morphology comparison
- Timeline of the "hobbit wars" scientific debate

**Cluster parent**: Seven Million Years
**Estimated scope**: 28–32 min, 9–11 visualizations, 18+ sources
**Priority**: High — the "island rule" angle is unique and visually rich

#### 4. Dmanisi (Georgia, 1991–2005)

**Photography**: Very good. Five skulls (D2700, D2280, D2282, D3444, D4500) are extensively published and photographed. The site itself — a medieval castle ruin above a 1.8-million-year-old fossil bed — is visually dramatic. Georgian National Museum holds the originals.

**Data**: Exceptional for taxonomy questions:
- Five crania from a single site, single time period, showing extreme variation
- Cranial capacities: 546cc to 730cc within one population
- D4500 (Skull 5): the most complete early Homo skull ever found
- Postcranial remains: small body size (~145–166cm), relatively primitive
- Associated Oldowan stone tools
- Faunal assemblage: saber-toothed cats, ostriches, giraffids
- Paleoecological reconstruction of 1.8 Ma Caucasus
- Challenge to multi-species Homo taxonomy (could all early Homo be one variable species?)

**Narrative**: The accidental discovery beneath a medieval ruin. David Lordkipanidze's decades-long excavation. The five skulls that collapsed the early Homo family tree — if this much variation exists in one population, how many "species" are real? Skull 5 (D4500): the toothless elder, the oldest evidence of social care for the elderly. The first hominins outside Africa — what drove them north? The saber-toothed cat that may have killed some of them.

**Potential visualizations**:
- Five-skull interactive comparison (overlay/toggle)
- Cranial capacity variation within Dmanisi vs. between "species"
- Site cross-section: medieval layers above Pleistocene deposits
- Migration route map: Africa to Caucasus
- Faunal association diagram
- Taxonomy challenge: traditional multi-species vs. Dmanisi single-species model
- D4500 toothless elder: bone remodeling and survival without teeth

**Cluster parent**: Seven Million Years
**Estimated scope**: 25–30 min, 8–10 visualizations, 16+ sources
**Priority**: High — the taxonomy-collapsing angle is a unique story

### Tier 2 — Strong candidates with tradeoffs

#### 5. Ardi (Ardipithecus ramidus, Ethiopia, 1994/2009)

**Photography**: Moderate. The skeleton was extremely fragile (crushed nearly flat over 4.4 million years). Tim White's team reconstructed it meticulously, but published images are more limited than Lucy or naledi. The Middle Awash site is documented but remote.

**Data**: Rich but concentrated in a few landmark publications:
- Skeletal completeness: ~45% of a single female individual
- Divergent big toe (grasping foot) yet upright pelvis — challenges bipedalism models
- No knuckle-walking adaptations — last common ancestor wasn't chimp-like
- Canine reduction: small canines suggest reduced male-male competition
- Woodland habitat (not savanna) — overturned the "savanna hypothesis"
- 4.4 Ma dating places it 1.2 million years before Lucy

**Narrative**: Tim White's legendary secrecy — 15 years between discovery and publication. The 47 co-authors on 11 simultaneous papers in Science (2009). The "Ardi wars" with Lee Berger and others questioning the reconstruction. The paradigm shift: our ancestor wasn't a chimp who stood up on the savanna; it was a woodland creature already walking upright in the trees. The philosophical question: if the ancestor wasn't chimp-like, what was it?

**Tradeoff**: More limited photography; reconstruction controversy may complicate data presentation.
**Cluster parent**: Seven Million Years
**Estimated scope**: 22–28 min, 7–9 visualizations, 15+ sources

#### 6. The Neanderthal Genome Revolution

**Photography**: Excellent across multiple sites. Vindija Cave (Croatia), Denisova Cave (Siberia), Shanidar Cave (Iraq), El Sidrón (Spain), and dozens more. Neanderthal reconstructions are among the most refined in paleoanthropology. Svante Pääbo's lab at Max Planck is well-documented.

**Data**: The most data-rich topic in paleoanthropology:
- Complete Neanderthal genome (Vindija, Altai)
- Introgression maps: 1–4% Neanderthal DNA in non-African populations
- Gene-by-gene functional analysis (immune system, skin pigmentation, pain sensitivity)
- Denisovan genome and the "ghost population" signal
- Archaeological datasets from hundreds of sites across Eurasia
- Isotopic diet analysis, use-wear on tools, symbolic behavior evidence
- Climate correlation data (glacial/interglacial cycles vs. range)

**Narrative**: Svante Pääbo's Nobel Prize journey — from extracting DNA from Egyptian mummies to sequencing an extinct species' genome. The bombshell: we interbred with them. The Shanidar "flower burial" debate (compassion or coincidence?). Neanderthal art at Bruniquel Cave and the symbolic revolution question. Their extinction (~40,000 years ago) coinciding with sapiens arrival. The Denisovan twist: a species known almost entirely from DNA.

**Tradeoff**: Sprawling topic — would need tight scoping (perhaps focused on the genome story specifically rather than all of Neanderthal archaeology). Excellent for data visualizations but risks becoming encyclopedic.
**Cluster parent**: Seven Million Years
**Estimated scope**: 35–40 min, 12+ visualizations, 25+ sources

---

#### 7. The Laetoli Footprints (Tanzania, 1978)

**Photography**: Iconic. Mary Leakey's original excavation photographs, museum casts, and recent re-excavation (2016) with photogrammetric documentation. The footprint trail itself is one of the most reproduced images in human evolution.

**Data**: Focused but deep:
- 3.66 million years old (dated by potassium-argon on bracketing tuffs)
- Two parallel trackways (possibly three individuals)
- Gait analysis: heel strike, longitudinal arch, adducted big toe
- Step length and stride cadence calculations
- Stature estimates from footprint dimensions
- Sadiman volcano tuff composition and preservation conditions
- Comparison with modern human and chimpanzee gait biomechanics
- 2016 re-excavation: new trackways with larger individuals

**Narrative**: Mary Leakey's team literally stumbling on them during a playful elephant-dung fight. The volcanic ash that preserved a moment — two (or three) individuals walking side by side 3.66 million years ago. Were they a family? A mating pair? The ash that fell from Sadiman volcano, dampened by rain, and hardened like cement before the next eruption sealed them. The reburial controversy — should they have been left exposed or covered? The 2016 rediscovery of new tracks.

**Tradeoff**: Narrower scope (footprints, not a full skeleton) — works best as a shorter, focused essay or paired with Lucy as a companion piece.
**Cluster parent**: Seven Million Years
**Estimated scope**: 18–22 min, 6–8 visualizations, 12+ sources
