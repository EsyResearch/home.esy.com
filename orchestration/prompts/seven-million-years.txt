TOPIC: Seven Million Years — The Complete Visual History of Our Kind

A visual essay, expository in nature with extensive photography, that explores the full breadth and depth of the hominid lineage through ancient history until the present day.

SCOPE & CONTENT REQUIREMENTS:

1. ALL KNOWN HOMINIDS THROUGH TIME
   - Sahelanthropus tchadensis (~7 MYA) — the earliest known candidate
   - Orrorin tugenensis (~6 MYA)
   - Ardipithecus kadabba and Ardipithecus ramidus (~5.8–4.4 MYA)
   - Australopithecus anamensis, A. afarensis (Lucy), A. africanus, A. garhi, A. sediba
   - Paranthropus aethiopicus, P. boisei, P. robustus — the robust lineage
   - Homo habilis, Homo rudolfensis — the first toolmakers
   - Homo erectus / Homo ergaster — the first global traveler
   - Homo heidelbergensis — the common ancestor of Neanderthals and sapiens
   - Homo naledi — the surprising late survivor
   - Homo floresiensis ("the Hobbit") — island dwarfism
   - Homo luzonensis — the Philippines mystery
   - Homo longi ("Dragon Man") — the Harbin cranium
   - Denisovans — known mostly from DNA
   - Homo neanderthalensis — our closest kin
   - Homo sapiens — us

2. THE ERAS THEY LIVED IN
   - Miocene context (where the story begins)
   - Pliocene — the rise of bipedalism
   - Early Pleistocene — the first tool technologies
   - Middle Pleistocene — brain expansion, fire mastery
   - Late Pleistocene — the cognitive revolution, art, symbolic thought
   - Holocene — the aftermath

3. CHALLENGES EACH SPECIES FACED
   - Climate oscillations (glacial/interglacial cycles)
   - Habitat fragmentation
   - Competition between species
   - Dietary pressures and adaptations
   - Predation
   - Disease and parasites

4. THEORETICAL CAUSES OF DECLINE FOR EACH NON-SURVIVING SPECIES
   - Climate-driven extinction
   - Competitive exclusion by Homo sapiens
   - Hybrid absorption / genetic swamping
   - Ecological niche collapse
   - Island isolation effects

5. THE RISE OF HOMO SAPIENS
   - African origins (~300,000 YA)
   - The "cognitive revolution" debate
   - The role of language
   - Social complexity and cooperation
   - Technological innovation rates

6. THEORIES ON WHY SAPIENS SURVIVED
   - Superior social networks (Dunbar, etc.)
   - Language and abstract thought
   - Dietary flexibility / generalist strategy
   - Higher reproductive rates
   - Technological adaptability
   - Long-distance trade networks
   - Cultural cumulation (ratchet effect)
   - Sheer luck and timing

VISUAL & INTERACTIVE REQUIREMENTS:

- EXTENSIVE PHOTOGRAPHY: Museum specimens, fossil reconstructions, archaeological sites, landscapes that represent ancient environments. Use high-quality Wikimedia Commons, museum collections, and public domain imagery.

- CHARTS AND GRAPHICS (must be high quality — NO shitty SVG animations, NO cheap-looking charts):
  - Hominid family tree / phylogenetic diagram (interactive if possible)
  - Timeline showing all species' temporal ranges with overlap visualization
  - Brain volume chart across species and time
  - Body size comparison across species
  - Tool technology progression (Oldowan → Acheulean → Mousterian → Upper Paleolithic)
  - Climate curve overlaid with species ranges
  - Geographic range maps for key species

- STRONG COMPARISONS BETWEEN GROUPS:
  - Side-by-side anatomical comparisons (skull shape, pelvis, teeth, hands)
  - Behavioral comparisons (tool use, fire use, burial practices, art)
  - Ecological niche comparisons
  - Brain-to-body ratio comparisons

- INTUITIVE ANIMATIONS AND MIGRATION VISUALIZATIONS:
  - Animated world map showing the spread of different hominid species over time
  - Migration routes out of Africa (multiple waves)
  - Interactive timeline that users can scrub through
  - Population density shifts over time

- TONE: Scientific but accessible. Not dumbed down — the reader should feel they've learned something substantial. Think longform New York Times or National Geographic visual feature.

DESIGN DIRECTION:
- Do NOT copy any existing essay in the codebase. This needs its own unique visual identity.
- The design should be derived from the subject matter: earth tones, geological strata, bone, stone, ochre, cave-dark backgrounds
- Photography-forward — this essay should feel like walking through a natural history museum
- Charts must be beautiful and publication-quality — think Nature journal or Scientific American
- Animations should be smooth, purposeful, and elegant — not gratuitous
