### Ancient Era (Before 4th Century CE)

| Date | Event | Location | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|----------|--------------|--------|
| c. 2400-1900 BCE | Bone forks discovered | Qijia culture, China | Earliest archaeological evidence of fork-like utensils | Wikipedia |
| c. 1600-1050 BCE | Forks in Shang dynasty | China | Continued use of pronged eating implements | Wikipedia |
| Ancient era | Large cooking forks used | Egypt, Greece, Rome | Forks as kitchen tools, not dining implements | Multiple |
| Roman period | Bronze and silver serving forks | Roman Empire | Two-pronged forks for serving, not personal dining | Wikipedia |

### Byzantine Era (4th–11th Century)

| Date | Event | Location | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|----------|--------------|--------|
| c. 4th century | Personal table fork emerges | Byzantine Empire | Fork transitions from serving tool to personal dining implement | Wikipedia |
| 10th century | Fork common among elite | Byzantium, Middle East | Established as mark of sophistication in Eastern courts | History.com |
| 972 | Theophanu marries Otto II | Germany | Byzantine princess brings fork customs to Holy Roman Empire | Wikipedia |
| 991 | Theophanu dies | Germany | Fork customs noted but not widely adopted after her death | Wikipedia |

|------|-------|----------|--------------|--------|

### English Resistance Era (17th Century)

| Date | Event | Location | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|----------|--------------|--------|

| 1611 | "Coryat's Crudities" published | England | First extensive English description of fork use | Museum of the Home |
| Early 1600s | Coryat mocked as "Furcifer" | England | English resistance to "effeminate" Italian custom | Multiple sources |
| 1633 | Charles I declares forks "decent" | England | Royal endorsement helps legitimize fork use | Historical accounts |
| Late 17th century | Fork gains acceptance in England | England | Gradual adoption among aristocracy and upper class | Wikipedia |
