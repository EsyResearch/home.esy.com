## Phase A: Background Context (1917–1947)

### 1. Balfour Declaration
**Date**: November 2, 1917
**Description**: British Foreign Secretary Arthur Balfour issues a letter to Lord Rothschild declaring British support for "a national home for the Jewish people" in Palestine, while noting that nothing should be done to prejudice "the civil and religious rights of existing non-Jewish communities."
**Significance**: Foundation document that shaped British Mandate policy and Zionist claims.
**Source**: [Primary document; public domain]

### 2. British Military Administration Begins
**Date**: December 1917
**Description**: Following the capture of Jerusalem, British military administration begins in Palestine, ending 400 years of Ottoman rule.
**Significance**: Transition of power that precedes formal Mandate.

### 3. British Mandate Formally Established
**Date**: July 24, 1922 (League of Nations approval); September 29, 1923 (formal start)
**Description**: The League of Nations grants Britain the Mandate for Palestine, incorporating the Balfour Declaration.
**Significance**: Legal framework for British rule and Jewish immigration policy.
**Source**: [Avalon Project, Yale Law School]

### 4. Herbert Samuel as First High Commissioner
**Date**: July 1, 1920
**Description**: Herbert Samuel, a British Zionist, becomes the first High Commissioner of Palestine.
**Significance**: Early British administration favorable to Zionist project.

### 5. 1929 Palestine Riots
**Date**: August 1929
**Description**: Violence erupts over access to the Western Wall/Al-Buraq area in Jerusalem; 133 Jews and 116 Arabs killed.
**Significance**: Major escalation of intercommunal violence; British investigate causes.
**Uncertainty**: Exact triggers and casualty numbers vary by source.

### 6. Arab Revolt Begins
**Date**: April 1936
**Description**: Palestinian Arabs launch a general strike and uprising against British rule and Jewish immigration. Lasts until 1939.
**Significance**: Largest Palestinian uprising before 1948; British response weakened Palestinian leadership.
**Source**: [Academic consensus]

### 7. Peel Commission Report
**Date**: July 7, 1937
**Description**: British commission recommends partition of Palestine into Jewish and Arab states—first official partition proposal.
**Significance**: Rejected by Arabs; establishes partition as a policy option.

### 8. British White Paper of 1939
**Date**: May 17, 1939
**Description**: Britain limits Jewish immigration to 75,000 over five years and restricts land sales to Jews; envisions independent Palestine within 10 years.
**Significance**: Attempt to appease Arabs before WWII; rejected by both communities.

### 9. Holocaust and Post-WWII Migration Pressure
**Date**: 1945–1947
**Description**: Following the Holocaust, survivors seek entry to Palestine; British restrict immigration; tensions escalate.
**Significance**: Moral and political pressure intensifies on Britain.

### 10. Anglo-American Committee of Inquiry
**Date**: January–April 1946
**Description**: Joint British-American committee recommends admitting 100,000 Jewish Holocaust survivors to Palestine.
**Significance**: Britain rejects key recommendations; pressure mounts.

### 11. UNSCOP Formed
**Date**: May 15, 1947
**Description**: United Nations Special Committee on Palestine (UNSCOP) established to investigate and recommend solutions.
**Significance**: Leads to partition recommendation.

### 12. British Announce Withdrawal
**Date**: February 14, 1947
**Description**: Britain announces it will turn the Palestine problem over to the United Nations and end its Mandate.
**Significance**: Sets timeline for end of Mandate and precipitates crisis.

## Phase B: Partition & Civil War (November 1947 – May 1948)

### 13. UNGA Resolution 181 (Partition Plan)
**Date**: November 29, 1947
**Description**: UN General Assembly votes 33–13 (with 10 abstentions) to recommend partition of Palestine into Jewish and Arab states, with Jerusalem under international control.
**Significance**: Immediately triggers civil war; Palestinians and Arab states reject the plan.
**Source**: [UN Archives; full text at Avalon Project]

### 14. Civil War Begins
**Date**: November 30, 1947
**Description**: Following the partition vote, Arab-Jewish violence escalates. Bus ambush near Netanya kills Jewish passengers.
**Significance**: Beginning of 1947–48 civil war phase.

### 15. Arab Higher Committee Strike
**Date**: December 2, 1947
**Description**: Arab Higher Committee announces three-day strike against partition; riots break out in Jerusalem.
**Significance**: Organized Palestinian rejection of partition.

### 16. Arab Liberation Army Infiltration
**Date**: January 1948
**Description**: First detachments of the Arab Liberation Army (ALA) begin infiltrating Palestine from Syria, some through Jordan.
**Significance**: Arab states begin military involvement before Mandate ends.

### 17. Plan Dalet (Plan D) Adopted
**Date**: March 10, 1948
**Description**: Haganah adopts Plan Dalet, which outlines operations to secure territory allocated to the Jewish state and beyond.
**Significance**: Historians debate whether this was a blueprint for expulsion or defensive planning.
**Uncertainty**: Interpretation of intent is contested.

### 18. Operation Nachshon
**Date**: April 5–15, 1948
**Description**: Haganah launches operation to open the road between Tel Aviv and Jerusalem.
**Significance**: First major Haganah offensive; marks shift from defense to offense.

### 19. Deir Yassin Massacre
**Date**: April 9, 1948
**Description**: Irgun and Lehi forces attack the village of Deir Yassin near Jerusalem. At least 107 Palestinians killed, including women and children.
**Significance**: News spread terror; significantly accelerated Palestinian flight from other areas.
**Source**: [Multiple historians; Red Cross documentation]

### 20. Hadassah Medical Convoy Massacre
**Date**: April 13, 1948
**Description**: Palestinian Arabs attack a convoy en route to Hadassah Hospital in Jerusalem, killing approximately 80 Jews.
**Significance**: Retaliatory violence cycle; both communities terrorized.

### 21. Haifa Falls / Mass Evacuation
**Date**: April 21–22, 1948
**Description**: After Haganah offensive including psychological warfare, mass Palestinian evacuation from Haifa begins. Approximately 60,000 of 70,000 Arab residents leave.
**Significance**: Major urban displacement before end of Mandate.
**Source**: [Morris, *Birth of the Palestinian Refugee Problem*]

### 22. Jaffa Falls
**Date**: May 13, 1948
**Description**: After weeks of siege and Irgun mortar attacks, Jaffa—the largest Arab city—falls. Mass evacuation occurs.
**Significance**: Loss of major Arab cultural and economic center.

### 23. British Mandate Ends
**Date**: May 14, 1948 (midnight)
**Description**: British High Commissioner Sir Alan Cunningham departs; British Mandate officially ends.
**Significance**: Power vacuum; state declarations follow.

### 24. Israeli Declaration of Independence
**Date**: May 14, 1948 (8 hours before Mandate end)
**Description**: David Ben-Gurion declares the establishment of the State of Israel in Tel Aviv.
**Significance**: Creates new state; triggers Arab state intervention.

## Phase C: 1948 War (May – December 1948)

### 25. Arab Armies Enter
**Date**: May 15, 1948
**Description**: Armies of Egypt, Syria, Transjordan, Iraq, and Lebanon enter Palestine/Israel.
**Significance**: Civil war becomes interstate war.

### 26. First Truce
**Date**: June 11 – July 8, 1948
**Description**: UN-mediated truce; both sides rearm.
**Significance**: Breathing space; Israel significantly strengthens.

### 27. Lydda and Ramle Expulsion
**Date**: July 10–13, 1948
**Description**: Israeli forces capture Lydda and Ramle; 50,000–70,000 Palestinians expelled, many on foot in extreme heat (the "Lydda death march").
**Significance**: Largest single expulsion of the war; documented expulsion orders.
**Source**: [Morris; Israeli archives contain expulsion order]

### 28. Second Truce Begins
**Date**: July 18, 1948
**Description**: Second UN-mediated truce begins.
**Significance**: Fighting continues intermittently despite truce.

### 29. Count Bernadotte Submits Plan
**Date**: September 16, 1948
**Description**: UN Mediator Bernadotte submits peace plan to UN General Assembly.
**Significance**: Plan rejected by Zionist militants; leads to his assassination.

### 30. Assassination of Count Bernadotte
**Date**: September 17, 1948
**Description**: Four Lehi members assassinate UN Mediator Count Bernadotte and Colonel André Serot in Jerusalem.
**Significance**: Murder of first UN mediator; international outrage; Ralph Bunche succeeds him.
**Source**: [UN documentation; widely documented]

### 31. Operation Hiram (Galilee)
**Date**: October 28–31, 1948
**Description**: Israeli forces capture the Upper Galilee, including remaining Arab-held areas.
**Significance**: Completes Israeli control of Galilee; further displacement.

### 32. UNGA Resolution 194
**Date**: December 11, 1948
**Description**: UN General Assembly passes Resolution 194, stating refugees "wishing to return to their homes and live at peace with their neighbours should be permitted to do so."
**Significance**: Basis for Palestinian "right of return" claims; Israel does not implement.
**Source**: [UN Archives; full text available]

### 33. End of Major Combat
**Date**: Late December 1948
**Description**: Major combat operations wind down; armistice negotiations begin.
**Significance**: Transition from war to armistice phase.

## Phase D: Aftermath (1949–1950)

### 34. Rhodes Armistice Negotiations Begin
**Date**: January 1949
**Description**: Ralph Bunche mediates armistice talks on the island of Rhodes.
**Significance**: Path to formal armistice agreements.

### 35. Egypt-Israel Armistice
**Date**: February 24, 1949
**Description**: First armistice agreement signed between Egypt and Israel.
**Significance**: Establishes armistice lines (later "Green Line").

### 36. All Armistice Agreements Complete
**Date**: July 20, 1949 (Syria-Israel, the last)
**Description**: Armistice agreements signed with all neighboring Arab states (Egypt, Lebanon, Transjordan, Syria).
**Significance**: War formally ends; no peace treaties; issues unresolved.

### 37. UNGA Resolution 302 (UNRWA Established)
**Date**: December 8, 1949
**Description**: UN General Assembly establishes UNRWA to provide relief and works programs for Palestine refugees.
**Significance**: Creates institution that still operates today; refugee status institutionalized.
**Source**: [UN Archives; UNRWA official history]

### 38. UNRWA Begins Operations
**Date**: May 1, 1950
**Description**: UNRWA begins operations, taking over from United Nations Relief for Palestine Refugees (UNRPR).
**Significance**: Marks transition to long-term refugee management.
