## Origins and Classification

### Jerry Wexler — Coining the Term
> "In my capacity as a Billboard reporter I was also responsible for a minor semantic and, as it turned out, musical revolution... In June 1949, I coined the term 'rhythm and blues' to replace the onerous designation 'race records.'"
- **Source**: Wexler autobiography
- **Date**: Referring to June 1949
- **Context**: Billboard magazine classification change

## Founding Era Voices

### Big Mama Thornton — On Exploitation
> "That song sold over 2 million records. I got one check for $500 and never saw another."
- **Source**: Jet magazine interview
- **Date**: 1970s
- **Context**: Discussing "Hound Dog" royalties

### Ruth Brown — On Rights Advocacy
> "Leveraged Atlantic Records into paying her back royalties - and she didn't stop there. The deal she cut with the label also allowed dozens of other musicians to recoup their earnings as well."
- **Source**: NPR "Forebears" profile
- **Date**: December 2017
- **Context**: R&B Foundation founding

### LaVern Baker — On Cover Versions
> Baker was "infuriated at the blatant rip-off" when Georgia Gibbs copied her arrangement note-for-note.
- **Source**: 500 Songs podcast, music histories
- **Date**: 1955
- **Context**: "Tweedle Dee" controversy

### B.B. King — On T-Bone Walker
> "When I first heard Walker, I thought 'Jesus Himself had returned to earth playing electric guitar.' T-Bone's blues filled my insides with joy and good feeling. I became his disciple. And remain so today. My biggest musical debt is to T-Bone."
- **Source**: King autobiography
- **Date**: Various
- **Context**: Influence of electric blues pioneer

## Soul Era

### Aretha Franklin — On Creative Control
> "The main difference was Jerry (Wexler) asked me to sit at the piano and bring in some things, where, at Columbia, they pretty much were telling me what to do rather than just letting me do."
- **Source**: Academy of Achievement interview
- **Date**: 1991
- **Context**: Difference between Columbia and Atlantic Records

### Aretha Franklin — On "Respect"
> "It was the need of a nation, the need of the average man and woman in the street, the businessman, the mother, the fireman, the teacher - everyone wanted respect."
- **Source**: Aretha autobiography
- **Context**: Meaning of the song

### Etta James — On Musical Identity
> "Sure, I can sing the blues all night long. I love the blues. I honor the form. But another part of me knows I can sing country and western just as soulfully. Not to mention hard rock."
- **Source**: Etta James autobiography
- **Date**: 1995
- **Context**: Genre versatility

### Etta James — On Gospel Origins
> "Matter of fact, it is a gospel song. We wrote that song, and we adapted it from a gospel song. And the gospel song was 'Something's Got a Hold on Me, It Must be the Lord.'"
- **Source**: NPR Fresh Air interview with Terry Gross
- **Date**: 1994
- **Context**: Origins of "Something's Got a Hold on Me"

### Sam Phillips — On Crossover
> "If I could find a white man who had the Negro sound and the Negro feel, I could make a billion dollars."
- **Source**: Various interviews
- **Date**: Pre-1954
- **Context**: Before discovering Elvis Presley

## New Jack Swing and 1990s

### Teddy Riley — Defining New Jack Swing
> "New Jack Swing is a movement... When you're doing New Jack Swing, you're doing a singer and a rapper together -- that's New Jack Swing."
- **Source**: Red Bull Music Academy oral history
- **Date**: May 2017
- **Context**: Genre definition

### Hank Shocklee — On NJS Sound
> "What made it new jack swing was the snare. The snare had to be loud and obnoxious and not in tune with the rest of the stuff."
- **Source**: Industry interviews
- **Context**: Production technique

### Janet Jackson/Jimmy Jam — On *Control*
> "I think on Control, she realized that it was going to be about things she was thinking, as opposed to the first two albums were basically people just handing her music and saying 'sing'."
- **Source**: Rolling Stone
- **Date**: 2021
- **Context**: Creative independence

### Janet Jackson — On Leaving Her Father
> "I just wanted to get out of the house, get out from under my father, which was one of the most difficult things that I had to do, telling him that I didn't want to work with him again."
- **Source**: Rolling Stone
- **Context**: Breaking free

## Women in R&B

### Missy Elliott — On Production
> "I didn't want to just be an artist and let someone else have all that control over me. I knew I would have to produce."
- **Source**: Grammy.com roundtable
- **Context**: Taking creative control

### Missy Elliott — On Recognition
> "A lot of people don't know a lot of records that I've written or produced, so that's a highlight for me as a woman. I always said if a man would have done half the records that I've done we would know about it."

- **Context**: Gender disparity in credits

### Timbaland — On Missy Elliott
> "You will play her songs for 20 years; it will pass over generations. She's in that Sam Cooke, Miles Davis, Stevie Wonder [club]. She is the Aretha Franklin of hip-hop."

- **Context**: Legacy assessment

### Mary J. Blige — On Connection

### Mary J. Blige — On Story
