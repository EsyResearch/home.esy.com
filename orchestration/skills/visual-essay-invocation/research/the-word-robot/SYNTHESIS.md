## Executive Summary

The word "robot" is one of the 20th century's most consequential linguistic innovations—a single term that has shaped how humanity imagines, builds, and relates to artificial beings for over a century. This research synthesis distills findings from 22 verified sources across linguistics, history, technology, and cultural studies.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Word's DNA: Servitude and Rebellion

The word "robot" carries its origin story in its etymology. Derived from Czech "robota" (forced labor, corvée, serfdom), the term was deliberately chosen to evoke the condition of workers under feudal or industrial subjugation. Karel Čapek's brother Josef suggested the word specifically because it captured the play's theme: artificial workers who would eventually reject their servile condition.

**Insight**: The very first use of "robot" contained both the promise of artificial labor AND the fear of rebellion. This duality has persisted for 105 years.

### 2. Fiction Preceded Reality by Decades

For approximately 40 years (1920-1961), "robot" existed primarily as a fictional concept. Isaac Asimov's stories, Fritz Lang's Metropolis, and countless science fiction works shaped public expectations of what robots could be—before real robots existed in any meaningful form.

**Insight**: Public perception of robots was formed by storytellers, not engineers. This means fiction continues to influence how people respond to real robots.

### 3. Three Distinct Perception Eras

Research reveals three broad eras of robot perception:

| Era | Dominant Frame | Key Drivers |
|-----|----------------|-------------|
| **1920-1984** | Threat/Wonder | Fiction (R.U.R., Asimov, Terminator) |
| **1984-2002** | Industrial Anxiety | Factory automation, job displacement |
| **2002-Present** | Ambivalent Acceptance | Consumer robots, AI assistants |

### 4. The Roomba Effect

The iRobot Roomba (2002) may be the single most important perception-shifting product. By introducing robots as affordable, helpful, and benign household appliances, it transformed "robot" from something threatening to something domestic.

**Insight**: Function, not form, drove acceptance. The Roomba doesn't look like a sci-fi robot—it looks like a vacuum.

### 5. The Perception Paradox

Contemporary survey data reveals a consistent paradox:
- **Abstract fear**: People believe robots will disrupt society and take jobs
- **Personal confidence**: The same people don't believe their own job is at risk
- **Elite/public gap**: Corporate leaders are far more optimistic about AI/robots than the general public (93% vs. 58%)

**Insight**: Robot perception operates on two levels—societal concern and personal confidence. Most people worry about others, not themselves.

### 6. The Uncanny Valley Remains Relevant

Masahiro Mori's 1970 uncanny valley hypothesis continues to explain why some humanoid robots succeed (ASIMO) while others disturb (early Sophia). The closer to human without achieving it, the more discomfort.

**Insight**: Robot design is psychological as much as engineering. The "right" level of human-likeness varies by application.

### 7. "Robot" Is Expanding Beyond Physical Machines

With ChatGPT and AI assistants, the colloquial use of "robot" is expanding to include software-only entities. The original word meant physical artificial workers—now it increasingly includes disembodied AI.

**Insight**: The word's meaning is evolving faster than at any point since 1920. "Robot" may soon need subdivision: physical robots vs. AI "robots."
