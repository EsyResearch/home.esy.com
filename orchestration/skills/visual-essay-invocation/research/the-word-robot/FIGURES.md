## Figure 1: Karel Čapek (1890–1938)

### Biography
Czech writer, playwright, and journalist who introduced the word "robot" to the world through his 1920 play *R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots)*. Čapek was one of the most important Czech writers of the 20th century, known for his science fiction and satirical works that explored themes of technology, totalitarianism, and human nature.

### Key Contributions
- Wrote *R.U.R.* (premiered January 25, 1921 in Prague), introducing "robot" to global vocabulary
- Popularized science fiction themes in mainstream literature
- Explored dystopian themes of artificial workers and rebellion
- Coined "robot" with the etymological suggestion from his brother Josef

### Verified Quote
> "I did not invent the word 'robot,' my brother Josef did. It was like this: I was hesitating whether to call these artificial workers 'laborers' or 'labori'... Josef suggested 'robots.'"
> — Karel Čapek, *Lidové noviny* newspaper column, 1933

## Figure 2: Josef Čapek (1887–1945)

### Biography
Czech painter, writer, and poet. Elder brother of Karel Čapek. Suggested the word "robot" when Karel was struggling to name the artificial workers in his play. Josef was a prominent Cubist painter and later died in the Bergen-Belsen concentration camp.

### Key Contributions
- **Invented the word "robot"** — suggested it to Karel for R.U.R.
- Drew from Czech "robota" (forced labor, serfdom) for the term
- Contributed to Czech avant-garde art movement
- His linguistic contribution shaped the 20th century's most important technological term

### Verified Quote
> (Suggested "robot" from "robota" meaning corvée/serf labor)
> — Family correspondence, referenced in Karel Čapek's writings

### Photography Availability
- ✅ Multiple photographs available (Czech National Gallery)
- ✅ Self-portraits (as artist)
- ✅ Photos with brother Karel

### Era Representation
Czech Cubism, interwar Central European culture

## Figure 3: Isaac Asimov (1920–1992)

### Biography
Russian-born American writer, professor of biochemistry at Boston University, and one of the most prolific writers of all time. Known as the "father of robot fiction" for his extensive robot-themed stories that shaped public perception of robots for generations.

### Key Contributions
- Created the **Three Laws of Robotics** (1942, "Runaround")
- Coined the word "robotics" (first use in "Liar!" 1941)
- Wrote over 500 books including *I, Robot* collection
- Reframed robots as potentially benevolent, countering "Frankenstein complex"
- Established robot ethics as a literary and philosophical field

### Verified Quote
> "1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.
> 2. A robot must obey orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.
> 3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law."
> — Isaac Asimov, "Runaround" (1942)

### Photography Availability
- ✅ Extensive photo archive available
- ✅ Portraits from 1950s-1980s
- ✅ Photos at writing desk, lectures, conventions

### Era Representation
Golden Age of Science Fiction, American mid-century

## Figure 4: Fritz Lang (1890–1976)

### Biography
Austrian-German filmmaker who directed *Metropolis* (1927), featuring one of cinema's first and most iconic robot characters—the Maschinenmensch (Machine-Human), also known as Maria.

### Key Contributions
- Created the first iconic **cinematic robot** in *Metropolis* (1927)
- Established visual language for robots in film
- Influenced all subsequent robot design in cinema
- The Maschinenmensch became the template for feminine robot representation

### Verified Quote
> "I was deeply impressed by New York... I saw a street, lit as if in full daylight... the buildings seemed to be a vertical veil, a background to daily life."
> — Fritz Lang, on the inspiration for Metropolis (1927)

## Figure 5: Joseph Engelberger (1925–2015)

### Biography
American physicist, engineer, and entrepreneur known as the "father of robotics." Co-founded Unimation Inc. and developed the first industrial robot, Unimate, which was installed at General Motors in 1961.

### Key Contributions
- Created **Unimate** — first industrial robot (1956, deployed 1961)
- Founded Unimation Inc. — first robotics company
- Made robots practical industrial tools
- Later pioneered service robots for healthcare

### Verified Quote
> "The key is to keep making robots more capable and less expensive."
> — Joseph Engelberger (attributed)

### Photography Availability
- ✅ Photos with Unimate robot arm
- ✅ Factory floor deployment images
- ✅ Later portraits with service robots

### Era Representation

## Figure 6: Masahiro Mori (1927–present)

### Biography
Japanese roboticist and professor emeritus at Tokyo Institute of Technology. Proposed the "uncanny valley" concept in 1970, which explains why near-human robots can cause discomfort.

### Key Contributions
- Developed the **"Uncanny Valley" theory** (1970)
- Explained psychological response to humanoid robots
- Influenced robot design philosophy globally
- His work explains why some robots are accepted and others rejected

### Verified Quote
> "In climbing toward the goal of making robots appear human, our affinity for them increases until we come to a valley, which I call the uncanny valley."
> — Masahiro Mori, *Energy* magazine (1970)
