# Key Quotes: The Word "Essay"

## Meta-Quotes — Essayists on the Essay Form

### Michel de Montaigne (1533–1592)

> **"I am myself the matter of my book."**
> — *Essais*, "To the Reader" (1580)

*Context*: Opening address to readers. Montaigne declares the essay as self-examination.

---

> **"Que sais-je?"** (What do I know?)
> — Personal motto

*Context*: Montaigne had this inscribed on a medal. Encapsulates his skeptical approach — the essay as question, not answer.

---

> **"I present myself standing and lying, front and back, on the right side and the left, and in all my natural postures."**
> — *Essais*, "Of the Resemblance of Children to Fathers"

*Context*: The essay as complete self-presentation, hiding nothing.

---

### Francis Bacon (1561–1626)

> **"Some books are to be tasted, others to be swallowed, and some few to be chewed and digested."**
> — "Of Studies" (1625)

*Context*: Bacon on reading practices. The essay itself — short, pointed — is for tasting.

---

> **"Reading maketh a full man, conference a ready man, and writing an exact man."**
> — "Of Studies" (1625)

*Context*: Bacon's aphoristic style. Writing (the essay) as precision tool.

---

### Joseph Addison (1672–1719)

> **"I shall endeavour to enliven morality with wit, and to temper wit with morality."**
> — *The Spectator*, No. 10 (1711)

*Context*: The periodical essay's mission statement. Entertainment and instruction combined.

---

### Richard Steele (1672–1729)

> **"Reading is to the mind what exercise is to the body."**
> — *The Tatler*

*Context*: The essay as intellectual workout — short, regular, beneficial.

---

### Charles Lamb (1775–1834)

> **"I am determined my children shall be brought up in their father's religion, if they can find out what it is."**
> — *Essays of Elia*

*Context*: Lamb's whimsy and self-deprecation. The familiar essay's warmth.

---

### Ralph Waldo Emerson (1803–1882)

> **"A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds, adored by little statesmen and philosophers and divines."**
> — "Self-Reliance" (1841)

*Context*: The essay as permission to contradict oneself. Evolution of thought welcomed.

---

> **"To be yourself in a world that is constantly trying to make you something else is the greatest accomplishment."**
> — Attributed

*Context*: The essay as assertion of individual voice against conformity.

---

### Virginia Woolf (1882–1941)

> **"A good essay must have this permanent quality about it; it must draw its curtain round us, but it must be a curtain that shuts us in, not out."**
> — "The Modern Essay" (1922)

*Context*: The essay as enclosure — not escape, but immersion. The reader enters the writer's space.

---

> **"Of all forms of literature, however, the essay is the one which least calls for the use of long words. The principle which controls it is simply that it should give pleasure."**
> — "The Modern Essay" (1922)

*Context*: Simplicity as virtue. The essay's purpose is delight.

---

> **"There is no room for the impurities of literature in an essay. Somehow or other, by dint of labour or bounty of nature, or both combined, the essay must be pure—pure like water or pure like wine, but pure from dullness, deadness, and deposits of extraneous matter."**
> — "The Modern Essay" (1922)

*Context*: Purity as aesthetic ideal. The essay distilled, uncontaminated.

---

### George Orwell (1903–1950)

> **"Never use a long word where a short one will do."**
> — "Politics and the English Language" (1946)

*Context*: Rule 2 of six rules for clear prose. The essay as exercise in precision.

---

> **"If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out."**
> — "Politics and the English Language" (1946)

*Context*: Rule 3. Economy as ethic.

---

> **"Political language is designed to make lies sound truthful and murder respectable, and to give an appearance of solidity to pure wind."**
> — "Politics and the English Language" (1946)

*Context*: The essay as counter-propaganda. Clarity as resistance.

---

> **"Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous."**
> — "Politics and the English Language" (1946)

*Context*: Rule 6. Even rules must yield to decency.

---

### James Baldwin (1924–1987)

> **"Hatred, which could destroy so much, never failed to destroy the man who hated, and this was an immutable law."**
> — "Notes of a Native Son" (1955)

*Context*: Personal reflection as universal truth. The essay discovers moral law through experience.

---

> **"Not everything that is faced can be changed, but nothing can be changed until it is faced."**
> — Attributed

*Context*: The essay as confrontation. Change requires visibility.

---

### Joan Didion (1934–2021)

> **"We tell ourselves stories in order to live."**
> — "The White Album" (1979)

*Context*: Opening line. The essay examines the stories we construct — and questions them.

---

> **"We look for the sermon in the suicide, for the social or moral lesson in the murder of five. We interpret what we see, select the most workable of the multiple choices."**
> — "The White Album" (1979)

*Context*: The essay exposes our narrative-making compulsion.

---

> **"We live entirely, especially if we are writers, by the imposition of a narrative line upon disparate images, by the 'ideas' with which we have learned to freeze the shifting phantasmagoria which is our actual experience."**
> — "The White Album" (1979)

*Context*: The essay interrogates the narratives it creates.

---

> **"I write entirely to find out what I'm thinking."**
> — "Why I Write"

*Context*: The essay as discovery, not declaration. Return to Montaigne's "attempt."
