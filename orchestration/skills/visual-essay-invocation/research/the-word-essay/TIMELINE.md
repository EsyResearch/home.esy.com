# Timeline: The Word "Essay"

## Etymology Chain

### Latin Origins (Classical Era)
```
exigere (Latin)
├── Meaning: "to drive out; require, exact; examine, try, test"
├── Root: ex- (out) + agere (to drive)
└── Evolved to:
    
    exagium (Late Latin)
    ├── Meaning: "a weighing, a weight"
    ├── Sense: to weigh something, to balance
    └── Evolved to:

        essayer (Old French)
        ├── Meaning: "to try, to attempt, to test"
        ├── 16th century usage: attempt, practice, trial
        └── Adopted by:

            Essais (Montaigne, 1580)
            ├── Meaning: "Attempts" or "Trials"
            ├── Revolutionary: named the form after process, not product
            └── Borrowed into:

                Essay (English, c. 1597)
                ├── Via Francis Bacon's "Essayes"
                └── Retained meaning of "attempt" before becoming "short prose piece"
```

### The Semantic Journey

| Era | Word Form | Meaning | Usage |
|-----|-----------|---------|-------|
| Classical | *exigere* | to examine, test | Legal/commercial weighing |
| Late Latin | *exagium* | a weighing | Balance, measurement |
| Old French | *essayer* | to try, attempt | Testing, practicing |
| 1580 | *Essais* | Attempts | Montaigne's literary invention |
| 1597+ | *Essay* | Short prose work | Literary form |
| Modern | *Essay* | Multiple meanings | Academic, personal, video, etc. |

## Historical Milestones

### 1580 — THE BIRTH
**Michel de Montaigne publishes *Essais*, Books I & II**
- Printed by Simon Millanges in Bordeaux
- First use of "essai" for a literary form
- 496 pages (Book I) + 650 pages (Book II)
- Featured wood-engraved bands and initials
- Montaigne's titles listed on title page: "Chevalier de l'ordre du Roy"

*Significance*: The word "essay" enters literary vocabulary. Montaigne names his works after the process of thinking, not the product.

---

### 1582 — FIRST REVISION
**Second edition of *Essais* published in Bordeaux**
- Corrections and additions by Montaigne
- Also printed by Millanges

---

### 1588 — THE EXPANSION
**Third edition published in Paris by Abel L'Angelier**
- Introduces Book III (entirely new)
- Significant revisions to Books I & II
- Essay form gains complexity

---

### 1592 — MONTAIGNE'S DEATH
**Montaigne dies, leaving annotated "Bordeaux Copy"**
- Final revisions inscribed in margins
- Basis for all future editions
- Manuscript preserved in Bordeaux municipal library

---

### 1597 — ENGLISH ADOPTION
**Francis Bacon publishes *Essayes: Religious Meditations. Places of Persuasion and Dissuasion***
- First essays in English language
- Only 10 essays in first edition
- Concise, aphoristic style (contrast to Montaigne's meandering)
- Topics: Truth, Death, Revenge, Adversity

*Significance*: The essay crosses the English Channel. Bacon transforms the form — from introspection to counsel.

---

### 1603 — ENGLISH MONTAIGNE
**John Florio translates Montaigne's *Essais* into English**
- Shakespeare likely read this translation
- Influence on *The Tempest* documented
- Essay form spreads to English readers

---

### 1612 — BACON EXPANDS
**Second edition of Bacon's *Essays***
- Expanded to 38 essays
- Topics include: Gardens, Studies, Wisdom

---

### 1625 — BACON'S FINAL EDITION
**Third and final edition of *Essays***
- 58 essays total
- Definitive Baconian collection
- "Of Studies" includes famous reading quote

---

### 1709 — THE PERIODICAL ESSAY BEGINS
**Richard Steele founds *The Tatler***
- First periodical essay publication
- Essays for coffeehouse reading
- Short, accessible, frequent

---

### 1711–1712 — THE SPECTATOR ERA
**Joseph Addison and Richard Steele publish *The Spectator***
- Daily publication (555 issues)
- ~2,500 words per essay
- Introduced "Spectator Club" and Sir Roger de Coverley
- Circulation ~3,000, but readership much larger (coffeehouses)
- Aim: "enliven morality with wit, and to temper wit with morality"

*Significance*: The essay becomes a daily habit. Democratization of the form — from library to coffeehouse.

---

### 1714 — THE SPECTATOR REVIVED
**Brief revival of *The Spectator***
- 80 additional issues
- Addison primary author

---

### 1823 — THE PERSONAL ESSAY
**Charles Lamb publishes *Essays of Elia***
- Under pseudonym "Elia"
- Familiar, autobiographical, whimsical
- Famous essays: "A Dissertation upon Roast Pig"

*Significance*: The essay becomes intimate. Lamb's "Elia" creates persona-driven essay tradition.

---

### 1833 — LAMB'S LAST ESSAYS
**Lamb publishes *Last Essays of Elia***
- Continues personal essay tradition
- Cemented Romantic-era essay style

---

### 1836 — AMERICAN TRANSCENDENTALISM
**Ralph Waldo Emerson publishes *Nature***
- Essay as philosophical manifesto
- Transcendentalist movement begins

---

### 1841 — SELF-RELIANCE
**Emerson publishes *Essays: First Series***
- Includes "Self-Reliance"
- "A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds"
- American essay identity established

*Significance*: The essay becomes declaration. Emerson answers Montaigne's "What do I know?" with "Trust thyself."

---

### 1922 — MODERNIST THEORY
**Virginia Woolf publishes "The Modern Essay"**
- Meta-essay defining the form
- "Pure like water or pure like wine"
- Essay must "give pleasure"

*Significance*: Woolf theorizes the essay's essence — pleasure, purity, enclosure.

---

### 1929 — FEMINIST ESSAY
**Virginia Woolf publishes *A Room of One's Own***
- Essay/lecture hybrid
- "A woman must have money and a room of her own"
- Genre-bending masterwork

---

### 1946 — CLARITY AS POLITICS
**George Orwell publishes "Politics and the English Language"**
- Six rules for clear writing
- "Never use a long word where a short one will do"
- Essay as resistance to propaganda

*Significance*: Orwell makes prose style a moral and political question.

---

### 1955 — MORAL WITNESS
**James Baldwin publishes *Notes of a Native Son***
- Personal and political intertwined
- "Hatred... never failed to destroy the man who hated"
- Essay as Civil Rights testimony

*Significance*: The essay becomes moral reckoning — personal confession as public address.

---

### 1968 — NEW JOURNALISM
**Joan Didion publishes *Slouching Towards Bethlehem***
- California as subject and symptom
- Cool, precise, diagnostic prose
- Essay as cultural analysis

---

### 1979 — SELF-INTERROGATION
**Joan Didion publishes *The White Album***
- "We tell ourselves stories in order to live"
- Fragmentary, self-questioning structure
- Essay turns knife inward

*Significance*: Didion closes the loop from Montaigne — the essay questions its own narratives.

---

### 2005 — GRIEF ESSAY
**Joan Didion publishes *The Year of Magical Thinking***
- Essay as grief work
- Memoir/essay hybrid
- National Book Award

---

### 2000s–2020s — DIGITAL EVOLUTION
**The essay expands into new forms**
- Blog posts (personal essay online)
- Video essays (Every Frame a Painting, Nerdwriter, etc.)
- Podcast essays (narrative non-fiction audio)
- Newsletter essays (Substack era)

*Significance*: The word "essay" now applies to visual and audio media. The semantic range expands while core meaning (attempt, exploration) persists.
