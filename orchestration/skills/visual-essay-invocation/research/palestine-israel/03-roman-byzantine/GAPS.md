# Research Gaps: Roman/Byzantine & "Palaestina" Naming

> **Module**: 03-roman-byzantine
> **Last Updated**: December 23, 2024

---

## Overview

This module covers the Roman and Byzantine periods (~63 BCE - 638 CE), where key contested areas include:
- Casualty figures from the revolts (ancient sources exaggerate)
- The extent and nature of Jewish "exile"
- The motivation behind renaming to "Syria Palaestina"
- Continuity of Jewish population in the region

---

## Tier 1 Gaps: Do Not Claim

### Gap 1.1: Precise Casualty Figures

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | That specific casualty numbers from ancient sources are accurate |
| **Why Insufficient** | Ancient historians routinely exaggerated numbers for dramatic effect |
| **What We Know** | Josephus claims 1.1 million dead in 70 CE; Cassius Dio claims 580,000 in 135 CE |
| **What We Don't Know** | Actual casualties (likely much lower but still catastrophic) |
| **Recommendation** | Use "massive casualties" or "catastrophic losses"; cite ancient figures with "reportedly" or "ancient sources claim" |

### Gap 1.2: Complete Jewish Expulsion

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | That all Jews were expelled from the region in 70 or 135 CE |
| **Why Insufficient** | Archaeological and textual evidence shows continued Jewish presence |
| **What We Know** | Jews were banned from Jerusalem specifically; Galilee remained a Jewish center |
| **What We Don't Know** | Precise population figures pre- and post-revolts |
| **Scholarly Consensus** | "The diaspora was gradual; Jews remained in the region, especially Galilee" |
| **Recommendation** | State that Jewish population was "drastically reduced" but "not eliminated"; note Galilee continuation |

### Gap 1.3: Temple Destruction Date Precision

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | That the Temple was destroyed on exactly the 9th of Av |
| **Why Insufficient** | Traditional date; historical precision difficult to verify |
| **What We Know** | Temple was destroyed in August 70 CE during Titus's siege |
| **What We Don't Know** | Exact calendar correspondence |
| **Recommendation** | Use "traditionally dated to 9th of Av" or simply "August 70 CE" |

## Tier 2 Gaps: Scholarly Debate

### Gap 2.1: Hadrian's Motivation for Renaming

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | Why Hadrian renamed Judaea to Syria Palaestina |
| **Position A** | Deliberate erasure — using name of Jewish enemies (Philistines) to humiliate |
| **Proponents** | Traditional view; some Israeli scholars |
| **Position B** | Administrative reorganization — term already in Greek use (Herodotus) |
| **Proponents** | Some revisionist scholars |
| **Position C** | Both — punitive intent using available terminology |
| **Current View** | Context of brutal suppression suggests punitive element; but term predated Hadrian |
| **Esy Approach** | Note the renaming occurred; acknowledge debate on motivation; don't claim certainty on intent |

### Gap 2.2: Cause of Bar Kokhba Revolt

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | What specifically triggered the revolt |
| **Position A** | Hadrian's plan to build Aelia Capitolina with temple to Jupiter |
| **Position B** | Ban on circumcision (some sources) |
| **Position C** | Combination of religious and political grievances |
| **Evidence** | Various ancient sources give different emphases |
| **Esy Approach** | Present multiple causes without claiming one was primary |

### Gap 2.3: Bar Kokhba as Messiah

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | Whether Bar Kokhba claimed messianic status |
| **Position A** | Rabbi Akiva proclaimed him messiah; Bar Kokhba accepted |
| **Position B** | Bar Kokhba was military/political leader; messianic claims were supporters' interpretation |
| **Evidence** | Talmudic sources (later); Bar Kokhba coins (no explicit messiah claim) |
| **Esy Approach** | Note Rabbi Akiva's support; be cautious about Bar Kokhba's own claims |

### Gap 2.4: Scale of Byzantine Samaritan Revolts

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | Casualty and impact figures for Samaritan revolts |
| **Evidence** | Ancient sources claim tens of thousands killed |
| **Issue** | Same exaggeration problems as Jewish revolt sources |
| **Esy Approach** | Note revolts occurred and were brutally suppressed; avoid specific figures |

### Gap 2.5: Persian Invasion and Jewish Alliance

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | Extent of Jewish collaboration with Persian invaders (614 CE) |
| **Position A** | Jews actively allied with Persians; helped massacre Christians |
| **Position B** | Some Jewish cooperation; massacre claims exaggerated by Christian sources |
| **Evidence** | Christian sources (potentially biased); archaeological evidence limited |
| **Esy Approach** | Note that some Jews allied with Persians; be cautious about massacre claims |

## Tier 3 Gaps: Politically Contested

### Gap 3.1: "Exile" Narrative

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Issue** | The framing of 70/135 CE as "the exile" |
| **Historical Evidence** | Diaspora existed before 70 CE; Jews remained in region after 135 CE |
| **Israeli/Zionist Use** | "Exile and Return" narrative — Jews exiled, returned in modern era |
| **Palestinian/Arab Use** | Challenges the narrative — Jews left voluntarily; Arabs always there |
| **Scholarly Position** | Diaspora was gradual process, not single event; some Jews always remained; "exile" is theological/narrative concept more than historical event |
| **Esy Approach** | Use "diaspora" rather than "exile"; note that Jews remained in region; present demographic change without ideological framing |

### Gap 3.2: The "Syria Palaestina" Naming

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Issue** | Whether the Roman renaming has modern political implications |
| **Historical Evidence** | Romans renamed the province; this is documented fact |
| **Israeli Political Use** | Sometimes used to argue "Palestine" is Roman imposition, not authentic identity |
| **Palestinian Political Use** | Shows ancient use of "Palestine" name |
| **Scholarly Position** | Name was applied by Romans; predated in Greek usage; has no bearing on modern political legitimacy either way |
| **Esy Approach** | Present the naming as historical fact; note its origins; explicitly state it doesn't adjudicate modern claims |

### Gap 3.3: Jewish "Right of Return" to Jerusalem

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Issue** | Whether Roman ban on Jews in Jerusalem has modern implications |
| **Historical Evidence** | Jews were banned from Jerusalem (except 9th of Av) from 135 CE |
| **Modern Use** | Sometimes invoked in discussions of Jewish connection to Jerusalem |
| **Scholarly Position** | The ban was real; it was eventually lifted; Jews returned to Jerusalem in various periods |
| **Esy Approach** | Present the ban as historical fact; note it didn't last forever; avoid modern political extrapolation |

### Gap 3.4: Continuity Claims

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Issue** | Which modern group can claim "continuity" with ancient population |
| **Historical Evidence** | Both Jews and Arabs have historical connections to the region |
| **Political Use** | Both sides claim continuous presence to legitimize modern claims |
| **Scholarly Position** | Population history is complex; continuity exists but is not exclusive to one group |
| **Esy Approach** | Note Jewish continuity in Galilee; note that other populations also remained; avoid adjudicating who has "more" continuity |

## Narrative Framing Risks

### Risk 1: "Exile and Return" Teleology

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Framing Roman period as "the exile" that ended in 1948 |
| **Mitigation** | Use neutral demographic language; note diaspora was gradual; note Jews remained |
| **Recommended Language** | "Jewish population in the region was drastically reduced" not "Jews were exiled" |

### Risk 2: Temple Destruction as Only Loss

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Focusing solely on Jewish suffering without noting other populations |
| **Mitigation** | Note Samaritan suppression under Byzantines; note various populations affected by wars |
| **Recommended Language** | Acknowledge multiple communities affected by Roman/Byzantine policies |

### Risk 3: "Palestine" as Delegitimizing

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Using "Roman imposition" of name to suggest Palestinian identity is inauthentic |
| **Mitigation** | Note that all place names have historical origins; modern identity isn't determined by Roman naming |
| **Recommended Language** | "The name Palestine derives from..." not "Palestine is a Roman invention to..." |

### Risk 4: Christian Holy Land as "True" Identity

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Presenting Byzantine period as the region's "authentic" Christian character |
| **Mitigation** | Present Christianization as one era among many; note pre-Christian and post-Christian periods |

### Risk 5: Josephus as Unbiased Source

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Treating Josephus as neutral eyewitness without noting his complex position |
| **Mitigation** | Note Josephus wrote under Roman patronage after defecting |
| **Recommended Language** | "Josephus, writing under Roman patronage, describes..." |

## Terminology Guidance

| Term | Issue | Recommendation |
|------|-------|----------------|
| Exile | Theological/narrative concept | Use "diaspora" for demographic description |
| Destruction | Can be loaded | Use for Temple (accurate); be careful with "destruction of Judaism" |
| Palestine | Roman name | Historically accurate from 135 CE; note origins |
| Holy Land | Christian concept | Use when discussing Byzantine period; note it's a Christian framing |
| Judaea | Roman name before 135 | Accurate for pre-135 CE period |
| Jew/Jewish | Religious/ethnic | Appropriate for this period; "Judaism" as religion emerged post-Temple |
| Second Temple | Period marker | Use "Second Temple period" for 516 BCE - 70 CE |

## Key Principle for This Module

**The diaspora was gradual, not a single event of expulsion.**

Jews were banned from Jerusalem but not from the entire region. Jewish communities continued in Galilee and produced major religious works (Mishnah, Jerusalem Talmud). The idea of "complete exile in 70/135 CE" is a theological narrative, not a historical description.

At the same time, the revolts were catastrophic. Massive loss of life, destruction of the Temple, and transformation of Jewish religious life. This trauma is real and shapes Jewish memory. Both things are true: Jews remained AND the revolts were devastating.
