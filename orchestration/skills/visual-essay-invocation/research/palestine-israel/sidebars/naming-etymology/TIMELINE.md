# Timeline: Names of the Region

> **Module**: sidebars/naming-etymology
> **Last Updated**: December 23, 2024
> **Coverage**: Bronze Age through Present

## Naming Timeline

### Bronze Age (~1950-1150 BCE)

#### ~1950-1550 BCE — "Canaan" / "Kinahni" / "Retenu"
**Name**: Canaan (Hebrew: Kənaʿan), Kinahni (Akkadian), Retenu (Egyptian)
**Used By**: Egyptian New Kingdom, Akkadian diplomatic correspondence, Canaanite peoples
**Geographic Scope**: Levant region — present-day Lebanon, Syria, Jordan, Israel/Palestine
**Significance**: Earliest regional names in written records; Amarna letters use "Kinahni"
**Etymology**: Disputed — possibly from Hurrian for "purple" (cf. Greek "Phoenician"), or Semitic root for "lowlands"
**Sources**: Amarna letters, Egyptian Middle Kingdom records, Biblical texts

#### ~1150 BCE — "Peleset" First Attested
**Name**: Peleset (Egyptian hieroglyphic)
**Used By**: Egyptian records at Medinet Habu temple
**Geographic Scope**: Coastal region settled by Sea Peoples
**Significance**: First recorded appearance of root that becomes "Palestine"
**Context**: Ramesses III (~1186-1155 BCE) records battles with Sea Peoples including Peleset
**Sources**: Medinet Habu temple inscriptions

---

### Iron Age (~1150-63 BCE)

#### ~1200-1000 BCE — "Philistia" / "Peleshet"
**Name**: Philistia (Greek), Peleshet (Hebrew: פלשת)
**Used By**: Hebrew Bible, Greek writers
**Geographic Scope**: Southern coastal plain — Gaza, Ashkelon, Ashdod, Ekron, Gath (Philistine Pentapolis)
**Significance**: Name applies specifically to Philistine territory, not entire region
**Etymology**: From Peleset/Philistines — people of likely Aegean origin
**Sources**: Hebrew Bible, archaeological evidence from Philistine cities

#### ~800 BCE — "Palashtu" / "Pilistu"
**Name**: Palashtu, Palastu, Pilistu (Akkadian)
**Used By**: Assyrian Empire (Adad-nirari III, Esarhaddon)
**Geographic Scope**: Unclear boundaries — used in tribute records
**Significance**: Assyrian variant of the Peleset/Philistine name
**Sources**: Nimrud Slab (~800 BCE), Esarhaddon treaty tablets

---

### Classical Antiquity (~5th c. BCE - 135 CE)

#### ~450 BCE — "Palaistinē" (Herodotus)
**Name**: Palaistinē (Greek: Παλαιστίνη)
**Used By**: Herodotus in *The Histories*
**Geographic Scope**: "District of Syria" — area between Phoenicia and Egypt, including Judean mountains and Jordan Valley
**Significance**: **First clear use referring to entire region**, not just coastal Philistia
**Context**: Herodotus describes circumcision practices of "Syrians in Palestine"
**Sources**: Herodotus, *Histories* Book 2, 3, 4, 7

#### ~350 BCE — Aristotle's Usage
**Name**: Palaistinē (Greek)
**Used By**: Aristotle in *Meteorology*
**Geographic Scope**: Similar to Herodotus — includes Dead Sea
**Significance**: Continued Greek usage; Herodotus's terminology became canonical
**Sources**: Aristotle, *Meteorology*

#### ~63 BCE - 135 CE — "Judaea" (Roman Province)
**Name**: Iudaea (Latin), Judea (English)
**Used By**: Roman administration
**Geographic Scope**: Southern portion of former Hasmonean kingdom
**Significance**: Roman province name derived from "Judah" — explicitly Jewish association
**Administrative Status**: Client kingdom, then Roman province (6 CE)
**Sources**: Roman administrative records, Josephus

---

### Roman/Byzantine Period (135-636 CE)

#### 135 CE — "Syria Palaestina" (Hadrian's Renaming)
**Name**: Syria Palaestina (Latin)
**Used By**: Roman Empire, Emperor Hadrian
**Geographic Scope**: Expanded province incorporating Judaea
**Significance**: **Administrative renaming after Bar Kokhba revolt** — name changed specifically in response to Jewish rebellion (unique in Roman history)
**Context**: Hadrian also renamed Jerusalem to "Aelia Capitolina," banned Jews from city
**Scholarly Debate**:
- Seth Schwartz: "Celebrate the de-Judaization of the province"
- David Jacobson: Practical choice reflecting larger territory
- Ronald Syme: May have preceded revolt, reflecting Hadrian's views on Jews
**Sources**: Roman records, Eusebius, Dio Cassius

#### ~390 CE — Byzantine Administrative Divisions
**Name**: Palaestina Prima, Palaestina Secunda, Palaestina Tertia (Salutaris)
**Used By**: Byzantine Empire
**Geographic Scope**:
- Prima: Judea, Samaria, coast, Idumea (capital: Caesarea)
- Secunda: Galilee, Golan, western Decapolis (capital: Scythopolis)
- Tertia/Salutaris: Negev, southern Transjordan (capital: Petra)
**Significance**: Three-province administrative division under Diocese of the East
**Sources**: Byzantine administrative records, Notitia Dignitatum

---

### Early Islamic Period (636-1099 CE)

#### ~636-640 CE — "Jund Filastin" Established
**Name**: Jund Filasṭīn (Arabic: جُنْد فِلَسْطِيْن)
**Used By**: Rashidun, Umayyad, Abbasid Caliphates
**Geographic Scope**: Roughly Palaestina Prima + Tertia — Samaria, Judea, coastal plain from Mt. Carmel to Gaza (Galilee excluded — part of Jund al-Urdunn)
**Significance**: **Continuity of "Palestine" under Islamic rule**; Muslims maintained Byzantine administrative geography
**Capital**: Initially Ludd (Lydda), then Ramla (founded ~705-715 CE)
**Administrative Structure**: Military district (jund) with 11 sub-districts (kura)
**Sources**: Arab geographers (al-Muqaddasi, al-Istakhri), Umayyad papyri from Nessana

#### ~638 CE — "Al-Quds" for Jerusalem
**Name**: Al-Quds al-Sharif (Arabic: القدس الشريف, "The Holy")
**Used By**: Islamic administration, Arabic speakers
**Geographic Scope**: Jerusalem specifically
**Significance**: Islamic sacred name for Jerusalem, distinct from administrative terminology
**Sources**: Islamic historical texts, administrative records

---

### Crusader & Mamluk Period (1099-1517 CE)

#### 1099-1291 — "Kingdom of Jerusalem" / "Holy Land"
**Name**: Kingdom of Jerusalem, Terra Sancta (Holy Land)
**Used By**: Crusaders, European Christians
**Geographic Scope**: Crusader-controlled territories (varying boundaries)
**Significance**: European Christian naming conventions; "Palestine" continued in geographic usage
**Sources**: Crusader chronicles, European maps

#### 1260-1517 — Mamluk Period
**Name**: Filastin continued informally; administratively part of Mamluk Sultanate
**Used By**: Mamluk administration
**Geographic Scope**: Part of Syrian provinces
**Significance**: Name persisted in geographic usage without formal administrative status
**Sources**: Mamluk administrative records, Arab geographers

---

### Ottoman Period (1517-1917)

#### 1517-1864 — Ottoman Administrative Units
**Name**: Various sanjaks (districts) — no unified "Palestine" administrative unit
**Used By**: Ottoman Empire
**Geographic Scope**: Region divided among Damascus, Beirut vilayets and independent sanjaks
**Key Units**:
- Sanjak of Jerusalem
- Sanjak of Nablus
- Sanjak of Acre
**Significance**: "Palestine" not an Ottoman administrative term; used informally in European and some Arabic sources
**Sources**: Ottoman administrative records

#### 1872-1917 — "Mutasarrifate of Jerusalem"
**Name**: Kudüs-i Şerif Mutasarrıflığı (Ottoman Turkish)
**Used By**: Ottoman administration (elevated status)
**Geographic Scope**: Jerusalem, Hebron, Jaffa, Gaza, Beersheba
**Significance**: **Special administrative status** — reported directly to Constantinople (not Damascus); established partly to counter European influence over holy sites
**Context**: Many documents of this period refer to the Mutasarrifate as "Palestine" — aligning with European usage
**Note**: The three sanjaks (Jerusalem, Nablus, Acre) together roughly corresponded to what Europeans called "Palestine"
**Sources**: Ottoman records, European diplomatic correspondence

---

### Mandate Period (1917-1948)

#### 1920-1948 — "Palestine" / "Mandatory Palestine"
**Name**: Palestine (English), فلسطين Filasṭīn (Arabic), פלשתינה א"י Palestina (E.Y.) (Hebrew — with "Eretz Yisrael" added)
**Used By**: British Mandate administration, League of Nations
**Geographic Scope**: Roughly the three Ottoman sanjaks combined — defined borders established 1922
**Significance**: **First modern administrative use of "Palestine"** as unified territory with defined boundaries
**Official Status**: League of Nations Class A Mandate
**Languages**: English, Arabic, Hebrew all official
**Note**: Hebrew currency and stamps included "א"י" (Eretz Yisrael) alongside Palestine
**Sources**: League of Nations records, British Mandate documents

---

### Post-1948 Period

#### 1948 — "Israel" Declared
**Name**: Medinat Yisrael (Hebrew: מְדִינַת יִשְׂרָאֵל), State of Israel
**Used By**: Israeli government, international recognition
**Geographic Scope**: Territory within 1949 armistice lines (expanded in 1967)
**Significance**: Jewish state established; name "Israel" from biblical patriarch Jacob/Israel
**Sources**: Israeli Declaration of Independence, UN records

#### 1948-1967 — Divided Territories
**Names Used**:
- **West Bank** (الضفة الغربية): Jordanian-annexed territory west of Jordan River (1950-1967)
- **Gaza Strip**: Egyptian-administered territory (1948-1967)
- **Judea and Samaria** (יהודה ושומרון): Israeli term for West Bank (historical biblical regions)
**Significance**: Different names reflect different political claims and administrations
**Sources**: Jordanian, Egyptian, Israeli administrative records

#### 1964/1988/2012 — "State of Palestine"
**Name**: Dawlat Filasṭīn (Arabic: دولة فلسطين), State of Palestine
**Used By**: Palestine Liberation Organization, Palestinian Authority, UN
**Key Dates**:
- 1964: PLO founded
- 1988: Palestinian Declaration of Independence (Algiers)
- 2012: UN General Assembly grants "non-member observer state" status
**Geographic Scope (claimed)**: West Bank, Gaza Strip, East Jerusalem
**Significance**: Palestinian national identity crystallized around "Palestine" name
**Sources**: PLO documents, UN resolutions

## Summary: Key Naming Transitions

| Period | Primary Name(s) | Administrative Status | Used By |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|---------|
| Bronze Age | Canaan, Retenu | Egyptian vassal region | Egyptians, Canaanites |
| ~1150 BCE | Peleset | Sea Peoples settlement | Egyptians (records) |
| Iron Age | Philistia, Israel, Judah | Independent kingdoms | Various |
| 5th c. BCE | Palaistinē | Geographic term | Greek writers |
| 63 BCE-135 CE | Judaea | Roman province | Rome |
| 135-636 CE | Syria Palaestina, then divided | Roman/Byzantine province(s) | Rome/Byzantium |
| 636-1099 | Jund Filastin | Islamic military district | Caliphates |
| 1099-1291 | Kingdom of Jerusalem | Crusader state | Crusaders |
| 1517-1917 | Various sanjaks | Ottoman administrative units | Ottoman Empire |
| 1920-1948 | Palestine | British Mandate | Britain/League of Nations |
| 1948-present | Israel / Palestine | State / Disputed territories | Various |

## Etymology Summary

```
Peleset (Egyptian, ~1150 BCE)
    ↓
Peleshet (Hebrew) / Palashtu (Assyrian)
    ↓
Palaistinē (Greek, 5th c. BCE — Herodotus)
    ↓
Palaestina (Latin — Roman administrative use, 135 CE)
    ↓
Filastin (Arabic, 7th c. CE)
    ↓
Palestine (English, via Latin/Greek)
```

**Core Etymology**: Named after the Philistines (Peleset), a people of likely Aegean origin who settled the southern coast ~1200-1150 BCE. The Greek form was applied to the broader region by Herodotus, then adopted administratively by Romans after 135 CE.
