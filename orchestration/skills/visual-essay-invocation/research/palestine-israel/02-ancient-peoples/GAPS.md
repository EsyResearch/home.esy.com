# Research Gaps: Ancient Peoples (Canaan, Israelites, Philistines, Empires)

> **Module**: 02-ancient-peoples
> **Last Updated**: December 23, 2024

---

## Overview

This module covers the Iron Age (~1200-332 BCE), a period where:
- Religious traditions make historical claims
- Modern national identities invoke ancient peoples as ancestors
- Scholarly debate is active and sometimes politicized
- Genetic evidence complicates simple ancestry narratives

**Critical principle**: Ancient peoples do NOT map neatly onto modern national identities.

---

## Tier 1 Gaps: Do Not Claim

### Gap 1.1: Direct Lineage Claims

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | That any modern national group is the "direct descendant" of a single ancient people |
| **Why Insufficient** | Genetic evidence shows extensive admixture; all modern Levantine populations have mixed ancestry |
| **What We Know** | Modern Jews, Palestinians, Lebanese, and other Levantine peoples share substantial Canaanite ancestry |
| **What We Don't Know** | Precise proportions vary; other ancestral components also present |
| **Recommendation** | Use "share ancestry with" not "descend from"; emphasize mixture over lineage |

### Gap 1.2: Biblical Conquest as Historical Fact

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | That the biblical conquest of Canaan occurred as described in Joshua |
| **Why Insufficient** | Archaeological evidence contradicts large-scale conquest model |
| **What We Know** | Israelites emerged gradually in highlands; many "conquered" cities show no destruction layer at relevant time |
| **What We Don't Know** | Specific mechanisms of Israelite ethnogenesis |
| **Scholarly Consensus** | "The earliest Israelites were essentially Canaanites" — emergence, not conquest |
| **Recommendation** | Present archaeological evidence; note biblical narrative separately as religious tradition |

### Gap 1.3: Canaanite "Extinction"

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | That Canaanites "disappeared" or were "replaced" by Israelites |
| **Why Insufficient** | Genetic evidence shows substantial continuity |
| **What We Know** | "No archaeological evidence has so far been found to support widespread destruction of Canaanite cities between the Bronze and Iron Ages" |
| **What We Don't Know** | Specific social processes of identity transformation |
| **Recommendation** | Emphasize continuity and cultural transformation, not replacement |

### Gap 1.4: Philistine "Extermination"

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | That Philistines were destroyed or driven out |
| **Why Insufficient** | Genetic evidence shows rapid integration with local population |
| **What We Know** | Philistine European-related ancestry disappears within generations due to intermarriage |
| **What We Don't Know** | Specific timeline of cultural assimilation |
| **Recommendation** | Frame as integration/absorption, not extermination |

## Tier 2 Gaps: Scholarly Debate

### Gap 2.1: Scale of United Monarchy

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | The size and nature of David and Solomon's kingdom |
| **Position A (Maximalist)** | Large kingdom as described in biblical texts; Jerusalem was major center |
| **Proponents** | Yosef Garfinkel, Eilat Mazar (Hebrew University) |
| **Position B (Minimalist)** | Small chiefdom at best; biblical accounts are later literary constructions |
| **Proponents** | Copenhagen School (Thomas Thompson, Philip Davies) |
| **Position C (Moderate)** | David/Solomon were real but ruled a modest polity; expansion came later |
| **Proponents** | Israel Finkelstein (Tel Aviv University) |
| **Current Evidence** | Tel Dan Stele confirms "House of David" existed; scale remains debated |
| **Esy Approach** | Note that David and Solomon likely existed; present debate on kingdom's scale without adjudication |

### Gap 2.2: Timing of Israelite Emergence

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | When distinct "Israelite" identity emerged |
| **Position A** | By ~1200 BCE (Merneptah Stele references "Israel") |
| **Position B** | Gradually during Iron Age I (~1200-1000 BCE) |
| **Position C** | Not until Iron Age II (~9th-8th century BCE) as political entity |
| **Current Evidence** | Merneptah Stele (~1207 BCE) mentions "Israel" as a people |
| **Esy Approach** | Note earliest reference (~1207 BCE); emphasize gradual emergence of distinct identity |

### Gap 2.3: Philistine Precise Origins

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | Exactly where the Philistines came from |
| **Position A** | Mycenaean Greece |
| **Position B** | Crete (Caphtor in biblical texts) |
| **Position C** | Cyprus |
| **Position D** | Anatolia |
| **Current Evidence** | Genetic and ceramic evidence points to "Aegean/Mediterranean" broadly |
| **Esy Approach** | Use "likely Aegean or Mediterranean origin"; avoid claiming specific homeland |

### Gap 2.4: Samaritan Origins

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | Whether Samaritans descend from Israelites or Assyrian settlers |
| **Position A (Samaritan tradition)** | Direct descendants of northern Israelites who remained |
| **Position B (Jewish tradition)** | Descendants of Assyrian settlers who adopted local religion |
| **Position C (Scholarly)** | Complex mixture of both populations |
| **Current Evidence** | Genetic studies show Samaritans share ancestry with Jews |
| **Esy Approach** | Note the debate; present evidence of shared ancestry |

### Gap 2.5: Extent of Exile Deportations

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Claim** | What proportion of population was deported in Assyrian/Babylonian conquests |
| **Position A** | Majority deported (traditional reading) |
| **Position B** | Elite deportation only; majority remained |
| **Current Evidence** | Archaeological evidence suggests continuity of habitation |
| **Esy Approach** | Note that deportations occurred but were not total population replacement |

## Tier 3 Gaps: Politically Contested

### Gap 3.1: Who Are the "Original" Inhabitants

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Issue** | Ancient presence used to claim modern territorial rights |
| **Historical Evidence** | Region continuously inhabited for millennia; no single "original" people after initial human migration |
| **Israeli Political Use** | Ancient Israelite presence invoked for Jewish historical connection |
| **Palestinian Political Use** | Canaanite ancestry and continuous presence invoked for Palestinian rootedness |
| **Scholarly Position** | Both modern populations share ancient ancestry; ancient presence does not confer modern political rights |
| **Esy Approach** | Present genetic evidence of shared ancestry; explicitly note that ancient history does not determine modern claims |

### Gap 3.2: Merneptah Stele Interpretation

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Issue** | First mention of "Israel" used in political arguments |
| **Historical Evidence** | The stele (~1207 BCE) mentions "Israel" as a people in Canaan |
| **Political Use** | Cited as evidence of "ancient Jewish presence" |
| **Scholarly Position** | Describes an entity called "Israel" but says nothing about territory, religion, or continuity to present |
| **Esy Approach** | Present the stele as archaeological evidence; note it identifies a people, not a state; avoid extrapolating to modern claims |

### Gap 3.3: Philistine-Palestine Naming Connection

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Issue** | Whether Philistine etymology implies Palestinian connection to ancient Philistines |
| **Historical Evidence** | "Palestine" derives from "Philistine" via Greek/Roman usage |
| **Scholarly Position** | This is a naming convention imposed by Greeks/Romans; modern Palestinians are not descendants of Philistines specifically |
| **Political Use** | Sometimes used to delegitimize Palestinian identity ("you're named after invaders") |
| **Esy Approach** | Explain etymology clearly; note that place names don't determine identity; modern Palestinians share Canaanite (not Philistine) ancestry |

### Gap 3.4: "Land Without a People"

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Issue** | Whether the region was "empty" at any point |
| **Historical Evidence** | Continuous habitation throughout all periods covered in this module |
| **Political Use** | Early Zionist slogan "A land without a people for a people without a land" |
| **Scholarly Position** | The land was never without inhabitants; slogan was political rhetoric, not historical fact |
| **Esy Approach** | Document continuous population throughout; note the slogan as political rhetoric in later module (06-nationalism) |

## Narrative Framing Risks

### Risk 1: Presenting Israelite Emergence as "Invasion"

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Framing gradual emergence as external conquest reinforces both religious and political narratives |
| **Mitigation** | Emphasize scholarly consensus of indigenous emergence from Canaanite population |
| **Recommended Language** | "Israelites emerged from within Canaanite culture" not "Israelites conquered Canaan" |

### Risk 2: "Genetic Rights"

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Genetic ancestry data being used to argue for territorial rights |
| **Mitigation** | Explicitly state that genetic ancestry does not confer political claims |
| **Recommended Language** | Add explicit statement: "Ancient ancestry does not determine modern political rights" |

### Risk 3: Anachronistic Identity Labels

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Using modern identity terms for ancient peoples |
| **Mitigation** | Use period-appropriate terms |
| **Terminology** | Use "ancient Israelites" not "ancient Jews"; "Judahites" for kingdom inhabitants; never "Palestinians" for ancient peoples |

### Risk 4: Empire as "Good" or "Bad"

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Framing Assyrian/Babylonian empires as purely destructive villains |
| **Mitigation** | Present empires neutrally; note both destruction and continuity |
| **Recommended Language** | "Assyria conquered" not "Assyria destroyed"; note that populations continued |

### Risk 5: Teleological Narrative

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Risk** | Presenting ancient history as inevitably leading to modern conflict |
| **Mitigation** | Treat each period on its own terms; avoid foreshadowing modern conflict |
| **Recommended Language** | Avoid "this would later become..." framing |

## Terminology Guidance

| Term | Issue | Recommendation |
|------|-------|----------------|
| Israelites | Ancient vs. modern | Always use "ancient Israelites" or "Iron Age Israelites" |
| Judahites | Kingdom inhabitants | Use for southern kingdom population |
| Jews | Religious/ethnic identity | Anachronistic before Persian period; use "Judahites" or "Judeans" for earlier |
| Canaanites | Ancient peoples | Use for Bronze/Early Iron Age; note continuity into later populations |
| Philistines | Ancient peoples | Use for Iron Age coastal population; note integration |
| Palestinians | Modern national identity | NEVER use for ancient peoples |
| Hebrews | Biblical term | Avoid unless quoting; prefer "Israelites" |
| Tribes of Israel | Religious tradition | Use with caution; frame as tradition, not verified history |

## Key Principle for This Module

**Ancient peoples do NOT map onto modern identities.**

The Canaanites became the Israelites became the Jews — but also became the Samaritans, and also contributed ancestry to modern Palestinians, Lebanese, and other Levantine peoples. There is no clean line from ancient to modern. Population history is mixture and transformation, not replacement and continuity.

Any framing that suggests "People X were here first, therefore they have rights" should be avoided. Ancient history informs our understanding but does not adjudicate modern politics.
