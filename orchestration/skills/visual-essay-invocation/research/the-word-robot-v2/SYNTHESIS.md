## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Fiction Preceded and Shaped Reality

The word existed for 40+ years before functional robots did. During this gap, fiction (ÄŒapek, Asimov, Lang, pulp magazines) programmed public expectations. When real robots arrived, they stepped into a pre-built cultural container.

**Implication for Essay**: The narrative should emphasize how imagination created the template reality had to fill.

### Finding 2: Fear and Utility Cycle Endlessly

Every era shows the same pattern:
1. New capability emerges
2. Fear peaks (cultural products crystallize anxiety)
3. Domestication normalizes
4. Next capability emerges, restart

**Examples**:
- 1920s: R.U.R. rebellion â†’ normalized factory machines (1960s)
- 1984: Terminator apocalypse â†’ normalized Roomba (2002)
- 2023: ChatGPT anxiety â†’ ongoing normalization

**Implication for Essay**: Structure chapters around this fear/acceptance cycle.

### Finding 3: The Word Is Expanding Beyond Bodies

2022â€“2025 shows a semantic shift: "robot" now applies to disembodied AI (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.). The word is unmooring from its original mechanical connotation.

**Implications**:
- Historical purists: robot = embodied
- Popular usage: robot = any artificial agent
- The essay should acknowledge this tension

### Finding 4: Human-Robot Intimacy Is No Longer Speculative

Research by Levy (2007), Devlin (2018), Turkle (2011), and others shows:
- Emotional attachment to robots is real and measurable
- Sexual/romantic robot relationships exist now (companion bots, intimacy tech)
- Ethical frameworks are emerging but incomplete
- Cultural attitudes vary dramatically (Japan vs. West)

**Key Tension**: 
- Optimists (Levy, some tech): Therapeutic potential, new relationship forms
- Skeptics (Turkle): Danger of preferring machines to humans
- Ethicists (Devlin): Need consent frameworks, avoid objectification traps

**Implication for Essay**: The intimacy chapter must present this complexity, not simple advocacy or dismissal.

### Finding 5: Perception Gap Between Elites and Public

Survey data (Pew 2017, ongoing) shows:
- Tech leaders: largely optimistic about robots/AI
- General public: skeptical, worried about jobs, uncomfortable with intimacy
- This gap is itself a story worth telling

### Finding 6: The Uncanny Valley Remains Central

Mori's 1970 hypothesis continues to explain:
- Why near-human robots disturb us
- Why successful robots are either clearly mechanical (Roomba) or clearly social/cartoonish (Pepper)
- Why crossing the valley (Ishiguro's Geminoids) is a research frontier
