## Executive Summary

The English word "war" tells a remarkable story of linguistic collision. It is a Germanic word that came to English through French, replacing native Old English terms after the Norman Conquest. Its journey from Proto-Indo-European "confusion" to modern "armed conflict" spans over 3,000 years and reveals how languages borrow, adapt, and transform meaning.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Etymological Pathway is Well-Established

```
PIE *wers- "to confuse, mix up"
    → Proto-Germanic *werz-a-
    → Frankish *werra (RECONSTRUCTED)
    → Medieval Latin werra (858 CE)
    → Old North French werre
    → Anglo-Norman werre
    → Middle English werre (c.1121)
    → Modern English war
```

**Confidence Level**: High. Supported by OED, MED, Etymonline, and comparative evidence.

---

### 2. The First English Attestation is Precisely Located

- **Document**: Peterborough Chronicle (MS. Laud Misc. 636)
- **Date**: Written c.1121-1122, describing events of 1116
- **Form**: "wyrre" (possibly inverted spelling for "werre")
- **Context**: King Henry I's war in Normandy
- **Access**: Digitized at Bodleian Library

### 3. Romance Languages Borrowed from Germanic - A Linguistic Anomaly

The standard Latin word for war, *bellum*, was abandoned by Romance languages in favor of the Germanic *werra*. This is unusual because Romance languages typically derive vocabulary from Latin.

**Primary Theory**: Homophony with *bellus* ("beautiful") caused confusion, prompting speakers to adopt a clearer term from Germanic.

**This appears in**: French (guerre), Spanish (guerra), Italian (guerra), Portuguese (guerra)

**Exceptions**: Romanian (război, from Slavic), Aromanian (polim, from Greek)

---

### 4. Germanic Languages Lack a Common Word for "War"

Despite being language relatives, Germanic languages use completely different words:
- **English**: war (from Frankish via French)
- **German**: Krieg (from "stubbornness")
- **Dutch**: oorlog (from "fate/decree")

The OED notes: "No Germanic nation in early historic times had in living use any word properly meaning 'war'."

**Visual Essay Opportunity**: This Germanic diversity is a striking fact that challenges assumptions about language families.

---

### 5. Semantic Evolution Shows Conceptual Shift

| Era | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| PIE | "to confuse, mix up" |
| Proto-Germanic | "to bring into confusion" |
| Frankish | "confusion, strife, quarrel" |
| Middle English | "armed conflict" |
| Modern English | "organized armed conflict between nations" |
| 1930s+ | Figurative "war on X" |

The word transformed from describing disorder to describing organized violence.

---

### 6. The Figurative "War on X" is a 20th-Century Innovation

| Phrase | First Use |
|--------|-----------|
| "War on Crime" | 1933 (AG Homer Cummings) |
| "War on Poverty" | 1964 (LBJ) |
| "War on Drugs" | 1971 (Nixon) |

This metaphorical construction is now ubiquitous in political discourse.

## Narrative Possibilities

### Narrative 1: "The Conqueror's Word"
Focus on the Norman Conquest as the moment English acquired "war." The Normans brought their word for conflict along with their armies. The irony: the word for war came through an act of war.

### Narrative 2: "The Language that Forgot Its Word"
Focus on the curious fact that Latin *bellum* was abandoned. Romance languages "forgot" their word for war because it sounded too much like "beautiful." They had to borrow from Germanic invaders.

### Narrative 3: "Confusion to Conflict"
Trace the semantic journey from PIE "confusion" to modern "armed conflict." War was originally conceptualized as disorder, chaos, mixing things up. This ancient metaphor persists in the modern word.

### Narrative 4: "Every Language Has Its Own War"
Focus on cross-linguistic comparison. Each culture named war according to its own understanding: confusion (English), stubbornness (German), fate (Dutch), the warrior (Russian), the blade (Arabic).
