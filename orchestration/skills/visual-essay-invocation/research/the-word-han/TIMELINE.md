# Timeline: The Word "Han"

> Chronological events in the history of "Han" across East Asia
> Research Date: December 14, 2025

---

## Ancient Origins (Pre-206 BCE)

### c. 1250–1046 BCE — Oracle Bone Script Era
**Event**: Earliest Chinese writing; character forms that would evolve into 漢 begin to take shape
**Significance**: Foundation of the script tradition that would spread across East Asia
**Source**: Archaeological evidence, Shang Dynasty oracle bones (Yinxu site)

### c. 1046–256 BCE — Bronze Inscription Period
**Event**: Chinese characters formalized on bronze ritual vessels
**Significance**: Character 漢 evolves into more standardized forms
**Source**: Zhou Dynasty bronze vessels, museum collections

### c. 400 BCE — Han River Naming
**Event**: The Han River (漢水/汉水) is documented in Chinese texts
**Significance**: The river in central China becomes the geographic origin of the word "Han"
**Source**: Early Chinese geographic texts

---

## Han Dynasty Era (206 BCE–220 CE)

### 206 BCE — Han Dynasty Founded ⭐
**Event**: Liu Bang defeats Xiang Yu, establishes the Han Dynasty
**Significance**: The dynasty takes its name from Liu Bang's earlier title "King of Han" (漢王), derived from his territory near the Han River
**Source**: Sima Qian, *Shiji* (Records of the Grand Historian)
**Visual Note**: This is the foundational moment—"Han" becomes a dynastic name

### 141–87 BCE — Reign of Emperor Wu
**Event**: Emperor Wu expands Han territory and establishes Confucianism as state ideology
**Significance**: The Han Dynasty becomes so dominant that "Han" becomes synonymous with Chinese civilization
**Source**: *Shiji*, *Han Shu* (Book of Han)

### c. 100 BCE — Clerical Script Development
**Event**: Clerical script (隶书) emerges during Han Dynasty
**Significance**: The character 漢 takes on its recognizable modern form
**Source**: Calligraphic historical records

### 220 CE — Han Dynasty Falls
**Event**: The Han Dynasty collapses after 400+ years
**Significance**: Despite the dynasty's end, "Han" remains as a cultural-ethnic identifier
**Source**: *Sanguo Zhi* (Records of the Three Kingdoms)

---

## Spread to Korea (1st–7th Century CE)

### c. 400 BCE–108 BCE — Early Contact
**Event**: Chinese writing begins appearing in Korean peninsula (Gojoseon period)
**Significance**: Earliest archaeological evidence of Han characters in Korea
**Source**: Archaeological artifacts, inscribed knife money, Yalu River finds

### 108 BCE — Han Commanderies Established
**Event**: Emperor Wu conquers Gojoseon, establishes Chinese commanderies in Korea
**Significance**: Direct administration accelerates the spread of Han characters
**Source**: *Han Shu*, Korean historical records

### 4th Century CE — Elite Adoption
**Event**: Han characters (Hanja) become prominent among Korean elite
**Significance**: Classical Chinese becomes the language of scholarship and governance
**Source**: Three Kingdoms of Korea period records

### c. 4th Century — Wani's Mission to Japan
**Event**: Scholar Wani (王仁) brings Chinese texts from Baekje (Korea) to Japan
**Significance**: Han characters reach Japan via Korean intermediary, not directly from China
**Source**: *Kojiki*, *Nihon Shoki* (Japanese chronicles)

---

## Spread to Japan (5th–8th Century CE)

### 5th Century CE — Kanji Systematization Begins
**Event**: Japanese scholars begin systematically adopting Han characters
**Significance**: "Kanji" (漢字, literally "Han characters") becomes the Japanese term
**Source**: Japanese archaeological and textual evidence

### 574–622 CE — Prince Shōtoku's Reforms
**Event**: Prince Shōtoku promotes Chinese learning, Buddhism, and character usage
**Significance**: Institutionalizes Kanji in Japanese governance and culture
**Source**: *Nihon Shoki*, temple records

### 607 CE — First Embassy to Sui China
**Event**: Prince Shōtoku sends embassy with famous "Land of the Rising Sun" letter
**Significance**: Japan asserts distinct identity while embracing Han cultural forms
**Source**: Chinese and Japanese diplomatic records

### 8th Century — Kana Development
**Event**: Japanese develop hiragana and katakana from simplified Kanji
**Significance**: Creates uniquely Japanese scripts while retaining Kanji for meaning
**Source**: Japanese manuscripts (Man'yōshū, Kokin Wakashū)

---

## Spread to Vietnam (111 BCE–10th Century CE)

### 111 BCE — Chinese Conquest of Nam Việt
**Event**: Han Dynasty conquers territory of modern Vietnam
**Significance**: Begins 1,000+ years of Chinese rule and character usage
**Source**: *Shiji*, Vietnamese historical records

### 939 CE — Vietnamese Independence
**Event**: Ngô Quyền defeats Chinese forces, establishes Vietnamese independence
**Significance**: Vietnam continues using Chữ Hán (漢字) despite political independence
**Source**: Vietnamese historical chronicles

### 13th Century — Chữ Nôm Development
**Event**: Vietnamese scholars develop Chữ Nôm, adapting Han characters for Vietnamese
**Significance**: Creates a uniquely Vietnamese writing system using Han character elements
**Source**: Vietnamese literary texts

---

## Korean Identity Transformation (15th–20th Century)

### 1443 — Hangul Created ⭐
**Event**: King Sejong the Great creates Hangul (한글), originally named Hunminjeongeum
**Significance**: Korea develops its own alphabet to reduce dependence on Han characters; deliberately names it with Korean "한" (han)
**Source**: *Hunminjeongeum Haerye* (Explanations and Examples)

### 1446 — Hangul Promulgated
**Event**: Official promulgation of Hangul with the *Hunminjeongeum* text
**Significance**: King Sejong's preface explicitly states Korean sounds differ from Chinese
**Source**: Royal Joseon records, *Hunminjeongeum*

### 1897 — Korean Empire Proclaimed ⭐
**Event**: King Gojong renames Korea "Daehan Jeguk" (大韓帝國, "Great Han Empire")
**Significance**: Uses 韓 (Korean Han), NOT 漢 (Chinese Han), asserting distinct identity
**Source**: Korean Empire proclamation documents

### 1910–1945 — Japanese Colonial Period
**Event**: Japan annexes Korea, attempts to suppress Korean language
**Significance**: Hangul becomes symbol of Korean resistance and identity
**Source**: Colonial-era records, independence movement documents

### 1948 — Republic of Korea Founded
**Event**: South Korea adopts name "Daehan Minguk" (大韓民國)
**Significance**: Continues use of 韓 for national identity; "Hanguk" (韓國) becomes standard
**Source**: ROK founding documents

---

## Modern Chinese Identity (19th–20th Century)

### 1902 — "Zhonghua Minzu" Coined
**Event**: Liang Qichao coins the term "Zhonghua minzu" (中華民族)
**Significance**: Initially equated with "Han Chinese" (漢族); later expanded to include all Chinese peoples
**Source**: Liang Qichao's political writings

### 1911–1912 — Republic of China Founded
**Event**: Qing Dynasty falls; Republic of China established
**Significance**: Han Chinese identity becomes central to Chinese nationalism
**Source**: ROC founding documents, Sun Yat-sen's writings

### 1949 — People's Republic Established
**Event**: PRC officially codifies "Han Chinese" (漢族) as an ethnic category
**Significance**: "Han" becomes the official designation for 92% of China's population
**Source**: PRC ethnic classification system documents

### 1956 — Simplified Characters Introduced
**Event**: PRC introduces simplified Chinese characters
**Significance**: 漢 becomes simplified to 汉 in mainland China
**Source**: PRC language reform documents

---

## Vietnam's Departure (19th–20th Century)

### 1919–1945 — Quốc Ngữ Promotion
**Event**: French colonial authorities promote Latin-based Quốc ngữ script
**Significance**: Vietnam begins transition away from Han characters
**Source**: French colonial records, Vietnamese literary history

### 1945 — Independence and Script Change
**Event**: Vietnam declares independence; Quốc ngữ becomes official script
**Significance**: Vietnam fully abandons Han characters (Chữ Hán) for daily use
**Source**: DRV founding documents

---

## Contemporary Era (21st Century)

### 2014 — Source Han Sans Released
**Event**: Adobe and Google release pan-CJK font Source Han Sans
**Significance**: Modern typography unifies Han character heritage across China, Japan, Korea
**Source**: Adobe and Google typography announcements

### Present — Geopolitical Resonance
**Event**: "Han" remains politically significant across East Asia
**Significance**: Han Chinese identity, Hanguk/Hangul nationalism, and regional relationships continue to be shaped by this word
**Source**: Contemporary political and cultural analysis
