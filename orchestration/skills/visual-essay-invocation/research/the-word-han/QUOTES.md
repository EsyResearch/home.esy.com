## Chinese Figures

### Liu Bang (劉邦)

**Quote 1:**
> "I conquered the empire on horseback, but I cannot rule it from horseback."

**Context**: Attributed to Liu Bang when transitioning from military conquest to civil governance
**Source**: Traditional attribution, cited in historical commentary on the Han Dynasty
**Verification**: Widely attributed in Chinese historical literature; exact original source uncertain
**Use Note**: Use with "attributed to" qualifier

### Emperor Wu of Han (漢武帝)

**Quote 2:**
> "A man must not forget benevolence and righteousness through poverty and obscurity, nor alter his moral integrity through wealth and honor."

**Context**: Statement on Confucian virtue during his promotion of Confucianism as state ideology
**Source**: Han Dynasty court records, quoted in subsequent historical commentaries
**Verification**: Attributed in traditional sources
**Use Note**: Reflects his Confucian ideological program

### Sima Qian (司馬遷)

**Quote 3:**
> "A person's death can be weightier than Mount Tai or lighter than a feather."

**Chinese**: 人固有一死，或重于泰山，或轻于鸿毛。
**Context**: Letter to Ren An, explaining why he accepted castration rather than death to complete his historical work
**Source**: *Bao Ren An Shu* (Letter to Ren An), attributed to Sima Qian
**Verification**: ✅ Well-documented in Chinese literary tradition
**Use Note**: Central to understanding his sacrifice for historical documentation

**Quote 4:**
> "The whole world bustles for profit; the whole world is astir for profit."

**Chinese**: 天下熙熙，皆为利来；天下攘攘，皆为利往。
**Context**: Observation on human motivation in *Huozhi Liezhuan* (Biographies of the Money-Makers)
**Source**: Sima Qian, *Shiji* (Records of the Grand Historian)
**Verification**: ✅ Verified in *Shiji*
**Use Note**: Shows his philosophical depth beyond mere chronicle

**Quote 5:**
> "The wise may have a thousand considerations, but there will still be one oversight; the fool may have a thousand considerations, but there will still be one gain."

**Chinese**: 智者千虑，必有一失；愚者千虑，必有一得。
**Context**: Commentary on human judgment in *Huaiyin Hou Liezhuan*
**Source**: Sima Qian, *Shiji*
**Verification**: ✅ Verified in *Shiji*

### Liang Qichao (梁啟超)

**Quote 6:**
> "Today's Zhonghua minzu is what is commonly called the Han people."

**Chinese**: 今之中华民族，即普遍俗称所谓汉族者。
**Context**: Early articulation of Chinese nationalism linking "Zhonghua minzu" with Han ethnicity
**Source**: Liang Qichao, *Observations on Chinese History* (*Lishi shang Zhongguo minzu zhi guancha*), 1905
**Verification**: ✅ Documented in academic sources (Rawski, et al.)
**Use Note**: Critical for understanding the modern invention of "Han Chinese" as ethnic category

---

### Sun Yat-sen (孫中山)

**Quote 7:**
> "Chinese people are entirely Han people: sharing a common bloodline, language, religion, and customs, entirely a single nation."

**Context**: Post-revolution statement on Chinese national unity centered on Han identity
**Source**: Sun Yat-sen's political writings after 1911 Revolution
**Verification**: ✅ Cited in multiple academic sources on Chinese nationalism
**Use Note**: Shows how "Han" was weaponized for nationalist purposes

## Korean Figures

### King Sejong the Great (世宗大王)

**Quote 8:**
> "The sounds of our country's language are different from those of China and do not correspond to the sounds of Chinese characters. Therefore, among the ignorant people, there have been many who, having something to put into writing, have in the end been unable to express their feelings. I have been distressed by this and have designed twenty-eight new letters, which I wish to have everyone practice at their ease and make convenient for their daily use."

**Context**: Preface to the *Hunminjeongeum*, explaining his motivation for creating Hangul
**Source**: *Hunminjeongeum* (訓民正音), 1446
**Verification**: ✅ Primary source, UNESCO Memory of the World
**Use Note**: THE defining statement on Korean linguistic independence from Chinese

**Quote 9:**
> "You say that combining letters based on pronunciation goes against tradition, but did not Seol Chong do the same? Moreover, was not the motivation for creating Idu to improve the lives of the people? If so, then does the creation of Hangul not have the same objective?"

**Context**: King Sejong responding to scholarly opposition to Hangul
**Source**: Royal Joseon court records, *Hunminjeongeum Haerye*
**Verification**: ✅ Documented in historical records
**Use Note**: Shows the political resistance Sejong faced

## Academic/Scholarly Quotes

### On the Word "Han"

**Quote 13:**
> "The term 'Han' was originally a reference to the Han River, which flows through central China. When Liu Bang established his dynasty, he adopted this name from his earlier title, and it eventually came to designate not just the dynasty but the entire Chinese civilization."

**Source**: Academic synthesis from multiple sources on Han Dynasty naming
**Verification**: Synthesized from scholarly consensus
**Use Note**: Useful for narrative explanation

**Quote 14:**
> "The spread of Chinese characters to Korea, Japan, and Vietnam created what scholars call the 'Sinosphere' or the 'Chinese character cultural sphere' (漢字文化圏)."

**Source**: Academic terminology from East Asian Studies
**Verification**: Standard scholarly term
**Use Note**: Establishes the conceptual framework

---

### On Korean Identity

**Quote 15:**
> "The use of 韓 (Han) in Korea's name Hanguk (韓國) is etymologically unrelated to the Chinese 漢 (Han). It derives from the ancient Samhan confederacies and likely meant 'great' or 'leader.'"

**Source**: Linguistic scholarship on Korean etymology
**Verification**: Academic consensus documented in multiple sources
**Use Note**: Critical distinction for the narrative

---

### On Typography Heritage

**Quote 16:**
> "Ming typefaces, known as 'Song' in China and 'Minchō' in Japan and Korea, represent a shared typographic heritage that transcends national boundaries—a visual reminder that the Han character cultural sphere remains unified even as its member nations assert distinct identities."

**Source**: Typography scholarship on East Asian fonts
**Verification**: Synthesized from typography literature
**Use Note**: Useful for typography-as-narrative section
