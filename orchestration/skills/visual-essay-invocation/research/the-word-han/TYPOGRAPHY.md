## Typography Philosophy for This Essay

This visual essay treats typography as a **storytelling mechanism**, not decoration. The evolution of type across East Asia mirrors the spread of "Han" itself—from ancient inscriptions to modern digital fonts, typography carries the physical memory of this linguistic journey.

**Core Principle**: When discussing ancient bronze inscriptions, let the reader *feel* the weight of bronze. When discussing Hangul's democratic purpose, let the type *embody* accessibility. Typography shifts should mark narrative transitions.

## Historical Script Evolution

### The Character 漢 Through Time

| Era | Script Name | Character Form | Visual Weight |
|-----|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| Shang (c. 1250–1046 BCE) | Oracle Bone (甲骨文) | Pictographic, angular | Raw, carved |
| Zhou (1046–256 BCE) | Bronze (金文) | Formalized, decorative | Heavy, ritual |
| Qin (221–206 BCE) | Small Seal (小篆) | Symmetrical, elegant | Refined, imperial |
| Han (206 BCE–220 CE) | Clerical (隶书) | Horizontal emphasis, squared | Efficient, official |
| Post-Han | Regular (楷书) | Balanced, calligraphic | Standard, readable |
| Modern | Simplified (简体) | Reduced strokes | Contemporary |

**Visual Application**: Animate this evolution during the "Origins" chapter. The character 漢 morphing through scripts conveys 3,000 years in moments.

## East Asian Typeface Families

### 1. Ming/Song (明朝/宋体)

**Origin**: Song Dynasty China (960–1279 CE)
**Characteristics**:
- Thick vertical strokes, thin horizontal strokes
- Triangular serifs ("fish scales" / 鱗 / uroko)
- Geometrical regularity
- High readability in print

**Regional Names**:
- China: 宋体 (Sòngtǐ) or 明体 (Míngtǐ)
- Japan: 明朝体 (Minchōtai)
- Korea: 명조체 (Myeongjoche) / 바탕 (Batang)

**Use Case**: Body text, formal chapters, historical passages

**Example Fonts**:
- SimSun (简体, China)
- PMingLiU (繁體, Taiwan)
- MS Mincho (Japan)
- Batang (Korea)

---

### 2. Fangsong (仿宋)

**Origin**: Southern Song Dynasty imitation of earlier Song printing
**Characteristics**:
- Straight strokes for easier woodblock carving
- Even stroke width
- Subtle serifs
- Official document aesthetic

**Use Case**: Formal declarations, official text, historical documents

**Regional Significance**: Standard for PRC official documents

---

### 3. Heiti/Gothic (黑体/ゴシック/고딕)

**Origin**: 20th century, influenced by Western sans-serif
**Characteristics**:
- Even stroke thickness
- No serifs
- Modern, clean appearance
- High legibility on screens

**Regional Names**:
- China: 黑体 (Hēitǐ)
- Japan: ゴシック (Goshikku)
- Korea: 고딕 (Godikh)

**Use Case**: Headlines, chapter titles, contemporary sections

**Example Fonts**:
- Microsoft YaHei (China)
- Hiragino Sans (Japan)
- Malgun Gothic (Korea)
- Source Han Sans (pan-CJK)

---

### 4. Kaishu/Kaiti (楷书/楷体)

**Origin**: Han Dynasty, formalized post-Han
**Characteristics**:
- Calligraphic brush feel
- Balanced, readable
- Traditional aesthetic
- Handwritten quality

**Use Case**: Quotes, pull quotes, calligraphic moments

**Example Fonts**:
- KaiTi (China)
- AR Kaisho (Japan)

## Korean Typography

### Hangul-Specific Typefaces

**Batang (바탕)**
- Meaning: "Foundation" or "background"
- Renamed from "Myeongjo" in 1993 to use indigenous terminology
- Ming-style with Hangul adaptations
- Serif, formal, literary

**Dotum (돋움)**
- Meaning: "Standing out"
- Sans-serif, modern
- Good for digital, headlines

**Gulim (굴림)**
- Rounded sans-serif
- Casual, approachable

**Historical Note**: The 1993 renaming from "Myeongjo" to "Batang" reflects Korean linguistic nationalism—a direct parallel to the essay's theme of reclaiming identity.

## Japanese Typography

### Kanji-Kana Balance

Japanese typography uniquely balances three scripts:
- Kanji (漢字) — Chinese-derived logographs
- Hiragana (ひらがな) — Cursive-derived phonetic
- Katakana (カタカナ) — Angular phonetic

**Typographic Challenge**: Kanji are dense; kana are simple. Good Japanese typography balances these visually.

**Minchō (明朝)**
- Ming-derived serif
- Classic, literary
- Preferred for body text

**Gothic (ゴシック)**
- Sans-serif, modern
- Headlines, digital

## Vietnamese Typography Transition

### From Han to Latin

Vietnam's script history offers a unique narrative:

1. **Chữ Hán (漢字)**: Pure Chinese characters (111 BCE–20th c.)
2. **Chữ Nôm (𡨸喃)**: Vietnamese-adapted characters (13th–20th c.)
3. **Quốc Ngữ**: Latin-based alphabet (20th c.–present)

**Typographic Narrative**: Vietnam's complete departure from Han characters is the essay's counterpoint. While China, Korea, and Japan retain Han character elements, Vietnam fully transitioned to Latin script.

**Visual Treatment**: Show this transition through typeface—Chữ Hán specimens fading into French-influenced Latin fonts, then modern Vietnamese typography.

## Modern Pan-CJK Typography

### Source Han Family (思源)

**Released**: 2014–2017
**Developers**: Adobe and Google
**Significance**: First truly pan-CJK open-source font family

**Source Han Sans (思源黑体)**
- Sans-serif
- Unified design across Chinese, Japanese, Korean
- Multiple weights

**Source Han Serif (思源宋体)**
- Serif (Ming-derived)
- Regional variants for CN, TW, JP, KR
- Represents shared heritage with local adaptations

**Thematic Significance**: Source Han fonts literally unify the Han character heritage across borders. This is the contemporary manifestation of 2,000 years of shared script culture.

## Typography Recommendations for This Essay

### Display Typography (Chapter Titles)

**Recommendation**: Use a historically-informed display font that shifts by era

| Era | Suggested Approach |
|-----|-------------------|
| Ancient Origins | Calligraphic brush, seal script flavor |
| Han Dynasty | Classical Ming/Song with imperial weight |
| Korean Chapters | Korean-designed typeface (Nanum Myeongjo) |
| Japanese Chapters | Japanese Minchō with grace |
| Vietnamese Chapter | Transitional: Han → Latin visual |
| Contemporary | Source Han Serif, clean modern |

---

### Body Typography

**Primary Recommendation**: Source Han Serif (regional variants as appropriate)

**Why**: 
- Open-source, freely available
- Represents the shared heritage theme
- Clean readability
- Multiple weights for hierarchy
- Pan-CJK nature embodies the essay's message

**Alternative**: Noto Serif CJK (Google's version of Source Han)

---

### Quote Typography

**Recommendation**: Kaishu-style calligraphic font

**Why**:
- Quotes are human moments—calligraphy adds warmth
- Distinguishes quotes visually from body text
- Honors the calligraphic tradition

**Treatment**:
- King Sejong quotes: Consider Hangul calligraphic treatment
- Chinese historical quotes: Kaishu
- Japanese quotes: Japanese calligraphic style

---

### Technical/Caption Typography

**Recommendation**: Gothic/Heiti style (Source Han Sans)

**Why**:
- Clear and functional
- Modern contrast to serif body
- Good for dates, labels, captions
