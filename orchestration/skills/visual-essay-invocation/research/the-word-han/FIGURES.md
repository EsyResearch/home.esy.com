# Key Figures: The Word "Han"

> 12 verified figures across cultures with available imagery
> Research Date: December 14, 2025

---

## Chinese Figures

### 1. Liu Bang (劉邦) — Founder of the Han Dynasty

**Lived**: 256–195 BCE
**Role**: Peasant rebel leader who overthrew the Qin Dynasty and established the Han Dynasty

**Key Contributions**:
- Founded the Han Dynasty in 206 BCE
- Named the dynasty after his title "King of Han" (漢王), which derived from the Han River region
- Established the precedent of "Han" as a civilizational marker
- His pragmatic approach to governance laid foundations for 400 years of rule

**Defining Quote**: 
> "I conquered the empire on horseback, but I cannot rule it from horseback."
> — Attributed to Liu Bang, on transitioning from military to civil governance

**Legacy**: His decision to name the dynasty "Han" would echo across millennia, with the word becoming synonymous with Chinese civilization itself.

**Visual**: Historical portraits available; most famous depictions are from later artistic representations. Statues and artistic renderings in Chinese museums (National Museum of China, Xi'an).

---

### 2. Emperor Wu of Han (漢武帝) — The Martial Emperor

**Lived**: 156–87 BCE
**Role**: Fifth emperor of the Han Dynasty, reigned 141–87 BCE

**Key Contributions**:
- Expanded Han territory into Central Asia, Korea, and Vietnam
- Established Confucianism as the state ideology
- Created the Imperial Academy for training administrators
- Secured the Silk Road, spreading Han influence globally
- His reign consolidated what it meant to be "Han"

**Defining Quote**:
> "A man must not forget benevolence and righteousness through poverty and obscurity, nor alter his moral integrity through wealth and honor."
> — Emperor Wu, on Confucian virtue

**Legacy**: Under his rule, the Han Dynasty reached its peak, and "Han" became indelibly linked with Chinese cultural and political supremacy.

**Visual**: Artistic portraits and sculptures available in Chinese museums; historical illustrations in National Palace Museum, Beijing and Taipei.

---

### 3. Sima Qian (司馬遷) — Father of Chinese Historiography

**Lived**: c. 145–86 BCE
**Role**: Court historian who authored the *Shiji* (Records of the Grand Historian)

**Key Contributions**:
- Documented the founding and early history of the Han Dynasty
- Created the first comprehensive Chinese history
- Established biographical-style historical writing
- Preserved the narrative of how "Han" became a dynastic name
- Suffered castration rather than abandon his historical work

**Defining Quotes**:
> "A person's death can be weightier than Mount Tai or lighter than a feather."
> — Sima Qian, on the meaning of sacrifice

> "The whole world bustles for profit; the whole world is astir for profit."
> — Sima Qian, *Shiji* (货殖列传)

**Legacy**: His *Records of the Grand Historian* became the foundational source for understanding how "Han" entered Chinese consciousness.

**Visual**: Traditional artistic portraits available; manuscripts and stone inscriptions of his work in Chinese archives.

---

### 4. Liang Qichao (梁啟超) — Architect of Modern Chinese Nationalism

**Lived**: 1873–1929
**Role**: Scholar, journalist, philosopher, reformer

**Key Contributions**:
- Coined the term "Zhonghua minzu" (中華民族) in 1902
- Initially equated this with Han Chinese (漢族)
- Later developed "Greater Nationalism" including non-Han peoples
- Bridged traditional Chinese identity with modern nation-state concepts
- His writings shaped how modern China thinks about ethnicity

**Defining Quote**:
> "Today's Zhonghua minzu is what is commonly called the Han people."
> — Liang Qichao, *Observations on Chinese History* (1905)

**Legacy**: Liang transformed "Han" from a historical/cultural term into a modern ethnic and national identity category.

**Visual**: Extensive photography available from early 20th century; formal portraits in Chinese historical archives.

---

### 5. Sun Yat-sen (孫中山) — Father of Modern China

**Lived**: 1866–1925
**Role**: Revolutionary leader, founder of the Republic of China

**Key Contributions**:
- Developed the Three Principles of the People
- Promoted Han Chinese identity as the core of Chinese nationalism
- Declared the unity of Han people after Qing Dynasty fall
- His philosophy shaped both ROC (Taiwan) and PRC concepts of Chinese identity

**Defining Quote**:
> "Chinese people are entirely Han people: sharing a common bloodline, language, religion, and customs, entirely a single nation."
> — Sun Yat-sen, after 1911 Revolution

**Legacy**: His framing of Han identity continues to influence Chinese nationalism and cross-strait relations.

**Visual**: Extensive photography available; one of the most photographed Chinese historical figures. Portraits in museums worldwide.

## Korean Figures

### 6. King Sejong the Great (世宗大王) — Creator of Hangul

**Lived**: 1397–1450
**Role**: Fourth king of the Joseon Dynasty (reigned 1418–1450)

**Key Contributions**:
- Created Hangul (한글) in 1443, originally named Hunminjeongeum
- Deliberately named the script to reflect Korean identity (한)
- Democratized literacy by creating an accessible writing system
- Reduced Korean dependence on Chinese characters (Hanja)
- His scientific approach to language design was revolutionary

**Defining Quote**:
> "The sounds of our country's language are different from those of China and do not correspond to Chinese characters. Therefore, among the ignorant people, there have been many who, having something to put into writing, have in the end been unable to express their feelings. I have been distressed by this and have designed twenty-eight new letters."
> — King Sejong, Preface to *Hunminjeongeum* (1446)

**Legacy**: Hangul allowed Koreans to distinguish their linguistic identity from China while still using Hanja for classical texts. His choice of "한" (Han) connected to Korean rather than Chinese identity.

**Visual**: Paintings and statues widely available; iconic statue in Gwanghwamun Square, Seoul. Historical portraits in National Museum of Korea.

---

### 7. King Gojong (고종) — Emperor of the Korean Empire

**Lived**: 1852–1919
**Role**: Last king of Joseon, first Emperor of the Korean Empire

**Key Contributions**:
- Renamed Korea "Daehan Jeguk" (大韓帝國, "Great Han Empire") in 1897
- Chose the character 韓 (Korean Han), not 漢 (Chinese Han)
- Asserted Korean independence from Chinese suzerainty
- His naming choice established modern Korean national identity

**Legacy**: His use of 韓 in the national name distinguished Korean identity from Chinese "Han," a distinction that persists in "Hanguk" (한국/韓國) today.

**Visual**: Extensive photography available; formal imperial portraits in National Palace Museum of Korea.

## Japanese Figures

### 8. Prince Shōtoku (聖徳太子) — Father of Japanese Civilization

**Lived**: 574–622 CE
**Role**: Regent under Empress Suiko, cultural reformer

**Key Contributions**:
- Promoted adoption of Chinese characters (Kanji 漢字) in Japan
- Authored early Japanese texts using Chinese writing
- Sent embassies to Sui Dynasty China (607 CE)
- Introduced the term "Land of the Rising Sun" for Japan
- His reforms systematized Japanese use of Han characters

**Defining Quote**:
> "The sovereign of the land where the sun rises addresses a letter to the sovereign of the land where the sun sets."
> — Opening of Shōtoku's 607 CE letter to Sui Emperor Yang

**Legacy**: His embrace of Chinese characters while asserting Japanese distinctiveness established the pattern of Japanese cultural borrowing and adaptation.

**Visual**: Historical paintings and artistic representations; most famous portrait is on old Japanese currency. Images in temples he founded.

---

### 9. Wani (王仁) — Legendary Transmitter of Chinese Learning

**Lived**: c. late 4th century CE (legendary dates)
**Role**: Scholar from Baekje (Korea) credited with introducing Chinese writing to Japan

**Key Contributions**:
- Traditionally credited with bringing the *Analects* and *Thousand Character Classic* to Japan
- His mission represents the Korea → Japan transmission route for Han characters
- Though partially legendary, he symbolizes the cultural bridge

**Legacy**: Represents how "Han characters" traveled not directly from China but through Korea to Japan.

**Visual**: Artistic representations available; memorial sites in Japan and Korea. Statue in Osaka.

## Vietnamese Figure

### 10. Nguyễn Trãi (阮廌) — Father of Vietnamese Independence Literature

**Lived**: 1380–1442
**Role**: Confucian scholar, poet, strategist

**Key Contributions**:
- Authored the *Bình Ngô đại cáo* (Great Proclamation on the Pacification of the Wu)
- Used Hán characters to declare Vietnamese independence from Ming China
- Pioneered the use of chữ Nôm (Vietnamese adaptation of Han characters)
- His work demonstrates how Han characters served Vietnamese national identity

**Defining Quotes**:
> "Our country, Đại Việt, has long been established. Its culture is distinct from that of the North."
> — Nguyễn Trãi, *Bình Ngô đại cáo* (1428)

> "The key to righteousness lies in securing the peace of the people. The army's first duty is to rid the land of tyranny."
> — Nguyễn Trãi

**Legacy**: Showed how Vietnamese used Han characters while asserting distinct Vietnamese identity—then later abandoned them entirely for Quốc ngữ.

**Visual**: Historical paintings available; statues in Vietnam. Temple of Literature in Hanoi has related imagery.

## Modern Scholars

### 11. John DeFrancis — Western Scholar of Chinese Language

**Lived**: 1911–2009
**Role**: American linguist specializing in Chinese language and writing

**Key Contributions**:
- Authored *The Chinese Language: Fact and Fantasy*
- Studied the spread and adaptation of Han characters across East Asia
- Provided Western scholarship on the "Sinosphere"

**Legacy**: His work helped English-language audiences understand the concept of Han character cultural sphere.

**Visual**: Academic photographs available; Harvard University archives.

---

### 12. Victor Mair — Contemporary Sinologist

**Lived**: 1943–present
**Role**: Professor of Chinese Language and Literature, University of Pennsylvania

**Key Contributions**:
- Extensive scholarship on Chinese language history
- Studies on the spread of writing systems in East Asia
- Analysis of Han character borrowing and adaptation

**Legacy**: Leading contemporary voice on the linguistic dimensions of Han character diffusion.

**Visual**: Academic photographs available.
