# Timeline: Etymology of "Pussy"

## Pre-History: The Roots

| Period | Event | Source Language | Notes |
|--------|-------|-----------------|-------|
| Ancient | *pūss* meaning "pocket/purse" | Old Norse | Possible anatomical metaphor root |
| Ancient | *pusa* meaning "bag/pouch" | Old English | Related to purse semantics |
| Medieval | *puse* (vulva) | Low German | Direct anatomical usage documented |
| Medieval | *poes* (cat) | Middle Dutch | Call-word for felines |
| Medieval | *pūse* (cat) | Middle Low German | Germanic feline term |

---

## Documented English Usage

### 1500s: The Cat Arrives

| Year | Event | Evidence | Significance |
|------|-------|----------|--------------|
| **1533** | First English attestation of "puss" for cat | Written records | Earliest documented use |
| **1583** | Philip Stubbes notes "The word pussie is now used of a woman" | *Anatomie of Abuses* | First documented feminine usage |

### 1600s: Semantic Branching

| Year | Event | Evidence | Significance |
|------|-------|----------|--------------|
| **Early 1600s** | "Puss" begins anatomical association | Contemporary texts | Start of vulgar meaning |
| **1664** | Charles Cotton writes "A Health to Pusse" | *Virgile Travestie* | Explicit double meaning documented |

### 1700s: Literary Establishment

| Year | Event | Evidence | Significance |
|------|-------|----------|--------------|
| **1755** | Samuel Johnson's *Dictionary* **omits** the word | Lexicographic decision | Reflects editorial choices on vulgarity |
| **1773** | "Pussycat" compound first documented | Written records | Term of endearment established |

### 1800s: Victorian Tensions

| Year | Event | Evidence | Significance |
|------|-------|----------|--------------|
| **1805** | "Pussy Cat, Pussy Cat" nursery rhyme published | Children's literature | Innocent feline usage cemented |
| **Late 1800s** | Continued dual usage in literature | Period texts | Tension between innocent and vulgar |

### 1900s: American Innovations

| Year | Event | Evidence | Significance |
|------|-------|----------|--------------|
| **Early 1900s** | Applied to "effeminate" men | American slang | Gender-based pejorative emerges |
| **1960s-1970s** | "Coward" meaning becomes widespread | American English | Third major meaning branch established |
| **1970s+** | Word becomes increasingly taboo | Social shifts | Meaning #2 dominates, word avoidance grows |

## Key Inflection Points

1. **1533**: Germanic cat-calling word enters English
2. **1583**: First documented use for women (affectionate)
3. **1664**: Clear anatomical double-meaning documented
4. **1755**: Johnson's omission reflects growing vulgarity
5. **1960s**: American slang adds "coward" meaning
6. **1970s+**: Word becomes primarily taboo in public discourse
