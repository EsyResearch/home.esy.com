# Verified Quotes: Etymology of "Pussy"

## Historical Documentation

### Philip Stubbes (1583)
> "The word pussie is now used of a woman."

- **Source**: *The Anatomie of Abuses*, Philip Stubbes, 1583
- **Context**: Puritan pamphleteer documenting contemporary language use
- **Significance**: First documented use of word applied to females
- **Verification**: ✅ Multiple scholarly sources cite this
- **Citation**: Etymonline, Wikipedia (verified)

---

### Charles Cotton (1664)
> "Aeneas, here's a Health to thee, To Pusse and to good company."

- **Source**: *Virgile Travestie*, Charles Cotton, 1664
- **Context**: Burlesque poetry with deliberate wordplay
- **Significance**: Demonstrates double meaning in active use
- **Verification**: ✅ Academic sources confirm
- **Citation**: lacydanes.com etymology analysis

### The Euphemism Treadmill

> "The euphemism treadmill is the linguistic phenomenon where euphemisms eventually become taboo themselves and must be replaced by new euphemisms."

- **Source**: Steven Pinker, *The Blank Slate* (popularized term)
- **Context**: Explaining how "clean" words become "dirty"
- **Verification**: ✅ Well-documented linguistic concept
- **Citation**: Wikipedia "Euphemism"

## Dictionary Definitions (Historical)

### Oxford English Dictionary

**Sense 1**: A cat (informal, esp. as a form of address)
- First documented: 16th century
- Origin: Probably from Low German or Dutch

**Sense 2**: A woman (archaic, affectionate)
- First documented: 1583
- Origin: Extension from cat (softness, affection)

**Sense 3**: Female genitalia (vulgar slang)
- First documented: 17th century
- Origin: Possibly from Old Norse *pūss* (pocket)

**Sense 4**: A weak or cowardly person (derogatory)
- First documented: 1960s (American English)
- Origin: From Sense 3, implying lack of masculinity

---

## Notable Absences

### Samuel Johnson (1755)
- **Did not include the word** in *A Dictionary of the English Language*
- Silence itself is evidence of the word's vulgar status
- Included other body-related terms but drew editorial lines
