# Timeline: RAP History (1970-2024)

> Comprehensive chronology of rap music from Bronx block parties to global dominance

---

## 1970s: Birth and Party Era

### 1970
**Event**: Gil Scott-Heron releases "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised"
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Spoken-word-over-rhythm template establishes blueprint for conscious rap; direct precursor combining political poetry with funk rhythms.
**Source**: Primary recording; Charnas, "The Big Payback" (2010)

### 1971
**Event**: The Last Poets release "This Is Madness"
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Continues the spoken-word tradition with political content over percussion, influencing MCs for decades.
**Source**: Primary recording; Fernando, "The New Beats" (1994)

### 1972
**Event**: DJ Kool Herc begins experimenting with break isolation at house parties
**Location**: Bronx, New York
**Significance**: Early technical development of the "merry-go-round" technique that would define Hip-Hop DJing.
**Source**: Herc oral histories; Chang, "Can't Stop Won't Stop" (2005)

### 1973-08-11
**Event**: DJ Kool Herc and Cindy Campbell throw back-to-school party at 1520 Sedgwick Avenue rec room
**Location**: 1520 Sedgwick Avenue, Bronx, New York
**Significance**: Widely recognized as Hip-Hop's birth date; Herc premieres "merry-go-round" technique isolating breaks. Admission was 25 cents for ladies, 50 cents for guys.
**Source**: Chang, "Can't Stop Won't Stop" (2005); Herc oral histories; Smithsonian documentation

### 1973
**Event**: Coke La Rock and Clark Kent become first MCs at Herc's parties
**Location**: Bronx, New York
**Significance**: Birth of MCing as distinct element; transition from DJ-only to DJ+MC format.
**Source**: Herc oral histories; Chang (2005)

### 1974
**Event**: Afrika Bambaataa founds the Universal Zulu Nation
**Location**: Bronx River Houses, Bronx, New York
**Significance**: Transforms gang structure into cultural organization; adds "knowledge" as fifth element; redirects youth from violence to culture.
**Source**: Chang, "Can't Stop Won't Stop" (2005); Zulu Nation archives

### 1974
**Event**: Grandmaster Flash begins developing "quick mix theory"
**Location**: Bronx, New York
**Significance**: Innovates punch phrasing, cutting, and backspin techniques that elevate DJing to virtuosic art form.
**Source**: Flash interviews; Fernando, "The New Beats" (1994)

### 1975
**Event**: Grand Wizard Theodore accidentally invents scratching
**Location**: Bronx, New York
**Significance**: Revolutionary turntable technique discovered while stopping a record to hear his mother; becomes defining element of Hip-Hop DJing.
**Source**: Theodore oral histories; Chang (2005)

### 1976
**Event**: Rock Steady Crew founded by Jo-Jo and Jimmy D
**Location**: Bronx, New York
**Significance**: First formalized breaking crew; later led by Crazy Legs; documents b-boy culture.
**Source**: "The Freshest Kids" documentary (2002)

### 1976
**Event**: Grandmaster Flash performs at Audubon Ballroom
**Location**: Harlem, New York
**Significance**: Hip-Hop moves from outdoor jams to indoor venues; signals growing professionalization.
**Source**: Flash interviews; Chang (2005)

### 1977
**Event**: DJ Hollywood and DJ Lovebug Starski popularize party rapping in Harlem clubs
**Location**: Harlem, New York
**Significance**: Parallel tradition to South Bronx; "disco rap" style influences commercial direction.
**Source**: George, "Hip Hop America" (1998)

### 1977-07-13
**Event**: New York City blackout leads to widespread looting of DJ equipment
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Often cited as catalyzing Hip-Hop expansion; sudden availability of turntables and mixers to aspiring DJs.
**Source**: Contemporary news archives; Chang (2005) (Note: disputed by some historians)

### 1978
**Event**: Harlem World disco opens
**Location**: 116th Street, Harlem, New York
**Significance**: Hip-Hop moves into larger venues with better sound systems; transition from parks to clubs.
**Source**: George, "Hip Hop America" (1998)

### 1978
**Event**: Love Bug Starski and/or Keith Cowboy coin term "Hip-Hop"
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Cultural movement gains a name; attribution disputed between multiple claimants.
**Source**: Various oral histories; Chang (2005)

### 1979-03
**Event**: Fatback Band releases "King Tim III (Personality Jock)"
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: First commercially released record with rapping; often overlooked in favor of "Rapper's Delight."
**Source**: Charnas, "The Big Payback" (2010)

## 1979-1984: First Records

### 1979-09-16
**Event**: "Rapper's Delight" by Sugarhill Gang released on Sugar Hill Records
**Location**: Englewood, New Jersey (Sugar Hill Records)
**Significance**: First rap record to chart nationally; reaches #36 on Billboard Hot 100; brings rap to mainstream audience worldwide.
**Source**: Billboard charts; Charnas, "The Big Payback" (2010)

### 1979-10
**Event**: Sylvia Robinson establishes Sugar Hill Records
**Location**: Englewood, New Jersey
**Significance**: First dedicated Hip-Hop label; discovers and records early rap acts; controls early rap industry.
**Source**: Charnas, "The Big Payback" (2010)

### 1979
**Event**: Kurtis Blow signs to Mercury Records
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: First rapper signed to a major label; legitimizes rap as commercial genre.
**Source**: George, "Hip Hop America" (1998)

### 1979-12
**Event**: "Rapper's Delight" reaches Top 40 in multiple countries
**Location**: Global
**Significance**: Proves international appetite for rap music; sells over 2 million copies worldwide.
**Source**: Billboard International charts; Charnas (2010)

### 1980-06
**Event**: Kurtis Blow's "The Breaks" becomes first gold rap single
**Location**: United States
**Significance**: First rap record to sell 500,000 copies; reaches #4 R&B; commercial viability proven.
**Source**: RIAA certification; Billboard charts

### 1980-09
**Event**: Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five release "Freedom"
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Early recorded output from pioneering crew; establishes Sugar Hill as rap's home.
**Source**: Primary recording; Charnas (2010)

### 1981-02-14
**Event**: Funky Four + 1 performs on Saturday Night Live
**Location**: NBC Studios, New York City
**Significance**: First Hip-Hop act on national television; Sha-Rock becomes first prominent female MC on national TV.
**Source**: SNL archives; Chang (2005)

### 1981
**Event**: "Rapper's Delight" achieves worldwide sales of 2 million copies
**Location**: Global
**Significance**: Demonstrates rap's global commercial potential before MTV era.
**Source**: Charnas, "The Big Payback" (2010)

### 1981
**Event**: Afrika Bambaataa releases "Zulu Nation Throwdown"
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Zulu Nation's first recording; documents alternative to Sugar Hill's dominance.
**Source**: Primary recording

### 1982-07-01
**Event**: "The Message" by Grandmaster Flash & The Furious Five released
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Transforms rap from party music to social commentary; "It's like a jungle sometimes, it makes me wonder how I keep from going under" becomes iconic.
**Source**: Chang (2005); Rose, "Black Noise" (1994)

### 1982-04
**Event**: Afrika Bambaataa & Soulsonic Force release "Planet Rock"
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Fuses Hip-Hop with electronic music (Kraftwerk's "Trans-Europe Express"); creates electro-funk genre; influences techno and house music.
**Source**: Primary recording; Chang (2005)

### 1982
**Event**: Russell Simmons and Rick Rubin meet at a New York club
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Future Def Jam founders begin collaboration that will reshape Hip-Hop industry.
**Source**: Charnas, "The Big Payback" (2010)

### 1983
**Event**: "Wild Style" film released (Charlie Ahearn)
**Location**: New York City premiere
**Significance**: First Hip-Hop film; features Fab 5 Freddy, Rock Steady Crew, Grandmaster Flash; documents culture for history.
**Source**: Film primary source

### 1983
**Event**: "Style Wars" documentary airs on PBS
**Location**: National broadcast
**Significance**: Definitive documentary of graffiti and breaking era; brings Hip-Hop culture to mainstream audiences.
**Source**: Film primary source

### 1983-07
**Event**: Run-DMC releases "It's Like That" / "Sucker M.C.'s"
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Harder, stripped-down sound signals transition from old school to new school; marks shift away from disco influence.
**Source**: Charnas (2010); Primary recording

### 1984
**Event**: "Beat Street" film released by Orion Pictures
**Location**: Nationwide theatrical release
**Significance**: Major studio Hip-Hop film brings culture to wider audience; soundtrack goes gold.
**Source**: Box office records; film primary source

### 1984
**Event**: Def Jam Recordings officially founded
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Russell Simmons and Rick Rubin create label that will dominate Hip-Hop for decades.
**Source**: Charnas, "The Big Payback" (2010)

### 1984-11
**Event**: LL Cool J, age 16, releases "I Need a Beat" on Def Jam
**Location**: Queens, New York
**Significance**: First artist on Def Jam; signals new generation of rap talent.
**Source**: Def Jam archives; Charnas (2010)

### 1984
**Event**: UTFO's "Roxanne, Roxanne" sparks first major rap rivalry
**Location**: New York City
**Significance**: Generates over 100 answer records including Roxanne Shante's "Roxanne's Revenge"; establishes battle tradition.
**Source**: Chang (2005)

### 1984-11
**Event**: Roxanne Shante records "Roxanne's Revenge" at age 14
**Location**: Queens, New York
**Significance**: First successful female battle rapper; record sells over 250,000 copies; sparks "Roxanne Wars."
**Source**: Chang (2005); Rose, "Black Noise" (1994)

## 1985-1992: Golden Age

### 1985-01
**Event**: Run-DMC's "King of Rock" album released
**Location**: United States
**Significance**: Rock-rap fusion opens new white audience; title track features prominent rock guitar; goes platinum.

### 1985
**Event**: The first Fresh Fest tour grosses $3.5 million
**Location**: National tour
**Significance**: Proves rap can fill arenas; largest rap tour to date.
**Source**: Charnas, "The Big Payback" (2010)

### 1985
**Event**: Philadelphia's Schoolly D releases "P.S.K. What Does It Mean?"
**Location**: Philadelphia, Pennsylvania
**Significance**: Widely considered first gangsta rap record; establishes template for street narratives.
**Source**: Academic sources; Ice-T acknowledgment

### 1986-05-15
**Event**: Run-DMC's "Raising Hell" released
**Location**: United States
**Significance**: First rap album to go triple platinum; "Walk This Way" collaboration with Aerosmith reaches #4 on Billboard Hot 100.

### 1986-07
**Event**: Run-DMC signs endorsement deal with Adidas

**Significance**: First major corporate brand partnership for a rap act; Hip-Hop fashion enters commerce.

### 1987-02
**Event**: Public Enemy releases "Yo! Bum Rush the Show"

### 1987-03

### 1987-08-27

### 1987

**Event**: MC Lyte releases "Lyte as a Rock"

**Event**: Biz Markie's "Just a Friend" reaches #9 on Billboard Hot 100

**Significance**: Controversial achievement; white rapper with fabricated street credibility; prompts authenticity debates.

### 1991-04
**Event**: N.W.A releases "Niggaz4Life"
**Location**: Los Angeles, California
**Significance**: First hardcore rap album to debut at #1 on Billboard 200; proves gangsta rap commercial dominance.

**Event**: A Tribe Called Quest releases "The Low End Theory"
**Location**: Queens, New York
**Significance**: Jazz-rap fusion peak; Ron Carter bass samples; alternative Hip-Hop commercial and critical success.

**Location**: New York Federal Court
**Significance**: Biz Markie loses landmark case; sampling without clearance becomes legally untenable; changes production economics.

### 1992-12-15

**Location**: Los Angeles, California
**Significance**: G-funk sound defines West Coast for decade; Death Row era begins; Snoop Dogg introduced.

### 1992-04
**Event**: Los Angeles riots following Rodney King verdict
**Location**: Los Angeles, California
**Significance**: N.W.A's "Fuck tha Police" proves prescient; Ice-T's "Cop Killer" controversy follows; rap and politics collide.
**Source**: News archives
