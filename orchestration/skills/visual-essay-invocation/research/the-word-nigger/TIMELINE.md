# Timeline: The Word "Nigger"

## ⚠️ Content Advisory

This document traces the historical evolution of a racial slur for scholarly purposes. The full term appears only in primary source citations and specific historical contexts.

---

## Timeline Overview

This timeline documents the word's journey from Latin origins through its weaponization in the slave trade, its entrenchment in American law and culture, and contemporary debates about its use and prohibition.

---

## Era 1: Origins & Linguistic Pathways (Antiquity–1600)

### Latin Foundation

| Period | Development | Source |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Classical Latin | *niger* — adjective meaning "black" (color), "dark," "dusky" | Oxford Latin Dictionary |
| | Neutral descriptor: *niger color* (black color), *niger oceanus* (dark ocean) | Standard Latin texts |
| | No racial connotation in classical usage | — |

### Romance Language Development

| Date | Language | Form | Meaning | Source |
|------|----------|------|---------|--------|
| c. 1400s | Spanish | *negro* | Black (color), person of African descent | Real Academia Española |
| c. 1400s | Portuguese | *negro* | Black, enslaved African person | Portuguese dictionaries |
| c. 1500s | French | *nègre* | Black person, enslaved person | French historical dictionaries |

**Note**: In Spanish and Portuguese colonial contexts, *negro* became the standard term in the Atlantic slave trade, carrying connotations of enslaved status.

### Entry into English

| Date | Text/Context | Form | Significance | Source |
|------|--------------|------|--------------|--------|
| 1574 | Earliest OED attestation | "Negar" | First documented English use | OED, "nigger" entry |
| 1587 | English travel writings | "negar," "niger" | Describing African peoples | OED citations |
| 1589 | *The Arte of English Poesie* | "niger" | Color reference in literary context | Puttenham |

**Linguistic Pathway**: Latin *niger* → Spanish/Portuguese *negro* → English *negar/niger/negro* → dialectal shift to "nigger"

## Era 2: Colonial & Slave Trade Period (1600–1776)

### 17th Century: Term Crystallizes

| Date | Event/Text | Usage Context | Significance | Source |
|------|------------|---------------|--------------|--------|
| 1619 | First enslaved Africans arrive at Jamestown | — | Beginning of American slavery | National Archives |
| c. 1640s | Colonial legal codes | "Negro," "negar" variants | Legal classification begins | Berlin (1998) |
| 1662 | Virginia Hereditary Slavery Act | Racial terminology in law | Enslavement made hereditary | Virginia colonial records |
| 1680s-1690s | Slave codes (Virginia, Maryland) | "Negro" and variants | Formal legal category | Colonial legal archives |

### 18th Century: Usage Hardens

| Date | Event/Text | Usage Context | Significance | Source |
|------|------------|---------------|--------------|--------|
| 1705 | Virginia Slave Codes | Legal terminology | Comprehensive racial categorization | LOC colonial records |
| 1755 | Johnson's *Dictionary* | "Negro" as entry | No separate slur entry — suggests "nigger" was dialectal variant | Johnson (1755) |
| 1773 | *Poems on Various Subjects* | Phillis Wheatley's self-description | First African American published poet uses "negro" | Library Company of Philadelphia |

**Key Observation**: In 18th century, "negro" was the "formal" term; "nigger" existed as dialectal/vulgar variant, increasingly used by lower-class whites and in commercial/casual contexts.

## Dictionary Definition Evolution Timeline

| Year | Dictionary | Definition/Label | Significance |
|------|------------|------------------|--------------|
| 1755 | Johnson | No separate entry (see "negro") | Term considered dialectal |
| 1828 | Webster | No separate entry (see "negro") | American standard |
| 1889 | OED (1st ed.) | Entry exists, neutral etymology | Historical documentation |
| 1934 | Webster's 2nd | Entry with "vulgar" label | First explicit usage warning |
| 1961 | Webster's 3rd | Entry with "usually taken to be offensive" | Modern labeling begins |
| 1989 | OED (2nd ed.) | "Colloquial and usually contemptuous" | Updated labeling |
| 2000s | Major dictionaries | "Offensive," "taboo," "highly offensive" | Contemporary consensus |
