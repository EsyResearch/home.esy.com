# Key Figures: The Word "Nigger"

## ⚠️ Content Advisory

This document profiles historical figures relevant to the evolution of a racial slur. Included are those who spread/normalized the term AND those who challenged, documented, and shaped norms against it.

---

## Figure Selection Criteria

Figures are included ONLY when:
1. Reputable sources clearly document their influence on the word's history
2. Verified photography or portraiture exists (public domain or licensed)
3. Their role is specific and documented (not general association)

Categories:
- **Documenters**: Those who recorded, analyzed, or formally defined the term
- **Resisters**: Those who challenged, condemned, or shaped norms against it
- **Normalizers**: Those whose influence spread/entrenched usage (documented critically)
- **Reappropriators**: Those who reclaimed or complicated the term's meaning

## Category 1: Documenters & Lexicographers

### Samuel Johnson (1709–1784)
**Epithet**: The Great Lexicographer

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Created *A Dictionary of the English Language* (1755)
- Entry for "negro" as standard term; absence of "nigger" as main entry suggests it was considered dialectal/vulgar variant
- His dictionary set English lexicographic standards for over a century

**Key Contribution**: Established "negro" as the formal English term, implicitly classifying "nigger" as non-standard

**Image Source**: 
- Joshua Reynolds portrait (c. 1756–1757)
- National Portrait Gallery, London
- Rights: Public domain

**Bio (45 words)**: Samuel Johnson was England's foremost lexicographer, whose 1755 *Dictionary of the English Language* defined English usage for generations. His treatment of racial terminology—including "negro" as standard and no separate entry for "nigger"—reflected 18th-century distinctions between formal and vulgar speech.

---

### Noah Webster (1758–1843)
**Epithet**: Father of American English

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Created *An American Dictionary of the English Language* (1828)
- First major American dictionary to standardize American English
- Treatment of racial terminology reflected early American usage

**Key Contribution**: Documented American English usage patterns, including regional variations

**Image Source**:
- James Herring portrait (c. 1833)
- Yale University Art Gallery
- Rights: Public domain

**Bio (48 words)**: Noah Webster created the foundational American dictionary in 1828, distinguishing American from British English. His documentation of racial terminology in early America provides a lexicographic record of how terms were classified and used in the young republic's commercial and legal language.

---

### James Murray (1837–1915)
**Epithet**: Editor of the Oxford English Dictionary

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Led the creation of the Oxford English Dictionary (1879–1915)
- OED's historical method documented earliest uses of English words
- Entry for the slur traces etymology and attestations systematically

**Key Contribution**: Created the definitive historical record of the word's English origins and evolution

**Image Source**:
- Photograph, Scriptorium (Oxford)
- Oxford University Press archives
- Rights: Likely public domain (pre-1929)

**Bio (52 words)**: James Murray led the monumental Oxford English Dictionary project, applying historical principles to document the full scope of English vocabulary. The OED's entry on the slur—tracing its roots to Latin *niger* and documenting earliest English uses from the 1570s—remains the authoritative etymological reference for scholars.

## Category 2: Resisters & Reformers

### Frederick Douglass (1818–1895)
**Epithet**: The Great Orator and Abolitionist

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Born enslaved; became foremost African American orator and writer
- Documented and condemned the dehumanizing language of slavery
- His speeches and writings challenged the term's normalization

**Key Contribution**: Used oratory and writing to demonstrate the humanity that racist language denied

**Verified Quote**: "What, to the American slave, is your 4th of July?" —"What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" (1852)

**Image Source**:
- Daguerreotype (c. 1847–1852)
- National Portrait Gallery, Smithsonian
- Rights: Public domain

**Bio (55 words)**: Frederick Douglass escaped slavery to become the 19th century's most influential African American voice. His oratory, journalism, and autobiographies challenged every premise of white supremacy, including its language. His existence—brilliant, eloquent, uncompromising—was itself a refutation of the dehumanization that racist terminology sought to enforce.

---

### Ida B. Wells (1862–1931)
**Epithet**: Anti-Lynching Crusader and Journalist

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Documented over 700 lynchings in *Southern Horrors* (1892) and *A Red Record* (1895)
- Exposed how racial terrorism was justified through racist language
- Challenged the myths used to rationalize violence

**Key Contribution**: Connected racist language to racist violence in investigative journalism

**Image Source**:
- Mary Garrity photograph (c. 1893)
- National Portrait Gallery, Smithsonian
- Rights: Public domain

**Bio (50 words)**: Ida B. Wells pioneered investigative journalism documenting the epidemic of lynching in the American South. Her work exposed how dehumanizing language—the slur among the tools—created conditions where white mobs could murder Black Americans with impunity, often to cheering crowds.

---

### W.E.B. Du Bois (1868–1963)
**Epithet**: Scholar and Civil Rights Pioneer

**Relevance to Word History**:
- First African American to earn a Harvard Ph.D.
- *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903) addressed the "color line"
- Helped found the NAACP (1909)
- Challenged racial terminology in academic and public discourse

**Key Contribution**: Established intellectual framework for challenging racist language and structures

**Verified Quote**: "The problem of the Twentieth Century is the problem of the color-line." —*The Souls of Black Folk* (1903)

**Image Source**:
- C.M. Battey photograph (c. 1918)
- Library of Congress
- Rights: Public domain

**Bio (52 words)**: W.E.B. Du Bois was the towering intellectual of African American liberation, whose scholarship and activism over seven decades challenged white supremacy at every level. His 1903 *The Souls of Black Folk* remains foundational to understanding race in America, including the power of language to define and divide.

---

### James Weldon Johnson (1871–1938)
**Epithet**: Renaissance Man of the Harlem Renaissance

**Relevance to Word History**:
- First African American executive secretary of NAACP (1920–1930)
- Poet, novelist, diplomat, and civil rights leader
- Documented Black vernacular and culture while challenging dehumanizing terminology

**Key Contribution**: Elevated Black cultural expression as counter-narrative to racist language

**Image Source**:
- Carl Van Vechten photograph (1932)
- Library of Congress
- Rights: Public domain

**Bio (48 words)**: James Weldon Johnson was diplomat, poet, and civil rights leader who led the NAACP during the Harlem Renaissance. His work celebrated African American culture and language while fighting the political and linguistic structures that sought to diminish Black humanity.

---

### Thurgood Marshall (1908–1993)
**Epithet**: The Great Dissenter, First Black Supreme Court Justice

**Relevance to Word History**:
- NAACP Legal Defense Fund architect
- Argued *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954)
- Challenged legal structures built on racial classification

**Key Contribution**: Dismantled "separate but equal" doctrine, challenging the legal language of segregation

**Image Source**:
- Official Supreme Court portrait
- Supreme Court of the United States
- Rights: Public domain (government work)

**Bio (50 words)**: Thurgood Marshall argued 32 cases before the Supreme Court, winning 29, including *Brown v. Board of Education* that dismantled school segregation. As the first African American Supreme Court Justice, he spent his career challenging the legal structures and language that codified racial hierarchy.

---

### Martin Luther King Jr. (1929–1968)
**Epithet**: The Dreamer, Leader of the Civil Rights Movement

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Led nonviolent resistance to segregation
- Reframed the language of American justice to include Black Americans
- His speeches countered dehumanizing rhetoric with appeals to shared humanity

**Key Contribution**: Offered dignified counter-language to racist terminology

**Verified Quote**: "I have a dream that my four little children will one day live in a nation where they will not be judged by the color of their skin but by the content of their character." —March on Washington (1963)

**Image Source**:
- Rowland Scherman photograph (March on Washington, 1963)
- National Archives
- Rights: Public domain (government work)

**Bio (55 words)**: Martin Luther King Jr. led the American Civil Rights Movement through nonviolent resistance, reshaping the nation's moral language. His oratory—from Birmingham jail to the Lincoln Memorial—offered a vocabulary of dignity, justice, and beloved community that directly countered the dehumanization embedded in racist terminology.

---

### James Baldwin (1924–1987)
**Epithet**: The Witness, Essayist of American Conscience

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Essays including *Notes of a Native Son* (1955) and *The Fire Next Time* (1963)
- Examined the psychological damage of racist language
- Analyzed how language constructs and enforces racial hierarchy

**Key Contribution**: Articulated how racist language wounds both those targeted and those who wield it

**Verified Quote**: "Not everything that is faced can be changed; but nothing can be changed until it is faced."

**Image Source**:
- Allan Warren photograph (1969)
- Creative Commons licensed
- Rights: CC BY-SA 3.0

**Bio (58 words)**: James Baldwin was the 20th century's most incisive essayist on race in America. From Harlem to Paris and back, he examined how racist language—including "the word"—functioned as psychological weapon, wounding Black Americans while corrupting the souls of white Americans who deployed it. His prose remains unmatched in its moral clarity.

## Category 3: Scholars & Analysts

### Randall Kennedy (b. 1954)
**Epithet**: Harvard Law Professor, Scholar of the Word

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Wrote *Nigger: The Strange Career of a Troublesome Word* (2002)
- First book-length scholarly study of the term
- Examined etymology, legal history, contemporary debates

**Key Contribution**: Brought scholarly rigor to a topic most academics avoided

**Image Source**:
- Harvard Law School faculty photograph
- Harvard University
- Rights: Educational/fair use

**Bio (48 words)**: Randall Kennedy, Harvard Law professor, published the groundbreaking 2002 study *Nigger: The Strange Career of a Troublesome Word*, bringing legal and historical analysis to the term's evolution. His work remains the most comprehensive academic treatment, examining everything from etymology to contemporary debates.

---

### Geneva Smitherman (b. 1940)
**Epithet**: Linguist of Black English

**Relevance to Word History**:
- *Black Talk* (1994, revised 2000) documents African American Vernacular English
- Analyzed the term's use within and outside Black communities
- Scholarly framework for understanding intra-group vs. inter-group usage

**Key Contribution**: Provided linguistic analysis distinguishing community use from external slur

**Image Source**:
- Michigan State University archives
- Rights: Educational/fair use

**Bio (45 words)**: Geneva Smitherman is the preeminent scholar of African American Vernacular English, whose work documents how Black Americans have transformed the English language. Her analysis of the N-word distinguishes between its weaponization by outsiders and its complex meanings within Black communities.

## Category 4: Normalizers (Documented Critically)

*Note: These figures are included for historical documentation only. Their inclusion does not endorse their views but acknowledges their documented role in spreading or entrenching the term.*

### Thomas "Daddy" Rice (1808–1860)
**Epithet**: Father of American Minstrelsy

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Created "Jim Crow" character (c. 1830)
- Pioneered blackface minstrelsy as mass entertainment
- His performances normalized racist caricature and language

**Key Contribution**: Established minstrelsy as entertainment form, normalizing racist terminology

**Image Source**:
- Period lithographs and sheet music covers
- Library of Congress
- Rights: Public domain

**Bio (52 words)**: Thomas Rice created the "Jim Crow" character around 1830, launching blackface minstrelsy as America's dominant entertainment form. His performances—and the industry they spawned—normalized racist caricature and language in American popular culture. The term "Jim Crow" eventually named the entire segregation system.

---

### D.W. Griffith (1875–1948)
**Epithet**: Pioneer Filmmaker, Racist Propagandist

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Directed *The Birth of a Nation* (1915)
- Film glorified the Ku Klux Klan and depicted Black Americans as subhuman
- Used intertitles with racist language; sparked Klan revival

**Key Contribution**: Demonstrated cinema's power to spread racist imagery and language to mass audiences

**Image Source**:
- Studio portraits (1910s-1920s)
- Academy of Motion Picture Arts archives
- Rights: Public domain

**Bio (55 words)**: D.W. Griffith's 1915 *The Birth of a Nation* was a technical masterpiece and moral catastrophe. The film glorified the Ku Klux Klan, depicted Black Americans as dangerous animals, and was screened at the White House. It sparked Klan revival and demonstrated cinema's terrifying power to spread racist narrative.

## Category 5: Reappropriators & Complicators

### Richard Pryor (1940–2005)
**Epithet**: The Prophet of Comedy

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Used the term extensively in 1970s comedy
- Later renounced usage after Africa trip (c. 1979)
- His arc embodies the reappropriation debate

**Key Contribution**: Demonstrated both power and limits of reclamation

**Image Source**:
- Performance photographs (1970s-1980s)
- Various archives
- Rights: Requires licensing

**Bio (50 words)**: Richard Pryor revolutionized comedy with raw, autobiographical material that used the N-word to expose American racism's absurdities. After a transformative trip to Africa around 1979, he publicly renounced using the term. His evolution embodies the ongoing debate about reclamation and its limits.

---

### Eazy-E (Eric Wright, 1964–1995)
**Epithet**: Godfather of Gangsta Rap

**Relevance to Word History**:
- Co-founded N.W.A. (Niggaz Wit Attitudes, 1986)
- Group name was deliberate provocation and reclamation
- Pioneered explicit reappropriation in hip-hop

**Key Contribution**: Made reappropriation a defining feature of gangsta rap

**Image Source**:
- Promotional photographs (late 1980s-early 1990s)
- Music archives
- Rights: Requires licensing

**Bio (48 words)**: Eric "Eazy-E" Wright co-founded N.W.A. in 1986, a group whose very name—Niggaz Wit Attitudes—was a deliberate reclamation. Their 1988 album *Straight Outta Compton* launched gangsta rap and intensified debates about whether reappropriation empowers or perpetuates a wound.
