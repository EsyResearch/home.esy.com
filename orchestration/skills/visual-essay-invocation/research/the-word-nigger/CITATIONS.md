## Tier 1: Primary Sources & Archives

### Dictionaries (Historical Editions)

1. **Oxford English Dictionary (OED)**
   - Entry: "nigger, n. and adj."
   - First documented use: 1574 (as "Negar")
   - URL: https://www.oed.com (subscription required)
   - Use: Etymology, earliest attestations, definition evolution
   - Rights: Fair use for scholarly quotation

2. **Webster's Dictionary (1828 — First Edition)**
   - Noah Webster, *An American Dictionary of the English Language*
   - Entry for "negro" and regional variants
   - Archive: https://webstersdictionary1828.com
   - Use: Early American lexicography, neutral-to-pejorative shift documentation

3. **Samuel Johnson, *A Dictionary of the English Language* (1755)**
   - Entry for "negro" — note absence of slur form as primary entry
   - Archive: Multiple facsimiles available
   - Use: 18th-century English usage standards

### Legal & Government Archives

4. **Library of Congress — Slave Narratives Collection**
   - Federal Writers' Project (1936-1938)
   - URL: https://www.loc.gov/collections/slave-narratives-from-the-federal-writers-project-1936-to-1938/
   - Use: Primary accounts of term usage in enslaved communities
   - Rights: Public domain

5. **National Archives — Slave Trade Records**
   - Records of the Bureau of Refugees, Freedmen, and Abandoned Lands
   - URL: https://www.archives.gov/research/african-americans
   - Use: Legal and commercial documentation of racial terminology
   - Rights: Public domain

6. **Dred Scott v. Sandford, 60 U.S. 393 (1857)**
   - Supreme Court opinion text
   - Contains explicit language about racial status
   - Archive: Library of Congress, Supreme Court records
   - Use: Legal codification of racial hierarchy

7. **Plessy v. Ferguson, 163 U.S. 537 (1896)**
   - Supreme Court opinion establishing "separate but equal"
   - Use: Legal language of segregation era
   - Archive: Library of Congress

### Newspaper Archives

8. **Chronicling America — Historic American Newspapers**
   - Library of Congress digitized newspapers (1777-1963)
   - URL: https://chroniclingamerica.loc.gov
   - Use: Frequency analysis, contextual usage, historical headlines
   - Rights: Public domain

9. **ProQuest Historical Newspapers**
   - Major papers including *The New York Times*, *Chicago Tribune*, etc.
   - Use: 19th-20th century usage patterns
   - Access: University libraries (subscription)

## Tier 2: Academic Sources

### Linguistics & Etymology

10. **Smitherman, Geneva. *Black Talk: Words and Phrases from the Hood to the Amen Corner*. Revised ed., Houghton Mifflin, 2000.**
    - Comprehensive linguistic study of African American Vernacular English
    - Chapter on slurs and reclamation
    - ISBN: 978-0395969199
    - Use: Linguistic analysis, community perspectives

11. **Rahman, Jacquelyn. "The N Word: Its History and Use in the African American Community." *Journal of English Linguistics*, vol. 40, no. 2, 2012, pp. 137-171.**
    - Peer-reviewed linguistic analysis
    - Addresses intra-group vs. inter-group usage
    - DOI: 10.1177/0075424211414807
    - Use: Scholarly framework for usage analysis

12. **Allan, Keith, and Kate Burridge. *Forbidden Words: Taboo and the Censoring of Language*. Cambridge UP, 2006.**
    - Comprehensive study of taboo language
    - Chapter on racial slurs and semantic pejoration
    - ISBN: 978-0521525640
    - Use: Theoretical framework

13. **Hughes, Geoffrey. *An Encyclopedia of Swearing: The Social History of Oaths, Profanity, Foul Language, and Ethnic Slurs in the English-Speaking World*. M.E. Sharpe, 2006.**
    - Extensive entry on racial epithets
    - Historical documentation
    - ISBN: 978-0765612311
    - Use: Historical context, comparative analysis

### History

14. **Kennedy, Randall. *Nigger: The Strange Career of a Troublesome Word*. Pantheon Books, 2002.**
    - Definitive book-length study by Harvard Law professor
    - Etymology, history, legal cases, cultural debates
    - ISBN: 978-0375421723
    - Use: Primary academic reference, legal history

15. **Asim, Jabari. *The N Word: Who Can Say It, Who Shouldn't, and Why*. Houghton Mifflin, 2007.**
    - Cultural history and contemporary debates
    - ISBN: 978-0618197163
    - Use: Cultural context, reappropriation debates

16. **Berlin, Ira. *Many Thousands Gone: The First Two Centuries of Slavery in North America*. Belknap Press, 1998.**
    - Prize-winning history of early American slavery
    - Documentation of racial terminology in legal and commercial contexts
    - ISBN: 978-0674002111
    - Use: Historical context for language development

17. **Baptist, Edward E. *The Half Has Never Been Told: Slavery and the Making of American Capitalism*. Basic Books, 2014.**
    - Economic history of slavery
    - Primary source quotations with terminology
    - ISBN: 978-0465002962
    - Use: Commercial and legal language documentation

### Minstrelsy & Popular Culture

18. **Lott, Eric. *Love and Theft: Blackface Minstrelsy and the American Working Class*. Oxford UP, 1993.**
    - Academic study of minstrel tradition
    - Documentation of language in performance
    - ISBN: 978-0195074758
    - Use: Popular culture history, visual sources guidance

19. **Toll, Robert C. *Blacking Up: The Minstrel Show in Nineteenth-Century America*. Oxford UP, 1974.**
    - Foundational study of minstrelsy
    - ISBN: 978-0195018905
    - Use: Historical documentation of entertainment industry language

### Civil Rights & Modern Era

20. **Branch, Taylor. *Parting the Waters: America in the King Years 1954-63*. Simon & Schuster, 1988.**
    - Pulitzer Prize-winning Civil Rights history
    - Documentation of language in movement context
    - ISBN: 978-0671687427
    - Use: Civil Rights era language conflicts

21. **Dyson, Michael Eric. *Know What I Mean?: Reflections on Hip Hop*. Basic Civitas Books, 2007.**
    - Academic analysis of hip-hop culture
    - Chapter on reclamation and reappropriation
    - ISBN: 978-0465017737
    - Use: Contemporary reappropriation debates
