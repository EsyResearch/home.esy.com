## Ancient & Medieval Era

### Figure 1: Darius the Great (550-486 BCE)

**Epithet**: The Persian King Whose Soldiers Invented Field Pizza

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | 6th Century BCE |
| **Role** | King of the Persian Empire |
| **Contribution** | Persian soldiers under Darius baked flatbreads with cheese and dates on their battle shields—one of the earliest documented "pizza-like" preparations |
| **Historical Significance** | Demonstrates pizza-like foods existed in military/practical contexts millennia before Naples |
| **Imagery** | Persian relief sculptures, Persepolis carvings showing royal figures; historical illustrations |
| **Verified Quote** | None specific to food; military context only |
| **Source** | Cambridge World History of Food |

## Naples Origins (18th-19th Century)

### Figure 2: Antonio Testa

**Epithet**: The First Documented Pizzaiolo

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Early 18th Century |
| **Role** | Pizzaiolo in Naples |
| **Contribution** | Operated one of the earliest documented pizza-making establishments in Naples; part of the 54 pizzerias recorded by 1807 |
| **Historical Significance** | Represents the anonymous working-class pizzaioli who created the foundations of pizza culture |
| **Imagery** | No known photographs; era-appropriate illustrations of Neapolitan street vendors |
| **Source** | Naples Municipal Archives |

### Figure 3: Raffaele Esposito (c. 1850-1890s)

**Epithet**: The Father of the Margherita Pizza

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Late 19th Century |
| **Role** | Pizzaiolo at Pizzeria Brandi (then "Pietro... e basta così"), Naples |
| **Contribution** | Credited with creating the Pizza Margherita in 1889 for Queen Margherita of Savoy; the pizza featured tomato, mozzarella, and basil representing the Italian flag colors |
| **Historical Significance** | Central figure in pizza's most famous origin story; whether accurate or mythologized, his name is synonymous with pizza's elevation from peasant food to national dish |
| **Imagery** | Historical photographs from Pizzeria Brandi archives; 19th century Neapolitan portraiture |
| **Verified Quote** | Letter from royal household thanking him for the pizzas (held at Pizzeria Brandi—authenticity debated) |
| **Source** | Carol Helstosky, Pizzeria Brandi archives, multiple secondary sources |
| **Notes** | The Margherita story is likely embellished; similar pizzas existed before 1889, but the legend persists |

### Figure 4: Queen Margherita of Savoy (1851-1926)

**Epithet**: The Queen Who Gave Pizza Its Name

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Late 19th Century |
| **Role** | Queen consort of Italy (1878-1900) |
| **Contribution** | According to legend, during a visit to Naples in 1889, she expressed delight at Esposito's patriotic pizza, lending her name to the "Pizza Margherita" |
| **Historical Significance** | Royal endorsement—whether factual or legendary—helped legitimize pizza beyond its working-class origins |
| **Imagery** | Extensive royal portrait photography; formal portraits in regalia; images of 1889 Naples visit |
| **Verified Quote** | None directly about pizza; appreciation letter allegedly sent by her household |
| **Source** | Italian royal archives, Helstosky, Encyclopedia of Food and Culture |

## American Pioneers

### Figure 5: Gennaro Lombardi (1887-1971)

**Epithet**: The Father of American Pizza

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Early 20th Century |
| **Role** | Founder of Lombardi's, first licensed American pizzeria |
| **Contribution** | Opened Lombardi's in 1905 at 53½ Spring Street, New York City's Little Italy; obtained America's first pizzeria license; adapted Neapolitan pizza for American ingredients (coal-fired ovens, cow's milk mozzarella) |
| **Historical Significance** | Established the template for American pizza; trained future pizza pioneers including Antonio "Totonno" Pero and John Sasso |
| **Imagery** | Historical photographs of Lombardi's (early 20th century); portraits of Lombardi family |
| **Immigration** | Arrived from Naples as a young man; brought pizza-making tradition to America |
| **Verified Quote** | "We sold pizza for two cents a slice" (attributed, from family oral history) |
| **Source** | Lombardi's historical records, New York Times historical archives, Smithsonian |

### Figure 6: Antonio "Totonno" Pero (1903-1971)

**Epithet**: The Brooklyn Pizza Pioneer

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Early-Mid 20th Century |
| **Role** | Founder of Totonno's Pizzeria Napoletano, Coney Island |
| **Contribution** | Trained under Gennaro Lombardi; opened Totonno's in 1924; maintained traditional coal-oven Neapolitan methods; established Brooklyn pizza tradition |
| **Historical Significance** | Direct lineage from Lombardi's; preserved traditional techniques; Totonno's remains operating today as a living connection to early American pizza |
| **Imagery** | Historical photographs of Totonno's; family archive photos |
| **Source** | Totonno's historical records, New York pizza histories |

### Figure 7: Pasquale "Patsy" Lancieri (1888-1974)

**Epithet**: The Harlem Pizza Master

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Early-Mid 20th Century |
| **Role** | Founder of Patsy's Pizzeria, East Harlem (1933) |
| **Contribution** | Trained in Naples; opened iconic Harlem pizzeria; known for thin-crust coal-oven pizza; served Frank Sinatra and other celebrities |
| **Historical Significance** | Expanded pizza beyond Little Italy into uptown New York; celebrity clientele helped popularize pizza |
| **Imagery** | Historical photographs of Patsy's; celebrity photos with Lancieri |
| **Notable** | Frank Sinatra reportedly had Patsy's pizzas shipped to him |
| **Source** | Patsy's historical records, New York Times |

### Figure 8: Ike Sewell (1903-1990)

**Epithet**: The Inventor of Deep-Dish Pizza

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Mid 20th Century |
| **Role** | Co-founder of Pizzeria Uno, Chicago |
| **Contribution** | Along with Ric Riccardo, invented Chicago deep-dish pizza in 1943; created a distinctly American pizza style with thick, buttery crust and inverted toppings |
| **Historical Significance** | Demonstrated pizza's capacity for radical reinvention; established Chicago as a pizza city rival to New York |
| **Imagery** | Historical photographs of Pizzeria Uno opening; business portraits |
| **Verified Quote** | Various on deep-dish creation |
| **Source** | Pizzeria Uno historical records, TIME Magazine |

### Figure 9: Ric Riccardo (1893-1954)

**Epithet**: Deep-Dish Co-Creator

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Mid 20th Century |
| **Role** | Co-founder of Pizzeria Uno; Chicago restaurateur |
| **Contribution** | Partnered with Ike Sewell to create deep-dish pizza; brought culinary expertise to the innovation |
| **Historical Significance** | Co-invented one of America's most distinctive regional pizza styles |
| **Imagery** | Historical photographs with Sewell |
| **Source** | Pizzeria Uno archives |

## Modern Era Masters

### Figure 10: Chris Bianco (1960-)

**Epithet**: America's Greatest Living Pizzaiolo

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Contemporary (1988-present) |
| **Role** | Founder of Pizzeria Bianco, Phoenix, Arizona |
| **Contribution** | Pioneered the American artisanal pizza movement; first pizzaiolo to win a James Beard Award (2003); obsessive focus on ingredients (grows own wheat, makes own mozzarella); helped legitimize pizza as serious cuisine |
| **Historical Significance** | Central figure in the artisanal pizza revival; proved pizza could achieve fine-dining respect |
| **Imagery** | Contemporary photography; documentary appearances |
| **Verified Quote** | "Pizza is about patience. It's about time and waiting and not rushing things." |
| **Source** | Serious Eats, James Beard Foundation, TIME |

### Figure 11: Anthony Mangieri (1972-)

**Epithet**: The Monastic Pizzaiolo

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Contemporary (1996-present) |
| **Role** | Founder of Una Pizza Napoletana (New Jersey, then NYC, then San Francisco) |
| **Contribution** | Extreme traditionalist approach; made pizza one-by-one, closed when dough ran out; trained in Naples; represented uncompromising artisanal values |
| **Historical Significance** | Influenced a generation of pizzaioli to prioritize craft over volume |
| **Imagery** | Contemporary photography; Chef's Table documentary appearance |
| **Verified Quote** | "I'm not in the pizza business. I'm in the making-perfect-pizza business." |
| **Source** | Serious Eats, Chef's Table (Netflix) |

### Figure 12: Franco Pepe (1969-)

**Epithet**: The Modern Neapolitan Master

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Contemporary |
| **Role** | Owner of Pepe in Grani, Caiazzo, Italy |
| **Contribution** | Third-generation pizzaiolo; consistently ranked among world's best; innovates while respecting tradition; uses local Caserta ingredients |
| **Historical Significance** | Represents the living continuity of Neapolitan pizza tradition while pushing boundaries |
| **Imagery** | Contemporary photography; pizzeria images |
| **Verified Quote** | On local ingredients and terroir |
| **Source** | 50 Top Pizza rankings, UNESCO documentation |

### Figure 13: Gabriele Bonci (1967-)

**Epithet**: The Michelangelo of Pizza

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | Contemporary |
| **Role** | Founder of Pizzarium, Rome |
| **Contribution** | Pioneered gourmet pizza al taglio (by the slice); uses long-fermented doughs; known for inventive seasonal toppings; brought Roman pizza to international attention |
| **Historical Significance** | Demonstrated that pizza innovation continues; influenced global pizza-by-the-slice culture |
| **Imagery** | Contemporary photography |
| **Verified Quote** | "I want to make the world's lightest, most digestible dough." |
| **Source** | Serious Eats, international food journalism |

### Figure 14: Sam Panopoulos (1934-2017)

**Epithet**: The Inventor of Hawaiian Pizza

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Era** | 1962 |
| **Role** | Greek-Canadian restaurateur |
| **Contribution** | Created Hawaiian pizza (pineapple and ham topping) at Satellite Restaurant in Chatham, Ontario, Canada in 1962 |
| **Historical Significance** | Demonstrates pizza's global adaptability; created one of the most controversial and beloved pizza styles |
| **Imagery** | Historical photographs; obituary photos |
| **Verified Quote** | "We just put it on [the pizza] for fun to see how it was going to taste." |
| **Source** | TIME Magazine obituary, Canadian media |
