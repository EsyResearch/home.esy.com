## ⚠️ Content Advisory

This design research report documents visual identity decisions for an essay about a racial slur. All design choices serve historical documentation with dignity and gravity.

---

## Design Research Overview

This essay traces the etymology, history, and applied use of the most offensive word in the English language — from its neutral Latin origins through weaponization in the slave trade, entrenchment in American law and culture, and contemporary debates about its use.

**Key Design Challenge**: Create a visual experience that conveys gravity without exploitation, precision without coldness, dignity without sentimentality. The design must feel like a museum exhibit combined with a premium editorial magazine — scholarly, authoritative, and humane.

---

## Visual Identity Analysis

### Subject Matter Characteristics

**Subject IS**:
- Heavy, grave, consequential
- Historical and documentary (not abstract)
- About language as infrastructure (words that build systems)
- A wound that persists (not a closed chapter)
- Both harm AND resistance (dual narrative)
- Scholarly and investigative
- Spanning 400+ years of American history

**Subject Is NOT**:
- Entertainment or spectacle
- Resolved or concluded
- Only about harm (resistance is coextensive)
- Abstract or theoretical (deeply concrete)
- Suitable for bright, playful, or decorative treatment
- Generic Americana (this is specific history)

### Visual Treatment Decision

**Primary Medium**: Archival Documentary Photography

**Rationale**:
1. **Historical grounding**: The subject is historical-linguistic; archival materials make abstract history concrete
2. **Documentary authenticity**: Museum/editorial approach requires real documents, not illustrations
3. **Dignity through reality**: Actual portraits of resistance figures honor their humanity
4. **Evidence-based**: Legal documents, dictionary pages, newspapers are primary evidence
5. **Era-specific processing**: Different eras require different visual treatments (sepia, B&W, digital)

## Typography Selection

### Typography Stack

**Display**: Playfair Display  
*Because*: High contrast, authoritative serif with gravitas appropriate for a subject of this weight. Elegant without being ornate. Reads as "serious publication" not "playful article."

**Serif (Body)**: Source Serif Pro  
*Because*: Highly readable for extended text, scholarly warmth without coldness, open letterforms for accessibility. Designed for digital reading while maintaining typographic sophistication.

**Accent/Quote**: Georgia Italic  
*Because*: Distinguished yet accessible for quotations. The slant creates visual differentiation for resistance voices. Widely available as fallback.

**Documentary/Monospace**: IBM Plex Mono  
*Because*: Evokes typewriter, court transcripts, official documents. Used for Civil Rights era captions and legal document references.

**Sans-serif (Captions)**: Source Sans Pro  
*Because*: Clean, functional, readable at small sizes. Pairs with Source Serif Pro in the same family for visual coherence.

### Era-Specific Typography

| Era | Primary | Rationale |
|-----|---------|-----------|
| Latin/Early Modern | EB Garamond | Period-appropriate humanist serif, Renaissance printing |
| Colonial (1700s) | Libre Caslon | English printing tradition, transitional serif |
| Antebellum (1800s) | Spectral | High contrast, Didone influence, abolitionist press |
| Jim Crow | Lora + Oswald | Lora for documents, Oswald for signage (industrial sans) |
| Civil Rights | Source Serif + IBM Plex Mono | Documentary serif + typewriter aesthetic |
| Contemporary | Inter | Clean digital sans, platform-native feel |

### Typography Justification

- **Playfair Display**: Selected for display because its high contrast and vertical stress evoke authority and tradition — appropriate for museum-grade presentation. NOT decorative or playful.

- **Source Serif Pro**: Selected for body because it balances scholarly character with digital readability. The Adobe Source family is designed for extended reading on screens.

- **IBM Plex Mono**: Selected for documentary contexts because it evokes typewriters, court transcripts, and official documents — the material culture of legal and journalistic record-keeping.

- **EB Garamond**: Selected for Latin/Early Modern sections because it authentically represents Renaissance printing — the era when the word entered English.

## Animation Philosophy

### Subject Nature → Animation Approach

**Subject Is**:
- Heavy, consequential, grave
- Documentary and archival
- About revelation of hidden history
- Spanning centuries (time as dimension)
- Both wounding and resistant (dual energy)

**Animation Should**:
- Feel like "opening archives" — careful, deliberate revelation
- Support the documentary/museum aesthetic
- Reveal slowly, allowing time to absorb
- Be dignified, never flashy or decorative
- Mark era transitions as significant events
- Honor the weight of the content

### Animation Principles

**Scroll-Lock Sequences**:
- **Purpose**: Reveal key moments with appropriate weight — etymology transformation, document reveals, portrait emergences
- **Feel**: Deliberate, measured, unhurried — like a museum exhibit unfolding
- **Timing**: 800-1200ms for major reveals, appropriate for gravity
- **Easing**: cubic-bezier(0.25, 0.1, 0.25, 1) — smooth, dignified, no bounce

**Parallax**:
- **Intensity**: Moderate (not dramatic) — parallax serves depth, not spectacle
- **Rationale**: Documents exist on surfaces; parallax creates layered reading experience like examining archival materials at different depths
- **Background**: 0.3x speed (era textures, atmospheric elements)
- **Mid-ground**: 0.6x speed (supporting documents, secondary images)
- **Subject**: 1x speed (primary content, portraits, key documents)

**Animation Timing**:
- **Era transitions**: 800ms crossfade — significant enough to mark passage of time
- **Quote reveals**: 400ms per line (typing effect) — for resistance voices, deliberate revelation
- **Document fades**: 600ms fade-in — emerging from archival darkness
- **Portrait reveals**: 500ms scale + fade — dignified appearance
- **Text reveals**: 300ms ease-out — readable, not dramatic

### Signature Animations

1. **"The Etymology"**: Word transforms through linguistic history (niger → negro → nigger)
   - **Character**: Scholarly, linguistic, revelatory
   - **Timing**: Slower (deliberate transformation)

2. **"The Document Reveal"**: Legal documents, dictionary entries fade from darkness
   - **Character**: Archival, discovery, evidence
   - **Timing**: Moderate (allow reading time)

3. **"The Portrait Gallery"**: Resistance figures emerge with quotes typing on screen
   - **Character**: Dignified, honoring, humanizing
   - **Timing**: Quote typing at readable pace

4. **"The Era Transition"**: Full-screen color and texture shift
   - **Character**: Marking passage of time as significant
   - **Timing**: Longer (800ms) to create sense of temporal journey

## Visual Style Guide

### Documentary Photography Approach

**Treatment by Era**:

| Era | Processing | Character |
|-----|------------|-----------|
| Colonial | Warm sepia, paper texture overlay | Antiquarian, document-like |
| Slavery | Split treatment — B&W for violence, sepia for resistance | Documentary, serious |
| Jim Crow | High contrast B&W, institutional | Stark, official, courthouse |
| Civil Rights | Documentary B&W, film grain | Journalistic, movement |
| Contemporary | Clean, minimal processing | Digital, immediate |

**Portrait Treatment**:
- Resistance figures: Full dignity, good light, direct gaze honored
- Historical figures (documented critically): Documentary framing, context provided
- All portraits: High quality, never degraded for "historical" effect

**Document Treatment**:
- Actual archive scans where possible
- Clean presentation, readable
- Context captions always present
- Never decorative — always evidentiary

### Visual Progression

The essay's visual journey:
1. **Warmth to cold**: Opens in warm sepia (Latin origins), cools through slavery, reaches stark B&W (Jim Crow), warms slightly with resistance (Civil Rights), settles into clean contemporary
2. **Darkness to light to darkness to light**: Hero in darkness → etymology reveals light → slavery darkens → resistance brings light → contemporary balance
3. **Paper to screen**: Material textures (parchment, newsprint) transition to digital interfaces by essay's end

## Progress Bar Concept

**Concept**: "The Weighing Scale"

**Visual Design**:
- Abstract balance scale silhouette on left edge, vertical orientation
- Left pan represents "question/darkness" — starts weighted down
- Right pan represents "understanding/light" — fills as user progresses
- Scale tips gradually toward balance as essay completes
- Never fully balanced — the debate continues

**Subject Connection**:
- *Exagium* (Latin: "a weighing") is the root of "essay"
- The essay IS a weighing — of evidence, of history, of language
- Justice is traditionally symbolized by scales
- The weighing never fully completes — appropriate for ongoing struggle

**Micro-Interactions**:
- Chapter names appear on hover
- Subtle scale motion on scroll
- Color shifts through era (sepia → B&W → contemporary)

**Justification**:
- Derived directly from etymology (*exagium* → essay)
- Matches the essay's thesis about weighing language and justice
- Visually distinct from previous essays
- Appropriate for grave subject matter

## Layout Pattern Strategy

### Recommended Patterns (minimum 3, no consecutive same)

1. **Full Bleed Documentary** (`full-bleed-documentary`)
   - **For**: Era transitions, resistance portraits, key moments
   - **Rationale**: Creates immersive moments appropriate for significant content; honors subject with visual weight

2. **Document Gallery** (`document-gallery`)
   - **For**: Dictionary entries, legal documents, newspaper headlines
   - **Rationale**: Presents evidence in clean, museum-like arrangement; emphasizes archival research

3. **Split Narrative** (`split-narrative`)
   - **For**: Harm vs. resistance moments, before/after comparisons
   - **Rationale**: Shows duality — dehumanization alongside resistance; appropriate for balanced presentation

4. **Text-Forward Scroll** (`text-forward`)
   - **For**: Etymology explanations, scholarly analysis, narrative passages
   - **Rationale**: Gives space for ideas without visual distraction; appropriate for linguistic content

5. **Timeline Band** (`timeline-band`)

   - **Rationale**: Visualizes temporal scope; appropriate for historical essay

6. **Portrait Focus** (`portrait-focus`)
   - **For**: Key figure profiles, resistance voices
   - **Rationale**: Centers humanity; counters dehumanization with dignity

## Mobile-Native Considerations

### Mobile-First Design

- **Primary canvas**: Phone screen (375-428px width)
- **Touch targets**: Minimum 44x44px for all interactive elements
- **Skip buttons**: Larger and more prominent on mobile (56x56px)
- **Parallax**: Reduced to 2 layers maximum on mobile (performance)
- **Scroll-lock**: Simplified intermediate states on mobile

### Typography for Mobile

- **Body text**: Minimum 16px (never smaller)
- **Line height**: 1.65 minimum for readability
- **Quote text**: 18px minimum (prominence for resistance voices)
- **Captions**: 14px minimum (never smaller)
- **Headlines**: Responsive scaling (clamp() functions)

### Touch Interactions

- **Scroll-lock skip**: Prominent button, easy to tap
- **Content warnings**: Clear dismiss targets
- **Progress bar**: Tap to see chapter, swipe to scrub (optional)
- **Image zoom**: Double-tap for full-resolution view

### Performance Budget (Mobile)

- **Hero animation**: <100KB total assets
- **Images**: WebP format, responsive srcset
- **Fonts**: Subset to necessary characters, WOFF2
- **Scroll-lock**: GPU-accelerated transforms only
- **Total page weight**: Target <2MB on initial load

## Accessibility Requirements

### Reduced Motion

- **prefers-reduced-motion**: Respected for all animations
- **Fallback**: Static versions of all scroll-lock sequences
- **Essential motion**: Minimal necessary movement retained
- **Skip controls**: Always visible, always functional

### Screen Reader Support

- **All images**: Comprehensive alt text describing content and significance
- **Scroll-lock sequences**: Hidden text provides narrative context
- **Progress bar**: ARIA labels for chapter positions
- **Era transitions**: Announced via live regions
- **Content warnings**: Properly labeled and focusable

### Color Contrast

- **Body text**: Minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio (WCAG AA)
- **Large text**: Minimum 3:1 contrast ratio
- **Interactive elements**: Minimum 3:1 against background
- **Era-specific palettes**: All verified for contrast compliance
- **B&W sections**: Maximum contrast (white on black or vice versa)

### Keyboard Navigation

- **Full keyboard access**: All interactive elements focusable
- **Skip links**: Skip to main content, skip to sources
- **Focus indicators**: Visible, high-contrast focus rings
- **Scroll-lock exit**: Keyboard shortcut to skip (Escape or Tab)
