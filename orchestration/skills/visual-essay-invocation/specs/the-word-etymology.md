### Executive Brief

Create an immersive, photo-driven visual essay that chronicles the 2,500-year journey of the word "etymology" — from ancient Greek philosophy to modern digital dictionaries. This experience uses archival photography, manuscript imagery, and portrait photography to transform a meta-linguistic subject into a cinematic time-travel documentary.

The narrative spans from Plato's *Cratylus* (c. 360 BCE) through Roman translation, medieval encyclopedia, scientific revolution, and into the digital age. We explore how a word meaning "true sense" evolved to mean "word history," and how the discipline it names transformed from mystical speculation to rigorous science.

This essay matters because etymology is the discipline that reveals language itself as archaeology. Every word has fingerprints — traces of migration, conquest, trade, and thought. Understanding how "etymology" became "etymology" teaches the reader to see language as living history.

The user who completes this essay will understand that words are biographies, that origins don't dictate meanings, and that they are now participants in a 2,500-year conversation about where language comes from.

### Chapter 8: The Etymology Fallacy
*Modern Misconception*

**Metaphor:** "Knowing where a word came from doesn't tell you where it must go."

**Central Visuals:**
- Split-screen: "Origin" (Roman coin) vs "Meaning" (modern dictionary)
- Large ≠ symbol dividing them
- Example cards: nice, decimate, villain
- "Myth-busting" banner design

**Content Focus:**
Here's the trap: if you know a word's origin, you might think you've found its "real" meaning. This is the etymological fallacy — the mistaken belief that a word's historical origin determines its current meaning. Consider "nice." In the 13th century, it meant "foolish" from Latin nescius ("ignorant"). Today it means "pleasant." Should we correct people? Of course not — usage determines meaning, not history. Etymology is a map, not a rulebook.

**Content Focus:**
In 1786, William Jones observed that Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin were too similar to be coincidence. This sparked comparative linguistics. Franz Bopp systematically compared verb conjugations. Jacob Grimm formulated Grimm's Law: regular sound shifts explaining why Latin pater became English father. Suddenly, etymologies weren't just stories — they were predictable. Etymology became archaeology. The shovel was sound.

**Key Figure Profiles:**

**Sir William Jones** — Father of Comparative Linguistics
- Delivered the famous 1786 lecture proposing Indo-European
- Founded the Asiatic Society of Bengal (1784)
- Fluent in Greek, Latin, Persian, Arabic, Hebrew, Sanskrit
- Died in Calcutta at 47, buried at South Park Street Cemetery
- Photograph: Engraving after Joshua Reynolds (Wikimedia Commons, Public Domain)

**Jacob Grimm** — The Fairy Tale Philologist
- Formulated Grimm's Law (1822) with brother Wilhelm
- Showed sound change is regular, not random
- Also famous for Grimm's Fairy Tales
- Founded the Deutsches Wörterbuch, still being completed
- Photograph: Oil portrait by Elisabeth Jerichau-Baumann (Wikimedia Commons, Public Domain)

**Franz Bopp** — The First Comparative Philologist

- Professor at University of Berlin (1821-67)
- Proved Albanian as a separate Indo-European branch

## [Layer 5: Design System]

### Color Palette

- **Primary Background:** #0D0D0D (Deep black — archaeological excavation)
- **Secondary Background:** #1A1A1F (Dark charcoal — manuscript night)
- **Accent Primary:** #C9A227 (Gold — illuminated manuscript gold leaf)
- **Accent Secondary:** #8B4513 (Saddle brown — aged paper, leather binding)
- **Text Primary:** #FFFFFF at 95% opacity
- **Text Secondary:** #FFFFFF at 60% opacity
- **Era: Ancient:** #D4AF37 (Antique gold) — stone and Greek
- **Era: Medieval:** #722F37 (Wine red) — manuscripts and scriptoria
- **Era: Print:** #4A3728 (Dark walnut) — wood type, early books
- **Era: Modern:** #1E3A5F (Oxford blue) — academic, dictionary
- **Era: Digital:** #00A0B0 (Teal) — screens, interfaces

### Era-Based Visual Treatment

**Ancient (360 BCE – 500 CE):**
- Desaturated, warm sepia tones
- Stone/marble textures
- High contrast, weathered edges
- Typography: Inscriptional serif (Trajan-like)

**Medieval (500 – 1400 CE):**
- Rich jewel tones, gold accents
- Vellum/parchment textures
- Warm candlelight color temperature
- Typography: Uncial, Carolingian, or Blackletter

**Print Revolution (1400 – 1800 CE):**
- Cream paper, black ink contrast
- Wood type, printer's ornaments
- Clean but warm
- Typography: Transitional serifs (Baskerville, Caslon)

**Scientific Era (1750 – 1920 CE):**
- Sepia to B&W photography
- Period-appropriate grain
- Academic, formal
- Typography: Victorian serifs (Clarendon, Century)

**Digital Era (1920 – Present):**
- Full color, high contrast
- Clean, minimal interfaces
- UI-style elements
- Typography: Clean sans-serif (Inter, system fonts)

### Typography

- **Headlines:** "Playfair Display" or "Crimson Text" — literary elegance, historical weight
- **Body:** "Source Serif Pro" or "Merriweather" — readable, scholarly
- **Quotes:** "Cormorant Garamond" italic — evocative, period-appropriate
- **Technical/IPA/Greek:** "Fira Code" or "Source Code Pro" — clear monospace
- **Captions/Data:** "Inter" or "IBM Plex Sans" — clean, modern, legible at small sizes

### Animation Principles

- **Scroll-lock zones:** 1500-3000px depth depending on complexity
- **Photo/content transitions:** 400-600ms for major reveals, 200-300ms for text
- **Text reveals:** 150ms stagger between elements
- **Parallax ratios:** Background 0.2x, Mid 0.5x, Subject 1x, Overlay 1.2x
- **Stagger values:** 50-100ms between sequential elements in same group
- **Easing:** cubic-bezier(0.22, 1, 0.36, 1) — smooth deceleration

## [Layer 6: Implementation]

### Responsive Considerations

#### Mobile Adaptations
- Scroll-lock sequences simplified (fewer percentage breakpoints)
- Parallax depth reduced to 2-3 layers maximum
- Gallery presentation: swipeable cards instead of static grid
- Touch-optimized skip controls (larger hit areas)
- Progress bar moves to bottom edge on mobile

#### Tablet
- Full scroll-lock experience maintained
- Gallery can use 2-column grid
- Progress bar remains on left edge

### Accessibility

- **Reduced motion:** All scroll-lock sequences have static fallback
- **Skip controls:** Every locked section has visible skip button
- **Alt text:** All images have descriptive alt text conveying both content and context
