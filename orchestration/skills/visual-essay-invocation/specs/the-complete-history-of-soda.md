### Executive Brief

**Central Question**: How did bubbles in water become one of humanity's most successful commercial achievements?

**Emotional Throughline**: Wonder at transformation. From a scientist's curiosity about brewery air, through pharmacists mixing tonics at marble counters, to wartime patriotism, Cold War absurdity, and the bizarre reality of 1.9 billion daily servings. The reader should feel the cumulative weight of small decisions becoming global empire.

**Stakes**: This is the story of how scientific discovery becomes commerce, how brands become identity, and how marketing can both spectacularly succeed (Candler, Woodruff) and catastrophically fail (New Coke). It's about the gap between what we drink and why we drink it.

**Transformation Promise**: The reader will never look at a soda bottle the same way again. They'll see the pharmacy counter, the WWII soldier, the Nixon-Khrushchev standoff, the consumer rebellion. A simple commodity becomes a window into how modern commerce works.

### Chapter 1: The Scientific Spark (1767-1780s)

**Title**: The Scientific Spark
**Temporal Marker**: 1767-1780s
**Era Tag**: Scientific Discovery

**Central Metaphor**: The Accidental Elixir
*A minister's curiosity about brewery air creates the foundation for a global industry.*

**Visual Assets**:
1. Joseph Priestley portrait (National Portrait Gallery)
2. 18th-century Leeds brewery illustration
3. Scientific apparatus for carbonation (Science History Institute)
4. "Impregnating Water with Fixed Air" publication pages
5. Copley Medal imagery
6. Torbern Bergman portrait

**Content Focus (Narrative Beats)**:
1. **The Brewery Next Door**: Priestley lives beside Old Red Lion Brewery on Meadow Lane; observes "fixed air" (CO2) blanketing fermenting beer; knows this air kills mice
2. **The Suspended Bowl**: He hangs a bowl of water over the vat; the gas infuses the water; he tastes what he calls "an exceedingly pleasant sparkling water"
3. **The Gift**: 1772 publication shares method freely; awarded Copley Medal 1773; calls it his "happiest" discovery
4. **The Scientific Network**: Bergman in Sweden improves the process (1771); apparatus now enables commercial-scale production
5. **The Missed Fortune**: Priestley doesn't commercialize; the 16-year gap between discovery (1767) and commerce (1783) begins

**Key Figure Profile**:

**Joseph Priestley** (1733-1804) - The Father of Carbonation
- English minister, natural philosopher, and discoverer of oxygen
- Discovered method of infusing water with carbon dioxide in 1767 at Leeds brewery
- Published "Impregnating Water with Fixed Air" in 1772
- Awarded Copley Medal by Royal Society in 1773
- Reportedly called carbonation his "happiest" discovery
- Did not commercialize; gave method freely to humanity
- Also credited with discovering oxygen ("dephlogisticated air")
- **Photograph**: Formal portrait in clerical dress, aged 50s-60s, thoughtful expression (National Portrait Gallery, multiple versions available)

- Opened soda fountain in New Haven, 1806
- Marketed mineral waters for medicinal properties

**Title**: The Pharmacy Counter
**Temporal Marker**: 1850s-1920
**Era Tag**: Pharmacy Soda Fountain Golden Age

**Content Focus (Narrative Beats)**:
1. **The Social Center**: By 1875, soda fountains in almost every American city; ornate marble and brass; social gathering places
2. **The Root Beer Revolution**: Charles Hires presents root beer at 1876 Centennial Exhibition; marketed as "temperance drink"; Quaker background
3. **The Birth of Coca-Cola**: May 8, 1886: Pemberton creates syrup at Atlanta pharmacy; first sold at Jacobs' Pharmacy for 5 cents; Frank Robinson names it, designs script logo
4. **The Tragic Inventor**: Pemberton: Confederate veteran, wounded, morphine-addicted; sells rights while ill; dies poor in 1888, never seeing success

6. **The Rival Emerges**: 1893: Caleb Bradham creates "Brad's Drink" in New Bern, NC; renamed Pepsi-Cola 1898 (for dyspepsia + cola)
7. **The Transition**: 1903: Cocaine removed from Coca-Cola; drinks shift from medicine to pleasure

**Key Figure Profiles**:

**John Stith Pemberton** (1831-1888) - The Inventor of Coca-Cola
- American pharmacist, Confederate veteran
- Born July 8, 1831, in Knoxville, Georgia
- Served as lieutenant colonel in Third Georgia Cavalry
- Wounded at Battle of Columbus (April 1865); developed morphine addiction
- Created Coca-Cola on May 8, 1886, in Atlanta
- First sold at Jacobs' Pharmacy for 5 cents per glass
- Partner Frank Robinson suggested name and designed script logo
- Sold rights while ill; died poor and addicted, August 16, 1888
- **Photograph**: Iconic seated portrait, formal attire, bearded (Atlanta History Center, Coca-Cola archives)

**Asa Griggs Candler** (1851-1929) - The Marketing Genius
- Atlanta businessman, later mayor, philanthropist
- Purchased Coca-Cola formula for $2,300 total by 1891
- Founded The Coca-Cola Company, 1892
- Pioneered aggressive advertising: $11,000 in 1891 (vs. typical $100)
- First year: 45,000 calendars with logo; celebrity endorsements (Hilda Clark)
- Introduced free sample coupons (revolutionary for 1890s)
- 1899: Sold bottling rights for $1 (the "billion-dollar mistake")
- **Photograph**: Formal portrait, businessman's attire (Emory University, Coca-Cola archives)

**Caleb Bradham** (1867-1934) - The Creator of Pepsi-Cola
- American pharmacist from Chinquapin, North Carolina
- Attended UNC Chapel Hill; dropped out of medical school
- Invented Pepsi-Cola in 1893 at his New Bern drugstore
- Originally called "Brad's Drink"; renamed Pepsi-Cola August 28, 1898
- Name from "dyspepsia" (digestive benefits) and "cola"
- By 1910: 250+ bottlers in 24 states
- 1923: Declared bankruptcy after sugar speculation disaster
- Sold trademark for $35,000; also served as naval rear admiral
- **Photograph**: Period portrait, pharmacist context (NC State Archives, Pepsi archives)

**Charles Hires** (1851-1937) - The Root Beer King
- Philadelphia pharmacist, Quaker
- Created root beer formula in 1875 (sarsaparilla, sassafras, wild cherry, wintergreen, ginger)
- Presented at 1876 Centennial Exhibition in Philadelphia
- Marketed as "temperance drink" to distance from alcohol
- 1890: Company incorporated; 1893: Began selling bottled version
- **Photograph**: Period portrait, serious expression (Historical Society of Pennsylvania)

### Chapter 6: The Cola Wars (1959-1990)

**Title**: The Cola Wars
**Temporal Marker**: 1959-1990
**Era Tag**: Cola Wars Peak

**Central Metaphor**: The 79-Day Mistake
*In a commoditized market, perception is reality. Even the world's most successful brand can panic.*

**Visual Assets**:
1. Nixon-Khrushchev Pepsi photograph (July 24, 1959)
2. Pepsi Challenge setup and materials
3. New Coke launch press conference (April 23, 1985)
4. Roberto Goizueta photograph

6. Coca-Cola Classic return announcement

8. Market share charts (1975-1990)
9. Celebrity endorsement imagery
10. VHS-aesthetic period footage

**Content Focus (Narrative Beats)**:
1. **The Cold War Toast**: July 24, 1959: Donald Kendall gets Pepsi into Khrushchev's hand at Moscow exhibition; Nixon assists; photo seen worldwide
2. **The Pepsi Challenge**: 1975, Dallas: Blind taste tests show consumers prefer Pepsi; market share shifts from 60.5%/23.3% to 38.8%/31.7% by 1984
3. **The Decision**: Roberto Goizueta becomes CEO 1980; "no sacred cows"; 200,000 taste tests; decides to change the formula
4. **The Launch**: April 23, 1985, Lincoln Center: Goizueta announces New Coke as "smoother, rounder, yet bolder—a more harmonious flavor"
5. **The Rebellion**: 1,500 calls per day to hotline (vs. 400 before); "Old Coca-Cola Drinkers of America" formed; cultural outrage
6. **The 79-Day Retreat**: July 11, 1985: Emergency board meeting; Coca-Cola Classic returns; 24 million cases ordered in 48 hours
7. **The Lesson**: Taste tests measured the wrong thing. Brands are emotional relationships, not product attributes.
8. **The Absurd Climax**: Late 1980s: Pepsi trades for 17 Soviet submarines and 3 warships; Kendall jokes to Scowcroft: "We're disarming the Soviet Union faster than you are"

**Key Figure Profiles**:

**Roberto Goizueta** (1931-1997) - The New Coke Decision-Maker
- Cuban-American businessman, Coca-Cola CEO 1980-1997
- Born November 18, 1931
- Increased company market value from $4 billion (1981) to $150 billion (1997)
- Made the fateful decision to change Coca-Cola's formula in 1985
- April 23, 1985: Described New Coke as "smoother, rounder, yet bolder—a more harmonious flavor"
- Oversaw reversal and return of Coca-Cola Classic 79 days later
- Also introduced Diet Coke (1982) and Cherry Coke (1985)
- 1995 reflection: "We set out to change the dynamics of sugar colas... albeit not in the way we had planned"
- **Photograph**: Press conference, executive portrait (Coca-Cola archives, 1985 press photos)

**Donald Kendall** (1921-2020) - The Cold War Cola Diplomat
- PepsiCo CEO, Cold War figure
- Started as worker in Pepsi bottling plant; became head of international division by mid-30s
- Orchestrated 1959 Moscow exhibition Pepsi moment with Nixon and Khrushchev
- Told Nixon: "I had to get a Pepsi in Khrushchev's hand"
- Made Pepsi first U.S. consumer product produced and sold in Soviet Union (1972)
- Negotiated extraordinary barter: 17 submarines and 3 warships for Soviet market access
- Joked to National Security Adviser Brent Scowcroft: "We're disarming the Soviet Union faster than you are"
- Died at age 99 in 2020
- **Photograph**: 1959 Moscow exhibition with Nixon/Khrushchev (Nixon Library, AP archives)
