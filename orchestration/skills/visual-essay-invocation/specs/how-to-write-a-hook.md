# LAYER 1: STRATEGIC FOUNDATION

## Project Title

**How to Write a Hook for an Essay**
*The Cognitive Architecture of Openings*

## Executive Brief

This visual essay transforms hook-writing from a collection of tips into a deep understanding of cognitive mechanism. The reader journey moves from confusion ("What makes a good hook?") through revelation ("Hooks are cognitive thresholds") to capability ("I can now design hooks deliberately").

**Emotional Throughline**: From feeling dependent on formulas → understanding the mechanism → gaining agency

**Stakes**: Students and writers everywhere struggle with openings. Most advice reduces hooks to gimmicks ("Start with a surprising fact!"). This essay reveals the cognitive architecture that makes openings work, giving readers genuine understanding rather than shallow techniques.

**Transformation Promise**: The reader will leave understanding not just WHAT hooks are, but WHY they work. They will see hooks as cognitive events—thresholds between worlds—rather than decorative first sentences.

## Visual Treatment Philosophy

**Primary Medium**: TYPOGRAPHIC-FORWARD EDITORIAL DESIGN

- Typography itself as illustration—letterforms that demonstrate concepts
- No photography; this is a world of ideas visualized through type
- Diagrammatic elements for cognitive models (attention curves, information gaps)
- The doorway/threshold metaphor visualized through typographic portals
- Scroll-based reveals that literally move the reader through thresholds

**Visual Rules**:
1. Type is the primary visual element
2. Color conveys cognitive states (before threshold, crossing, after)
3. White space carves doorways and thresholds
4. Diagrams emerge from typography, not added to it
5. Every visual choice reinforces the threshold metaphor

### Content Focus

### Key Figure Profiles

**George Loewenstein** — The Cartographer of Curiosity
- Proposed Information Gap Theory (1994)

**Daniel Kahneman** — The Dual-Mind Discoverer
- Nobel Prize winner (2002) without taking an economics course
- Developed System 1/System 2 framework
- Quote: "You can also feel a surge of conscious attention whenever you are surprised. System 2 is activated when an event is detected that violates the model of the world that System 1 maintains."

### Temporal/Contextual Marker

### Central Metaphor

### Visual Assets
- Cracked/broken door imagery

- Macrorie's "Engfish" exemplified

### Content Focus
1. Cicero's seven faults (separatum, longum, vulgare, etc.)
2. The overselling problem: creating expectations you can't fulfill
3. The mismatch problem: hook disconnected from body
4. The Engfish problem: formulaic writing that sounds fake
5. The gap problem: too large (confusing) or too small (boring)
6. Visible artifice: Quintilian's warning about obvious technique

### Key Figure Profiles

**Quintilian** — The Educator's Rhetorician
- First public teacher of rhetoric in Rome
- Warned against visible artifice in openings
- Quote: "Care must also be taken to avoid exciting suspicion, and speakers should therefore give no hint of elaboration in the exordium."

**Ken Macrorie** — The Crusader Against Phony Prose
- Coined "Engfish": lifeless prose that sounds like what essays should sound like
- Championed authentic voice over formula
- Quote: "The biggest lesson is this: tell the truth when you write."

### Scroll-Lock Sequence: "Broken Threshold"

- Quotes: Distinctive treatment honoring source voices

## Animation Principles

**Overall Philosophy**: Animations serve the threshold metaphor

- **Scroll-lock zones**: Decisive, meaningful (not decorative)
- **Transitions**: Opening, crossing, arriving (three-phase pattern)
- **Easing**: Smooth entry, emphasis at threshold, smooth resolution
- **Timing**: Deliberate pace—this is about understanding, not spectacle

**Specific Timings**:
- Section transitions: 600ms (noticeable but not slow)
- Text reveals: 400ms per element
- Threshold crossings: 800ms (the key moment)
- Parallax: Continuous, subtle
