### Executive Brief

Create an immersive, photo-driven visual essay that chronicles the complete history of R&B (Rhythm & Blues)—from its 1949 naming as a marketing classification through its continuous reinvention across Soul, Funk, Quiet Storm, New Jack Swing, Hip-Hop Soul, and today's alternative R&B. This experience uses archival photography, documentary imagery, and era-specific visual treatments to immerse readers in the sounds, business, and cultural networks that built America's most influential popular music tradition.

The narrative spans eight decades and traces R&B through its geographic networks (New Orleans, Memphis, Detroit, Chicago, Philadelphia, Atlanta), its technological revolutions (electric amplification, multitrack recording, drum machines, Auto-Tune), and its recurring themes of economic exploitation, artistic innovation, and women as invisible architects. The essay centers human faces and stories—the session musicians who never got credit, the women who built empires, the engineers who invented new sounds.

This matters now because R&B is often reduced to "love songs" or absorbed into broader "Black music" categories, obscuring its specific innovations, economics, and cultural importance. Understanding R&B means understanding how Black popular music navigated American commerce while teaching pop how to feel.

The user who completes this essay will understand: how a Billboard classification became a genre; how specific cities produced specific sounds through studio-label-band ecosystems; how technology determined era shifts; how women were architects, not appendices; how R&B and Hip-Hop co-evolved in symbiosis; and why "authenticity" debates are cyclical rather than meaningful.

### Chapter 1: The Classification
*Jerry Wexler and the Birth of a Genre (June 1949)*

**Metaphor:** The christening—a name creates a market, and a market creates possibility.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- Billboard magazine chart (1949)
- Jerry Wexler portrait
- Atlantic Records office/label
- Ruth Brown glamour shot
- Ahmet Ertegun

**Content Focus:**
In June 1949, Jerry Wexler—a young reporter at Billboard—proposed replacing "race records" with "rhythm and blues." The change was partly humanitarian (the old term was offensive) and partly commercial (the new term was marketable).

The classification created a chart. The chart created radio formats. Radio formats created audiences. And suddenly, Black popular music had infrastructure—flawed, exploitative, but functional.

Atlantic Records, founded in 1947 by Ahmet Ertegun with $10,000, was positioned to ride this wave. They signed Ruth Brown in 1949, and she became "Miss Rhythm"—building the label hit by hit until it could afford to sign Ray Charles.

**Key Figure Profiles:**

**Jerry Wexler** — "The Name-Giver"
- Born January 10, 1917, Bronx, New York; died August 15, 2008
- Billboard reporter who coined "rhythm and blues" (June 1949)
- Later produced Aretha Franklin's Atlantic sessions
- "In my capacity as a Billboard reporter I was also responsible for a minor semantic and, as it turned out, musical revolution."
- Photograph: Wexler in Atlantic offices

**Ruth Brown** — "Miss Rhythm"
- Born January 12, 1928, Portsmouth, Virginia; died November 17, 2006
- Built Atlantic Records with consecutive hits (1949–1960)
- Co-founded R&B Foundation to recover unpaid royalties
- "Atlantic Records was the house that Ruth Brown built."
- Photograph: Ruth Brown in 1950s glamour styling

### Chapter 2: New Orleans Crucible
*J&M Recording Studio and the Rolling Piano (1945–1960)*

**Metaphor:** The birthplace—where the river's rhythms became the genre's foundation.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- J&M Recording Studio exterior
- Cosimo Matassa at console
- Fats Domino at piano
- Little Richard in early career
- Dave Bartholomew leading band
- Dew Drop Inn exterior

**Content Focus:**
New Orleans was R&B's first capital. In a 15-by-16-foot room at J&M Recording Studio, teenage engineer Cosimo Matassa captured a sound that would define the genre: rolling piano triplets, second-line rhythms, warm vocal tones.

Fats Domino's "The Fat Man" (1949) sold a million copies—one of the first R&B records to cross over to white audiences. Little Richard improvised "Tutti Frutti" at the Dew Drop Inn, then recorded it at J&M, launching rock and roll's most flamboyant career.

Behind them was producer Dave Bartholomew, who assembled the house band and wrote many of Domino's hits. The system was already in place: a studio, a producer, a house band, a label relationship. Every major R&B city would replicate this model.

**Key Figure Profiles:**

**Cosimo Matassa** — "The Teen Engineer"
- Born April 13, 1926, New Orleans; died September 11, 2014
- Opened J&M Recording Studio at age 18 (1945)
- Recorded virtually every New Orleans R&B hit (1945–1970s)
- Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductee (2012)
- Photograph: Matassa at mixing console

**Fats Domino (Antoine Domino Jr.)** — "The Rolling Piano"
- Born February 26, 1928, New Orleans; died October 24, 2017
- "The Fat Man" (1949)—million-seller crossover hit
- Sold 65+ million records; more than any 1950s artist except Elvis
- "A lot of fellows nowadays have a B.A., M.D., or Ph.D. Unfortunately, I have only a T.F. That's Tenth Foolishness."
- Photograph: Fats Domino at piano, smiling

### Chapter 3: Memphis Soul Stew
*Stax, Hi Records, and the Southern Sound (1957–1975)*

**Metaphor:** The converted theater—where an integrated band created the grittiest soul.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- Stax Records building (Capitol Theater)
- Booker T. & the M.G.'s
- Otis Redding performing
- Royal Studios interior (sloped floor)
- Al Green in white suit
- Isaac Hayes with chains

**Content Focus:**
Memphis developed two distinct but related sounds. At Stax Records—a converted movie theater where the sloped floor created unique acoustics—an integrated house band (Booker T. & the M.G.'s) backed virtually every recording. The result was grittier, rawer than Motown: the delayed backbeat, the horn punch, the unpolished emotion.

At Royal Studios (also a converted theater), Willie Mitchell discovered Al Green and created smooth Southern soul. "As you go down the slope, the music gets bigger, it separates," Mitchell explained.

Both sounds were shaped by architecture. Both were built by bands who created distinctive styles. And both were destroyed by business failures—Stax collapsed in 1975 after a disastrous distribution deal.

**Key Figure Profiles:**

**Otis Redding** — "The King of Soul"
- Born September 9, 1941, Dawson, Georgia; died December 10, 1967
- Stax Records' biggest star; "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay" went #1 posthumously
- Died in plane crash at age 26
- "These arms of mine, they are lonely..."
- Photograph: Otis Redding in performance

**Booker T. Jones** — "The M.G.'s Architect"
- Born November 12, 1944, Memphis, Tennessee
- Led Booker T. & the M.G.'s; composed "Green Onions"
- Backed virtually every Stax recording
- "We were a family. Black and white didn't matter in that studio."
- Photograph: Booker T. & the M.G.'s integrated lineup

**Al Green** — "The Smooth Reverend"
- Born April 13, 1946, Forrest City, Arkansas
- "Let's Stay Together" (1971), "Love and Happiness" (1972)

- Photograph: Al Green in signature white suit

### Chapter 4: Hitsville U.S.A.
*Motown and the Detroit Assembly Line (1959–1972)*

**Metaphor:** The factory—where soul was manufactured with precision and sold to the world.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- Hitsville U.S.A. exterior with sign
- Berry Gordy in office
- The Supremes in sequined gowns
- The Temptations choreographed
- Studio A "Snake Pit" interior
- Funk Brothers group shot
- Smokey Robinson

**Content Focus:**
Berry Gordy borrowed $800 from his family in 1959 and built an empire. Motown applied Detroit's automotive assembly-line principles to music: quality control, in-house production, artist development, and relentless polish.

In Studio A—nicknamed "The Snake Pit" for its claustrophobic intimacy—the Funk Brothers played on more #1 hits than the Beatles, Rolling Stones, Beach Boys, and Elvis combined. They were paid $10 per song. They received no royalties. They were not credited on album covers until 1971.

Motown's "charm school" taught artists to appeal to white audiences—proper diction, choreography, etiquette. It was crossover by design, and it worked: Motown became the most successful Black-owned business in America. But in 1972, Gordy moved operations to Los Angeles. The Funk Brothers discovered this when they saw a notice on the studio door.

**Key Figure Profiles:**

**Berry Gordy** — "The Mogul"
- Born November 28, 1929, Detroit, Michigan
- Founded Motown Records with $800 family loan (1959)
- Created "assembly line" approach to hit-making
- Sold Motown to MCA for $61 million (1988)
- "I realized that a hit record was a product of its own."
- Photograph: Gordy in Hitsville U.S.A. office

**The Funk Brothers** — "The Invisible Hit Makers"
- Motown's house band (James Jamerson, Benny Benjamin, Earl Van Dyke, and others)
- Played on more #1 hits than any group in history
- Not credited until 1971; documented in "Standing in the Shadows of Motown" (2002)
- "We played on everything. We were Motown."
- Photograph: Funk Brothers rare group shot

**Smokey Robinson** — "The Poet Laureate"
- Born February 19, 1940, Detroit, Michigan
- The Miracles frontman; Motown VP; songwriter ("My Girl," "The Tracks of My Tears")
- Bob Dylan called him "America's greatest living poet"
- Photograph: Smokey Robinson in 1960s

*Sigma Sound and Soul in a Tuxedo (1968–1979)*

**Metaphor:** The orchestral elevation—where R&B put on strings and reached new audiences.

**Content Focus:**
Philadelphia developed R&B's most sophisticated sound. At Sigma Sound Studios, producer Thom Bell and songwriter-producers Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff orchestrated what Joe Tarsia called "soul music in a tuxedo."

MFSB—"Mother Father Sister Brother"—was a 30+ member orchestra that played on Philadelphia International hits. Their own "TSOP (The Sound of Philadelphia)" became Soul Train's theme and the first TV theme song to reach #1.

The Philadelphia Sound was consciously upmarket: lush strings, sophisticated arrangements, positive messages. It was crossover by elevation rather than dilution. But like every R&B capital before it, Philadelphia's moment faded as trends shifted.

### Chapter 6: The Invisible Engine
*Session Musicians and the Economics of Credit (1950–Present)*

**Metaphor:** The ghost writers of groove—the players who made the music but not the credits.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- Funk Brothers rare portrait
- James Jamerson with bass
- Booker T. & the M.G.'s
- The Swampers at FAME Studios
- Benny Benjamin at drums
- Session log book / studio documentation

**Content Focus:**
Every major R&B sound was built by largely uncredited session musicians. The Funk Brothers at Motown, Booker T. & the M.G.'s at Stax, MFSB in Philadelphia, the Swampers at Muscle Shoals—these bands created the sounds that made stars.

They were paid per song. They received no royalties. They were rarely credited. At Motown, the rate was $10 per song—regardless of how many millions that song sold.

This invisibility was structural. The "artist" model of popular music requires a face, a name, a story. The musicians who actually played the music were hidden behind that story. It took decades and documentaries (like 2002's "Standing in the Shadows of Motown") to begin correcting the record.

**Key Figure Profiles:**

**James Jamerson** — "The Phantom Bassist"
- Born January 29, 1936, Edisto Island, South Carolina; died August 2, 1983
- Motown's principal bassist; played on more #1 hits than any instrumentalist
- Developed melodic bass style that influenced all pop music
- Never credited on albums; struggled financially late in life
- Photograph: Jamerson with Fender Precision bass

**Benny Benjamin** — "Papa Zita"
- Born July 15, 1925, Birmingham, Alabama; died April 20, 1969
- Motown's principal drummer; invented the "Motown beat"
- Died at 43; never saw his influence recognized
- "Benny was the heartbeat of Motown." — Funk Brothers
- Photograph: Benjamin at drums (rare)

### Chapter 7: Technology as Instrument
*From Electric Guitar to Auto-Tune (1940–Present)*

**Metaphor:** The laboratory—every technology shift created a new R&B era.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- T-Bone Walker with electric guitar
- Stevie Wonder with TONTO synthesizer
- Marvin Gaye with TR-808
- Teddy Riley with equipment
- Timbaland with MPC
- Pro Tools interface
- Andy Hildebrand (Auto-Tune inventor)

**Content Focus:**
R&B's evolution tracks precisely with technological change. Each major innovation didn't just change the sound—it created an entirely new era.

**Electric amplification (1940s)** allowed solo instruments to compete with big bands. **Multitrack recording (1958+)** enabled layered production. **Synthesizers (1972+)** with Stevie Wonder's TONTO began the electronic era.

**Drum machines (1980–82)** transformed everything. Marvin Gaye's "Sexual Healing" (1982) was the first US hit to feature the TR-808—and the first to use the machine as an instrument of intimacy rather than cold rhythm. This shifted R&B's entire trajectory.

**Samplers (1987–88)** enabled the sampling era and hip-hop soul fusion. **Auto-Tune (1997)** was created by Andy Hildebrand, who admits: "I put that setting in the software. But I didn't think anyone in their right mind would ever use it." Cher's "Believe" (1998) proved him wrong.

**Key Figure Profiles:**

**Stevie Wonder** — "The Synthesizer Pioneer"
- Born May 13, 1950, Saginaw, Michigan
- "Music of My Mind" (1972)—first classic album with TONTO synthesizer
- Proved synthesizers could convey soul and emotion
- "I wanted to use technology to expand what the human spirit could express."
- Photograph: Wonder with TONTO setup

**Marvin Gaye** — "The 808 Intimist"
- Born April 2, 1939, Washington, D.C.; died April 1, 1984
- "Sexual Healing" (1982)—first US hit to feature TR-808
- Made drum machines sensual rather than mechanical
- Photograph: Gaye in studio, early 1980s

### Chapter 8: Women as Architects

**Content Focus:**
R&B history typically centers male producers—Berry Gordy, Quincy Jones, Teddy Riley. But women were equally foundational, though systematically undervalued.

**Ruth Brown** didn't just sing for Atlantic—she built it, hit by hit, then fought for royalties reform that benefited all artists. **Sylvia Robinson** created hip-hop's first hit ("Rapper's Delight") and first socially conscious hit ("The Message"). **Valerie Simpson** was one of Motown's first female producers. **Sylvia Rhone** became the first African-American woman to run a major label (Elektra, 1994).

Missy Elliott summarized the disparity: "A lot of people don't know a lot of records that I've written or produced... I always said if a man would have done half the records that I've done we would know about it."

Beyoncé founded Parkwood Entertainment to control her own career—valued at $300M+. She represents the culmination of a tradition that began with Ruth Brown demanding her royalties.

### Chapter 9: The Symbiosis
*R&B and Hip-Hop Co-Evolution (1979–1999)*

**Metaphor:** The twin rivers—two genres that flow from the same source and constantly merge.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- Mary J. Blige *What's the 411?* era
- Teddy Riley with equipment
- TLC *CrazySexyCool*
- Aaliyah in Tommy Hilfiger
- Timbaland with MPC
- New Jack Swing era imagery
- Atlanta's Dungeon basement
- Billboard chart merger moment

**Content Focus:**
The narrative that "hip-hop killed R&B" is false. The genres have been in continuous mutual exchange since the late 1980s.

Hip-hop sampled R&B from the beginning—DJ Kool Herc spinning funk and soul breaks. R&B adopted hip-hop production starting with **New Jack Swing** (Teddy Riley, 1987). The relationship was bidirectional: R&B provided melodic/harmonic vocabulary; hip-hop provided rhythmic/production vocabulary.

**Mary J. Blige's** *What's the 411?* (1992) is credited with inventing hip-hop soul—not hip-hop taking over R&B, but a genuine hybrid created by an R&B vocalist working over hip-hop production.

On December 11, 1999, Billboard officially recognized the merger: the chart became "Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Songs." The genres had been evolving together for a decade; the industry finally caught up.

**Key Figure Profiles:**

**Mary J. Blige** — "The Queen of Hip-Hop Soul"
- Born January 11, 1971, Bronx, New York
- *What's the 411?* (1992)—invented hip-hop soul
- "I started speaking about what I was dealing with through my music, and 4 million women responded and said, 'Us too, Mary.'"
- Photograph: Blige in *411* era—blonde, boots, baseball caps

**Teddy Riley** — "The New Jack Swing Architect"
- Born October 8, 1967, Harlem, New York
- Coined "New Jack Swing" sound (Village Voice credited Barry Michael Cooper with term, October 1987)
- "New Jack Swing is a movement... When you're doing New Jack Swing, you're doing a singer and a rapper together."
- Photograph: Riley in studio with equipment

**Timbaland (Timothy Mosley)** — "The Sound of the Future"
- Born March 10, 1972, Norfolk, Virginia
- Produced Aaliyah, Missy Elliott, Justin Timberlake
- Pioneered sample-heavy, rhythmically complex production
- "You will play her songs for 20 years... She is the Aretha Franklin of hip-hop." (on Missy Elliott)
- Photograph: Timbaland with MPC

### Chapter 10: Crossover Economics
*Race, Money, and the Mainstream (1950–Present)*

**Metaphor:** The bridge toll—crossing over always cost something.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- Pat Boone covering Little Richard
- LaVern Baker with Georgia Gibbs comparison
- Motown "charm school" imagery
- Big Mama Thornton
- Philadelphia International sophisticated styling
- Sam Phillips quote visualization

**Content Focus:**
"Crossover" in R&B has always meant: music created by Black artists reaching white audiences through controlled sanitization or strategic positioning.

In the 1950s, white artists like Pat Boone recorded note-for-note covers of Black originals for segregated radio. LaVern Baker watched Georgia Gibbs copy her arrangements and outsell her—leading Baker to petition Congress for arrangement copyright protection (she failed).

Big Mama Thornton recorded "Hound Dog" in 1952. It sold 2 million copies. She received $500 total. "That song sold over 2 million records. I got one check for $500 and never saw another."

Motown's "charm school" was explicit about the goal: make Black artists palatable to white audiences through diction, choreography, and etiquette. Philadelphia International's "soul in a tuxedo" was similar—orchestral arrangements for broader appeal.

The economic reality: artists who crossed over earned more money, but often at the cost of Black cultural specificity. The industry extracted value while limiting Black ownership.

**Scroll-Lock Sequence: "The Cover Versions"**
- **0–25%:** Black artist performing original—Little Richard's "Tutti Frutti"
- **25–50%:** Split screen: white artist's cover version appears beside it
- **50–75%:** Sales figures appear: cover outsells original
- **75–100%:** Money flows upward—mostly away from original artist. Text: "The sound crossed over. The money didn't."

### Chapter 11: The Myth of Real R&B
*Authenticity Cycles and Continuous Reinvention*

**Metaphor:** The eternal return—every generation believes the previous one was "real."

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- 1950s R&B imagery
- 1960s Motown/Stax
- 1970s disco transition
- 1980s synthesizer era
- 1990s hip-hop soul
- 2000s Auto-Tune era
- Contemporary alternative R&B (Frank Ocean, SZA)

**Content Focus:**
Every generation believes the previous generation's R&B was "real" and the current generation is corrupted. This pattern has repeated for 75 years:

- **1960s**: Jump blues was "real"; soul is too smooth
- **1970s**: Classic soul was "real"; disco is selling out
- **1980s**: Motown/Stax was "real"; synthesizers ruined vocals
- **1990s**: 80s quiet storm was "real"; hip-hop is taking over
- **2000s**: 90s hip-hop soul was "real"; Auto-Tune ruined singing
- **2010s**: 2000s R&B was "real"; alternative R&B isn't "real"

The evidence against these claims is overwhelming: technology has always shaped R&B sound, each era produced enduring classics, and "authenticity" claims typically mask genre boundary policing.

SZA summarized it: "When you try to label it, you remove the option for it to be limitless. It diminishes the music."

**Key Figure Profile:**

**SZA (Solána Imani Rowe)** — "The Genre Refuser"
- Born November 8, 1989, St. Louis, Missouri
- *Ctrl* (2017)—3.5M+ sold; alternative R&B mainstream breakthrough
- First female artist at TDE (Top Dawg Entertainment)
- "When you try to label it [her music], you remove the option for it to be limitless."
- Photograph: SZA in natural styling

### Chapter 12: Global Heartbeat
*R&B Beyond America (1960s–Present)*

**Metaphor:** The seeds on wind—R&B took root everywhere and grew differently in each soil.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- British R&B/Northern Soul scene
- Japanese R&B artists
- Korean R&B/K-pop fusion
- African R&B influences
- Brazilian soul movement
- UK garage/R&B fusion
- Global streaming visualization

**Content Focus:**
R&B was never purely American. Almost as soon as it had a name, it began to travel.

**Britain** embraced R&B early—the "Northern Soul" movement preserved 1960s R&B records while America moved on. British artists from Dusty Springfield to Amy Winehouse absorbed R&B and reflected it back.

**Japan** developed a sophisticated R&B scene, with artists like Hikaru Utada achieving massive domestic success while drawing explicitly on American R&B traditions.

**Korea's** K-pop industry borrowed heavily from R&B vocal techniques, production approaches, and visual aesthetics. Contemporary K-R&B is a recognized genre.

Streaming globalized everything. Today, R&B artists from any country can reach any audience. The genre that emerged from a Billboard classification in 1949 is now a global language.

### Epilogue: The Feeling Itself
*What R&B Taught and What It Became*

**Metaphor:** The absorbed lesson—R&B didn't die; it became the feeling pop learned.

**Central Photographs/Visuals:**
- Contemporary R&B artists (H.E.R., Daniel Caesar, Summer Walker)
- Streaming platform interface
- Bedroom producer setup
- Multi-generational R&B gathering
- Vinyl record returning to turntable (echo of prologue)

**Content Focus:**
In 1949, Jerry Wexler gave a name to music that already existed. That name created a category, and that category created an industry, and that industry created wealth (mostly for others) and art (mostly from Black creators).

R&B didn't die—it did something more profound. It taught pop how to feel. The melismatic vocals, the groove-based rhythm, the emotional directness, the technological innovation—all of this is now standard in popular music. R&B won so completely that it became invisible, absorbed into the mainstream.

But the genre persists. In bedroom studios and major labels, artists continue to make music they call R&B. They argue about what it means. They claim authenticity. They innovate.

The vinyl still spins. The groove still hits. The heartbeat continues.

- **30–60%:** Decades flash—each era's defining images
- **60–90%:** Contemporary streaming interface—R&B playlists with millions of followers
- **90–100%:** Return to vinyl record, needle dropping. Text: "The feeling never stopped."

- **Era Marker - Alternative (2010–Present):** `#8BC34A` (SZA sage)

### Era-Based Visual Treatment

**Era 1: Race Records & Jump Blues (1920–1948)**
- Color: Sepia, warm browns, cream
- Processing: Heavy grain (40%+), high contrast
- Vignette: Heavy, juke joint atmosphere
- Filter: `sepia(0.8) contrast(1.2)`

**Era 2: The Naming & Early R&B (1949–1959)**
- Color: Warm B&W with occasional hand-tinting
- Processing: Medium grain (25%), classic portrait contrast
- Filter: `grayscale(0.9) sepia(0.2) contrast(1.1)`

**Era 3: Soul (1960–1969)**
- Color: Transition to Kodachrome warmth
- Processing: Rich midtones (15% grain), Motown polish
- Filter: `saturate(0.9) warmth(1.1) contrast(1.05)`

**Era 4: Funk & Philly Soul (1970–1979)**
- Color: Saturated 1970s Kodak
- Processing: Warm color saturation (10% grain), disco sheen
- Filter: `saturate(1.15) warmth(1.2) contrast(1.0)`

**Era 5: Quiet Storm & 80s R&B (1980–1987)**
- Color: Studio-lit perfection, soft focus
- Processing: Minimal grain (5%), MTV aesthetics
- Filter: `saturate(0.95) contrast(1.05) soft-glow`

**Era 6: New Jack Swing & Hip-Hop Soul (1987–1999)**
- Color: Bold, high-contrast, urban
- Processing: Minimal grain, video-sharp
- Filter: `contrast(1.15) saturate(1.0) sharpen`

**Era 7: 2000s Pop-R&B (2000–2009)**
- Color: HD clarity, commercial polish
- Processing: No grain, glossy
- Filter: `saturate(1.1) contrast(1.05)`

**Era 8: Alternative R&B (2010–Present)**
- Color: Film revival, intentional grain
- Processing: Artistic desaturation
- Filter: `saturate(0.85) grain(intentional) contrast(variable)`

**Headlines:**

### Animation Principles

**Hero (NO SCROLL-LOCK):**
- Parallax only: 4-layer depth system
- Transitions: Crossfade between eras (800ms)
- Color morphing: Sepia → B&W → Color tied to scroll position

**Chapter Scroll-Lock Zones:**
- Lock trigger: Element enters 40% viewport position
- Lock depth: Minimum 800px, maximum 2000px scroll equivalent
- Release: Smooth easing over 800ms

**Photo Transitions:**
- Reveal: 400ms, ease-out
- Crossfade: 600ms
- Era color shift: 1000ms transition

**Parallax Ratios:**
- Background (Layer 0): 0.3x
- Mid-ground (Layer 1): 0.6x
- Subject (Layer 2): 1.0x
- Overlay (Layer 3): 1.15x

**Easing:**
- Default: `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)`
- Era transitions: `cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)` (smooth morph)
