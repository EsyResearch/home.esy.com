## FAQ Section

### 1. Was this legal under U.S. law? Under international law?

**Answer**: Both are contested. U.S. law: The Constitution gives Congress war declaration power, and the War Powers Resolution limits unilateral action. The administration claims Article II authority to protect U.S. personnel. Critics (including Senator Tim Kaine) say the action was unauthorized. International law: UN Charter Art. 2(4) prohibits use of force against territorial integrity. No self-defense claim applies, and there was no UN authorization. UN Secretary-General Guterres called it a "dangerous precedent." Both legal tests remain unsettled.

### 2. Is this about oil, drugs, democracy, or all three?

**Answer**: All three, but in different proportions depending on who you ask. The formal justification is narco-terrorism (drugs). The political justification is restoring democracy (Maduro's elections were disputed). The structural reality is that Venezuela sits on 303 billion barrels of oil that U.S. refineries need. These motives are not mutually exclusive.

### 3. What changes in global norms if leaders can be seized abroad?

**Answer**: This is the precedent question. If the U.S. can capture a sitting head of state without UN authorization, what stops other powers from claiming similar authority? International law scholars argue this undermines sovereignty norms. Supporters argue criminal leaders shouldn't hide behind sovereignty. The UN Security Council will debate this January 5.

### 4. What happens to PDVSA, ports, and exports next?

**Answer**: Uncertain. PDVSA facilities were reportedly operating normally immediately after the strikes. However, exports were already halted under the blockade, and 25+ million barrels are in storage. Who controls export decisions going forward—Venezuelan officials, U.S. administrators, or no one—remains unclear.

### 5. Why do refineries care about heavy crude specifically?

**Answer**: Gulf Coast refineries were built with billions of dollars in specialized equipment (cokers, hydrocrackers, desulfurization units) designed for heavy sour crude. U.S. shale produces light sweet crude—a different product that these refineries can't efficiently process. Heavy and light crude are not interchangeable.

### 6. What would a "clean exit" actually require?

**Answer**: At minimum: legitimate successor government, military cooperation, PDVSA operational control, clarified sanctions framework, international recognition, humanitarian migration corridor, and resolution of legal challenges. Each item has dependencies. This is a systems checklist, not a prediction.

### 7. What are the risks of escalation?

**Answer**: Military fragmentation in Venezuela, proxy resistance supported by Cuba/Russia/China, cyber attacks on U.S. infrastructure, migration surge to neighboring countries, regional diplomatic breakdown, and extended occupation costs. Venezuela is close to U.S. borders, which changes the calculus compared to distant interventions.

### 8. How should I evaluate social media videos and images right now?

**Answer**: With extreme skepticism. AI-generated images of Maduro's arrest have been confirmed circulating. Old footage from 2024 has been recycled as current. This essay uses diagrams and maps instead of unverified photography. Look for wire service (Reuters, AP) sourcing. Check SynthID and reverse image search. When in doubt, wait for verification.
