# Key Figures: The Scramble for Africa

> **Total Figures**: 15  
> **Categories**: European Architects (7), African Leaders (6), Reformers/Critics (2)

---

## European Architects of Colonization

### 1. King Leopold II of Belgium (1835–1909)
**Epithet**: "The Butcher of the Congo"

**Key Contributions**:
- Personally acquired the Congo Free State as private property (not Belgian colony) in 1885
- Created rubber extraction system enforced through terror and mutilation
- Responsible for estimated 10 million deaths during his rule (1885–1908)
- Pioneered use of humanitarian rhetoric to mask exploitation
- Forced to cede Congo to Belgian state in 1908 after international outcry

**Defining Quote**:
> "I do not want to miss a good chance of getting us a slice of this magnificent African cake."
— Leopold II, 1876 (widely attributed, describing Africa at geographic conference)

**Fate/Legacy**: Died wealthy and largely unpunished; Congo's trauma reverberates today

**Photograph Description**: Official portrait showing Leopold in military regalia with his distinctive long white beard; contrast with images of Congolese amputees

**Source**: Hochschild, *King Leopold's Ghost*; Pakenham, *Scramble for Africa*

---

### 2. Otto von Bismarck (1815–1898)
**Epithet**: "The Iron Chancellor"

**Key Contributions**:
- Convened the Berlin Conference (1884–1885) that formalized Africa's partition
- Established "effective occupation" principle requiring demonstrated control for territorial claims
- Initially skeptical of colonies ("The whole of Africa is not worth the bones of a single Pomeranian grenadier")
- Later acquired German colonies in Togoland, Cameroon, Southwest Africa, East Africa
- Used colonial expansion to manage domestic political pressures

**Defining Quote**:
> "My map of Africa lies in Europe. Here is Russia and here is France, and we are in the middle. That is my map of Africa."
— Bismarck, 1888 (attributed, emphasizing European power balance over African territories)

**Fate/Legacy**: Dismissed by Kaiser Wilhelm II in 1890; died 1898

**Photograph Description**: Stern portrait in Prussian military uniform; images of Berlin Conference setting with European delegates

**Source**: Pakenham, *Scramble for Africa*; Wesseling, *Divide and Rule*

---

### 3. Cecil Rhodes (1853–1902)
**Epithet**: "The Empire Builder"

**Key Contributions**:
- Founded De Beers diamond monopoly and British South Africa Company
- Colonized territories named Rhodesia (now Zimbabwe and Zambia)
- Championed Cape-to-Cairo railway/telegraph vision
- Prime Minister of Cape Colony (1890–1896)
- Funded the Rhodes Scholarship to spread British imperial values

**Defining Quote**:
> "I contend that we are the finest race in the world and that the more of the world we inhabit the better it is for the human race."
— Rhodes, *Confession of Faith*, 1877

**Alternate Quote**:
> "Remember that you are an Englishman, and have consequently won first prize in the lottery of life."
— Attributed to Rhodes

**Fate/Legacy**: Died at 48; statues later removed amid controversy; Rhodes Scholarship continues

**Photograph Description**: Formal Victorian portrait; images of Rhodes in Africa with colonial administrators; political cartoons of "Colossus of Rhodes" straddling Africa

**Source**: Rotberg, *The Founder*; Rhodes' *Confession of Faith* (primary)

---

### 4. Henry Morton Stanley (1841–1904)
**Epithet**: "Explorer and Agent of Empire"

**Key Contributions**:
- Famous for "Dr. Livingstone, I presume?" encounter (1871)
- Mapped the Congo River for Leopold II (1874–1877)
- Negotiated over 400 treaties with African chiefs for Leopold's Congo claim
- Led Emin Pasha Relief Expedition (1886–1889)
- Published *In Darkest Africa* (1890), shaping European imagination of the continent

**Defining Quote**:
> "The Dark Continent" 
— Title of Stanley's 1878 work; phrase became shorthand for European perception of Africa

**Fate/Legacy**: Knighted; controversial for violence during expeditions

**Photograph Description**: Explorer with pith helmet in African bush; formal portraits; expedition imagery

**Source**: Pakenham, *Scramble for Africa*; Stanley's published works

---

### 5. Jules Ferry (1832–1893)
**Epithet**: "Architect of French Colonialism"

**Key Contributions**:
- French Prime Minister who aggressively expanded French colonial empire
- Justified colonialism through "civilizing mission" ideology
- Oversaw French expansion in Tunisia, Indochina, Madagascar, and West Africa
- Defended colonialism in French Assembly with explicit racial arguments

**Defining Quote**:
> "The superior races have a right, because they have a duty. They have the duty to civilize the inferior races."
— Jules Ferry, French Chamber of Deputies, July 28, 1885

**Fate/Legacy**: Assassinated 1893; French empire expanded massively under his policies

**Photograph Description**: Formal political portrait; images of French colonial administration

**Source**: Ferry's parliamentary speeches (primary); Wesseling, *Divide and Rule*

---

### 6. Frederick Lugard (1858–1945)
**Epithet**: "Father of Indirect Rule"

**Key Contributions**:
- Developed "indirect rule" policy using indigenous leaders under British supervision
- Served in Uganda, Nigeria, and Hong Kong
- First High Commissioner of Northern Nigeria (1900–1906)
- Governor-General of Nigeria (1914–1919), amalgamating North and South
- Published *The Dual Mandate in British Tropical Africa* (1922), codifying indirect rule

**Defining Quote**:
> "The British Empire... has only one raison d'être, and that is the benefit of the subject races."
— Lugard, *The Dual Mandate*, 1922

**Fate/Legacy**: Influential in shaping British colonial administration globally

**Photograph Description**: Colonial administrator in tropical uniform; images of Nigerian colonial structures

**Source**: Lugard, *The Dual Mandate* (primary); UNESCO *General History of Africa VII*

---

### 7. Lothar von Trotha (1848–1920)
**Epithet**: "Architect of Genocide"

**Key Contributions**:
- German general who commanded forces in German Southwest Africa (Namibia)
- Issued the Vernichtungsbefehl (extermination order) against Herero people, October 1904
- Ordered systematic killing and deportation to concentration camps
- Responsible for deaths of approximately 65,000 Herero (80% of population) and 10,000 Nama (50%)
- First genocide of the 20th century

**Defining Quote**:
> "Within the German borders, every Herero, with or without a gun, with or without cattle, will be shot. I will no longer accept women and children, I will drive them back to their people or I will shoot them."
— Lothar von Trotha, Vernichtungsbefehl, October 2, 1904

**Fate/Legacy**: Recalled in 1905; died 1920; Germany officially recognized genocide in 2021

**Photograph Description**: German military portrait; images of Herero prisoners in concentration camps

**Source**: Olusoga & Erichsen, *The Kaiser's Holocaust*

## African Leaders and Resistance Figures

### 8. Menelik II of Ethiopia (1844–1913)
**Epithet**: "Victor of Adwa"

**Key Contributions**:
- Emperor of Ethiopia (1889–1913)
- Modernized Ethiopian army with European weapons purchased through clever diplomacy
- Exploited Italian misunderstanding of Treaty of Wuchale (1889) to maintain sovereignty
- Commanded Ethiopian forces at Battle of Adwa (March 1, 1896), crushing Italian invasion
- Preserved Ethiopia as only African nation (with Liberia) to avoid colonization
- Expanded Ethiopian territory through own conquests

**Defining Quote**:
> "I have no intention of being an indifferent spectator if the distant powers have the idea of dividing up Africa."
— Menelik II, circular letter to European powers, April 1891

**Fate/Legacy**: Stroke in 1906 left him incapacitated; Ethiopia remained independent until 1936

**Photograph Description**: Emperor in full regalia with crown and robes; battlefield images from Adwa; photos with Empress Taytu

**Source**: Marcus, *A History of Ethiopia*; Vandervort, *Wars of Imperial Conquest*

---

### 9. Empress Taytu Betul (c. 1851–1918)
**Epithet**: "Warrior Empress"

**Key Contributions**:
- Wife and co-ruler with Menelik II
- Led 5,000 troops personally at Battle of Adwa
- Key strategic advisor who opposed Treaty of Wuchale's ambiguous terms
- Commanded the right flank during the battle
- Symbol of Ethiopian resistance and women's leadership

**Defining Quote**:
> "I am a woman. I do not love war. But rather than accept this, I prefer war."
— Attributed to Taytu regarding Italian ultimatum

**Fate/Legacy**: Marginalized after Menelik's incapacitation; died 1918

**Photograph Description**: Empress in royal Ethiopian dress; images alongside Menelik; battlefield representations

**Source**: Marcus, *A History of Ethiopia*

---

### 10. Samori Ture (c. 1830–1900)
**Epithet**: "Napoleon of the Sudan"

**Key Contributions**:
- Founder of the Wassoulou Empire in West Africa (present-day Guinea, Mali, Ivory Coast)
- Resisted French conquest for 16 years (1882–1898)
- Implemented scorched-earth tactics, relocating entire empire eastward
- Manufactured his own firearms, one of few African leaders to do so
- Created mobile guerrilla warfare strategies that frustrated French forces

**Defining Quote**:
> "I will fight the French as long as my powder lasts."
— Attributed to Samori Ture

**Fate/Legacy**: Captured 1898, exiled to Gabon where he died in 1900

**Photograph Description**: Warrior in traditional dress; images of capture by French forces; Wassoulou Empire maps

**Source**: Vandervort, *Wars of Imperial Conquest*; UNESCO *General History of Africa VII*

---

### 11. Cetshwayo kaMpande (c. 1826–1884)
**Epithet**: "Last Great Zulu King"

**Key Contributions**:
- King of the Zulu nation (1873–1879)
- Commanded forces that annihilated British column at Battle of Isandlwana (January 22, 1879)
- Inflicted worst defeat on British army by indigenous forces in the Victorian era
- Employed innovative "horns of the buffalo" military formation
- Later captured and exiled to Cape Town; briefly restored but died soon after

**Defining Quote**:
> "I have done nothing wrong to the English. What have I done to the great ones of the English that they should come to my land?"
— Cetshwayo, prior to Anglo-Zulu War

**Fate/Legacy**: Died 1884 under mysterious circumstances; Zulu kingdom fragmented

**Photograph Description**: Royal portrait taken during exile in Cape Town; images of Zulu warriors; Isandlwana battlefield

**Source**: Vandervort, *Wars of Imperial Conquest*; Pakenham, *Scramble for Africa*

---

### 12. Lobengula Khumalo (c. 1845–1894)
**Epithet**: "Last King of the Ndebele"

**Key Contributions**:
- King of the Ndebele (Matabele) kingdom in present-day Zimbabwe
- Signed the Rudd Concession (1888), tricked into granting Rhodes mining rights
- Attempted to resist British South Africa Company takeover
- Lost First Matabele War (1893) against maxim guns and superior firepower
- Disappeared during retreat; fate unknown

**Defining Quote**:
> "I have been lied to by Rhodes and his people. The mouth that speaks these lies should be closed."
— Attributed to Lobengula after realizing Rudd Concession's implications

**Fate/Legacy**: Disappeared 1894; kingdom absorbed into Rhodesia

**Photograph Description**: King in formal dress; images of Ndebele warriors; photos of treaty negotiations

**Source**: Pakenham, *Scramble for Africa*; Rotberg, *The Founder*

---

### 13. Muhammad Ahmad (The Mahdi) (1844–1885)
**Epithet**: "The Expected One"

**Key Contributions**:
- Sudanese religious leader who declared himself the Mahdi (guided one) in 1881
- United Sudanese against Egyptian-British rule
- Captured Khartoum (1885) and killed General Gordon
- Established Mahdist state that resisted colonization until 1898
- Combined religious fervor with anti-colonial resistance

**Defining Quote**:
> "I am the Mahdi, the Successor of the Prophet of God."
— Muhammad Ahmad, 1881 proclamation

**Fate/Legacy**: Died 1885 shortly after Khartoum victory; successor Abdallahi ruled until 1898 defeat at Omdurman

**Photograph Description**: Religious iconography; images of Khartoum siege; Mahdist forces

**Source**: Pakenham, *Scramble for Africa*

## Reformers and Critics

### 14. E.D. Morel (1873–1924)
**Epithet**: "Crusader Against the Congo Horrors"

**Key Contributions**:
- British journalist who exposed Congo Free State atrocities
- Founded Congo Reform Association (1904)
- Published *Red Rubber* (1906) documenting forced labor and mutilation
- Analyzed shipping records to prove exploitation (only weapons in, only rubber out)
- Campaigned for over a decade until Congo was transferred to Belgian state

**Defining Quote**:
> "The rubber system of the Congo is slavery of the worst type."
— E.D. Morel, *Red Rubber*, 1906

**Fate/Legacy**: Later pacifist, imprisoned during WWI; died 1924

**Photograph Description**: Portrait of Victorian-era reformer; images of Congo Reform Association materials; atrocity photographs he circulated

**Source**: Hochschild, *King Leopold's Ghost*; Morel, *Red Rubber* (primary)

---

### 15. Roger Casement (1864–1916)
**Epithet**: "Witness to Atrocity"

**Key Contributions**:
- British consul who investigated and reported on Congo Free State horrors (1903)
- Casement Report (1904) provided official documentation of atrocities
- Later exposed similar abuses in Peruvian Amazon rubber trade
- Irish nationalist executed for treason during WWI

**Defining Quote**:
> "I saw mothers and wives forced to accept the embraces of their tormentors in lieu of the death of their loved ones."
— Roger Casement, Congo Report, 1904 (paraphrased from testimony)

**Fate/Legacy**: Hanged 1916 for Irish nationalist activities; posthumously pardoned and celebrated

**Photograph Description**: Consular portrait; images from Congo investigation; later Irish nationalist photographs

**Source**: Hochschild, *King Leopold's Ghost*; Casement Report (primary)
