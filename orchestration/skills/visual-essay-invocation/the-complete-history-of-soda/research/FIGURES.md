### 1. Joseph Priestley (1733-1804)
**The Father of Carbonation**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | English scientist, theologian, natural philosopher |
| **Dates** | 1733-1804 |
| **Era** | Scientific Discovery |
| **Priority** | P1 - MUST INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- Discovered method of infusing water with carbon dioxide in 1767 at a brewery in Leeds, Yorkshire
- Published "Impregnating Water with Fixed Air" in 1772, documenting his carbonation process
- Awarded the Copley Medal by the Royal Society in 1773 for this work
- Also credited with discovering oxygen (which he called "dephlogisticated air")
- Did not commercialize his discovery but laid the scientific foundation for the soft drink industry

**Verified Quote**:
> Priestley referred to his invention of carbonated water as being his "happiest" discovery.

> He described the result as "an exceedingly pleasant sparkling water, resembling Seltzer water."

**Leeds Brewery Context**:
- Priestley lived next to the Old Red Lion Brewery on Meadow Lane, near the River Aire
- He was fascinated by the "fixed air" (carbon dioxide) produced during beer fermentation
- The air blanketing fermenting beer was known to kill mice suspended in it
- He suspended a bowl of water above the beer vat and found the infused water had a pleasant taste

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- National Portrait Gallery, London (multiple portraits)
- Joseph Priestley House Museum, Northumberland, PA
- Royal Society archives
- Science History Institute

**Status**: ✅ Verified (multiple sources)

### 2. Johann Jacob Schweppe (1740-1821)
**The Commercial Pioneer**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | German-Swiss watchmaker, entrepreneur |
| **Dates** | 1740-1821 |
| **Era** | Commercial Pioneering |
| **Priority** | P6 - SHOULD INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- Founded the Schweppes company in Geneva in 1783, commercializing Priestley's discovery
- Developed the "Geneva System" or "Geneva principle" for commercially viable carbonation
- First to manufacture bottled carbonated mineral water at commercial scale
- Expanded to London in 1792, setting up factory at 141 Drury Lane
- Created apparatus that matched or exceeded natural mineral water carbonation levels
- Schweppes regarded Priestley as "the father of our industry"

**Company Milestones**:
- 1783: Company founded in Geneva
- 1792: Expansion to London
- 1831: Royal warrant from King William IV
- 1870: Ginger ale introduced
- 1871: First carbonated tonic water

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Schweppes corporate archives
- Geneva historical society
- British Museum collections (early Schweppes bottles)

**Status**: ✅ Verified (Wikipedia, corporate history)

### 3. John Stith Pemberton (1831-1888)
**The Inventor of Coca-Cola**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | American pharmacist, Confederate veteran |
| **Dates** | July 8, 1831 - August 16, 1888 |
| **Era** | Pharmacy Golden Age |
| **Priority** | P3 - MUST INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- Invented Coca-Cola on May 8, 1886, in Atlanta, Georgia
- Created the syrup at his pharmacy and brought it to Jacobs' Pharmacy
- Originally marketed as a patent medicine and "brain tonic"
- First served as a soda fountain drink at 5 cents per glass
- Developed the formula from his earlier "French Wine Coca" after temperance legislation

**Background**:
- Born in Knoxville, Georgia; raised in Rome, Georgia
- Earned medical degree at 19 from Reform Medical College of Georgia
- Served as lieutenant colonel in Third Georgia Cavalry during Civil War
- Suffered sabre wound at Battle of Columbus (April 1865), leading to morphine addiction
- Moved to Atlanta in 1870; established Pemberton, Wilson, Taylor and Company

**Original Formula Context**:
- Called for five ounces of coca leaf per gallon of syrup
- Original formula contained estimated nine milligrams of cocaine per glass
- Cocaine was removed from formula in 1903
- Pemberton's partner Frank Robinson suggested the name and designed the script logo

**Tragic End**:
- Sold rights to Coca-Cola formula while ill and nearly bankrupt
- Died of stomach cancer at age 57
- At death, was poor and addicted to morphine
- Never saw the commercial success of his creation

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Atlanta History Center
- World of Coca-Cola Museum, Atlanta
- Georgia Historical Society
- Coca-Cola Company archives

**Status**: ✅ Verified (multiple sources including Georgia Encyclopedia)

### 4. Asa Griggs Candler (1851-1929)
**The Marketing Genius**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | Businessman, Atlanta mayor, philanthropist |
| **Dates** | 1851-1929 |
| **Era** | Pharmacy Golden Age / Brand Building |
| **Priority** | P3 - MUST INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- Purchased Coca-Cola formula from Pemberton for $2,300 total (achieved full ownership by 1891)
- Founded The Coca-Cola Company in 1892
- Pioneered aggressive advertising when most viewed it as wasteful spending
- Invested $11,000 in marketing in 1891 (over $300,000 in today's dollars)
- Ordered 45,000 calendars with Coca-Cola logo in first year
- Introduced one of the first celebrity endorsements (actress Hilda Clark)
- Distributed coupons for free Coca-Cola glasses—revolutionary for the 1890s

**Business Achievements**:
- 1892: Founded The Coca-Cola Company
- 1893: Trademarked the brand
- 1895: Coca-Cola distributed nationwide
- Advertising budget grew from $100,000 (1901) to $1 million (1911)

**Famous Mistake**:
- In 1899, sold exclusive bottling rights for just one dollar
- Believed bottled Coca-Cola would fail
- This decision cost the company billions in future revenue

**Later Life**:
- Resigned from Coca-Cola in 1916
- Became Mayor of Atlanta
- Active philanthropist until death in 1929

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Emory University archives (major beneficiary of Candler family)
- Atlanta History Center
- Coca-Cola Company archives
- Georgia Historical Society

**Status**: ✅ Verified (multiple sources)

### 5. Caleb Bradham (1867-1934)
**The Creator of Pepsi-Cola**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | American pharmacist, entrepreneur |
| **Dates** | May 27, 1867 - February 19, 1934 |
| **Era** | Pharmacy Golden Age |
| **Priority** | P3 - MUST INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- Invented Pepsi-Cola in 1893 at his drugstore in New Bern, North Carolina
- Originally called the drink "Brad's Drink"
- Renamed to Pepsi-Cola on August 28, 1898
- Name derived from "dyspepsia" (indigestion) and "cola"—marketed for digestive benefits
- His assistant James Henry King was the first to taste the new drink
- Incorporated Pepsi-Cola Company on December 24, 1902

**Background**:
- Born in Chinquapin, North Carolina
- Attended University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill
- Studied at University of Maryland School of Medicine but dropped out when father's business failed
- Worked briefly as public school teacher
- Opened Bradham Drug Company in New Bern with soda fountain

**Rise and Fall**:
- By 1910: Over 250 bottlers in 24 states
- 1923: Declared bankruptcy after purchasing sugar at 28 cents/pound just before price crashed to 5 cents
- Sold trademark and business for $35,000 to Richmond investors
- Also served 25 years in naval reserve, retiring as rear admiral

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Pepsi-Cola birthplace site, New Bern, NC
- North Carolina State Archives
- Pepsi corporate archives
- Find A Grave memorial

**Status**: ✅ Verified (NCpedia, Wikipedia)

### 6. Robert W. Woodruff (1889-1985)
**"The Boss" of Coca-Cola**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | Coca-Cola president and de facto leader |
| **Dates** | December 6, 1889 - 1985 |
| **Era** | Brand Wars / WWII Expansion |
| **Priority** | HIGH - SHOULD INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- Led Coca-Cola for five decades, transforming it from financially struggling company to global corporation
- Became president in 1923
- Masterminded Coca-Cola's WWII global expansion strategy
- Famous directive: "Every man in uniform gets a bottle of Coca-Cola for five cents, wherever he is and whatever it costs our company"
- Deployed 148 "Technical Observers" (nicknamed Coca-Cola Colonels) to war zones
- Established 64 bottling plants around the world during WWII
- Oversaw distribution of 5 billion bottles to troops during the war

**Post-War Impact**:
- WWII plants became foundation for international expansion
- By 1968, 50% of Coca-Cola's net profits came from foreign operations
- Eisenhower sent top-secret telegram ordering 3 million bottles and 10 bottling plants for North Africa invasion

**Later Life**:
- Officially retired January 1, 1955 (company mandatory retirement age)
- Continued to wield enormous influence
- Major philanthropist; Emory University's greatest benefactor
- Known for anonymous civic-minded philanthropy

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Emory University Woodruff archives
- Atlanta History Center
- Columbus, Georgia historical society
- Coca-Cola Company archives

**Status**: ✅ Verified (Georgia Encyclopedia, USO)

### 7. Roberto Goizueta (1931-1997)
**The New Coke Decision-Maker**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | Coca-Cola CEO, Cuban-American businessman |
| **Dates** | November 18, 1931 - October 18, 1997 |
| **Era** | Cola Wars |
| **Priority** | HIGH - SHOULD INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- Served as chairman, president, and CEO of Coca-Cola from 1980 until death
- Increased company market value from $4 billion (1981) to $150 billion (1997)
- Made the fateful decision to change Coca-Cola's formula in 1985
- Announced "New Coke" on April 23, 1985, at Lincoln Center press conference
- Oversaw the reversal and return of "Coca-Cola Classic" on July 11, 1985
- Introduced Diet Coke (1982) and Cherry Coke (1985)

**The New Coke Saga**:
- Told employees there would be no "sacred cows" in how company did business
- Commissioned 200,000 taste tests before changing formula
- Described new formula as "smoother, rounder, yet bolder—a more harmonious flavor"
- Received 1,500 calls per day on consumer hotline during backlash (vs. 400 before)
- Called emergency board meeting July 11, 1985, to reverse decision
- "Official blank" strategy considered (1 in 9 bottles empty to raise effective price)

**Verified Quotes**:
> At press conference: "smoother, rounder, yet bolder—a more harmonious flavor"

> "If you take risks, you may fail. But if you don't take risks, you will surely fail. The greatest risk of all is to do nothing."

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Coca-Cola Company archives
- Emory University Goizueta Business School (named for him)
- 1985 press conference photographs and footage
- Time magazine archives

**Status**: ✅ Verified (Britannica, Coca-Cola archives)

### 8. Charles Hires (1851-1937)
**The Root Beer King**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | Philadelphia pharmacist, entrepreneur |
| **Dates** | 1851-1937 |
| **Era** | Pharmacy Golden Age |
| **Priority** | HIGH - SHOULD INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- Revolutionized manufacture and distribution of root beer in early 1870s
- Developed powder for easier home production
- Created his root beer formula in 1875 mixing sarsaparilla, sassafras, wild cherry, wintergreen, and ginger
- Presented root beer at 1876 Centennial Exhibition in Philadelphia
- Served free glasses and sold 25-cent packets (yielded 5 gallons)
- Marketed as "temperance drink" to distance from alcohol connotation

**Context**:
- As a Quaker, did not drink alcohol
- Touted purported medicinal benefits of root beer
- Operated drug store at Sixth and Spruce streets, Philadelphia
- 1890: Business incorporated as The Charles E. Hires Company
- 1893: Began selling pre-mixed bottles in addition to powder

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Historical Society of Pennsylvania
- Philadelphia History Museum

### 9. Donald Kendall (1921-2020)
**The Cold War Cola Diplomat**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | PepsiCo CEO, Cold War figure |
| **Dates** | 1921-2020 (died at 99) |
| **Era** | Cola Wars / Cold War |
| **Priority** | MEDIUM - SHOULD INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- Started as worker in Pepsi-Cola bottling plant
- Became head of international division by 1957 in mid-30s
- Orchestrated famous 1959 "Kitchen Debate" Pepsi moment
- Told Vice President Nixon he "had to get a Pepsi in Khrushchev's hand"
- Nixon guided Khrushchev to Pepsi booth at American National Exhibition in Moscow
- Became CEO in 1963
- Made Pepsi first U.S. consumer product produced and sold in Soviet Union (1972)

**The Pepsi Navy**:
- Negotiated extraordinary barter deals with USSR
- Pepsi became middleman for 17 submarines and 3 warships (frigate, cruiser, destroyer)
- Traded Soviet warships for Pepsi access to Soviet market
- Joked to National Security Adviser: "We're disarming the Soviet Union faster than you are"

**Nixon Connection**:
- After Nixon's 1960 and 1962 defeats, gave Nixon lifeline
- Promised Pepsi's legal accounts to any law firm that hired Nixon
- Mudge, Stern, Baldwin, & Todd hired Nixon as partner

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Nixon Presidential Library
- 1959 Moscow exhibition photographs

### 10. Torbern Bergman (1735-1784)
**The Scientific Improver**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | Swedish chemist and mineralogist |
| **Dates** | March 20, 1735 - July 8, 1784 |
| **Era** | Scientific Discovery |
| **Priority** | MEDIUM - SHOULD INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- In 1771, perfected Priestley's carbonation process
- Developed apparatus using sulfuric acid and chalk to produce carbon dioxide more efficiently
- Created the "Bergman Siphon" for precise, controlled carbonation
- His apparatus allowed large-scale production of artificial mineral water
- First chemist to use A, B, C notation system for chemical species
- Noted for 1775 "Dissertation on Elective Attractions" (largest chemical affinity tables ever published)

**Scientific Context**:
- Built upon Priestley's 1767 discovery
- Mineral water at the time was believed to have medicinal properties
- Enabled commercial-scale carbonation that Schweppe later exploited

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Uppsala University archives
- Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences
- Lunar crater Bergman named in his honor
- Uranium mineral torbernite named for him

**Status**: ✅ Verified (Wikipedia)

### 11. Benjamin Silliman (1779-1864)
**America's Soda Water Pioneer**

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Role** | Yale professor, chemist, science educator |
| **Dates** | August 8, 1779 - November 24, 1864 |
| **Era** | Commercial Pioneering (American) |
| **Priority** | MEDIUM - SHOULD INCLUDE |

**Key Contributions**:
- First American to produce soda water in bulk
- Pioneered mass production of carbonated water in the United States
- Purchased Nooth apparatus and opened soda fountain in New Haven in 1806
- Marketed mineral waters for medicinal properties
- Kick-started soda machine manufacture and sales across New York City and Baltimore in 1810s
- His success encouraged other businessmen to open fountains in New York and Philadelphia

**Academic Career**:
- Yale's first professor of chemistry (1802-1855)
- Founded American Journal of Science (oldest continuously published US scientific journal)
- Instrumental in founding Yale School of Medicine (1813)
- Founded Sheffield Scientific School (1847), Yale School of Engineering (1852)
- First to use fractional distillation in America

**Note**: While Silliman's soda business never achieved commercial success for him personally, he played important role in popularizing carbonated water in America and establishing the business model.

**Photograph/Image Sources**:
- Yale University archives
- Connecticut Historical Society
- American Chemical Society archives

**Status**: ✅ Verified (Connecticut History, Wikipedia)
