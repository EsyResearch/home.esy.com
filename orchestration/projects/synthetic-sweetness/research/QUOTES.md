## Discovery Quotes

### 1. Constantine Fahlberg on Saccharin Discovery (1886)
> "With the name 'saccharin' I am providing a formal designation for a substance discovered by me in June 1878."

**Source**: Fahlberg's 1886 publication
**Context**: Fahlberg's claim to sole discovery, which omitted Remsen's contribution

---

### 2. Ira Remsen on Fahlberg (Letter to Sir William Ramsay)
> "Fahlberg is a scoundrel. It nauseates me to hear my name mentioned in the same breath with him."

**Source**: Letter to Sir William Ramsay, cited in multiple historical accounts
**Context**: Remsen's bitter response to Fahlberg patenting saccharin without credit

---

### 3. Ira Remsen (Extended Quote)
> "It makes my blood boil to see the lies of that scoundrel Fahlberg constantly, constantly in print, and to see further, that they are generally believed."

**Source**: Personal correspondence
**Context**: Years after the patent dispute

---

### 4. Michael Sveda on Cyclamate Discovery (1937)
> "God looks after damn fools, children and chemists."

**Source**: Various interviews
**Context**: On the accidental discovery when he put a cigarette on his lab bench

---

### 5. James Schlatter on Aspartame Discovery (1965)
> "I licked my finger and it tasted good."

**Source**: Interview accounts
**Context**: The serendipitous discovery while synthesizing anti-ulcer compounds

---

### 6. Shashikant Phadnis on Sucralose Discovery (1976)
> "I thought I ought to taste it [...] so I took a little bit of it on a spatula and tasted it with the tip of my tongue. What a surprise! It was intensely and pleasantly sweet."

**Source**: Interview with Phadnis
**Context**: The famous "test/taste" miscommunication

---

### 7. Leslie Hough's Response to Phadnis
> "Are you crazy? How can you taste compounds, without knowing anything about their toxicity? That can be really dangerous."

**Source**: Recounted by Phadnis
**Context**: Supervisor's reaction to the dangerous but fortuitous tasting

---

## Regulatory and Political Quotes

### 8. Theodore Roosevelt on Saccharin (1907)
> "Anybody who says saccharin is injurious to health is an idiot."

**Source**: Meeting with Harvey Wiley, 1907, widely documented
**Context**: Roosevelt personally used saccharin on his doctor's orders

---

### 9. Arthur Hull Hayes Jr. on Aspartame (1981)
> "I'm not prepared to say there is no risk from aspartame, but I thought it had been demonstrated that there was not a significant risk."

**Source**: Post-approval statements
**Context**: Justifying his decision to overrule the PBOI

---

### 10. FDA Public Board of Inquiry (September 30, 1980)
> "The Board has not been presented with proof of a reasonable certainty that aspartame is safe for use as a food additive."

**Source**: PBOI Final Report
**Context**: 3-0 vote to keep aspartame off the market

---

### 11. GAO Report on Aspartame (1987)
> "FDA followed its food additive approval process in approving aspartame for marketing."

**Source**: GAO Report HRD-87-46
**Context**: Procedural review, though critics note it didn't evaluate scientific merits

---

## Historical Sugar Quotes

### 12. Sidney Mintz on Sugar and Power
> "Sugar was one of the first luxury goods to become a 'proletarian necessity.'"

**Source**: *Sweetness and Power* (1985)
**Context**: Foundational work on sugar's transformation from elite luxury to mass commodity

---

### 13. British Consumption Pattern
> "A rarity in 1650, a luxury in 1700, [sugar] had been transformed into a virtual necessity by 1850."

**Source**: Sidney Mintz, *Sweetness and Power*
**Context**: Describing the dramatic shift in British sugar consumption

---

### 14. Sugar and Slavery Connection
> "Without sugar, there would have been no slave trade on the scale that shaped the Atlantic world."

**Source**: Historical synthesis (various scholars)
**Context**: The fundamental link between sugar cultivation and transatlantic slavery

---

## Health and Science Quotes

### 15. WHO 2023 Guideline on Non-Sugar Sweeteners
> "WHO suggests that non-sugar sweeteners not be used as a means of achieving weight control or reducing risk of noncommunicable diseases."

**Source**: WHO Guideline (May 2023), ISBN 9789240073616
**Context**: Conditional recommendation based on systematic review

---

### 16. WHO Clarification (2023)
> "The recommendation applies to all people except individuals with pre-existing diabetes."

**Source**: WHO Guideline (2023)
**Context**: Important exception for diabetics

---

### 17. UK SACN Response (April 2025)
> "The evidence does not support the WHO recommendation to avoid non-sugar sweeteners for weight management."

**Source**: SACN Statement
**Context**: UK regulatory body challenging WHO guidance

---

### 18. IARC on Aspartame (July 2023)
> "Aspartame is classified as possibly carcinogenic to humans (Group 2B) based on limited evidence."

**Source**: IARC Monograph 134
**Context**: "Limited evidence" is the lowest positive classification

---

### 19. JECFA Reaffirmation (July 2023)
> "The Committee reaffirmed the acceptable daily intake of 40 mg/kg body weight for aspartame."

**Source**: JECFA 96th Meeting Report
**Context**: No change to safety limits despite IARC classification

---

## Wartime Quotes

### 22. On Sugar Rationing (1942)
> "Sugar was the first consumer commodity rationed during World War II... and the last to be removed from rationing."

**Source**: National Park Service
**Context**: Sugar rationed April 1942 - June 1947

---

### 23. OPA on Saccharin (WWII)
> "Saccharin production increased dramatically to compensate for sugar shortages during the war."

**Source**: Wartime production records
**Context**: Necessity-driven sweetener use

## Discovery Pattern Quote

### 24. On Accidental Discoveries
> "Every major artificial sweetener—saccharin, cyclamate, aspartame, sucralose, acesulfame-K—was discovered accidentally when a chemist tasted something they shouldn't have."

**Source**: Science History Institute, various accounts
**Context**: Remarkable pattern of serendipity

---

### 25. Karl Clauss on Acesulfame-K Discovery (1967)
> "I licked my fingers to pick up a piece of paper and noticed an intense sweet taste."

**Source**: Discovery accounts
**Context**: Yet another accidental licking discovery
