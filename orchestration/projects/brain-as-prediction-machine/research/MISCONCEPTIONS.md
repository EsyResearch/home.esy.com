## Misconception Strategy

The essay uses **misconception-first teaching**:
1. Make the misconception explicit
2. Show why it fails
3. Offer the correct model as a solution

---

## Primary Misconception: The Reactive Brain

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **The Error** | Brain reacts to the world |
| **Correct Model** | Brain predicts the world |
| **Why Held** | Intuitive; matches conscious experience |
| **Addressed In** | Sections 1-3 |

### The Misconception in Detail

> "The brain is like a receiver. Things happen in the world, signals come in through the senses, the brain processes them, and then it responds."

This model is:
- **Intuitive**: Matches how we experience the world
- **Reinforced by language**: "I saw something, so I reacted"
- **Scientifically outdated**: 20th century view

### Why It's Wrong

1. **Timing**: Reaction is too slow. Neural processing takes time, but we act in real-time.
2. **Ambiguity**: Sensory input is noisy and incomplete. If we only reacted to input, we couldn't function.
3. **Efficiency**: Pure reaction would require processing everything from scratch. Too expensive.

### The Correction

> "The brain is not primarily reacting — it's primarily predicting. And then checking its predictions against reality."

## Secondary Misconceptions

### Misconception: Perception = Reality

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **The Error** | What I see is what's really there |
| **Correct Model** | What I see is my brain's best guess |
| **Why Held** | Perception feels direct and unmediated |
| **Addressed In** | Section 5 |

**Correction approach**: Gentle. Avoid language that makes reader feel "crazy." Emphasize that the brain's guess is usually *very good* — that's why it feels like direct perception.

---

### Misconception: Mistakes = Failures

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **The Error** | Being wrong is bad |
| **Correct Model** | Being wrong is informative |
| **Why Held** | Cultural; mistakes are punished |
| **Addressed In** | Section 7 |

**Correction approach**: Reframe. "Without prediction error, the brain cannot learn. Mistakes are not failures — they're the only way forward."

---

### Misconception: Attention = Seeing More

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **The Error** | Paying attention means taking in more information |
| **Correct Model** | Paying attention means weighting certain prediction errors more heavily |
| **Why Held** | "Attention" in everyday language implies focus/intake |
| **Addressed In** | Section 7 |

**Correction approach**: Brief. "Attention doesn't let you see more — it makes certain surprises louder than others."

---

### Misconception: Learning = Absorbing Information

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **The Error** | Learning is putting information into the brain |
| **Correct Model** | Learning is updating predictions through error |
| **Why Held** | Traditional education model (teacher → student transfer) |
| **Addressed In** | Section 7 |

**Correction approach**: Practical. "You don't learn by passively receiving information. You learn by making predictions, being wrong, and updating."

## Misconception-Truth Mapping

| Reader Arrives With | Reader Should Leave With |
|--------------------|--------------------------|
| Brain reacts to world | Brain predicts world |
| Perception is reality | Perception is best guess |
| Mistakes are bad | Mistakes are learning signals |
| Attention = focus | Attention = precision weighting |
| Learning = absorption | Learning = error-driven updating |
