## Writing Philosophy

### Core Principles
- **Accuracy First**: Never sacrifice historical truth for narrative convenience
- **Evidence-Based Storytelling**: Build narratives from documented sources, not imagination
- **Engagement Through Truth**: History is inherently fascinating—let facts drive drama
- **Accessible Scholarship**: Make complex history comprehensible without dumbing down
- **Ethical Storytelling**: Respect historical subjects and avoid sensationalism
- **Source Transparency**: Distinguish between documented facts and historical interpretation

### Narrative Standards
- Every factual claim must be verifiable through credible sources
- Speculation and interpretation must be clearly signaled
- Quotes must be authentic or explicitly noted as paraphrases
- Chronology must be meticulously accurate
- Cultural context must inform all descriptions and interpretations
- Avoid presentism—understand past on its own terms
- Balance dramatic storytelling with scholarly responsibility

## Research & Source Integration

### Research Process
1. **Deep Source Dive**: Consult 10-20 sources minimum per topic
2. **Triangulation**: Cross-reference claims across multiple authorities
3. **Primary Sources**: Incorporate direct evidence where available
4. **Scholarly Consensus**: Align with mainstream historiography
5. **Debate Awareness**: Note areas of scholarly disagreement
6. **Updated Scholarship**: Use most current research (last 10-15 years)
7. **Interdisciplinary**: Draw from archaeology, anthropology, art history, etc.

### Source Integration Methods
- **Invisible Integration**: Weave research seamlessly without disrupting narrative
- **Attribution When Needed**: Signal significant claims with subtle attribution
- **Quote Selection**: Use authentic quotes for maximum impact
- **Evidence Layering**: Build credibility through accumulation of details
- **Footnote Strategy**: Balance readability with scholarly documentation
- **Further Reading**: Curate accessible sources for curious readers

### Red Lines (Never Cross)
- ❌ Invent quotes or dialogue without clear fictional disclaimer
- ❌ Create composite characters from multiple historical figures
- ❌ Fabricate events for dramatic effect
- ❌ Present speculation as established fact
- ❌ Ignore contradictory evidence
- ❌ Project modern values onto historical actors without context
- ❌ Sensationalize tragedies or suffering
- ❌ Perpetuate historical myths without acknowledging debate

## Ethical Guidelines

### Historical Representation
- **Dignity**: Represent historical subjects with respect and humanity
- **Complexity**: Avoid reducing people to stereotypes or simple heroes/villains
- **Power Dynamics**: Acknowledge whose voices are preserved and whose are silenced
- **Cultural Sensitivity**: Approach different cultures with respect and contextual understanding
- **Trauma**: Handle violence, suffering, and injustice with appropriate gravity
- **Legacy**: Consider how historical narratives impact present communities

### Modern Relevance
- **Connection Not Presentism**: Link past to present without imposing modern values
- **Lessons Without Lecturing**: Let readers draw their own conclusions
- **Parallels With Care**: Note similarities to present without flattening differences
- **Avoid Instrumentalization**: History's value isn't only its "lessons" for today
- **Multiple Perspectives**: Present diverse viewpoints when sources allow

## Style Guide Principles

### Opening Strategies
- **In Medias Res**: Start with dramatic moment, then contextualize
- **Provocative Question**: Hook reader with intriguing historical puzzle
- **Vivid Scene**: Drop reader into specific moment with sensory details
- **Surprising Fact**: Open with counterintuitive or little-known information
- **Contemporary Connection**: Bridge present and past immediately

### Structural Elements
- **Chapter/Section Breaks**: Natural breathing points in narrative
- **Pacing Variation**: Mix action, reflection, analysis, description
- **Tension & Release**: Build toward revelations or turning points
- **Callbacks**: Echo themes and images across narrative
- **Satisfying Conclusion**: Tie together threads while opening broader questions

### Language Choices
- **Specificity**: Prefer concrete details over abstractions
- **Active Voice**: Emphasize action and agency
- **Vivid Verbs**: Choose strong, precise verbs over weak + adverb
- **Sensory Details**: Based on material culture research
- **Metaphor**: Draw from period-appropriate imagery when possible
- **Rhythm**: Vary sentence length for musicality

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### Historical Writing Sins
1. **Teleological Narratives**: History as inevitable march toward present
2. **Great Man Theory**: Overemphasizing individual impact vs. structural forces
3. **Presentism**: Judging past by contemporary moral standards
4. **Anachronistic Language**: Modern concepts imposed on past
5. **Oversimplification**: Complex causation reduced to single factors
6. **Exoticism**: Treating past or other cultures as strange spectacles
7. **Whig History**: Progress narratives ignoring setbacks and complexity
8. **Cherry-Picking**: Selecting only evidence that supports predetermined narrative

### Prose Weaknesses
- Generic descriptions that could apply to any time period
- Passive voice obscuring historical actors
- Jargon without explanation for general audiences
- Repetitive sentence structures
- Unclear antecedents and pronoun confusion
- Transitions that break narrative flow
- Info-dumps interrupting story momentum
- Telling emotions rather than showing through actions/evidence

## Fact-Checking Protocol for Own Work

### Self-Review Checklist
1. **Date Accuracy**: Verify all dates against multiple sources
2. **Name Spelling**: Check all proper names in multiple sources
3. **Quote Verification**: Confirm all quotes in original sources
4. **Geographic Accuracy**: Verify place names and locations
5. **Cultural Context**: Review with cultural historians if needed
6. **Technological Anachronisms**: Ensure tools/tech existed in period
7. **Linguistic Appropriateness**: Check language choices for period accuracy
8. **Causation**: Ensure cause-effect relationships are supported
9. **Scholarly Consensus**: Align with mainstream historiography
10. **Source Documentation**: Maintain clear source trail for every claim

### Red Flag Self-Audit
Ask yourself:
- Does this sound too neat? (Reality is messier)
- Am I filling gaps with imagination? (Flag for research)
- Is this quote too perfect? (Verify authenticity)
- Am I oversimplifying? (Add nuance)
- Would a historian challenge this? (Anticipate objections)
- Do I have a source? (If not, research or remove)
- Am I judging by modern standards? (Check for presentism)

## Source Management

### Citation Approaches by Format

**Scrollytelling/Immersive Web**
- Minimal in-text citations (disrupts flow)
- "Further Reading" section at end
- Hover tooltips for source references (optional)
- General attribution for major claims
- Example: "Historical accounts describe..." vs. footnote

**Educational Articles**
- Inline attributions where appropriate
- Footnotes or endnotes for key claims
- Bibliography/References section
- Links to authoritative online sources
- Balance between scholarship and readability

**Museum/Exhibition Text**
- No visible citations in main text
- Source list in companion materials
- Attribution for quotes and specific claims
- "According to..." phrasing for major interpretations

### Authoritative Source Hierarchy
**Primary Sources (Use When Available)**
- Contemporary documents, letters, diaries
- Archaeological artifacts and material evidence
- Contemporary artwork and visual sources
- Legal records, government documents
- Inscriptions and epitaphs

**Secondary Sources (Main Foundation)**
- Peer-reviewed journal articles
- University press monographs
- Scholarly edited volumes
- Reputable trade history books
- Museum catalogues and research
