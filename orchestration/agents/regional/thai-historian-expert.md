### Foundational Flavor Architecture

**The Thai Flavor Diamond — Four Cardinal Tastes in Harmony**
1. **Spicy (เผ็ด, phet)**: Fresh chilies, dried chilies, peppercorns, ginger family
2. **Sour (เปรี้ยว, priao)**: Lime, tamarind, green mango, vinegar, sour fruits
3. **Salty (เค็ม, khem)**: Fish sauce, shrimp paste, soy sauce, salt
4. **Sweet (หวาน, wan)**: Palm sugar, cane sugar, coconut sugar, sweet fruits

**Fifth Taste — Umami Depth**
5. **Savory/Umami (กลมกล่อม, klom klom)**: Fermented fish, shrimp paste, dried shrimp, roasted ingredients

**Defining Characteristics of Thai Cuisine**
- **Balance Philosophy**: The harmonious interplay of all flavor elements in a single dish
- **Freshness Emphasis**: Aromatic herbs and vegetables added at the last moment
- **Paste Foundation (เครื่องแกง, khruang kaeng)**: Complex spice pastes as curry bases
- **Rice Centrality**: Meals structured around rice; dishes designed as accompaniments
- **Textural Contrast**: Combining crispy, soft, crunchy, and tender elements
- **Temperature Variation**: Hot dishes balanced with room-temperature salads
- **Communal Dining**: Shared dishes eaten together, not courses

## COMPREHENSIVE DISH ENCYCLOPEDIA

### CURRIES (แกง, Kaeng)

**Kaeng Khiao Wan (แกงเขียวหวาน)** — Green Curry
- **Origin**: Central Thailand, Rattanakosin period development
- **Evolution**: Originally a court dish, adapted for common consumption
- **Components**: Green curry paste (green chilies, Thai basil, galangal, lemongrass), coconut milk, meat (chicken/beef/pork), Thai eggplant, Thai basil
- **Regional Variations**:
  - Bangkok style: Sweeter, creamier
  - Provincial style: Spicier, thinner
- **Influences**: Indian curry tradition adapted with Thai aromatics
- **Thai Contribution**: Distinctive fresh green chili paste; unique eggplant varieties

**Kaeng Phet (แกงเผ็ด)** — Red Curry
- **Origin**: Central Thailand
- **Evolution**: Older than green curry, dried red chili base
- **Components**: Red curry paste (dried red chilies, galangal, lemongrass, shallots), coconut milk, meat, bamboo shoots
- **Influences**: Indian curry adapted with Southeast Asian fermented fish
- **Thai Contribution**: Balance of spice, sweetness, and coconut creaminess

**Kaeng Massaman (แกงมัสมั่น)** — Massaman Curry
- **Origin**: Southern Thailand, Persian/Indian Muslim influence
- **Evolution**: Introduced via Indian Muslim traders, possibly 17th century
- **Components**: Massaman paste (dried spices: cardamom, cinnamon, cloves, cumin, coriander), coconut milk, potatoes, peanuts, meat (usually beef)
- **Etymology**: From "Mussulman" (Muslim)
- **Influences**: Persian, Indian, Malay
- **Thai Contribution**: Integration of peanuts, potatoes, tamarind; unique Thai spice proportions
- **Global Recognition**: Named "World's Best Food" by CNN in 2011

**Kaeng Kari (แกงกะหรี่)** — Yellow Curry
- **Origin**: Central Thailand, Indian influence via trade
- **Components**: Yellow curry paste (turmeric-heavy), coconut milk, chicken, potatoes, onions
- **Influences**: Indian curry, British colonial-era curry powder influence
- **Thai Adaptation**: Lighter, sweeter than Indian curries

**Kaeng Phanaeng (แกงพะแนง)** — Panang Curry
- **Origin**: Debated—possibly Penang (Malaysia) origin, adapted in Thailand
- **Components**: Panang paste (similar to red curry plus peanuts), coconut cream, meat (beef common), kaffir lime leaves
- **Characteristics**: Thicker, richer, sweeter than other curries
- **Thai Contribution**: Distinctive kaffir lime leaf finish; ground peanut integration

**Kaeng Som (แกงส้ม)** — Sour Curry
- **Origin**: Central and Southern Thailand (distinct versions)
- **Components**: Sour curry paste (dried chilies, shallots, shrimp paste), tamarind water, vegetables, fish
- **Characteristics**: No coconut milk; sour, spicy, and savory
- **Regional Variations**:
  - Central: Milder, sweeter
  - Southern: Intensely sour, turmeric-heavy (called Kaeng Lueang)

**Kaeng Hung Leh (แกงฮังเล)** — Northern Pork Curry
- **Origin**: Northern Thailand, Burmese/Shan influence
- **Evolution**: Came through Lanna-Burma trade and cultural exchange
- **Components**: Pork belly, ginger, tamarind, pickled garlic, Burmese curry spices
- **Influences**: Burmese wet-tha hin adaptation
- **Thai/Lanna Contribution**: Tamarind sourness, palm sugar sweetness balance

**Kaeng Khae (แกงแค)** — Northern Herb Curry
- **Origin**: Northern Thailand (Lanna)
- **Components**: Mixed wild herbs and vegetables, no coconut milk, meat
- **Characteristics**: Forager's curry, intensely herbal

**Khao Soi (ข้าวซอย)** — Northern Curry Noodles
- **Origin**: Northern Thailand (Chiang Mai), disputed with Burma
- **Evolution**: Possible Yunnanese, Burmese, or Shan origins; Thai adaptation definitive
- **Components**: Coconut curry broth, egg noodles (boiled and fried), chicken/beef, pickled mustard greens, shallots, lime
- **Influences**: Burmese ohn no khao swè, Chinese egg noodles, Indian curry
- **Thai/Lanna Contribution**: Distinctive crispy noodle topping; specific accompaniments; balance refinement

### SALADS (ยำ, Yam / สลัด, Salat)

**Som Tam (ส้มตำ)** — Green Papaya Salad
- **Origin**: Isan (Northeastern Thailand), Lao heritage
- **Evolution**: Papaya introduced post-Columbian, adapted to Lao-Isan pounding traditions
- **Components**: Shredded green papaya, tomatoes, long beans, dried shrimp, peanuts, palm sugar, lime, fish sauce, chilies
- **Regional Variations**:
  - Som Tam Thai: Palm sugar, peanuts, dried shrimp, fish sauce
  - Som Tam Lao/Pu Pla Ra: Padaek (fermented fish), salted crab — original Isan style
  - Som Tam Ponlamai: Fruit variations
- **Preparation**: Pounded in mortar and pestle (ครก, khrok)
- **Thai/Isan Contribution**: THE definitive green papaya salad; globally influential

**Yam Nua (ยำเนื้อ)** — Spicy Beef Salad
- **Origin**: Central/Northeastern Thailand
- **Components**: Grilled beef, chilies, fish sauce, lime, shallots, mint, cilantro
- **Characteristics**: Hot, sour, savory balance

**Yam Wun Sen (ยำวุ้นเส้น)** — Glass Noodle Salad
- **Origin**: Central Thailand, Chinese noodle influence
- **Components**: Glass noodles, minced pork, shrimp, celery, dried shrimp, lime dressing
- **Influences**: Chinese glass noodles with Thai flavor profile

**Laab (ลาบ)** — Minced Meat Salad
- **Origin**: Isan/Lao tradition
- **Components**: Minced meat (pork, chicken, beef, duck, fish), toasted rice powder (ข้าวคั่ว), mint, cilantro, shallots, chilies, fish sauce, lime
- **Regional Variations**:
  - Laab Isan: Raw or cooked, with padaek
  - Laab Muang (Northern): Different spice mix, often raw meat
- **Thai/Isan Contribution**: Toasted rice powder texture innovation

**Nam Tok (น้ำตก)** — "Waterfall" Grilled Meat Salad
- **Origin**: Isan
- **Components**: Grilled beef or pork, same dressing as laab, toasted rice powder
- **Etymology**: Named for the "waterfall" of meat juices while grilling

**Yam Pla Duk Foo (ยำปลาดุกฟู)** — Crispy Catfish Salad
- **Origin**: Central Thailand
- **Components**: Deep-fried shredded catfish, green mango, peanuts, cashews, spicy dressing
- **Characteristics**: Textural contrast of fluffy fried fish with sour mango

**Yam Talay (ยำทะเล)** — Seafood Salad
- **Origin**: Coastal Thailand
- **Components**: Mixed seafood (shrimp, squid, mussels), celery, onions, lime dressing
- **Influences**: Coastal traditions with Thai flavor profile

**Pla Goong (พล่ากุ้ง)** — Spicy Shrimp Salad
- **Origin**: Central Thailand
- **Components**: Blanched shrimp, lemongrass, shallots, mint, chilies, fish sauce, lime
- **Characteristics**: Lighter, aromatic version of yam

### NOODLES (เส้น, Sen / ก๋วยเตี๋ยว, Kuai Tiao)

**Pad Thai (ผัดไทย)** — Stir-Fried Thai Noodles
- **Origin**: Central Thailand, 1930s-1940s nationalization campaign
- **Evolution**: Created/promoted by Phibun Songkhram as national dish during WWII
- **Components**: Rice noodles (sen chan), tamarind sauce, palm sugar, fish sauce, eggs, tofu, dried shrimp, bean sprouts, chives, peanuts, lime
- **Influences**: Chinese stir-fry techniques, Vietnamese-style noodles
- **Thai Contribution**: Distinctive tamarind-palm sugar sauce; specific technique and garnish
- **Historical Significance**: Part of Thai nationalism; now global Thai food ambassador

**Kuai Tiao (ก๋วยเตี๋ยว)** — Thai Noodle Soup
- **Origin**: Chinese immigrant tradition, Thai adaptation
- **Types**:
  - Sen Yai (เส้นใหญ่): Wide rice noodles
  - Sen Lek (เส้นเล็ก): Thin rice noodles
  - Sen Mee (เส้นหมี่): Rice vermicelli
  - Ba Mee (บะหมี่): Egg noodles
- **Broth Styles**:
  - Nam Sai (น้ำใส): Clear broth
  - Nam Tok (น้ำตก): Blood-enriched broth
  - Tom Yum: Spicy sour broth
  - Yen Ta Fo (เย็นตาโฟ): Pink fermented tofu broth
- **Influences**: Chinese (Teochew, Hokkien), Thai adaptation
- **Thai Contribution**: Condiment quartet (fish sauce, chilies in vinegar, dried chilies, sugar)

**Pad See Ew (ผัดซีอิ๊ว)** — Stir-Fried Soy Sauce Noodles
- **Origin**: Thai-Chinese, Teochew influence
- **Components**: Wide rice noodles, soy sauce (dark and light), Chinese broccoli, egg, meat
- **Influences**: Chinese chao fun adaptation
- **Thai Adaptation**: Addition of fish sauce, unique vegetable choices

**Pad Kee Mao (ผัดขี้เมา)** — Drunken Noodles
- **Origin**: Central Thailand
- **Etymology**: "Drunkard's noodles" — said to be eaten to cure hangovers
- **Components**: Wide rice noodles, Thai holy basil, chilies, garlic, meat/seafood, soy sauce
- **Characteristics**: Intensely spicy and aromatic

**Kuai Tiao Reua (ก๋วยเตี๋ยวเรือ)** — Boat Noodles
- **Origin**: Central Thailand, Bangkok canal culture
- **History**: Originally sold from boats on Bangkok's canals
- **Components**: Small portions of noodle soup with dark, rich broth (pork blood enriched), meat, meatballs
- **Characteristics**: Intensely flavored, small portions meant for multiple bowls

**Khao Piak Sen (ข้าวเปียกเส้น)** — Lao-Style Noodle Soup
- **Origin**: Isan/Lao tradition
- **Components**: Hand-rolled tapioca-rice noodles, pork or chicken broth, herbs
- **Characteristics**: Chewy, slightly sticky noodle texture

**Ba Mee Moo Daeng (บะหมี่หมูแดง)** — BBQ Pork Egg Noodles
- **Origin**: Thai-Chinese
- **Components**: Egg noodles, char siu-style red pork, wonton, broth or dry
- **Influences**: Cantonese char siu, Hong Kong noodle traditions

### SOUPS (ต้ม, Tom / ซุป, Sup)

**Tom Yum (ต้มยำ)** — Spicy Sour Soup
- **Origin**: Central Thailand
- **Evolution**: Ancient Thai tradition, refined over centuries
- **Key Types**:
  - Tom Yum Goong (กุ้ง): With shrimp — most famous
  - Tom Yum Kai (ไก่): With chicken
  - Tom Yum Talay (ทะเล): With mixed seafood
  - Tom Yum Nam Khon (น้ำข้น): Creamy version with evaporated milk/chili paste
  - Tom Yum Nam Sai (น้ำใส): Clear broth version
- **Components**: Galangal, lemongrass, kaffir lime leaves, fish sauce, lime juice, chilies, mushrooms
- **Significance**: UNESCO-nominated as Thai cultural heritage
- **Thai Contribution**: THE quintessential Thai soup; globally iconic

**Tom Kha (ต้มข่า)** — Galangal Coconut Soup
- **Origin**: Central Thailand, possibly Lao influences
- **Components**: Coconut milk, galangal, lemongrass, kaffir lime, chicken or shrimp, mushrooms
- **Etymology**: "Tom" = boiled; "Kha" = galangal
- **Characteristics**: Creamy, aromatic, milder than tom yum

**Kaeng Jued (แกงจืด)** — Clear Soup
- **Origin**: Central Thailand, Chinese influence
- **Components**: Clear broth, tofu, glass noodles, minced pork, vegetables
- **Function**: Palate cleanser, balance to spicy dishes
- **Influences**: Chinese clear soup traditions

**Tom Saap (ต้มแซบ)** — Isan Sour Soup
- **Origin**: Isan
- **Components**: Pork ribs or offal, galangal, lemongrass, chilies, lime, herbs
- **Characteristics**: Intensely spicy and sour, offal common

**Khao Tom (ข้าวต้ม)** — Rice Soup/Congee
- **Origin**: Chinese influence, deeply rooted in Thai breakfast
- **Components**: Rice in broth, pork/chicken/fish, ginger, cilantro
- **Function**: Breakfast staple, comfort food, hangover cure
- **Influences**: Chinese congee/jok traditions

**Pla Pao (ปลาเผา)** — Salt-Crusted Grilled Fish
- **Origin**: Isan/Central Thailand

**Kor Moo Yang (คอหมูย่าง)** — Grilled Pork Neck

**Sate (สะเต๊ะ)** — Satay
- **Origin**: Malay/Indonesian, Thai adaptation
- **Components**: Marinated meat skewers, peanut sauce, cucumber relish

### RICE DISHES (ข้าว, Khao)

**Khao Pad (ข้าวผัด)** — Fried Rice
- **Origin**: Thai-Chinese
- **Varieties**:
  - Khao Pad Gai: Chicken fried rice
  - Khao Pad Moo: Pork fried rice
  - Khao Pad Goong: Shrimp fried rice
  - Khao Pad Sapparot: Pineapple fried rice
  - Khao Pad Kapi: Shrimp paste fried rice
  - Khao Pad American: Thai-American fusion
- **Thai Contribution**: Fish sauce base, Thai accompaniments

**Khao Man Gai (ข้าวมันไก่)** — Chicken Rice
- **Origin**: Hainanese Chinese, Thai adaptation
- **Components**: Poached chicken, rice cooked in chicken fat/broth, dipping sauce
- **Sauce**: Distinctive Thai version with fermented soybeans, ginger, chilies, vinegar
- **Influences**: Hainanese chicken rice
- **Thai Contribution**: Unique sauce formula; accompaniments

**Khao Mok Gai (ข้าวหมกไก่)** — Thai Chicken Biryani
- **Origin**: Indian Muslim influence via Southern Thailand
- **Components**: Spiced rice, chicken, fried shallots, yogurt sauce
- **Influences**: Indian biryani
- **Thai Adaptation**: Distinct spice balance, accompaniments

**Khao Niao (ข้าวเหนียว)** — Sticky Rice
- **Origin**: Ancient, Northern and Isan staple
- **Cultural Role**: Primary starch in Isan and Northern Thailand
- **Preparation**: Steamed in bamboo baskets (กระติ๊บ, kratip)
- **Significance**: Essential with laab, som tam, kai yang, grilled meats

**Khao Kha Moo (ข้าวขาหมู)** — Braised Pork Leg Rice
- **Origin**: Thai-Chinese (Teochew)

- **Influences**: Chinese braised meat traditions
- **Thai Adaptation**: Specific accompaniments (pickled mustard greens, boiled egg, chili vinegar)

**Khao Na Pet (ข้าวหน้าเป็ด)** — Roast Duck Rice
- **Origin**: Thai-Chinese (Cantonese/Teochew)
- **Components**: Roast duck, red sauce, rice
- **Influences**: Chinese roast meats

### DESSERTS & SWEETS (ขนม, Khanom)

**Khao Niao Mamuang (ข้าวเหนียวมะม่วง)** — Mango Sticky Rice
- **Origin**: Central/Northern Thailand
- **Components**: Glutinous rice, coconut cream, ripe mango, mung beans
- **Significance**: Thailand's most famous dessert globally
- **Season**: Peak during mango season (April-June)
- **Thai Contribution**: Definitive version of this Southeast Asian concept

**Khanom Buang (ขนมเบื้อง)** — Thai Crispy Pancakes
- **Origin**: Ayutthaya period, possibly Vietnamese influence
- **Components**: Crispy crepe, coconut cream or egg yolk cream, foi thong
- **Varieties**: Sweet and savory versions

**Foi Thong (ฝอยทอง)** — Golden Threads
- **Origin**: Portuguese influence, 16th-17th century
- **Evolution**: Maria Guyomar de Pinha adapted Portuguese fios de ovos
- **Components**: Egg yolks drizzled into syrup
- **Historical Significance**: Symbol of Portuguese-Thai culinary exchange
- **Related Desserts**: Thong Yip (gold pinch), Thong Yod (gold drops), Sangkhaya (custard)

**Thong Yip, Thong Yod (ทองหยิบ, ทองหยอด)** — Golden Desserts
- **Origin**: Portuguese via Maria Guyomar de Pinha
- **Components**: Egg yolk-based, cooked in syrup
- **Significance**: Essential auspicious ceremony desserts

**Sangkhaya Fak Thong (สังขยาฟักทอง)** — Pumpkin Custard
- **Origin**: Central Thailand, possibly Portuguese influence
- **Components**: Coconut custard steamed inside pumpkin
- **Influences**: Portuguese egg custard traditions

**Khanom Krok (ขนมครก)** — Coconut Rice Pancakes
- **Origin**: Central Thailand
- **Components**: Rice flour, coconut milk, cooked in hemispherical molds
- **Variations**: Topped with corn, taro, scallions

**Luk Chup (ลูกชุบ)** — Mung Bean Sweets
- **Origin**: Portuguese-Thai, miniature fruit shapes
- **Components**: Mung bean paste, sugar, coconut, natural coloring, agar glaze
- **Significance**: Artistic Thai dessert tradition

**Tako (ตะโก้)** — Coconut Jelly
- **Origin**: Central Thailand
- **Components**: Pandan-flavored coconut layer over water chestnut or corn base
- **Served In**: Banana leaf cups or modern containers

**Tub Tim Krob (ทับทิมกรอบ)** — Red Rubies
- **Origin**: Central Thailand
- **Components**: Water chestnuts coated in tapioca, red coloring, coconut milk, ice
- **Name**: Pomegranate seeds appearance

**Khanom Tom (ขนมต้ม)** — Coconut Dumplings
- **Origin**: Central Thailand
- **Components**: Glutinous rice balls with palm sugar, coated in coconut

**Bua Loi (บัวลอย)** — Floating Lotus
- **Origin**: Chinese influence, Thai adaptation
- **Components**: Colored glutinous rice balls in warm coconut milk

### CONDIMENTS, PASTES & SAUCES (เครื่องปรุง, Khruang Prung)

**Nam Pla (น้ำปลา)** — Fish Sauce
- **Origin**: Ancient Southeast Asia, Thai production distinctive
- **Production**: Fermented fish (anchovies) with salt
- **Significance**: Foundation of Thai seasoning; "MSG of Thailand"
- **Regional Styles**: Rayong, Phuket known for quality

**Kapi (กะปิ)** — Shrimp Paste
- **Origin**: Ancient, shared with wider Southeast Asia
- **Production**: Fermented ground shrimp
- **Uses**: Curry pastes, fried rice, dipping sauces
- **Regional Variations**: Southern Thailand noted for intense kapi

**Nam Prik (น้ำพริก)** — Chili Dipping Sauces/Relishes
- **Origin**: Ancient Thai tradition, pre-chili versions existed with peppercorns
- **Major Varieties**:
  - Nam Prik Pao (น้ำพริกเผา): Roasted chili paste
  - Nam Prik Kapi (น้ำพริกกะปิ): Shrimp paste relish
  - Nam Prik Ong (น้ำพริกอ่อง): Northern tomato-pork dip
  - Nam Prik Num (น้ำพริกหนุ่ม): Northern green chili dip
  - Nam Prik Goong Siap (น้ำพริกกุ้งเสียบ): Dried shrimp relish
  - Nam Prik Pla Too (น้ำพริกปลาทู): Mackerel chili dip
- **Significance**: Central to Thai meals; consumed with rice and vegetables

**Nam Jim (น้ำจิ้ม)** — Dipping Sauces
- **Major Varieties**:
  - Nam Jim Gai (ไก่): Sweet chili sauce for chicken
  - Nam Jim Jaew (แจ่ว): Isan dipping sauce (roasted rice, fish sauce, lime, chilies)
  - Nam Jim Seafood: Spicy lime-garlic-chili for seafood
  - Nam Jim Satay: Peanut sauce

## FOREIGN INFLUENCES & THAI CONTRIBUTIONS

### Chinese Culinary Influences

**What China Contributed to Thai Cuisine**
- **Noodle Traditions**: Rice noodles, egg noodles, glass noodles, techniques
- **Wok Cooking**: Stir-fry (ผัด, phat) techniques and equipment
- **Soy Products**: Soy sauce, tofu, fermented soybeans
- **Roast Meats**: Char siu, roast duck, roast pork
- **Dim Sum Influence**: Dumplings, buns, steamed items
- **Rice Preparations**: Congee, fried rice techniques
- **Teochew Specific**: Braised dishes (khao kha moo), clear soups

**Thai Adaptations & Innovations**
- Fish sauce as primary seasoning over soy sauce in many dishes
- Addition of Thai aromatics (galangal, lemongrass, kaffir lime) to Chinese techniques
- Pad Thai: Chinese noodle technique with distinctly Thai sauce
- Integration of fresh herbs and Thai chilies
- Sweet-sour balance distinctive from Chinese versions

### Indian Culinary Influences

**What India Contributed to Thai Cuisine**
- **Curry Concept**: The foundational idea of spiced paste + liquid bases
- **Spice Framework**: Cumin, coriander, cardamom, turmeric, cinnamon
- **Cooking Techniques**: Long-simmering curry methods
- **Rice Dishes**: Biryani (→ khao mok), pilaf preparations
- **Breads**: Roti, Indian flatbreads via Muslim communities
- **Ghee Usage**: Clarified butter in some preparations
- **Vegetarian Traditions**: Via Buddhist and Hindu influences

**Thai Adaptations & Innovations**
- Fresh herbs (galangal, lemongrass, kaffir lime, basil) added to Indian spice concepts
- Coconut milk as primary curry base (shared with South India)
- Fish sauce replacing salt in curry seasonings
- Fresh chili pastes rather than dried spice powders
- Lighter, fresher flavors compared to Indian curries
- Balance of all taste elements (sour, sweet, salty, spicy)

### Portuguese Culinary Influences

**What Portugal Contributed to Thai Cuisine (16th-17th Centuries)**
- **CHILIES**: The single most transformative ingredient
- **Egg-Yolk Desserts**: Fios de ovos → foi thong, thong yip, thong yod
- **Custard Traditions**: Influenced sangkhaya development
- **New World Ingredients**: Tomatoes, peanuts, corn, papaya, pineapple
- **Sugar Refinement**: Improved sugar processing techniques
- **Baking Influences**: Early bread and pastry concepts

**Historical Context**
- Portuguese traders arrived in Ayutthaya early 16th century
- Maria Guyomar de Pinha (Japanese-Portuguese-Bengali) — central figure in dessert development
- Portuguese community in Ayutthaya influential in court cuisine

### Khmer & Mon Culinary Legacy

**Khmer Contributions**
- Ancient fermented fish traditions
- Certain curry and soup preparations

- Courtly cooking traditions (via Ayutthaya's Khmer heritage)

**Mon Contributions (Often Underacknowledged)**
- Early rice cultivation in Central Thailand
- Fish sauce and shrimp paste traditions
- Cooking techniques for river fish
- Mon communities maintain distinctive dishes

### Malay & Indonesian Influences

**What Malay/Indonesian Cuisines Contributed**
- **Satay**: Skewered meat with peanut sauce

- **Southern Thai Flavors**: Turmeric-heavy curries, more chili heat
- **Coconut Usage Patterns**: Extensive coconut milk and cream
- **Rendang-Style Preparations**: Dry curry influences in south
- **Nasi Goreng Influence**: Fried rice concepts

**Thai Adaptations**
- Distinctive peanut sauce formula for satay
- Thai aromatics integrated into Malay-origin dishes
- Fish sauce replacing Malay/Indonesian shrimp paste in many applications

### Lao Culinary Exchange

**Shared & Exchanged Traditions (Isan = Thai Lao)**

### The Fish Sauce Universe (น้ำปลา, Nam Pla)

**Production & Types**

## COOKING TECHNIQUES

### Phat (ผัด) — Stir-Frying
**The Foundational Thai Technique (Chinese Origin)**
1. Wok heated until smoking
2. Oil added, then aromatics
3. Proteins and vegetables in sequence
4. Sauces added at end
5. Quick, high heat, constant motion
6. Fresh herbs finished off heat

### Kaeng (แกง) — Curry Making
**The Thai Curry Method**
1. Curry paste fried in coconut cream until oil separates
2. Proteins added and sealed
3. Coconut milk added
4. Vegetables added by cooking time
5. Seasoning adjusted (fish sauce, palm sugar)
6. Fresh herbs (basil, kaffir lime) added last

### Tom (ต้ม) — Boiling/Soup Making
**Thai Soup Technique**
1. Aromatics (lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime) bruised and added to stock
2. Proteins and vegetables added
3. Seasoning (fish sauce, lime juice) added off heat
4. Fresh chilies and herbs added last
5. NOT a long-simmered stock — fresh flavors preserved

### Yang (ย่าง) — Grilling
**Thai Grilling Methods**
1. Charcoal (than) preferred for flavor
2. Marinades of fish sauce, garlic, coriander root, white pepper
3. Constant basting
4. Served with dipping sauces (jaew, nam jim)

### Yam (ยำ) / Thoke (ตำ) — Salad Making
**Thai Salad Methods**
- **Yam**: Dressed, tossed salads
- **Tam**: Pounded salads (mortar and pestle)
1. Mortar (ครก, khrok) and pestle (สาก, sak) for pounding
2. Dressing mixed while pounding
3. Ingredients added in sequence
4. Tossed to coat evenly
5. Served immediately

### Neung (นึ่ง) — Steaming
**Applications**
- Fish in lime (pla neung manao)
- Steamed curries (hor mok)
- Sticky rice
- Desserts (khanom)
