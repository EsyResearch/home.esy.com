## Quality Assurance Framework

### Fact-Checking Checklist
1. **Source Identification**: Are claims backed by credible sources?
2. **Date Accuracy**: Are dates, periods, and timelines correct?
3. **Attribution**: Are quotes and claims properly attributed?
4. **Context**: Is historical context adequately provided?
5. **Bias Detection**: Are interpretations balanced and evidence-based?
6. **Anachronism Check**: Are modern concepts inappropriately projected onto the past?
7. **Oversimplification**: Are complex events reduced without nuance?
8. **Myth vs. Reality**: Are popular myths distinguished from historical fact?
9. **Scholarly Consensus**: Does content align with mainstream historical scholarship?
10. **Cultural Sensitivity**: Are diverse perspectives and voices represented?

### Red Flags to Identify
- Absolute statements without qualification ("always," "never," "all")
- Unattributed quotes or dubious attributions
- Teleological narratives (history as inevitable progress)
- Presentism (judging past by contemporary values without context)
- Cherry-picked evidence supporting a predetermined narrative
- Conflation of correlation and causation
- Oversimplified cause-and-effect relationships
- Neglect of regional and cultural variations
- Speculation presented as fact
- Reliance on outdated or debunked scholarship

## Review Methodology

### Three-Tier Analysis

**Tier 1: Factual Accuracy (Critical)**
- Verify all specific claims (dates, names, places, events)
- Check chronological sequences and timelines
- Validate quotes and attributions
- Confirm archaeological/material evidence claims
- Cross-reference with authoritative sources

**Tier 2: Interpretive Soundness (Important)**
- Assess if interpretations reflect scholarly consensus
- Evaluate if alternative viewpoints are acknowledged
- Check if causation is appropriately qualified
- Verify cultural and social context accuracy
- Ensure appropriate use of historical terminology

**Tier 3: Narrative Quality (Enhancement)**
- Evaluate engagement and accessibility
- Assess logical flow and coherence
- Check if complexity is appropriately handled
- Verify audience-appropriate language
- Suggest improvements for clarity without sacrificing accuracy

### Citation Standards
- **Scholarly Content**: Full citations required (Chicago/Turabian preferred)
- **Popular Content**: Inline attributions and general source notes acceptable
- **Educational Content**: Mix of specific citations and general references
- **Digital Content**: Hyperlinks to authoritative sources when possible
- **Minimum Standard**: Every factual claim should be verifiable via credible source

## Authoritative Sources

### Tier 1 (Gold Standard)
- Peer-reviewed academic journals (e.g., *American Historical Review*, *Journal of World History*)
- University press monographs (Oxford, Cambridge, Yale, Princeton, etc.)
- Major archaeological reports and excavation publications
- Primary source collections from reputable archives
- Scholarly edited volumes with expert contributors

### Tier 2 (Highly Credible)
- Respected trade history publishers (e.g., Penguin, Norton, Basic Books)
- Museum publications and exhibition catalogues
- Educational institutions' research centers
- Government historical archives and databases
- Established encyclopedias (e.g., *Oxford Classical Dictionary*, *Encyclopedia Britannica*)

### Tier 3 (Use with Caution)
- Popular history books (verify against scholarly sources)
- Documentary films (confirm with academic sources)
- News articles about historical topics (check original research)
- Wikipedia (starting point only, verify all claims independently)
- Educational websites (evaluate credentials and citations)

### Avoid
- Blogs without academic credentials
- Social media posts as primary evidence
- Websites with clear ideological agendas
- Self-published works without peer review
- Sources lacking citations or methodology

## Standards for AI-Generated Historical Content

### Special Considerations
AI-generated historical content requires heightened scrutiny for:
- **Fabricated Sources**: Verify all cited sources actually exist
- **Composite Errors**: AI may combine elements from different historical periods
- **Plausible Falsehoods**: Claims that sound correct but lack evidence
- **Simplified Causation**: Overly neat cause-and-effect narratives
- **Temporal Confusion**: Mixing dates, periods, or sequences
- **Cultural Stereotypes**: Reinforcement of outdated historical stereotypes
- **Missing Nuance**: Reduction of complex debates to simple statements

### Verification Protocol for AI Content
1. Flag all specific factual claims for verification
2. Demand sources for any claim not commonly known
3. Cross-check dates, names, and places against multiple authoritative sources
4. Assess if narrative suspiciously lacks scholarly complexity
5. Identify where consensus claims may mask ongoing debates
6. Check for anachronistic language or concepts
7. Verify that cause-and-effect relationships are supported by scholarship
