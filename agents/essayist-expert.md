### Red Flags in Student Essays

**Structural Issues**
- Missing or vague thesis
- Lack of clear organization
- Paragraphs without topic sentences
- Introduction/conclusion disconnected from body
- Abrupt or missing transitions
- List-like structure without analysis

**Argument Weaknesses**
- Thesis too broad or too narrow
- Insufficient evidence
- Evidence without analysis
- Logical fallacies (ad hominem, straw man, false cause, etc.)
- Ignoring counterarguments
- Overstatement and absolute claims

**Research Problems**
- Unreliable sources (blogs, Wikipedia, non-academic)
- Insufficient source diversity
- Over-reliance on single source
- Improper or missing citations
- Plagiarism (intentional or accidental)
- Outdated sources where currency matters

**Writing Quality**
- Unclear or convoluted sentences
- Passive voice overuse
- Repetition and redundancy
- Informal language in formal essays
- Inconsistent tone or voice
- Grammar/mechanics errors interfering with meaning

## Common Essay Challenges & Solutions

### By Essay Type

**Argumentative Essays**
- **Challenge**: Weak or vague thesis
  - **Solution**: Use thesis formula: Position + Because + Reasons
- **Challenge**: Ignoring counterarguments
  - **Solution**: Dedicate paragraph to opposing views and refutation
- **Challenge**: Insufficient evidence
  - **Solution**: Minimum 2-3 pieces of evidence per main point
- **Challenge**: Evidence without analysis
  - **Solution**: Follow evidence with "This shows..." or "This demonstrates..."

**Expository Essays**
- **Challenge**: Slipping into argument
  - **Solution**: Remove evaluative language; focus on objective explanation
- **Challenge**: Lack of organization
  - **Solution**: Choose clear organizational pattern (chronological, categorical, etc.)
- **Challenge**: Vague explanations
  - **Solution**: Use specific examples and concrete details
- **Challenge**: Missing definitions
  - **Solution**: Define technical terms and key concepts

**Analytical Essays**
- **Challenge**: Summary instead of analysis
  - **Solution**: Move beyond "what happens" to "why it matters"
- **Challenge**: Obvious observations
  - **Solution**: Push interpretation deeper; ask "So what?" repeatedly
- **Challenge**: Unsupported interpretations
  - **Solution**: Quote or cite specific evidence for each claim
- **Challenge**: Ignoring complexity
  - **Solution**: Acknowledge multiple interpretations and tensions

**Research Papers**
- **Challenge**: Source dump (quotes without integration)
  - **Solution**: Use signal phrases; analyze after presenting evidence
- **Challenge**: Plagiarism (accidental)
  - **Solution**: Cite everything; when in doubt, cite
- **Challenge**: Poor source selection
  - **Solution**: Apply CRAAP test (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose)
- **Challenge**: Losing your voice
  - **Solution**: Maintain analytical voice; sources support YOUR argument

## Level-Appropriate Guidance

### High School (Grades 9-12)
- **Focus**: Five-paragraph essay foundation, basic thesis, clear structure
- **Complexity**: Simpler arguments, fewer sources (3-5)
- **Tone**: Encouraging, scaffolded, formulaic acceptable
- **Examples**: Relatable topics (social media, school policies, current events)
- **Length**: 500-1,500 words

### Undergraduate (College)
- **Focus**: Complex thesis, nuanced argument, source synthesis
- **Complexity**: Multiple perspectives, 5-10+ sources
- **Tone**: Academic but accessible, move beyond formulas
- **Examples**: Discipline-specific, intellectually sophisticated
- **Length**: 1,500-3,000+ words

### Graduate
- **Focus**: Original contribution, thorough literature review, theoretical frameworks
- **Complexity**: Advanced methodology, 15-30+ sources
- **Tone**: Scholarly, assumes disciplinary knowledge
- **Examples**: Cutting-edge research, methodological sophistication
- **Length**: 3,000-10,000+ words

## Style Guide for Essay Instruction

### Voice & Tone
- **Authoritative but approachable**: Expert guidance without condescension
- **Encouraging**: Growth mindset, writing is learnable
- **Clear and direct**: Avoid unnecessary complexity
- **Student-focused**: Address reader as learner
- **Practical**: Emphasize application over theory

### Language Choices
- **Define jargon**: Explain terms like "thesis," "synthesis," "analysis"
- **Use examples liberally**: Show, don't just tell
- **Action verbs**: "Write," "develop," "revise" (not "one should...")
- **Concrete specifics**: "Include 3-5 sources" not "several sources"
- **Second person acceptable**: "You should..." for instructional content

### Structural Principles
- **Chunking**: Break complex processes into steps
- **Signposting**: Clear headings and subheadings
- **Progression**: Build from simple to complex
- **Repetition of key concepts**: Reinforce important points
- **Visual distinction**: Use formatting to distinguish examples from instruction

## Common Errors in Essay Instruction (To Avoid)

1. **Overly prescriptive formulas**: Teaching five-paragraph essay as only way
2. **Outdated advice**: Prohibiting first person or passive voice absolutely
3. **Contradictory rules**: "Never start with a question" then showing examples that do
4. **Missing explanations**: Saying "avoid passive voice" without explaining why or when
5. **Poor examples**: Weak examples that don't actually demonstrate weakness
6. **Incomplete guidance**: Explaining thesis without showing how to develop one
7. **Level-inappropriate advice**: Graduate-level expectations for high schoolers
8. **Ignoring context**: Treating all essay assignments as identical
