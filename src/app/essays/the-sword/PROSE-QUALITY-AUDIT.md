## Transition Quality: STRONG

Transitions between sections, paragraphs, and ideas are organic, purposeful, and often elegant.

### Section-Level Transitions

**Bronze Age → Iron Age**:
> "Then the Bronze Age collapsed. Trade networks fractured. Tin became scarce. And in the ruins, a new material emerged that would transform the sword from an aristocratic luxury into something far more consequential."

Three short declaratives create urgency, then a longer sentence pivots to the new era. Both narrative and thematic. Perfect.

**European → Japanese tradition**:
> "While European smiths refined pattern-welding, a parallel tradition was reaching its peak on the other side of the world."

"While" signals parallel development without hierarchy. Specific and grounded.

### Paragraph-Level Transitions

**Material to meaning**:
> "From the beginning, the sword was two things at once: a weapon and a statement. To carry one was to declare your place in the hierarchy of violence and power."

First sentence establishes duality. Second unpacks the "statement" side. Logical, clear, elegant.

**Process to philosophy**:
> "The hamon was both functional and aesthetic: the hard edge held a razor sharpness while the soft spine absorbed shock without shattering. Form and function were inseparable. The blade's beauty was its engineering made visible."

Technical description → philosophical principle → aesthetic conclusion. Each sentence builds on the previous.

### Transition Techniques Used

- **Causal**: "When iron absorbs carbon during smelting or forging, it becomes steel"
- **Temporal**: "By the sixteenth century, the sword was splitting into two objects"
- **Contrastive**: "Early iron was not superior to good bronze. It was softer, more brittle, and harder to shape. But iron's advantage was availability."
- **Additive**: Sequential development through related ideas

## Sentence-Level Craft

### Rhythm and Variation

**Short sentences for emphasis**:
- "Iron ore is abundant."
- "Damascus steel was not made in Damascus. It was traded there."

**Medium sentences for clarity**:
- "A bronze sword was not a common soldier's tool."
- "Early iron was not superior to good bronze."

**Long sentences for complexity**:
- "When a skilled smith forged a wootz ingot into a blade and polished the surface, the carbide bands became visible as flowing, cloud-like patterns — the famous 'watered silk' of Damascus steel."

**Rhythmic patterns**:
- Three-part: "Trade networks fractured. Tin became scarce. And in the ruins, a new material emerged..."
- Parallel: "Not because of the damage it could inflict, but because of what it represented: mastery over materials, authority over others, identity in a world where identity was forged through skill and will."

### Precision and Economy

✅ "Bronze bends under its own weight" (not "has a tendency to bend due to its inherent material properties")  
✅ "The sword was two things at once" (not "The sword served dual purposes")  
✅ "Then the Bronze Age collapsed" (not "Then came the collapse of the Bronze Age civilization")

### Active Voice Dominance

Passive voice used sparingly and purposefully:
- Active (dominant): "Metalworkers discovered that copper alloyed with tin produced something harder"
- Passive (appropriate): "Approximately 170 blades have been recovered" (focus on blades)
