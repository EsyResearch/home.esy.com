# Glossary: Hip-Hop History

> 80 essential terms with historical context, etymology, and period/place notes

---

## Core Culture Terms

### B-boy / B-girl
**Definition**: A person who dances to breakbeats; a breaker.
**Etymology**: "B" stands for "break" — the instrumental break section of a record. DJ Kool Herc named them after the part of the song they danced to.
**Period**: Coined early 1970s, Bronx
**Usage Notes**: Original and preferred term over "breakdancer" (which came from media in 1980s)
**Source**: Chang (2005); Herc oral histories

### Beat
**Definition**: The rhythmic foundation of a song; the instrumental track that MCs rap over.
**Etymology**: From the drum pattern that provides the pulse.
**Period**: Universal Hip-Hop term
**Usage Notes**: "Making beats" = producing; "spitting over beats" = rapping

### Breakbeat
**Definition**: The instrumental break section of a funk, soul, or disco record, typically featuring drums.
**Etymology**: The "break" where the drums take over from melody.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: DJs would isolate and extend these sections. The foundation of Hip-Hop.
**Key Example**: The drum break from James Brown's "Funky Drummer"
**Source**: Rose (1994)

### Breaking / Breakdancing
**Definition**: The dance form of Hip-Hop, performed to breakbeats.
**Etymology**: "Breaking" comes from dancing during the "break" of a record.
**Period**: "Breaking" preferred by practitioners; "breakdancing" coined by media in 1984
**Usage Notes**: "Breakdancing" seen as outsider term; "breaking" is authentic
**Source**: "The Freshest Kids" (2002)

### Cipher
**Definition**: A circular gathering where MCs freestyle, or b-boys/b-girls take turns dancing.
**Etymology**: From Five Percenter term for the circle of knowledge.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Both lyrical ciphers and dance ciphers; central to communal Hip-Hop practice
**Source**: Chang (2005)

### Crate-digging
**Definition**: Searching through record store bins and collections for obscure vinyl to sample.
**Etymology**: From the literal crates that vinyl records are stored in.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Essential producer skill; finding rare breaks confers status
**Source**: Fernando (1994)

### Diss / Dis
**Definition**: A verbal attack or insult, especially in a rap.
**Etymology**: Short for "disrespect"
**Period**: 1980s–present
**Usage Notes**: Diss tracks are songs attacking another rapper
**Key Example**: "Roxanne's Revenge" (1984)

### DJ (Disc Jockey)
**Definition**: The person who plays and manipulates records, creating beats for MCs.
**Etymology**: "Disc jockey" = one who rides the disc.
**Period**: Term predates Hip-Hop, but Hip-Hop DJs transformed the role
**Usage Notes**: In Hip-Hop, the DJ is a musician, not just a song selector
**Source**: Rose (1994)

### Freestyle
**Definition**: Improvised rapping, either over a beat or a cappella; also can mean a written verse delivered without reading.
**Etymology**: From "free" + "style" — unscripted expression.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Original meaning was improvised; now sometimes means pre-written but delivered fresh
**Source**: Fernando (1994)

### Hip-Hop
**Definition**: The cultural movement comprising DJing, MCing, breaking, graffiti, and knowledge.
**Etymology**: Debated. Credited to either Love Bug Starski or Cowboy (Keith Wiggins). The term mimics the rhythmic hop of the culture.
**Period**: Coined late 1970s, Bronx
**Usage Notes**: "Hip-Hop" is the culture; "rap" is one element (MCing)
**Source**: Chang (2005)

### MC (Master of Ceremonies / Microphone Controller)
**Definition**: The person who raps; the vocalist in Hip-Hop.
**Etymology**: Originally from "Master of Ceremonies" (host), but transformed to "Microphone Controller" or just "MC"
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: "MC" often preferred over "rapper" by practitioners
**Source**: Herc oral histories

### Rap
**Definition**: The rhythmic vocal delivery of rhymes over a beat; the verbal element of Hip-Hop.
**Etymology**: From 1960s slang "rap" = to talk, converse (as in "rap session")
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: "Rap" is the sound; "Hip-Hop" is the culture

### Sample / Sampling
**Definition**: Taking a portion of an existing recording and reusing it in a new composition.
**Etymology**: From audio production term for a recorded sound excerpt.
**Period**: Became central to Hip-Hop in 1980s with digital samplers
**Usage Notes**: Foundation of Hip-Hop production; source of copyright lawsuits
**Source**: Rose (1994)

## DJ Techniques

### Backspin
**Definition**: Manually spinning a record backward to repeat a section.
**Etymology**: Literally spinning the disc back.
**Period**: Developed mid-1970s, Bronx
**Usage Notes**: Pioneered by Grandmaster Flash as part of "quick mix theory"
**Source**: "Scratch" (2001)

### Beat Juggling
**Definition**: Manipulating two copies of the same record to create new rhythms.
**Etymology**: Metaphor for "juggling" beats between turntables.
**Period**: Developed late 1970s–1980s
**Usage Notes**: Advanced DJ technique; competitive element of DMC battles
**Source**: "Scratch" (2001)

### Cutting
**Definition**: Rapidly switching between two turntables during playback, creating rhythmic patterns.
**Etymology**: From the action of "cutting" between channels on a mixer.
**Period**: Developed 1970s, Bronx
**Usage Notes**: Pioneered by Grandmaster Flash
**Source**: Flash oral histories

### Merry-Go-Round
**Definition**: DJ Kool Herc's technique of using two copies of a record to extend the break section indefinitely.
**Etymology**: Herc's term, referring to the circular, continuous nature of the technique.
**Period**: Early 1970s, Bronx
**Usage Notes**: The foundational DJ technique of Hip-Hop
**Source**: Chang (2005); Herc oral histories

### Needle Drop
**Definition**: Dropping the needle precisely onto a specific spot on a record.
**Etymology**: Literally dropping the turntable needle.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Pioneered by Grand Wizard Theodore; requires skill
**Source**: "Scratch" (2001)

### Punch Phrasing
**Definition**: Using the mixer crossfader to punch in a phrase from one turntable while the other plays.
**Etymology**: "Punching" the sound in.
**Period**: Developed mid-1970s, Bronx
**Usage Notes**: Part of Grandmaster Flash's "quick mix theory"
**Source**: Flash oral histories

### Scratching / Scratch
**Definition**: Moving a vinyl record back and forth under the needle to create rhythmic sound.
**Etymology**: From the scratching sound produced.
**Period**: Invented 1975 by Grand Wizard Theodore (accidental discovery)
**Usage Notes**: Foundational DJ technique; Theodore claims invention
**Source**: "Scratch" (2001); Chang (2005)

### Two Turntables and a Microphone
**Definition**: The basic setup of a Hip-Hop DJ: two turntables, a mixer, and a mic.
**Etymology**: Literal description of equipment.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Made famous by Beck's song "Where It's At" (1996)

## Graffiti Terms

### Beef
**Definition**: A feud or conflict between writers (or later, between rappers).
**Etymology**: Slang for conflict/problem.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Can result in "going over" (painting over) each other's work

### Bite / Biting
**Definition**: To copy someone else's style without credit; plagiarism.
**Etymology**: Like "biting" off someone else's work.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Serious offense in graffiti and Hip-Hop culture

### Bombing
**Definition**: Prolific tagging in public spaces; aggressive graffiti saturation.
**Etymology**: Like "bombing" an area with your name.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Goal is quantity and visibility

### Buff / Buffing
**Definition**: The removal or painting over of graffiti by authorities.
**Etymology**: From the scrubbing/buffing action used to remove tags.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Writers expect to be "buffed"; transience is part of the art

### Burner
**Definition**: An elaborate, colorful, large-scale graffiti piece.
**Etymology**: Because it "burns" (stands out, is hot/impressive).
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Requires time and skill; different from quick tags

### Cap / Can Control
**Definition**: The spray can tip that determines paint width; the skill of controlling the spray.
**Etymology**: From the literal cap on a spray can.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Different caps for different effects; "cap control" is fundamental skill

### Crew
**Definition**: A collective of graffiti writers or b-boys/b-girls who work/perform together.
**Etymology**: Common slang for group.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Examples: Rock Steady Crew, Fab 5
**Source**: Cooper & Chalfant (1984)

### Getting Up
**Definition**: Successfully executing graffiti in visible locations; building one's reputation.
**Etymology**: Rising in status/visibility.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: "I was getting up all over the 5 line."

### Going Over
**Definition**: Painting over another writer's work.
**Etymology**: Literally painting "over" existing graffiti.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Can be disrespectful (starting beef) or acceptable (if writer is less respected)

### King / Queen
**Definition**: A writer with the most visible work on a particular line or area.
**Etymology**: Royalty metaphor for dominance.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: "King of the IND lines"

### Piece
**Definition**: A graffiti painting, as opposed to a simple tag; short for "masterpiece."
**Etymology**: Shortened from "masterpiece."
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Larger and more elaborate than tags

### Tag
**Definition**: A stylized signature or name; the simplest form of graffiti.
**Etymology**: Like a name tag or identifier.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Every writer starts with tags before moving to pieces
**Source**: Cooper & Chalfant (1984)

### Throw-up / Throwie
**Definition**: A quickly executed bubble letter graffiti piece, usually two colors.
**Etymology**: Thrown up quickly.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Middle ground between tag and piece; emphasizes speed

### Top-to-Bottom (T2B)
**Definition**: A graffiti piece covering a subway car from top to bottom.
**Etymology**: Literal description.
**Period**: 1970s–1980s (subway era)
**Usage Notes**: Required substantial planning and time

### Whole Car
**Definition**: A graffiti piece covering an entire subway car.
**Etymology**: Literal description.
**Period**: 1970s–1980s (subway era)
**Usage Notes**: The pinnacle of subway graffiti achievement

### Writer
**Definition**: A person who creates graffiti; preferred term over "graffiti artist" in the culture.
**Etymology**: From writing one's name.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Community members call themselves "writers"
**Source**: "Style Wars" (1983)

### Yard
**Definition**: A subway train storage area where writers would paint.
**Etymology**: Railroad terminology for storage yards.
**Period**: 1970s–1980s (subway era)
**Usage Notes**: Writers would sneak into yards at night to paint uninterrupted

## MC/Rap Terms

### Bars
**Definition**: Lines of rap; measures of lyrics.
**Etymology**: From musical terminology (measures = bars).
**Period**: 1990s–present
**Usage Notes**: "He spit 16 bars" = he rapped 16 lines; "bars" also means skill/content

### Battle Rap
**Definition**: Competitive rapping where MCs exchange disses to determine who is superior.
**Etymology**: Rap as combat metaphor.
**Period**: 1970s–present
**Usage Notes**: Foundation of MC culture; continues in organized leagues today
**Source**: Chang (2005)

### Bite (MC context)
**Definition**: To steal another rapper's lyrics or style.
**Etymology**: Same as graffiti usage.
**Period**: 1980s–present
**Usage Notes**: Accusation of "biting" damages reputation

### Boom Bap
**Definition**: A style of Hip-Hop production characterized by hard-hitting drums and soul/jazz samples.
**Etymology**: Onomatopoeia for the kick and snare drum sounds.
**Period**: Defined late 1980s–1990s New York
**Usage Notes**: Associated with East Coast golden age; KRS-One, Nas, Wu-Tang
**Source**: Chang (2005)

### Chopper / Chopping
**Definition**: An extremely fast-paced rap delivery; the technique of rapping at high speed.
**Etymology**: "Chopping" syllables rapidly.
**Period**: Emerged 1990s, especially Midwest (Tech N9ne, Bone Thugs-n-Harmony)
**Usage Notes**: Regional style that became a subcategory

### Dope
**Definition**: Excellent, impressive, high quality.
**Etymology**: Originally drug slang repurposed to mean anything excellent.
**Period**: 1980s–present
**Usage Notes**: "That beat is dope"

### Flow
**Definition**: The rhythmic pattern and cadence of a rapper's delivery.
**Etymology**: How the words "flow" over the beat.
**Period**: 1980s–present
**Usage Notes**: Rakim credited with revolutionizing flow
**Source**: Rose (1994)

### Fresh
**Definition**: Cool, new, stylish, excellent.
**Etymology**: From general slang meaning new/good.
**Period**: 1970s–1980s; quintessential old school term
**Usage Notes**: "Fresh" epitomized early Hip-Hop aesthetics

### Ghostwriter / Ghostwriting
**Definition**: A person who writes lyrics for another artist without credit.
**Etymology**: Like literary ghostwriting.
**Period**: Controversial throughout Hip-Hop history
**Usage Notes**: Accusation of using ghostwriters damages authenticity claims
**Key Example**: Big Daddy Kane wrote for Biz Markie

### Ill
**Definition**: Excellent, impressive, sick (in a good way).
**Etymology**: Subverting "ill" from negative to positive meaning.
**Period**: 1980s–present
**Usage Notes**: "Illmatic" (Nas's album title)

### Lyricist
**Definition**: An MC who prioritizes complex, skilled wordplay over simple flows.
**Etymology**: Standard meaning with elevated Hip-Hop connotation.
**Period**: Became distinguishing term in late 1980s
**Usage Notes**: Rakim, Nas, Jay-Z considered "lyricists"

### OG (Original Gangster)
**Definition**: A respected veteran; someone who has been in the game from early on.
**Etymology**: Gang terminology that entered Hip-Hop.
**Period**: 1990s–present
**Usage Notes**: Ice-T's album "O.G. Original Gangster" (1991)

### Spit / Spitting
**Definition**: To rap; to deliver lyrics.
**Etymology**: From the physical action of speaking forcefully.
**Period**: 1990s–present
**Usage Notes**: "He spit fire" = he rapped excellently

### Wack
**Definition**: Bad, poor quality, weak.
**Etymology**: Uncertain; possibly from "wacky" or drug slang.
**Period**: 1980s–present
**Usage Notes**: "That beat is wack"

### Word / Word Up
**Definition**: An affirmation; "I agree," "that's true," "exactly."
**Etymology**: Affirming that what was said is "the word" (truth).
**Period**: 1980s–present
**Usage Notes**: "Word to your mother" (Vanilla Ice appropriated this)

## Cultural/Philosophy Terms

### Five Percenters / Five Percent Nation
**Definition**: A belief system (Nation of Gods and Earths) that influenced Hip-Hop philosophy.
**Etymology**: Belief that 5% of humanity knows and teaches truth.
**Period**: Founded 1964; influenced Hip-Hop from 1970s
**Usage Notes**: Influenced Rakim, Wu-Tang, Poor Righteous Teachers
**Source**: Chang (2005)

### Four Elements (+ Knowledge)
**Definition**: The pillars of Hip-Hop culture: DJing, MCing, Breaking, Graffiti (+ Knowledge).
**Etymology**: Systematized by Afrika Bambaataa and Zulu Nation.
**Period**: Codified 1970s
**Usage Notes**: Knowledge added as fifth element; represents consciousness/philosophy
**Source**: Chang (2005)

### Keep It Real
**Definition**: Maintain authenticity; stay true to one's origins and community.
**Etymology**: Standard slang with Hip-Hop emphasis on authenticity.
**Period**: 1990s–present
**Usage Notes**: Central to Hip-Hop authenticity debates

### Props
**Definition**: Respect, credit, acknowledgment.
**Etymology**: Short for "proper respect."
**Period**: 1980s–present
**Usage Notes**: "Give him props" = give him credit/respect

### Rep / Reppin'
**Definition**: To represent one's neighborhood, crew, or community.
**Etymology**: Short for "represent."
**Period**: 1990s–present
**Usage Notes**: Geographic pride central to Hip-Hop identity

### Street Cred / Credibility
**Definition**: Authenticity and respect earned from real-life experience, especially from difficult circumstances.
**Etymology**: Credibility on the streets.
**Period**: 1990s–present
**Usage Notes**: Essential to Hip-Hop authenticity claims

### Underground
**Definition**: Hip-Hop that exists outside the mainstream commercial structure.
**Etymology**: Hidden from mainstream view.
**Period**: Became meaningful distinction in 1990s
**Usage Notes**: Often associated with greater authenticity

## Technology Terms

### 808 (Roland TR-808)
**Definition**: A drum machine whose bass and kick sounds became foundational to Hip-Hop.
**Etymology**: From Roland's product number.
**Period**: Released 1980; became Hip-Hop staple by mid-1980s
**Usage Notes**: Afrika Bambaataa's "Planet Rock" (1982); defines Southern Hip-Hop bass
**Source**: Rose (1994)

### MPC (Akai MPC)
**Definition**: A sampling drum machine used to create beats.
**Etymology**: Music Production Center (Akai's product line).
**Period**: MPC60 released 1988; central to 1990s production
**Usage Notes**: Used by J Dilla, Dr. Dre, and most major producers

### SP-1200 (E-MU SP-1200)
**Definition**: A sampler/drum machine with distinctive gritty sound.
**Etymology**: E-MU's product number.
**Period**: Released 1987; defined late 1980s–early 1990s sound
**Usage Notes**: Pete Rock, DJ Premier famous for SP-1200 beats

### Technics 1200
**Definition**: The standard turntable for Hip-Hop DJs.
**Etymology**: Technics (Panasonic) product line.
**Period**: Released 1972; became DJ standard
**Usage Notes**: Direct drive motor made scratching possible
**Source**: "Scratch" (2001)

## Regional Slang

### Crunk (Southern)
**Definition**: A high-energy style of Southern Hip-Hop; also means excited/energized.
**Etymology**: Possibly "crazy" + "drunk" or "chronic" + "drunk."
**Period**: 1990s–2000s Atlanta/Memphis
**Usage Notes**: Lil Jon associated with the term

### G-Funk (West Coast)
**Definition**: A subgenre of Hip-Hop characterized by slow funk grooves and synthesizers.
**Etymology**: "G" for gangsta + funk.
**Period**: Early 1990s Los Angeles
**Usage Notes**: Dr. Dre's "The Chronic" (1992) defined the sound
**Source**: Charnas (2010)

### Grime (UK)
**Definition**: A British genre combining Hip-Hop, UK garage, and dancehall.
**Etymology**: From the "grimy" (dark, raw) sound.
**Period**: Early 2000s London
**Usage Notes**: Dizzee Rascal, Wiley pioneers; distinct from American Hip-Hop

### Trap (Southern)
**Definition**: A subgenre characterized by hi-hats, 808 bass, and drug-dealing themes.
**Etymology**: From "trap house" (drug dealing location).
**Period**: 2000s–present Atlanta
**Usage Notes**: T.I., Gucci Mane, Young Jeezy pioneers; now dominant commercial style

## Era-Specific Terms

### New School (1983–1987)
**Definition**: The second generation of Hip-Hop, characterized by harder drums and fewer disco elements.
**Etymology**: Opposed to "old school."
**Period**: Run-DMC, LL Cool J era
**Usage Notes**: Transition from party rap to aggressive style

### Old School (1973–1983)
**Definition**: The original era of Hip-Hop before widespread recording.
**Etymology**: The "old" original style.
**Period**: Kool Herc, Grandmaster Flash era
**Usage Notes**: Characterized by party-focused lyrics, disco breaks

### Golden Age (1987–1994)
**Definition**: The era considered Hip-Hop's creative peak.
**Etymology**: Common historical term for high-achievement periods.
**Period**: Eric B. & Rakim through Illmatic
**Usage Notes**: Diversity of styles; lyrical innovation; conscious and gangsta coexisting
**Source**: Chang (2005)
