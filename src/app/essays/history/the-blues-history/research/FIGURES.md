## The Blues: America's Haunted Foundation
### Key Historical Figures (35 Documented)

## SECTION 1: PRE-RECORDING ERA PIONEERS (Before 1920)

### 1. Henry Sloan (c. 1870s - Unknown)
**Role:** Earliest documented Delta blues influence
**Active Period:** 1890s-1900s
**Location:** Dockery Plantation, Mississippi
**Significance:** Taught Charley Patton; considered to play "an unusual style" that is now recognized as an early form of blues
**Documentation Level:** Oral history only; no recordings exist
**Quote:** None documented
**Photography:** No known photographs

---

### 2. W.C. Handy (November 16, 1873 - March 28, 1958)
**Role:** Composer, bandleader, "Father of the Blues"
**Birth/Death:** Florence, Alabama / New York City
**Key Achievement:** Published first commercial blues compositions; codified 12-bar blues form
**Major Works:** "Memphis Blues" (1912), "St. Louis Blues" (1914)
**Historic Moment:** Encountered blues c. 1903 at Tutwiler, MS train station - heard a man playing guitar with a knife

**Quote:** "The primitive Southern Negro, as he sang, was sure to bear down on the third and seventh tone of the scale, slurring between major and minor. Whether in the cotton field of the Delta or on the levee up St. Louis way, it was always the same."

**Key Facts:**
- Son of former slaves; father was a preacher
- "Memphis Blues" was first blues song published by a major publisher
- "St. Louis Blues" became one of the most recorded songs in history
- Memphis statue erected in Handy Park (1960)
- U.S. commemorative stamp issued 1969

**Photography Sources:** Library of Congress, Memphis archives (multiple portraits available)

## SECTION 2: DELTA BLUES FOUNDERS (1900s-1930s)

### 3. Charley Patton (c. April 1891 - April 28, 1934)
**Role:** "Father of the Delta Blues"
**Birth/Death:** Edwards, MS / Indianola, MS
**Primary Location:** Dockery Plantation (from 1897)
**Recording Period:** 1929-1934

**Quote:** [From his gravestone] "The Voice of the Delta - The Foremost Performer of Early Mississippi Blues Whose Songs Became Cornerstones of American Music"

**Key Facts:**
- Mixed heritage: Black, White, possibly Cherokee or Choctaw
- Learned from Henry Sloan at Dockery Plantation
- Mentored Son House, Howlin' Wolf, Robert Johnson, Willie Brown
- Known for showmanship: played guitar behind head, between legs (decades before Hendrix)
- Voice reportedly could carry 500 yards without amplification
- "Pony Blues" (1929) added to National Recording Registry (2006)
- Inducted into Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (2021, Early Influence)

**Physical Description:** About 5'5" tall, gravelly voice

**Photography Sources:** Only 2-3 known photographs exist; available through blues archives

---

### 4. Son House (March 21, 1902 - October 19, 1988)
**Full Name:** Edward James "Son" House Jr.
**Role:** Delta blues singer, guitarist, preacher
**Birth/Death:** Riverton, MS / Detroit, MI
**Recording Period:** 1930, 1941-1942, 1965-1970s

**Quote:** [On the conflict between church and blues] "I was fiercely torn between the sacred teachings of the church and the secular lure of the blues life."

**Key Facts:**
- Ordained preacher by age 15
- Turned to blues at age 25 after years of denouncing secular music
- Served time at Parchman Farm (1928-1929) for shooting a man
- Recorded for Paramount (1930) and Library of Congress (1941-42)
- Direct influence on Robert Johnson and Muddy Waters
- "Rediscovered" in Rochester, NY in 1964 working at a train station
- Recorded "Father of Folk Blues" album (1965)
- Inducted into Blues Hall of Fame (1980)

**Photography Sources:** Multiple photographs from 1960s folk revival; Library of Congress

---

### 5. Robert Johnson (May 8, 1911 - August 16, 1938)
**Role:** Delta blues singer, guitarist, legend
**Birth/Death:** Hazlehurst, MS / Greenwood, MS
**Recording Period:** 1936-1937 (only 2 sessions)

**Quote:** "I went to the crossroads, fell down on my knees..."

**Key Facts:**
- Recorded only 29 songs, yet considered one of most influential blues musicians ever
- Studied Charley Patton and Son House
- Subject of "crossroads legend" (sold soul to devil for musical ability)
- Died at age 27 under mysterious circumstances (possibly poisoned)
- First inductees to Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (1986)
- *King of the Delta Blues Singers* released 1961, sparked blues revival
- Eric Clapton called him "the most important blues musician who ever lived"

**The Crossroads Myth:** Legend originated from stories about Tommy Johnson, not Robert Johnson; conflated over time. No evidence Johnson himself ever claimed supernatural abilities.

**Photography Sources:** Only 2-3 confirmed photographs exist

---

### 6. Skip James (June 9, 1902 - October 3, 1969)
**Full Name:** Nehemiah Curtis "Skip" James
**Role:** Delta blues singer, guitarist, pianist
**Birth/Death:** Bentonia, MS / Philadelphia, PA
**Style:** Bentonia School blues (distinctive minor-key tuning)

**Quote:** "I'd rather be the devil, than to be that woman's man" - from "Devil Got My Woman"

**Key Facts:**
- Known for otherworldly falsetto voice and haunting guitar work
- Pioneered open D-minor tuning
- Recorded for Paramount (1931); records sold poorly during Depression
- "Devil Got My Woman" (1931) influenced Robert Johnson's "Hell Hound on My Trail"
- Rediscovered in 1964 in Tunica, MS hospital
- Newport Folk Festival performance (1964) launched comeback
- Cream recorded his "I'm So Glad" - provided only significant royalties of his career

**Photography Sources:** 1960s folk revival photographs

---

### 7. Tommy Johnson (c. 1896 - November 1, 1956)
**Role:** Delta blues singer, guitarist
**Birth/Death:** Terry, MS / Crystal Springs, MS
**Significance:** Source of the original "crossroads" legend (later attributed to Robert Johnson)

**Quote:** [As reported by his brother Ledell] He claimed he sold his soul to the devil at a crossroads at midnight in exchange for guitar mastery.

**Key Facts:**
- No relation to Robert Johnson
- Recorded for Victor (1928-1929)
- Known songs: "Canned Heat Blues," "Big Road Blues"
- The band Canned Heat named themselves after his song

---

### 8. Willie Brown (August 6, 1900 - December 30, 1952)
**Role:** Delta blues guitarist, associate of Charley Patton
**Birth/Death:** Clarksdale, MS / Tunica, MS
**Significance:** Mentor to Robert Johnson; mentioned in "Cross Road Blues"

**Key Facts:**
- Partner of Charley Patton from c. 1918
- Recorded with Patton for Paramount (1930)
- Library of Congress recordings (1941-42) with Son House

## SECTION 3: THE BLUES QUEENS (1920s-1940s)

### 9. Mamie Smith (May 26, 1891 - September 16, 1946)
**Role:** First Black artist to record a blues song commercially
**Birth/Death:** Cincinnati, OH / New York City
**Title:** "Queen of the Blues"

**Quote:** Her recording of "Crazy Blues" was described as "a catalyst" that "essentially created an industry for blues songs recorded by women."

**Key Facts:**
- "Crazy Blues" (1920) sold over 1 million copies in first year
- Recording supervised by Ralph Peer at Okeh Records
- Opened door for all subsequent "race records"
- No relation to Bessie Smith

**Photography Sources:** Multiple portraits available

---

### 10. Ma Rainey (April 26, 1886 - December 22, 1939)
**Full Name:** Gertrude "Ma" Rainey (nee Pridgett)
**Role:** "Mother of the Blues"
**Birth/Death:** Columbus, GA / Columbus, GA
**Recording Period:** 1923-1928 (Paramount Records)

**Quote:** Rainey claimed she "created the term 'blues'" when asked what kind of song she was singing.

**Key Facts:**
- Parents were minstrel performers; began performing as teenager
- Married William "Pa" Rainey (1904); toured as "Rainey and Rainey, Assassinators of the Blues"
- Recorded nearly 100 songs for Paramount
- "See See Rider Blues" (1924) with Louis Armstrong - added to National Recording Registry (2004)
- Mentored Bessie Smith
- Known for dramatic stage presence, gold teeth, sequined gowns
- Subject of Angela Davis's feminist analysis in *Blues Legacies and Black Feminism*
- Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductee (1990)

**Photography Sources:** Multiple portraits; theatrical photographs

---

### 11. Bessie Smith (April 15, 1892 - September 26, 1937)
**Role:** "Empress of the Blues"
**Birth/Death:** Chattanooga, TN / Clarksdale, MS (car accident)
**Recording Period:** 1923-1933 (Columbia Records)

**Quote:** "I ain't good-lookin', but I'm somebody's angel child."

**Key Facts:**
- Orphaned young; survived by street performing with siblings
- Met Ma Rainey c. 1912; toured with her troupe
- Most popular female blues singer of the 1930s
- Columbia Records' highest-paid Black artist
- Made Columbia "millions of dollars" but was "never paid royalties" (she could not read)
- "Down-Hearted Blues" (1923) sold 780,000 copies in six months
- Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductee (1989)
- Died in car accident; controversy surrounds whether she was denied care at white hospital

**Photography Sources:** Extensive archive from recording and theatrical career

---

### 12. Ida Cox (February 26, 1888/1896 - November 10, 1967)
**Role:** "The Uncrowned Queen of the Blues"
**Birth/Death:** Toccoa, GA / Knoxville, TN
**Recording Period:** 1923-1929, 1961 (Paramount, Riverside)

**Quote:** Described the highlights of her career as "appearing with Jelly Roll Morton at the 81 Theater in Atlanta, with King Oliver at the Grand Theater in Chicago, with the Count Basie band in Carnegie Hall."

**Key Facts:**
- Left home at early age for Southern tent show circuit
- Organized and managed her own touring troupe, "Raisin' Cain" (ran for a decade)
- One of few Black women to own/manage business in 1920s-30s
- One of few female blues singers to write her own songs
- Appeared at John Hammond's "From Spirituals to Swing" at Carnegie Hall (1939)
- Recorded 78 titles for Paramount (1923-1929)
- Rediscovered 1959; recorded final album with Coleman Hawkins (1961)
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee (1989)

**Photography Sources:** Period theatrical portraits

---

### 13. Alberta Hunter (April 1, 1895 - October 17, 1984)
**Role:** Blues and jazz singer, songwriter, nurse
**Birth/Death:** Memphis, TN / New York City
**Recording Period:** 1921-1940, 1977-1984

**Quote:** On her remarkable comeback: "I'm having the time of my life!"

**Key Facts:**
- Left home at age 11 for Chicago; worked in boardinghouse
- Began singing in brothels at age 14
- Performed with King Oliver's band at Dreamland Cafe (1917)
- Co-wrote "Down-Hearted Blues" - recorded by Bessie Smith (1923)
- Starred in London production of *Show Boat* (1928)
- Led first Black USO unit to China-Burma-India theater (WWII)
- Became a nurse (1955-1977) after mother's death
- "Rediscovered" at age 82 in 1977
- Performed at The Cookery until shortly before death at 89
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee (2011)

**Photography Sources:** Extensive archive spanning 7 decades

---

### 14. Memphis Minnie (June 3, 1897 - August 6, 1973)
**Full Name:** Lizzie Douglas
**Role:** Blues guitarist, vocalist, songwriter
**Birth/Death:** Tunica County, MS / Memphis, TN
**Recording Period:** 1929-1954 (approximately 200 songs)

**Quote:** Big Bill Broonzy, after losing a cutting contest to her: "[She] can pick a guitar and sing as good as any man I've ever heard."

**Key Facts:**
- Received first guitar at age 8
- Ran away to Beale Street at age 13
- One of first blues artists to use electric guitar (preceded Muddy Waters by a year)
- Beat Big Bill Broonzy in legendary 1933 cutting contest
- Key songs: "When the Levee Breaks" (later covered by Led Zeppelin), "Me and My Chauffeur Blues," "Bumble Bee"
- Three marriages, all to musicians (Casey Bill Weldon, Joe McCoy, Little Son Joe)
- Ranked with Ma Rainey, Bessie Smith, Big Mama Thornton as most influential female blues artists
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee (1980, first class)

**Photography Sources:** Performance photographs, period portraits

---

### 15. Big Mama Thornton (December 11, 1926 - July 25, 1984)
**Full Name:** Willie Mae "Big Mama" Thornton
**Role:** Blues/R&B singer, songwriter
**Birth/Death:** Ariton, AL / Los Angeles, CA

**Quote:** On "Hound Dog" earnings: "That song sold over 2 million records. I got one check for $500 and never saw another."

**Key Facts:**
- First to record "Hound Dog" (1952) - #1 R&B for 7 weeks
- Song was written for her by Leiber and Stoller in 15 minutes
- Elvis Presley's 1956 version used different (sanitized) lyrics
- Also wrote "Ball and Chain" - later made famous by Janis Joplin
- Grammy Hall of Fame inductee for "Hound Dog" (2013)
- National Recording Registry (2016)
- Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductee (2025, Early Influence)

**Photography Sources:** Performance photographs, R&B era publicity

## SECTION 4: TEXAS & PIEDMONT BLUES

### 16. Blind Lemon Jefferson (September 24, 1893 - December 19, 1929)
**Role:** "Father of the Texas Blues"
**Birth/Death:** Near Coutchman, TX / Chicago, IL
**Recording Period:** 1926-1929 (approximately 100 tracks)

**Key Facts:**
- Born blind; exact cause unknown
- First commercially successful male self-accompanied country blues recording artist
- Discovered by Paramount talent scout (1925)
- "Long Lonesome Blues" (1926) sold over 100,000 copies
- Influenced Lead Belly, Lightnin' Hopkins, T-Bone Walker
- Recorded approximately 100 tracks for Paramount
- Died under mysterious circumstances in Chicago (possibly exposure during snowstorm)
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee (1980)

**Photography Sources:** Only 1-2 confirmed photographs exist

---

### 17. Lead Belly (January 20, 1888 - December 6, 1949)
**Full Name:** Huddie William Ledbetter
**Role:** 12-string guitarist, songwriter
**Birth/Death:** Mooringsport, LA / New York City

**Quote:** "If it hadn't been for the Lomaxes, I'd have died in Angola." [Paraphrased]

**Key Facts:**
- Discovered at Angola Prison by John and Alan Lomax (1933)
- Served time in Louisiana and Texas prisons for murder and assault
- Recorded for Library of Congress extensively
- Recordings released in 6-volume series by Rounder Records
- Key songs: "Goodnight Irene," "Midnight Special," "Rock Island Line"
- Influenced folk and rock musicians including Bob Dylan
- Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductee (1988)

**Photography Sources:** Extensive Lomax collection photographs

---

### 18. Blind Blake (c. 1896 - c. December 1, 1934)
**Full Name:** Arthur Blake (possibly Arthur Phelps)
**Role:** Piedmont blues guitarist
**Birth/Death:** Unknown (possibly Jacksonville, FL) / Unknown (possibly Milwaukee)
**Recording Period:** 1926-1932

**Key Facts:**
- Master of ragtime-influenced Piedmont fingerpicking style
- Recorded over 80 tracks for Paramount
- Influenced Blind Boy Fuller, Rev. Gary Davis, many others
- Birth and death details remain disputed
- Nickname: "Blind Arthur"

---

### 19. Blind Boy Fuller (July 10, 1904 - February 13, 1941)
**Full Name:** Fulton Allen
**Role:** Piedmont blues guitarist, singer
**Birth/Death:** Wadesboro, NC / Durham, NC
**Recording Period:** 1935-1940

**Key Facts:**
- Recorded 135 sides between 1935-1940
- Played similar role in Piedmont blues that Robert Johnson did in Delta blues
- Influenced by Blind Blake (via recordings) and Rev. Gary Davis (in person)
- Brownie McGhee briefly recorded as "Blind Boy Fuller No. 2" after Fuller's death
- Influenced Rolling Stones, Eric Clapton, many rock artists

---

## SECTION 5: CHICAGO ELECTRIC BLUES ERA (1940s-1960s)

### 20. Muddy Waters (April 4, 1913 - April 30, 1983)
**Full Name:** McKinley Morganfield
**Role:** "Father of Modern Chicago Blues"
**Birth/Death:** Rolling Fork, MS / Westmont, IL

**Quote:** "When I went into the clubs, the first thing I wanted was an amplifier. Couldn't nobody hear you with an acoustic."

**Key Facts:**
- Grew up on Stovall Plantation near Clarksdale
- Recorded by Alan Lomax for Library of Congress (1941)
- Moved to Chicago (1943); bought first electric guitar (1944)
- Signed to Chess Records; first releases (1948)
- Band included Little Walter, Jimmy Rogers, Otis Spann, Willie Dixon
- Classic songs: "Hoochie Coochie Man," "I Just Want to Make Love to You," "Mannish Boy"
- Rolling Stones named themselves after his song
- Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductee (1987)
- 6 Grammy Awards including Lifetime Achievement

**Photography Sources:** Extensive archive from Chicago era

---

### 21. Howlin' Wolf (June 10, 1910 - January 10, 1976)
**Full Name:** Chester Arthur Burnett
**Role:** Chicago blues singer, guitarist, harmonica player
**Birth/Death:** White Station, MS / Hines, IL

**Quote:** "I couldn't do no yodelin', so I turned to howlin'. And it's done me just fine."

**Key Facts:**
- 6'3" tall, nearly 300 pounds
- Learned guitar from Charley Patton; harmonica from Sonny Boy Williamson II
- Hosted radio show on KWEM, West Memphis (1948)
- Moved to Chicago and signed to Chess (1952)
- Key songs: "Smokestack Lightnin'," "Spoonful," "Killing Floor," "Back Door Man"
- Guitarist Hubert Sumlin was essential collaborator
- Rivaled only Muddy Waters in Chicago blues popularity
- Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductee (1991)
- Rolling Stone ranked #54 on "100 Greatest Artists"

**Photography Sources:** Extensive Chess Records archive

---

### 22. Little Walter (May 1, 1930 - February 15, 1968)
**Full Name:** Marion Walter Jacobs
**Role:** Blues harmonica player, singer
**Birth/Death:** Marksville, LA / Chicago, IL

**Key Facts:**
- Left home at 13; played on street corners throughout South
- Arrived Chicago 1947; played Maxwell Street
- Revolutionized harmonica by cupping microphone and using amplifier distortion
- Member of Muddy Waters' band (1948-1952)
- "Juke" (1952) - only harmonica instrumental to reach #1 on Billboard R&B
- 14 top-ten R&B hits (1952-1958)
- Died at 37 (coronary thrombosis after street fight)
- Only artist inducted into both Blues Hall of Fame (1980) and Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (2008)

---

### 23. Willie Dixon (July 1, 1915 - January 29, 1992)
**Role:** Songwriter, producer, bassist, "Poet Laureate of the Blues"
**Birth/Death:** Vicksburg, MS / Burbank, CA

**Quote:** "The blues are the roots and the other musics are the fruits."

**Key Facts:**
- Staff songwriter/producer at Chess Records (1950-1965)
- Wrote over 500 songs
- Key songs: "Hoochie Coochie Man," "I Just Want to Make Love to You," "Little Red Rooster," "Spoonful," "Back Door Man," "Wang Dang Doodle"
- Songs covered by Rolling Stones, Cream, Led Zeppelin, Yardbirds, Jeff Beck
- Founded Blues Heaven Foundation to help exploited artists recover royalties
- Blues Hall of Fame (1980); Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (1994)
- Rolling Stone: #51 on "100 Greatest Songwriters"

---

### 24. B.B. King (September 16, 1925 - May 14, 2015)
**Full Name:** Riley B. King
**Role:** "King of the Blues"
**Birth/Death:** Itta Bena, MS / Las Vegas, NV
**Guitar:** "Lucille"

**Quote:** "The blues are three L's - living, loving, and hopefully laughing."

**Key Facts:**
- Raised in Mississippi Delta; gospel influence from church
- Cousin of Bukka White
- Moved to Memphis (1946); DJ at WDIA - nicknamed "Blues Boy" (shortened to B.B.)
- "Three O'Clock Blues" #1 R&B (1951-52)
- *Live at the Regal* (1964) considered one of greatest live blues albums
- "The Thrill Is Gone" (1969) - signature song
- 15 Grammy Awards including Lifetime Achievement
- Presidential Medal of Freedom; Kennedy Center Honors
- Blues Hall of Fame (1984); Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (1987)

---

### 25. John Lee Hooker (August 17, 1917 - June 21, 2001)
**Role:** "King of the Boogie"
**Birth/Death:** Coahoma County, MS / Los Altos, CA

**Quote:** "I don't play a lot of fancy guitar. I don't want to play it. The simpler the better for me."

**Key Facts:**
- Learned guitar from stepfather William Moore ("country boogie" style)
- Moved to Detroit (1943); worked at Ford River Rouge Plant
- "Boogie Chillen'" (1948) - best-selling race record of 1949
- Unique rhythmic style made it difficult to use backing musicians
- Played guitar and stomped on wooden pallet for early recordings
- "Boom Boom" used in *The Blues Brothers* film
- *The Healer* (1989) featured Carlos Santana, Bonnie Raitt
- Two songs in Rock Hall's "500 Songs That Shaped Rock and Roll"
- Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award (2000)
- Blues Hall of Fame (1980); Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (1991)

---

### 26. Bukka White (November 12, c. 1904 - February 26, 1977)
**Full Name:** Booker T. Washington White
**Role:** Delta blues guitarist, singer
**Birth/Death:** Houston, MS / Memphis, TN

**Key Facts:**
- Named after Booker T. Washington
- First cousin to B.B. King's mother
- Served time at Parchman Farm (1937-1940) for shooting
- "Shake 'Em On Down" became hit while imprisoned
- 1940 recording session considered pinnacle of blues recording
- "Parchman Farm Blues" and "Fixin' to Die Blues" are classics
- Rediscovered in 1960s; Bob Dylan recorded his songs
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee (1990)

## SECTION 6: INDUSTRY FIGURES

### 27. Ralph Peer (May 22, 1892 - January 19, 1960)
**Role:** Talent scout, recording engineer, record producer
**Significance:** Supervised first commercial blues recording; coined "race records"

**Key Facts:**
- Supervised Mamie Smith's "Crazy Blues" (1920) for Okeh Records
- Introduced Okeh's "race records" series (1921)
- Pioneered field recording: took equipment to Atlanta (1923), then throughout South
- Later recorded country music (Bristol Sessions, 1927)
- Recorded Memphis Jug Band, Tommy Johnson, Blind Willie McTell, Memphis Minnie, and many more
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee

---

### 28. Leonard Chess (March 12, 1917 - October 16, 1969)
**Full Name:** Lejzor Czyz (Polish immigrant)
**Role:** Co-founder, Chess Records
**Significance:** Built label that defined Chicago electric blues and early rock and roll

**Key Facts:**
- Polish Jewish immigrant
- Founded Chess Records with brother Phil (1950)
- Signed Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, Little Walter, Willie Dixon, Chuck Berry, Bo Diddley, Etta James
- 2120 S. Michigan Ave. became legendary recording address
- Rolling Stones recorded at Chess during 1964 U.S. tour

---

### 29. Lester Melrose (December 14, 1891 - April 12, 1968)
**Role:** Blues producer, talent scout
**Significance:** Monopolized pre-WWII Chicago blues recording

**Key Facts:**
- Created "Melrose Sound" / "Bluebird Beat" - full band arrangements anticipating R&B
- Worked for RCA Victor/Bluebird, Columbia/Okeh simultaneously
- Recorded Big Bill Broonzy, Sonny Boy Williamson I, Memphis Minnie, Roosevelt Sykes, Bukka White
- Owned copyright to over 3,000 songs (standard exploitative practice of era)
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee

---

### 30. John Lomax (September 23, 1867 - January 26, 1948)
**Role:** Folklorist, musicologist, field collector

**Key Facts:**
- Recorded over 10,000 songs for Library of Congress Archive of American Folk Song
- Discovered Lead Belly at Angola Prison (1933)
- Pioneered prison recording expeditions
- Co-author with Alan Lomax of *Negro Folk Songs As Sung by Lead Belly* (1936)
- Subject of ethical critique regarding exploitation and methodology
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee (with Alan)

---

### 31. Alan Lomax (January 31, 1915 - July 19, 2002)
**Role:** Folklorist, ethnomusicologist, field collector

**Quote:** "The main point of my activity was... to put sound technology at the disposal of The Folk, to bring channels of communication to all sorts of artists and areas."

**Key Facts:**
- Assistant in Charge of Archive of American Folk Song (1936-1942)
- Recorded Muddy Waters at Stovall Plantation (1941)
- Coined term "cultural equity"
- Wrote *The Land Where the Blues Began* (1993)
- Linked blues origins to debt peonage, segregation, forced labor
- Over 500,000 pages of papers now at Library of Congress
- Subject of ethical debate: preserved irreplaceable heritage but questions about power dynamics with imprisoned/impoverished artists
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee (with John)

---

## SECTION 7: BRITISH BLUES REVIVAL

### 32. John Mayall (November 29, 1933 - July 22, 2024)
**Role:** "Godfather of British Blues"
**Significance:** Bluesbreakers band served as training ground for British rock stars

**Key Facts:**
- John Mayall's Bluesbreakers alumni include:
  - Eric Clapton (later Cream)
  - Jack Bruce (later Cream)
  - Peter Green, John McVie, Mick Fleetwood (later Fleetwood Mac)
  - Mick Taylor (later Rolling Stones)
- *Blues Breakers with Eric Clapton* (1966) pivotal album in British blues
- Encouraged Clapton to sing, Green to write songs

---

### 33. Eric Clapton (b. March 30, 1945)
**Role:** Guitarist, singer, key figure in British blues revival

**Quote:** [On Robert Johnson] "The most important blues musician who ever lived."

**Key Facts:**
- Left Yardbirds due to commercial direction
- Joined Mayall's Bluesbreakers (1965-66)
- *Blues Breakers with Eric Clapton* pioneered overdriven amplifier tone
- Cream covered Skip James, Robert Johnson, Willie Dixon
- Introduced Delta blues to rock audience
- 18 Grammy Awards

## SECTION 8: ADDITIONAL NOTABLE FIGURES

### 34. Sonny Boy Williamson II (December 5, 1912 - May 25, 1965)
**Full Name:** Aleck "Rice" Miller
**Role:** Harmonica player, singer
**Birth/Death:** Glendora, MS / Helena, AR

**Key Facts:**
- Adopted name of original Sonny Boy Williamson after his death
- "King Biscuit Time" radio show (KFFA Helena) - oldest blues radio program
- Recorded for Chess and Trumpet Records
- Toured Europe with British blues bands
- Influenced Little Walter, Howlin' Wolf (taught Wolf harmonica)

---

### 35. Big Bill Broonzy (June 26, 1903 - August 15, 1958)
**Full Name:** Lee Conley Bradley
**Role:** Blues singer, guitarist
**Birth/Death:** Jefferson County, AR / Chicago, IL

**Key Facts:**
- Transition figure: country blues to Chicago blues to folk revival
- Recorded over 300 songs
- Toured Europe extensively in 1950s
- Informally adopted Little Walter
- Autobiography: *Big Bill Blues* (1955)
- Blues Hall of Fame inductee (1980)

## NOTES ON DOCUMENTATION GAPS

1. **Birth dates** for many early artists are approximate or disputed
2. **Pre-recording era** figures (before 1920) documented primarily through oral history
3. **Death circumstances** often unclear for early artists
4. **Financial records** documenting exploitation rarely survived
5. **Real names** sometimes unknown (Blind Blake's full identity still debated)
