## TIER 1: PRIMARY SOURCES (Institutional Archives & Collections)

### Library of Congress Collections

1. **American Folklife Center - Blues Collection**
   - URL: https://guides.loc.gov/folklife-blues/digital-collections
   - Description: Multiple collections relating to blues including sound recordings, photographs, and manuscripts
   - Access: Digital collections available online; some materials by appointment
   - Key Holdings: Field recordings, performer documentation, historical photographs

2. **The Alan Lomax Collection**
   - URL: https://www.loc.gov/collections/alan-lomax-manuscripts/about-this-collection/
   - Description: Over 500,000 pages of personal papers and office files from 1932-1990s
   - Key Materials: Field notes, correspondence, documentation of field projects, writing including *Land Where the Blues Began*
   - Access Date: December 2025

3. **The Lomax Collection (Sound Recordings)**
   - URL: https://www.loc.gov/collections/lomax/about-this-collection/
   - Description: Field recordings made by John and Alan Lomax from 1933-1942
   - Key Holdings: Recordings from Southern prisons, rural communities, and urban centers
   - Notable: First recordings of Lead Belly at Angola State Penitentiary

4. **Mississippi John Hurt Collection**
   - URL: https://www.loc.gov/item/afc1939007_afs02484a/
   - Description: Field recordings, studio recordings, interviews, and photographs (1963-1965)
   - Provenance: Tom Hoskins collection

5. **Fort Valley Music Festivals Collection (1938-1943)**
   - Description: Approximately 100 sound recordings of blues and gospel
   - Collectors: John Wesley Work III, Lewis Jones, Willis Laurence James
   - Geographic Scope: Tennessee, Alabama, Mississippi

6. **Negro Blues and Hollers Collection**
   - URL: https://www.loc.gov/item/2016655241/
   - Description: Recordings of Son House, Willie Brown, Fiddlin' Joe Martin, David "Honeyboy" Edwards
   - Location: Recorded in Clarksdale, Lula, Robinsonville, MS and Arkansas (1941-1942)
   - Collectors: Alan Lomax, John W. Work, Lewis Jones (Library of Congress/Fisk University joint study)

7. **National Recording Registry**
   - Charley Patton's "Pony Blues" (1929) - Added 2006
   - Ma Rainey's "See See Rider Blues" (1924) - Added 2004
   - Big Mama Thornton's "Hound Dog" (1952) - Added 2016

### Smithsonian Institution Collections

8. **Smithsonian Folkways Recordings**
   - URL: https://folkways.si.edu/genre/blues
   - Description: Blues recordings from Moses Asch's Folkways Records (founded 1948)
   - Key Collection: Harry Smith's *Anthology of American Folk Music*
   - Notable Artists: Lead Belly, Big Bill Broonzy, Charley Patton, Blind Lemon Jefferson
   - Policy: All recordings remain in print in perpetuity per Asch's stipulation

9. **The Robert "Mack" McCormick Archive**
   - URL: https://americanhistory.si.edu/press/releases/blues-archive
   - Location: National Museum of American History
   - Contents: 590 reels of sound recordings, 165 boxes of materials (70+ cubic feet)
   - Coverage: Research on hundreds of early- and mid-century blues artists
   - Key Artists Documented: Bernice Edwards, Robert Johnson, Lightnin' Hopkins, Mance Lipscomb

10. **National Museum of African American History and Culture**
    - URL: https://nmaahc.si.edu/
    - Relevant Holdings: Musical Crossroads exhibition, Alberta Hunter documentation
    - Context: Blues within broader African American cultural history

### Regional Archives & Museums

11. **Delta Blues Museum**
    - URL: https://www.deltabluesmuseum.org/
    - Location: Clarksdale, Mississippi (Yazoo and Mississippi Valley Passenger Depot)
    - Founded: January 31, 1979 (oldest blues museum in the world)
    - Key Holdings: Muddy Waters cabin from Stovall Plantation, "Muddywood" guitars, instruments, recordings, photographs
    - Artists Featured: Son House, Mississippi John Hurt, B.B. King, Big Mama Thornton, Ike Turner

12. **Mississippi Blues Trail**
    - URL: https://msbluestrail.org/
    - Description: 200+ historical markers documenting blues history throughout Mississippi
    - Key Sites: Dockery Plantation, Crossroads markers, birthplace markers
    - Managing Entity: Mississippi Blues Commission

13. **Mississippi Department of Archives and History**
    - URL: https://www.mshistorynow.mdah.ms.gov/
    - Relevant Articles: "Mississippi Blues" series
    - Coverage: State-level documentation of blues origins and development

### Historical Newspapers

14. **The Chicago Defender**
    - URL: https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chicago-Defender
    - Founded: 1905 by Robert S. Abbott
    - Significance: Primary African American newspaper during Great Migration
    - Coverage: Blues artist profiles, performance announcements, migration advocacy
    - Distribution: Circulated via Pullman porters throughout the South

## TIER 2: ACADEMIC & SCHOLARLY SOURCES

### Books - Primary Scholarly Works

15. **Ted Gioia - *Delta Blues: The Life and Times of the Mississippi Masters Who Revolutionized American Music* (2008)**
    - Publisher: W.W. Norton
    - Recognition: New York Times 100 Notable Books of the Year
    - Methodology: Personal fieldwork, archival research, musician interviews
    - Key Subjects: W.C. Handy, Charley Patton, Son House, Skip James, Robert Johnson, Muddy Waters, John Lee Hooker, Howlin' Wolf, B.B. King

16. **Angela Y. Davis - *Blues Legacies and Black Feminism: Gertrude "Ma" Rainey, Bessie Smith, and Billie Holiday* (1998)**
    - URL: https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/37351/blues-legacies-and-black-feminism-by-angela-y-davis/
    - Publisher: Pantheon/Vintage
    - Significance: First complete transcription of all extant Ma Rainey and Bessie Smith lyrics
    - Focus: Working-class African American feminist consciousness in blues
    - Blues Foundation Recognition: Blues Hall of Fame (Literature)

17. **Elijah Wald - *Escaping the Delta: Robert Johnson and the Invention of the Blues* (2004)**
    - URL: https://www.elijahwald.com/rjohnson.html
    - Publisher: Amistad/HarperCollins
    - Key Argument: Contemporary perception of Johnson as "primitive" artist is a construct of white promoters/archivists
    - Methodology: African American press research, musician recollections, original research
    - Recognition: Grammy Award-winning author

18. **Alan Lomax - *The Land Where the Blues Began* (1993)**
    - Publisher: Pantheon
    - Significance: Memoir linking blues origins to debt peonage, segregation, and forced labor
    - Based on: Decades of field research for Library of Congress

19. **Tom Graves - *Crossroads: The Life and Afterlife of Blues Legend Robert Johnson***
    - Recognition: Keeping the Blues Alive Award in Literature (2010)
    - Publisher: DaCapo Press
    - Status: Considered definitive Robert Johnson biography

20. **Daniel Beaumont - *Preachin' the Blues: The Life and Times of Son House* (2011)**
    - URL: https://nes.princeton.edu/publications/preachin-blues-life-and-times-son-house
    - Publisher: Oxford University Press

21. **David Evans (Editor) - *Ramblin' on My Mind: New Perspectives on the Blues* (2008)**
    - Publisher: University of Illinois Press
    - Scope: Collection of scholarly essays on blues history and interpretation

22. **Paul and Beth Garon - *Woman with Guitar: Memphis Minnie's Blues* (1992)**
    - Publisher: Da Capo Press
    - Focus: Comprehensive biography of Memphis Minnie

## SOURCE EVALUATION NOTES

### Tier 1 Reliability
- Library of Congress and Smithsonian sources represent the highest level of archival reliability
- Field recordings from these institutions constitute primary evidence of early blues performance
- Some documentation from the Lomax expeditions has been subject to scholarly critique regarding methodology and power dynamics

### Tier 2 Considerations
- Ted Gioia and Angela Davis works represent current scholarly consensus
- Elijah Wald's work importantly challenges earlier romanticized narratives
- Encyclopedia sources provide verified baseline facts but may lack depth

### Source Gaps Noted
- Limited documentation exists for pre-1920 blues performers
- Oral histories were often collected decades after events described
- Many early recording sessions have incomplete documentation
- Financial records of exploitation often did not survive
