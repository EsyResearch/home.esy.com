## PART I: ROOTS AND PRECURSORS (Pre-1900)

### 1619 - First Africans Arrive in English North America
**Event:** Twenty Africans arrive at Point Comfort, Virginia
**Significance:** Beginning of African presence in English colonies; African musical traditions will evolve over centuries into foundation for blues
**Location:** Virginia

---

### 1700s-1800s - Development of African American Musical Forms
**Event:** Evolution of work songs, field hollers, spirituals
**Significance:** Enslaved Africans create musical forms to "lighten the load," communicate, and express emotion
**Key Elements:**
- Call-and-response patterns
- Blue notes (flattened thirds and sevenths)
- Polyrhythmic structures
- Field hollers (solo vocal expressions)
**Location:** Throughout American South

---

### 1865 - End of Civil War / Emancipation
**Event:** Thirteenth Amendment abolishes slavery
**Significance:** Creates conditions for blues emergence: freed people face poverty, sharecropping, and new forms of oppression
**Context:** Many scholars place blues origins in the 1870s-1890s, the period following emancipation
**Location:** United States

---

### 1867 - Peonage Outlawed
**Event:** Congress outlaws debt peonage
**Significance:** Law largely ignored in South; debt servitude continues informing blues themes of economic despair
**Reality:** System not fully eradicated until 1940s
**Location:** Southern United States

---

### 1870s-1890s - Probable Blues Emergence
**Event:** Blues emerges as recognizable musical form
**Significance:** Coincides with transition from slavery to sharecropping; blues articulates post-emancipation Black experience
**Documentation:** Exact origins "lost to time" - oral tradition precedes documentation
**Note:** Blues, jazz, and ragtime all emerged in approximately the same period

---

### 1895 - Dockery Plantation Founded
**Event:** Will Dockery establishes 10,000-acre cotton plantation
**Significance:** Becomes "birthplace of the blues" according to B.B. King; home to Charley Patton, Henry Sloan, and training ground for Delta blues
**Location:** Ruleville, Mississippi (Sunflower County)

---

### 1897 - Patton Family Arrives at Dockery
**Event:** Charley Patton's family moves to Dockery Plantation
**Significance:** Young Patton learns from Henry Sloan; will become "Father of Delta Blues"
**Location:** Dockery Plantation, Mississippi

---

## PART II: DOCUMENTATION AND PUBLICATION ERA (1900-1920)

### 1901-1902 - First Academic Documentation
**Event:** Harvard archaeologist Charles Peabody documents blues lyrics
**Significance:** First scholarly notation of blues form during excavation near Clarksdale
**Location:** Clarksdale, Mississippi

---

### c. 1903 - W.C. Handy's Tutwiler Encounter
**Event:** Handy hears unknown man playing guitar with knife, singing blues at train station
**Significance:** Handy's later recollection becomes key document of blues as distinct style
**Quote:** Man sang about "the Southern" crossing "the Yellow Dog" - railroad lines
**Location:** Tutwiler, Mississippi

---

### 1908 - W.C. Handy Moves to Memphis
**Event:** Handy relocates to Memphis, begins leading bands on Beale Street
**Significance:** Establishes Memphis as blues center; Handy becomes composer/publisher rather than folk performer
**Location:** Memphis, Tennessee

---

### 1912 - First Blues Compositions Published
**Event:** W.C. Handy publishes "Memphis Blues" (originally "Mr. Crump")
**Significance:** First commercially published blues composition; establishes 12-bar blues format
**Additional:** Antonio Maggio published "I Got the Blues" earlier in 1908, but Handy's work established standard form
**Location:** Memphis, Tennessee

---

### 1914 - "St. Louis Blues" Published
**Event:** W.C. Handy publishes "St. Louis Blues"
**Significance:** Becomes one of most recorded songs in history; establishes Handy's "Father of the Blues" title
**Milestone:** First blues sheet music sold by white store (Gerber's Department Store, Memphis)
**Location:** Memphis, Tennessee

---

## PART III: THE RECORDING ERA BEGINS (1920-1930)

### February 1920 - First Recording by Black Blues Singer
**Event:** Mamie Smith records "That Thing Called Love" and "You Can't Keep a Good Man Down" for Okeh
**Significance:** First commercial recording by Black blues singer
**Producer:** Ralph Peer
**Location:** New York City

---

### August 1920 - "Crazy Blues" Releases
**Event:** Mamie Smith's "Crazy Blues" released
**Significance:** Sells 75,000 copies in first month, over 1 million in first year; launches race records industry
**Impact:** Proves commercial viability of Black artists recording for Black audiences
**Producer:** Ralph Peer
**Location:** New York City

---

### 1921 - Race Records Industry Expands
**Event:** Columbia Records enters race records market; Black Swan Records founded
**Significance:** First major label competition for Okeh; first Black-owned record label (Harry Pace)
**Context:** Black Swan issued about 500 race records annually
**Location:** New York City

---

### 1922 - Term "Race Records" Coined
**Event:** Okeh Records officially coins "race records" term
**Significance:** Not pejorative at time; reflected African American pride and solidarity
**Context:** Paramount Records also enters race records market
**Location:** United States

---

### 1923 - Classic Blues Era Begins
**Event:** Ma Rainey, Bessie Smith begin recording careers
**Details:**
- Ma Rainey signs with Paramount Records
- Bessie Smith signs with Columbia Records
- Both record throughout 1920s
**Significance:** Peak era of female-dominated blues recording
**Location:** Various (Chicago, New York)

---

### 1923 - First Field Recording Trip
**Event:** Ralph Peer takes recording equipment to Atlanta
**Significance:** Pioneered field recording; allowed capturing regional styles outside studio
**Method:** Mobile recording unit in hotels, ballrooms, warehouses
**Location:** Atlanta, Georgia (first stop)

---

### 1924 - Ma Rainey Records "See See Rider Blues"
**Event:** Ma Rainey records classic with Louis Armstrong on cornet
**Significance:** Added to National Recording Registry (2004)
**Label:** Paramount Records
**Location:** Recording session

---

### 1926 - Blind Lemon Jefferson's Breakthrough
**Event:** "Long Lonesome Blues" achieves major sales
**Significance:** First commercially successful male self-accompanied country blues artist
**Impact:** Transformed industry previously dominated by female Classic Blues singers
**Label:** Paramount Records
**Location:** Chicago (recording)

---

### 1929 - Charley Patton's First Recordings
**Event:** Patton records for Paramount including "Pony Blues"
**Significance:** Documents Delta blues style; "Pony Blues" later added to National Recording Registry (2006)
**Sessions:** Richmond, Indiana (Paramount studio)
**Location:** Richmond, Indiana

---

## PART IV: DEPRESSION, DOCUMENTATION, AND TRANSITION (1930-1945)

### 1930 - Son House Records for Paramount
**Event:** Son House, with Charley Patton and Willie Brown, records for Paramount
**Significance:** Documents key Delta blues figures before Depression devastates recording industry
**Location:** Grafton, Wisconsin (Paramount studio)

---

### 1931 - Skip James Recording Session
**Event:** Skip James records for Paramount including "Devil Got My Woman"
**Significance:** Captures unique Bentonia style; poor sales during Depression
**Impact:** James disappears into obscurity until 1964
**Location:** Grafton, Wisconsin

---

### 1933 - Great Depression Devastates Blues Industry
**Event:** Race records sales collapse from $100 million (1927) to $6 million
**Response:** Record prices drop from 75 cents to 35 cents
**Context:** Many artists stop recording; some labels fold
**Location:** United States

---

### 1933 - Lomax Discovers Lead Belly
**Event:** John and Alan Lomax record Huddie Ledbetter at Angola Prison
**Significance:** Beginning of systematic folk/blues documentation for Library of Congress
**Context:** First recording expedition under Library of Congress auspices
**Location:** Angola State Penitentiary, Louisiana

---

### 1936-1937 - Robert Johnson Recording Sessions
**Event:** Robert Johnson records 29 songs in San Antonio and Dallas
**Significance:** Only recordings by most mythologized blues figure
**Sessions:** November 1936 (San Antonio); June 1937 (Dallas)
**Label:** Vocalion/ARC
**Location:** Texas

---

### 1938 - Robert Johnson Dies
**Event:** Johnson dies near Greenwood, Mississippi, age 27
**Cause:** Disputed - poisoning commonly believed; death certificate says syphilis
**Context:** John Hammond had been searching for him for Carnegie Hall concert
**Location:** Near Greenwood, Mississippi

---

### 1939 - "From Spirituals to Swing" Concert
**Event:** John Hammond produces landmark concert at Carnegie Hall
**Significance:** Brought authentic Black music to white mainstream audience
**Performers:** Big Joe Turner, Ida Cox, Mitchell's Christian Singers
**Context:** Planned to feature Robert Johnson (learned he had died)
**Location:** Carnegie Hall, New York City

---

### 1940 - Bukka White Recording Session
**Event:** White records for Lester Melrose including "Parchman Farm Blues"
**Significance:** Considered pinnacle of blues recording; documented prison experience
**Context:** Recorded shortly after release from Parchman Farm
**Location:** Chicago, Illinois

---

### 1941 - Alan Lomax Records Muddy Waters
**Event:** Lomax records McKinley Morganfield at Stovall Plantation
**Significance:** Documents Waters before Chicago; pivotal in his career development
**Context:** Library of Congress / Fisk University joint project
**Location:** Stovall Plantation, near Clarksdale, Mississippi

---

### 1943 - Muddy Waters Moves to Chicago
**Event:** Waters relocates from Mississippi to Chicago
**Significance:** Catalyst for Chicago electric blues; Waters will become "Father of Modern Chicago Blues"
**Context:** Part of Great Migration
**Location:** Chicago, Illinois

---

## PART V: ELECTRIC ERA AND CHICAGO DOMINANCE (1945-1960)

### 1944 - Muddy Waters Electrifies
**Event:** Waters buys first electric guitar
**Reason:** "Couldn't nobody hear you with an acoustic" in Chicago clubs
**Significance:** Beginning of electric blues transformation
**Location:** Chicago, Illinois

---

### 1948 - Chess Records Era Begins
**Event:** Muddy Waters records "I Can't Be Satisfied" and "I Feel Like Going Home"
**Significance:** Waters' first hit; establishes Chess Records as Chicago blues center
**Label:** Aristocrat Records (renamed Chess in 1950)
**Location:** Chicago, Illinois

---

### 1950 - Chess Records Founded
**Event:** Leonard and Phil Chess officially rename label Chess Records
**Significance:** Becomes most important blues/early rock label
**Address:** 2120 S. Michigan Avenue, Chicago
**Location:** Chicago, Illinois

---

### 1951-1952 - B.B. King's Breakthrough
**Event:** "Three O'Clock Blues" reaches #1 on R&B charts
**Significance:** Establishes B.B. King as major blues star
**Label:** RPM Records (Modern subsidiary)
**Duration:** #1 for five weeks
**Location:** Memphis / Recording in various locations

---

### 1952 - Little Walter's "Juke" / Big Mama Thornton's "Hound Dog"
**Events:**
- Little Walter records "Juke" - only harmonica instrumental to reach #1 R&B
- Big Mama Thornton records "Hound Dog" - #1 R&B for 7 weeks
**Significance:** Peak of early 1950s blues innovation
**Labels:** Chess (Walter); Peacock (Thornton)

---

### 1956 - Howlin' Wolf Records "Smokestack Lightnin'"
**Event:** Classic recording released
**Significance:** Peak of Howlin' Wolf's Chicago success; #11 on R&B charts
**Location:** Chess Records, Chicago

---

### 1958 - Muddy Waters Tours England
**Event:** Waters brings electric blues to UK
**Impact:** Directly influenced future British blues boom; stunned audiences with amplified sound
**Legacy:** Within a decade, British bands following Waters' influence would reshape rock music
**Location:** United Kingdom

---

## PART VI: REVIVAL, REDISCOVERY, AND GLOBAL SPREAD (1960-1970)

### 1961 - Robert Johnson Compilation Released
**Event:** Columbia releases *King of the Delta Blues Singers*
**Significance:** Introduces Johnson to new generation; triggers blues revival
**Impact:** Influenced Bob Dylan, Rolling Stones, Eric Clapton, countless others
**Location:** United States

---

### 1964 - Multiple Rediscoveries
**Events:**
- Son House found in Rochester, NY (working at train station)
- Skip James found in Tunica, MS (hospital)
- Multiple Delta blues artists perform at Newport Folk Festival
**Significance:** Blues revival brings recognition to forgotten pioneers
**Location:** Various

---

### 1964 - Rolling Stones Record at Chess
**Event:** British band records at legendary Chess Records studio
**Significance:** Symbol of blues' return to America via British interpretation
**Context:** Band named after Muddy Waters song
**Location:** 2120 S. Michigan Ave., Chicago

---

### 1966 - John Mayall's Bluesbreakers with Eric Clapton
**Event:** Landmark album released
**Significance:** Pivotal between initial British blues boom and post-boom era of Hendrix, Led Zeppelin
**Impact:** Clapton's overdriven sound influenced rock guitar for decades
**Location:** United Kingdom

---

### 1969 - B.B. King Records "The Thrill Is Gone"
**Event:** B.B. King records his signature song
**Significance:** Crossover hit; first of 15 Grammy Awards
**Impact:** Brought blues to mainstream pop audience
**Label:** BluesWay (ABC subsidiary)

---

## PART VII: MODERN ERA (1970-Present)

### 1980 - First Blues Hall of Fame Class
**Event:** Blues Foundation inducts first class
**Inaugural Inductees Include:** Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, B.B. King, Memphis Minnie, Bessie Smith
**Location:** Memphis, Tennessee

---

### 1986 - Robert Johnson Inducted to Rock Hall
**Event:** Johnson among first inductees to Rock and Roll Hall of Fame
**Significance:** Recognition of blues as foundation of rock music
**Other Early Blues Inductees:** Muddy Waters (1987), Howlin' Wolf (1991), John Lee Hooker (1991)
**Location:** Cleveland, Ohio

---

### 1990 - Robert Johnson Centennial
**Event:** Columbia releases *The Complete Recordings* box set
**Sales:** Over 1 million copies - unprecedented for prewar blues
**Significance:** Established Johnson as most commercially successful prewar blues artist posthumously
**Grammy:** Won Grammy for Best Historical Album

---

### 2003 - Martin Scorsese Presents The Blues
**Event:** PBS documentary series airs
**Directors:** Seven films by Scorsese, Wim Wenders, Clint Eastwood, others
**Significance:** Major mainstream documentation of blues history
**Impact:** Renewed interest in blues origins and artists

---

### 2021 - Charley Patton Inducted to Rock Hall
**Event:** Patton inducted as Early Influence
**Significance:** Recognition of Delta blues founder 87 years after death
**Location:** Cleveland, Ohio

## TIMELINE THEMES

### The Great Migration Arc
- **1916-1970:** Approximately 6 million African Americans move from South to North and West
- **Musical Impact:** Blues travels from Mississippi Delta to Chicago, Detroit, other urban centers
- **Key Dates:**
  - World War I begins (1914): European immigration stops, creating Northern labor demand
  - World War II begins (1941): Second major migration wave

### The Recording Industry Arc
- **1920:** First race records
- **1927:** Peak sales ($100 million)
- **1933:** Depression collapse ($6 million)
- **1950s:** Revival via Chicago electric blues
- **1960s:** British blues boom reintroduces blues to white audiences

### The Exploitation Arc
- **Continuous:** Artists paid flat session fees, denied royalties
- **Continuous:** Copyright routinely taken by producers
- **Examples:**
  - Bessie Smith: "made Columbia millions" - "never paid royalties"
  - Big Mama Thornton: $500 for "Hound Dog" that sold 2 million copies
  - Willie Dixon's Blues Heaven Foundation: founded to recover stolen royalties
