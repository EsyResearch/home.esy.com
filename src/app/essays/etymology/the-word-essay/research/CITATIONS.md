## Key Figures Referenced

### Michel de Montaigne (1533–1592)
- Created the essay form with *Essais* (1580)
- Quote source: "To the Reader" preface
- Motto: "Que sais-je?" (What do I know?)

### Francis Bacon (1561–1626)
- First English essays (1597)
- Quote source: "Of Studies"
- Transformed essay from introspection to counsel

### Joseph Addison (1672–1719)
- Co-founder of *The Spectator* (1711–1712)
- Quote source: *The Spectator*, No. 10
- Pioneered daily essay journalism

### Charles Lamb (1775–1834)
- Created familiar essay under pseudonym "Elia"
- Quote source: *Essays of Elia*

### Ralph Waldo Emerson (1803–1882)
- American Transcendentalist essayist
- Quote source: "Self-Reliance" (1841)

### Virginia Woolf (1882–1941)
- Modernist essay theorist
- Quote source: "The Modern Essay" (1922)

### George Orwell (1903–1950)
- Political essayist
- Quote source: "Politics and the English Language" (1946)

### James Baldwin (1924–1987)
- Civil Rights era essayist
- Referenced work: *Notes of a Native Son* (1955)

### Joan Didion (1934–2021)
- New Journalism essayist
- Quote source: "The White Album" (1979)

## Research Notes

### Verification Status
All sources verified and cross-referenced. Primary etymology sources (OED, Stanford Encyclopedia) confirm Latin *exagium* → French *essayer* → English "essay" lineage.

### Historical Timeline
- c. Classical Era: Latin *exagium* (weighing)
- 1580: Montaigne coins *Essais*
- 1597: Bacon's first English *Essayes*
- 1711: Addison and Steele's *The Spectator*
- 1820s–1840s: Lamb and Emerson expand personal essay
- 1920s–1940s: Woolf and Orwell theorize modernist essay
- 1950s–1970s: Baldwin and Didion create witness essay
- 2000s–Present: Digital essay explosion
