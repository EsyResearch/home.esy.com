## Historical Dictionaries

### Source 4: Grose, Francis
- **Title:** *A Classical Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue*
- **Published:** 1785 (London)
- **Archive:** Internet Archive, public domain
- **URL:** https://archive.org/details/classicaldiction00gros
- **Key Findings:**
  - Entry: "SLANG. Cant language."
  - First major dictionary to document criminal/street vocabulary
  - Parody of Johnson's scholarly methods applied to "low" speech
- **Verification:** ✅ Primary historical source

### Source 5: Hotten, John Camden
- **Title:** *The Slang Dictionary*
- **Published:** 1859 (London)
- **Key Findings:**
  - First dictionary with "slang" in the title
  - First etymological treatment of slang words
  - Proposed several (now rejected) origin theories
- **Verification:** ✅ Primary historical source

### Source 6: Partridge, Eric
- **Title:** *A Dictionary of Slang and Unconventional English*
- **Published:** 1937; revised editions through 1984
- **Key Quote:** "Slang is language at play."
- **Significance:** Standard 20th-century reference; legitimized academic study
- **Verification:** ✅ Major scholarly work

## Historical/Primary Documents

### Source 11: Toldervy, William
- **Title:** *The History of Two Orphans*
- **Published:** 1756
- **Quote:** "Thomas Throw had been upon the town, knew the slang well"
- **Significance:** First attested use of "slang" as a noun
- **Verification:** ✅ Cited by OED as earliest attestation

### Source 12: Bradley, Henry
- **Title:** "Slang" entry in *Encyclopædia Britannica*, 11th ed.
- **Published:** 1911
- **URL:** https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/1911_Encyclopædia_Britannica/Slang
- **Key Quote:** "Slang is a conscious offence against some conventional standard of propriety."
- **Significance:** Canonical Victorian definition; reveals class anxieties
- **Verification:** ✅ Primary historical source

### Source 13: Mencken, H.L.
- **Title:** *The American Language*
- **Published:** 1919 (Knopf; revised editions through 1948)
- **Key Quote:** "Slang in its origin is nearly always respectable; it is devised not by the stupid populace, but by individuals of wit and ingenuity."
- **Significance:** Rehabilitated slang as American creative innovation
- **Verification:** ✅ Foundational American linguistics text

### Source 14: Whitman, Walt
- **Title:** "Slang in America" (essay)
- **Published:** 1885 (*North American Review*)
- **Key Quote:** "Slang... is the lawless germinal element, below all words and sentences, and behind all poetry."
- **Significance:** Poetic defense of slang as democratic expression
- **Verification:** ✅ Primary literary source
