## Primary Source Quotes (Pre-1900)

### Quote 1: First Attestation (1756)
> "Thomas Throw had been upon the town, knew the slang well"
- **Source:** William Toldervy, *The History of Two Orphans* (1756)
- **Context:** First recorded use of "slang" as a noun
- **OED Citation:** Yes, earliest attestation
- **Design Usage:** Hero quote for "Birth Certificate" panel

### Quote 2: Grose's Definition (1785)
> "SLANG. Cant language."
- **Source:** Francis Grose, *A Classical Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue* (1785)
- **Context:** Dictionary entry
- **Significance:** First major dictionary to document criminal vocabulary
- **Design Usage:** Dictionary facsimile recreation

### Quote 3: Whitman's Poetic Defense (1885)
> "Slang... is the lawless germinal element, below all words and sentences, and behind all poetry."
- **Source:** Walt Whitman, "Slang in America," *North American Review* (1885)
- **Context:** Essay championing American vernacular
- **Significance:** Literary legitimization of slang
- **Design Usage:** Pull quote card, poetic typography

### Quote 4: Whitman Extended (1885)
> "Language, be it remembered, is not an abstract construction of the learned, or of dictionary-makers, but is something arising out of the work, needs, ties, joys, affections, tastes, of long generations of humanity."
- **Source:** Walt Whitman, "Slang in America" (1885)
- **Context:** Democratic theory of language
- **Design Usage:** Secondary quote or closing panel

## Early 20th Century Quotes (1900-1950)

### Quote 5: Bradley's Victorian Judgment (1911)
> "Slang is a conscious offence against some conventional standard of propriety."
- **Source:** Henry Bradley, "Slang" entry, *Encyclopædia Britannica*, 11th ed. (1911)
- **Context:** Authoritative reference definition
- **Significance:** Reveals class anxieties; "offence" framing
- **Design Usage:** Contrast card (rigid, institutional typography)

### Quote 6: Mencken's Inversion (1919)
> "Slang in its origin is nearly always respectable; it is devised not by the stupid populace, but by individuals of wit and ingenuity."
- **Source:** H.L. Mencken, *The American Language* (1919)
- **Context:** Chapter on American slang
- **Significance:** Inverts Victorian stigma; celebrates creativity
- **Design Usage:** Hero quote card, bold American display

### Quote 7: Mencken on Function (1919)
> "The essence of slang is that it is of general dispersion, but still not accepted as good, formal speech."
- **Source:** H.L. Mencken, *The American Language* (1919)
- **Context:** Definition attempt
- **Design Usage:** Sidebar or annotation

### Quote 8: Partridge's Playful Definition (1937)
> "Slang is language at play."
- **Source:** Eric Partridge, *A Dictionary of Slang and Unconventional English* (1937)
- **Context:** Preface
- **Significance:** Frames slang as creative/ludic rather than deviant
- **Design Usage:** Section epigraph

## Late 20th Century Quotes (1950-1999)

### Quote 9: Eble on Solidarity (1996)
> "Slang is a colloquial vocabulary that creates solidarity within a social group."
- **Source:** Connie Eble, *Slang and Sociability* (1996)
- **Context:** Sociolinguistic analysis
- **Significance:** Reframes slang as social glue, not impropriety
- **Design Usage:** Key definition quote

### Quote 10: Eble on Ephemerality (1996)
> "One of the defining features of slang is its ephemerality. Slang is the verbal flare: intense, visible, and short-lived."
- **Source:** Connie Eble, *Slang and Sociability* (1996)
- **Context:** Chapter on slang lifespan
- **Design Usage:** Modern era section

## 21st Century Quotes (2000-present)

- **Design Usage:** Concept section

## Historical Etymology Quotes

### Quote 15: Taylor's Scandinavian Theory (1864)
> "The word 'slang' appears to be of Scandinavian origin, cognate with Norwegian *sleng*, a slinging, *slengjeord*, slang words."
- **Source:** Isaac Taylor, *Words and Places* (1864)
- **Context:** First scholarly etymology proposal
- **Design Usage:** Etymology panel; labeled "Plausible"

### Quote 16: Liberman's Argument (2016)
> "The Scandinavian origin of 'slang' is virtually certain."

- **Context:** Modern argument for Scandinavian etymology
- **Note:** OED remains unconvinced; label as "Strong argument, not consensus"
- **Design Usage:** Etymology uncertainty section
