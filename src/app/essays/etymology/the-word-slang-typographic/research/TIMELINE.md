# Timeline: "SLANG" — Word History & Documentation

## Complete Milestone List (19 entries)

### Pre-Word Era (Cant Period)

#### 1566 — The First Cant Glossary
- **Location:** London
- **Medium:** Book
- **Event:** Thomas Harman publishes *A Caveat or Warning for Common Cursitors*
- **Significance:** First systematic documentation of criminal vocabulary (cant), predating the word "slang"
- **Source:** Harman (1566); Coleman (2012), p. 26

#### 1698 — The Canting Crew
- **Location:** London
- **Medium:** Dictionary
- **Event:** B.E. publishes *A New Dictionary of the Terms Ancient and Modern of the Canting Crew*
- **Significance:** Major cant dictionary; "slang" still not used as a term
- **Source:** B.E. (1698); Green (2010), intro

---

### Word Emergence (1740s–1800)

#### 1741 — First Verb Usage
- **Location:** London
- **Medium:** Tyburn execution account
- **Event:** "Slanging" appears as verb meaning to use abusive language
- **Significance:** Earliest attested use of any form of "slang"
- **Source:** OED, "slang, v."

#### 1756 — First Noun Attestation ⭐
- **Location:** London
- **Medium:** Novel
- **Event:** William Toldervy's *History of Two Orphans*: "knew the slang well"
- **Significance:** FIRST recorded use of "slang" as a noun, meaning criminal cant
- **Source:** OED, "slang, n.³"; Toldervy (1756)

#### 1785 — Grose's Vulgar Tongue
- **Location:** London
- **Medium:** Dictionary
- **Event:** Francis Grose publishes *A Classical Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue*
- **Significance:** First major dictionary of "low" vocabulary; defines "SLANG" as "Cant language"
- **Source:** Grose (1785)

---

### Semantic Expansion (1800–1860)

#### 1801 — Expansion to Jargon
- **Location:** England
- **Medium:** OED attestation
- **Event:** "Slang" begins to include professional/trade jargon, not just criminal cant
- **Significance:** Semantic broadening begins
- **Source:** OED, sense 1b

#### 1818 — General Informal Speech
- **Location:** England
- **Medium:** Various
- **Event:** "Slang" attested meaning any "low" or informal speech
- **Significance:** Meaning expands beyond criminal origins
- **Source:** OED, sense 2

#### 1859 — The Slang Dictionary
- **Location:** London
- **Medium:** Dictionary
- **Event:** John Camden Hotten publishes *The Slang Dictionary*
- **Significance:** First dictionary with "slang" in the title; first etymological treatments
- **Source:** Hotten (1859)

---

### Scholarly Attention (1860–1920)

#### 1864 — Taylor's Etymology
- **Location:** England
- **Medium:** Academic publication
- **Event:** Isaac Taylor proposes Scandinavian origin (*slengjeord*)
- **Significance:** First serious etymology proposal; remains leading theory
- **Source:** Taylor, *Words and Places* (1864)

#### 1885 — Whitman's Defense
- **Location:** United States
- **Medium:** Magazine essay
- **Event:** Walt Whitman publishes "Slang in America" in *North American Review*
- **Significance:** Poetic/literary legitimization of slang as democratic expression
- **Source:** Whitman (1885)

#### 1911 — Britannica Definition
- **Location:** England
- **Medium:** Encyclopedia
- **Event:** Henry Bradley defines slang as "conscious offence against propriety"
- **Significance:** Canonical Victorian definition; class-based moral framing
- **Source:** *Encyclopædia Britannica*, 11th ed.

#### 1919 — The American Language
- **Location:** United States
- **Medium:** Book
- **Event:** H.L. Mencken publishes *The American Language*
- **Significance:** Rehabilitates slang as American creative innovation; inverts stigma
- **Source:** Mencken (1919)

---

### Academic Legitimization (1930–2000)

#### 1937 — Partridge's Dictionary
- **Location:** England
- **Medium:** Dictionary
- **Event:** Eric Partridge publishes *A Dictionary of Slang and Unconventional English*
- **Significance:** Standard 20th-century reference; academic legitimization
- **Source:** Partridge (1937)

#### 1960s — Sociolinguistics Emerges
- **Location:** Global (academic)
- **Medium:** Academic discipline
- **Event:** William Labov and others establish sociolinguistics
- **Significance:** Slang studied as social phenomenon, not deviance
- **Source:** Labov (1966); various

#### 1996 — Slang and Sociability
- **Location:** United States
- **Medium:** Academic monograph
- **Event:** Connie Eble publishes *Slang and Sociability*
- **Significance:** Reframes slang as "vocabulary that creates solidarity"
- **Source:** Eble (1996)

---

### Digital Era (1999–Present)

#### 1999 — Urban Dictionary Founded
- **Location:** Internet (global)
- **Medium:** Website
- **Event:** Aaron Peckham launches Urban Dictionary
- **Significance:** Crowdsourced slang documentation; democratized lexicography
- **Source:** Urban Dictionary history

#### 2010 — Green's Dictionary
- **Location:** England/Global
- **Medium:** Dictionary (print + online)
- **Event:** Jonathon Green publishes *Green's Dictionary of Slang* (3 volumes)
- **Significance:** Largest historical slang dictionary ever compiled (125,000+ entries)
- **Source:** Green (2010)

#### 2019 — Because Internet
- **Location:** Global
- **Medium:** Book
- **Event:** Gretchen McCulloch publishes *Because Internet*
- **Significance:** Analyzes internet's impact on informal language; slang acceleration
- **Source:** McCulloch (2019)

#### 2020s — Platform Velocity
- **Location:** Global (digital)
- **Medium:** Social media
- **Event:** TikTok, Discord, and AI platforms accelerate slang cycles
- **Significance:** Slang lifespan compresses from years to months to weeks
- **Source:** McCulloch (2019); ongoing observation
