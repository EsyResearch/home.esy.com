## Figure Selection Criteria

Figures included here are lexicographers, writers, linguists, or cultural figures who:
- Documented slang systematically
- Defended slang's legitimacy
- Theorized about slang's social function
- Popularized awareness of slang

---

## Figure 1: Francis Grose (1731–1791)

**Role:** Antiquary, lexicographer  
**Why They Matter:** Published the first major English slang dictionary, legitimizing the study of "vulgar" language

### Key Contribution
*A Classical Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue* (1785)

> "A work which, however it may offend the delicate, will be found useful to the philologist."

**Portrait:** Engraving available (pre-photography)  
**Era:** Georgian England  
**Significance:** Grose treated slang as worthy of scholarly attention when polite society dismissed it as corruption.

---

## Figure 2: John Camden Hotten (1832–1873)

**Role:** Publisher, lexicographer  
**Why They Matter:** Created the first dictionary explicitly titled "slang," moving the term from cant to broader usage

### Key Contribution
*A Dictionary of Modern Slang, Cant, and Vulgar Words* (1859)

**Portrait:** Photograph available  
**Era:** Victorian England  
**Significance:** Hotten's work made slang a respectable subject for the middle-class reader.

---

## Figure 3: John S. Farmer (1854–1916) & W.E. Henley (1849–1903)

**Role:** Lexicographers, literary figures  
**Why They Matter:** Produced the monumental 7-volume *Slang and Its Analogues* (1890–1904)

### Key Contribution
*Slang and Its Analogues Past and Present* — the most comprehensive Victorian slang compilation

**Portrait:** Photographs available for both  
**Era:** Late Victorian  
**Significance:** Treated slang with scholarly rigor comparable to the OED; included historical citations and cross-references.

---

## Figure 4: Eric Partridge (1894–1979)

**Role:** Lexicographer, etymologist  
**Why They Matter:** The 20th century's most prolific slang scholar; shifted perception of slang from "low" to "playful"

### Key Contribution
*A Dictionary of Slang and Unconventional English* (1937, 8 editions)

> "Slang is language at play."

**Portrait:** Photographs widely available  
**Era:** 20th century  
**Significance:** Partridge single-handedly made slang lexicography a respectable field. His work remains foundational.

---

## Figure 5: H.L. Mencken (1880–1956)

**Role:** Journalist, cultural critic, linguist  
**Why They Matter:** Championed American English (including slang) as a legitimate, vibrant language distinct from British English

### Key Contribution
*The American Language* (1919, multiple editions)

> "Slang originates in the effort of ingenious individuals to make the language more pungent and picturesque."

**Portrait:** Photographs widely available  
**Era:** Early-mid 20th century America  
**Significance:** Mencken treated American slang as evidence of national vitality, not decline.

---

## Figure 6: Walt Whitman (1819–1892)

**Role:** Poet, essayist  
**Why They Matter:** Celebrated slang as democratic, creative, and essentially American

### Key Contribution
Essay: "Slang in America" (1885)

> "Slang, profoundly consider'd, is the lawless germinal element, below all words and sentences, and behind all poetry."

**Portrait:** Photographs available  
**Era:** 19th century America  
**Significance:** Whitman gave slang poetic dignity—it wasn't just language, it was *the people's* language.

---

## Figure 7: J.E. Lighter (b. 1949)

**Role:** Lexicographer  
**Why They Matter:** Editor of the most scholarly American slang dictionary

### Key Contribution
*Random House Historical Dictionary of American Slang* (1994–)

**Portrait:** Contemporary photograph available  
**Era:** Late 20th century  
**Significance:** Brought rigorous historical methodology to American slang documentation.

---

## Figure 8: Jonathon Green (b. 1948)

**Role:** Lexicographer, slang historian  
**Why They Matter:** Creator of the largest slang database; contemporary authority on English slang

### Key Contribution
*Green's Dictionary of Slang* (2010, online database)

> "Slang is the poetry of everyday life."

**Portrait:** Contemporary photograph available  
**Era:** Contemporary  
**Significance:** Green's database contains 135,000+ slang terms with 620,000+ citations—the most comprehensive slang resource ever compiled.

---

## Figure 9: Gretchen McCulloch (b. 1988)

**Role:** Linguist, author  
**Why They Matter:** Leading voice on internet linguistics and digital slang

### Key Contribution
*Because Internet: Understanding the New Rules of Language* (2019)

**Portrait:** Contemporary photograph available  
**Era:** Contemporary digital  
**Significance:** McCulloch explains how the internet has accelerated slang creation and spread, making linguistics accessible to general readers.

---

## Figure 10: Tom Dalzell

**Role:** Slang collector, author  
**Why They Matter:** Documents American youth slang across generations

### Key Contribution
*Flappers 2 Rappers: American Youth Slang* (1996)

**Portrait:** Contemporary photograph available  
**Era:** Contemporary  
**Significance:** Dalzell's work maps how each generation creates slang to differentiate itself.
