# FIGURES.md — The Word "Slang"

## Key Figures

### Figure 1: Thomas Harman (fl. 1566)
- **Role**: Kentish justice of the peace; first systematic documenter of criminal cant
- **Key Work**: *A Caveat or Warning for Common Cursitors, Vulgarly Called Vagabonds* (1566)
- **Significance**: Created first glossary of underworld vocabulary (precursor to "slang" as a concept)
- **Imagery Available**: Period woodcuts from 16th-century rogue literature ✓
- **Quote**: N/A (wrote about cant, before the word "slang" existed)
- **Source**: Harman, *Caveat* (1566); Etymonline "cant"

### Figure 2: B.E. Gentleman (fl. 1699)
- **Role**: Anonymous lexicographer
- **Key Work**: *A New Dictionary of Terms, Ancient and Modern, of the Canting Crew* (1699)
- **Significance**: First English dictionary dedicated entirely to slang/cant vocabulary
- **Imagery Available**: Title page reproductions ✓
- **Quote**: N/A
- **Source**: University of Chicago Press, *The First English Dictionary of Slang, 1699*

### Figure 3: William Toldervy (fl. 1756)
- **Role**: Novelist
- **Key Work**: *The History of Two Orphans* (1756)
- **Significance**: Author of the **first known attestation of the word "slang"**
- **Imagery Available**: Book cover/title page reproductions ✓
- **Quote**: "Thomas Throw had been upon the town, knew the slang well; had often sate a flasher at M——d——g——n's, and understood every word in the scoundrel's dictionary."
- **Source**: Toldervy (1756); OED "slang, n.³"

### Figure 4: Captain Francis Grose (1731–1791)
- **Role**: Antiquary, lexicographer, militia captain
- **Key Work**: *A Classical Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue* (1785)
- **Significance**: First major slang dictionary; 9,000 words; conducted fieldwork in London's underworld
- **Imagery Available**: Portrait engravings; title pages; dictionary pages ✓
- **Image Source**: Wikimedia Commons, Category:Francis Grose
  - "Francesco Bartolozzi - Francis Grose - Yale Center for British Art.jpg"
- **Quote**: Described by Eric Partridge as "the greatest antiquary, joker, and porter-drinker of his day"
- **Source**: Public Domain Review; Cambridge Core Blog; Peter Harrington Books

### Figure 5: John Camden Hotten (1832–1873)
- **Role**: Publisher, lexicographer
- **Key Work**: *A Dictionary of Modern Slang, Cant, and Vulgar Words* (1859)
- **Significance**: First etymological histories of slang varieties; expanded from 3,000 to 5,000+ words
- **Imagery Available**: Victorian-era photographs/engravings possible; book covers ✓
- **Quote**: Called it "a dictionary of the people for the people"
- **Source**: Library of Congress; Oxford Academic; HathiTrust

### Figure 6: Isaac Taylor (1787–1865)
- **Role**: Philologist
- **Key Work**: Etymology theory advanced in 1860s
- **Significance**: Proposed the leading (still unproven) etymology: "slang" from narrow strips of land where hawkers camped
- **Imagery Available**: Limited; Victorian scholar portraits possible
- **Quote**: N/A
- **Source**: OED "The rise and rise of slang"

### Figure 7: Henry Bradley (1845–1923)
- **Role**: OED editor, encyclopedist
- **Key Work**: "Slang" article, *Encyclopædia Britannica* 11th ed. (1911)
- **Significance**: Canonical Victorian definition; framed slang as "conscious offence against propriety"
- **Imagery Available**: Photographs exist ✓
- **Quote**: "Slang is a conscious offence against some conventional standard of propriety... a deliberate substitute for a word of the vernacular."
- **Source**: Britannica 1911; OUPblog "Unsung heroes of English etymology"

### Figure 8: H.L. Mencken (1880–1956)
- **Role**: Journalist, critic, lexicographer
- **Key Work**: *The American Language* (1919)
- **Significance**: Rehabilitated slang as creative, respectable; defended American English
- **Imagery Available**: Many photographs ✓
- **Image Source**: Library of Congress
  - https://www.loc.gov/pictures/item/2004663326/ (Carl Van Vechten, 1932)
  - https://www.loc.gov/item/2017657361 (bust portrait, c. 1928)
- **Quote**: "Slang in its origin is nearly always respectable; it is devised not by the stupid populace, but by individuals of wit and ingenuity."
- **Source**: Mencken, *American Language* (1919); Project Gutenberg

### Figure 9: Eric Partridge (1894–1979)
- **Role**: Lexicographer
- **Key Work**: *A Dictionary of Slang and Unconventional English* (1937)
- **Significance**: Standard 20th-century reference; 7 editions; 50 years at British Museum desk K1
- **Imagery Available**: Photographs exist ✓
- **Quote**: On Grose: "the greatest antiquary, joker, and porter-drinker of his day"
- **Source**: Britannica biography; Wikipedia

### Figure 10: Connie Eble (b. 1942)
- **Role**: Sociolinguist, University of North Carolina
- **Key Work**: *Slang and Sociability: In-Group Language Among College Students* (1996)
- **Significance**: Academic legitimization of slang studies; functional definition
- **Imagery Available**: Academic author photos possible
- **Quote**: Defined slang as "a colloquial vocabulary that creates solidarity within a social group"
- **Source**: Eble, *Slang and Sociability* (1996)

### Figure 11: Anatoly Liberman (b. 1937)
- **Role**: Etymologist, University of Minnesota
- **Key Work**: "The origin of the word 'slang' is known!" (OUPblog, 2016)
- **Significance**: Most detailed recent argument for Scandinavian etymology
- **Imagery Available**: Academic author photos ✓
- **Quote**: Credits John Sampson's 1898 article for "solving" the etymology
- **Source**: OUPblog, September 2016
