## Sources

### S1 — Beekes, *Etymological Dictionary of Greek* (2010)
- **Author**: Robert S. P. Beekes (with Lucien van Beek)
- **Publisher**: Brill (Leiden Indo-European Etymological Dictionary Series), 2 vols.
- **Type**: Scholarly etymological dictionary
- **Tier**: 1
- **ISBN**: 978-9004174184
- **Relevance**: The definitive English-language etymological dictionary of Greek. Contains the entry for φωνή (phonē) with Proto-Indo-European reconstruction, attestation history, dialectal variants, derivatives, and compounds. Essential for establishing the scholarly consensus on phonē's origin.
- **Key Claims Supported**: PIE root *bʰeh₂- ("to speak, say"), earliest attestation in Homeric Greek, semantic range (voice, sound, utterance), derivative compounds
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — standard reference work in Indo-European linguistics, published by Brill

### S2 — Liddell, Scott & Jones, *A Greek-English Lexicon* (LSJ, 9th ed.)
- **Authors**: Henry George Liddell, Robert Scott, revised by Henry Stuart Jones
- **Publisher**: Oxford University Press (1940, with supplements)
- **Type**: Scholarly Greek lexicon
- **Tier**: 1
- **Relevance**: The standard comprehensive lexicon of ancient Greek. Provides the full semantic range of φωνή across ancient Greek literature — from Homer through the Hellenistic period. Documents every major sense: voice, sound, utterance, language, dialect, tone.
- **Key Claims Supported**: φωνή usage in Homer (Iliad, Odyssey), Aristotle, Plato, tragic poets; semantic breadth from "voice" to "language" to "sound"
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — the standard Greek-English dictionary since 1843, continuously revised

### S3 — *Oxford English Dictionary* (OED, 3rd ed.)
- **Publisher**: Oxford University Press (online, continuously updated)
- **Type**: Historical dictionary of English
- **Tier**: 1
- **Relevance**: Provides full entry histories for *phone* (n., 1878), *telephone* (n., 1835/1876), *phonograph* (n., 1835/1877), *microphone* (n., 1827/1878), and all -phone/-phon- compounds. Documents first attestations with dated quotations, semantic evolution, and usage notes.
- **Key Claims Supported**: *phone* first attested 1878 (Des Moines Register), *telephone* 1835 (Sudré musical system) / 1876 (Bell device), clipping chronology, 'phone apostrophe convention
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — the historical authority on English word origins

### S4 — Chantraine, *Dictionnaire étymologique de la langue grecque* (1968–1980)
- **Author**: Pierre Chantraine
- **Publisher**: Klincksieck, Paris
- **Type**: Scholarly Greek etymological dictionary (French)
- **Tier**: 1
- **ISBN**: 978-2252032770
- **Relevance**: The major French-language etymological dictionary of Greek. Provides an independent scholarly analysis of φωνή, including its relationship to φημί (phēmi, "I say") and the broader *bʰeh₂- word family. Essential for cross-referencing Beekes' reconstructions.
- **Key Claims Supported**: Relationship between φωνή and φημί, compound-formation patterns in Greek, French scholarly tradition on the word
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — standard reference in French classical philology

### S5 — Klein, *A Comprehensive Etymological Dictionary of the English Language* (1971)
- **Author**: Ernest Klein
- **Publisher**: Elsevier
- **Type**: Etymological dictionary of English
- **Tier**: 1
- **ISBN**: 978-0444409300
- **Relevance**: Traces English words back to their ultimate Indo-European roots. Provides derivation chains for *phone*, *telephone*, *phonograph*, and all major -phone compounds. Cross-references with Greek and Latin intermediaries.
- **Key Claims Supported**: English derivation chains, compound word formation, semantic evolution from Greek through Latin/French to English
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — established etymological reference

### S6 — Marchand, *The Categories and Types of Present-Day English Word-Formation* (1969)
- **Author**: Hans Marchand
- **Publisher**: C. H. Beck, Munich (2nd ed.)
- **Type**: Academic monograph on morphology
- **Tier**: 1
- **Relevance**: The definitive study of English word-formation processes, including clipping (telephone → phone), compounding (smartphone), and neo-classical combining forms (-phone, phono-). Essential for the linguistic analysis of how *phone* became an independent word.
- **Key Claims Supported**: Clipping as productive word-formation process, neo-classical compounds in English, the -phone combining form's productivity
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — foundational text in English morphology, widely cited in linguistic literature

### S7 — Aristotle, *De Interpretatione* (4th c. BCE)
- **Author**: Aristotle
- **Type**: Primary philosophical text
- **Tier**: 1
- **Relevance**: Contains Aristotle's systematic analysis of φωνή as meaningful sound — voice as "a sound significant by convention." This is the earliest philosophical treatment of phonē as a linguistic concept, establishing the voice/meaning relationship that persists in modern linguistics.
- **Key Claims Supported**: φωνή as "sound significant by convention," distinction between human voice and animal sound, voice as bearer of meaning
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — primary source, multiple modern translations (Ackrill, 1963; Whitaker, 1996)

### S8 — Bell, US Patent 174,465 (1876)
- **Inventor**: Alexander Graham Bell
- **Filed**: February 14, 1876
- **Granted**: March 7, 1876
- **Type**: Primary patent document
- **Tier**: 1
- **Relevance**: The patent that made *telephone* a legal and commercial reality. Title: "Improvement in Telegraphy." The document itself uses "telephone" to describe the apparatus, codifying the Greek compound into American patent law and, subsequently, global vocabulary.
- **Key Claims Supported**: Bell's use of the word "telephone," patent filing date (same day as Gray's caveat), the moment the word became commercially significant
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — US Patent Office records, freely accessible

### S9 — Reis, "On Telephony" (1861)
- **Author**: Johann Philipp Reis
- **Presented**: Physical Society of Frankfurt, October 26, 1861
- **Type**: Primary scientific communication
- **Tier**: 1
- **Relevance**: Reis coined and used the term *Telephon* (from Greek tele + phonē) to describe his sound-transmitting device in 1861, fifteen years before Bell's patent. Establishes that the compound word *telephone* was in use before Bell.
- **Key Claims Supported**: Reis as coiner of *Telephon*, 1861 demonstration, German-language first use of the compound
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — documented in Nature (1878), multiple historical accounts

### S11 — Casson, *The History of the Telephone* (1910)
- **Author**: Herbert N. Casson
- **Publisher**: A.C. McClurg & Co., Chicago
- **Type**: Early historical account
- **Tier**: 2
- **Relevance**: One of the earliest comprehensive histories of the telephone, written within living memory of Bell's invention. Documents the rapid adoption of the word *telephone* into American English, early usage patterns, and the social impact of the device and its name.
- **Key Claims Supported**: Early adoption of the word, social context of naming, telephone vs. telegraphic terminology
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — primary historical source (public domain, available via Project Gutenberg/Internet Archive)

### S16 — Wiktionary, *Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/bʰeh₂-*
- **Type**: Collaborative etymological reference
- **Tier**: 3
- **URL**: en.wiktionary.org
- **Relevance**: Comprehensive catalogue of all descendants from the PIE root *bʰeh₂- ("to speak, say, tell"), organized by daughter language. Useful for mapping the full family tree: Greek φωνή, φημί, φάσις; Latin fāma, fātum, fābula, for, fateor; English ban, fame, fate, phone.
- **Key Claims Supported**: PIE root descendant catalogue, cross-language cognate mapping
- **Verification**: ⚠️ Collaborative source — cross-referenced with S1 (Beekes) and S4 (Chantraine) for all claims used

### S17 — Bourseul, "Transmission électrique de la parole" (1854)
- **Author**: Charles Bourseul
- **Published**: *L'Illustration*, August 26, 1854
- **Type**: Primary historical document
- **Tier**: 1
- **Relevance**: The earliest published description of transmitting the human voice electrically. While Bourseul did not coin *téléphone* (the word predates him, used by Sudré in 1828 for a musical signaling system), his 1854 article is the first to describe what we now mean by a telephone — electrical voice transmission.
- **Key Claims Supported**: 1854 concept publication, distinction between Sudré's 1828 musical *téléphone* and the electrical meaning
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — archived in French periodical records

### S18 — Century Dictionary and Cyclopedia (1895)
- **Publisher**: The Century Company, New York
- **Type**: Historical dictionary
- **Tier**: 2
- **Relevance**: The 1895 entry for *phone* documents early usage: "colloquial shortening of telephone; generally applied to the receiver, but sometimes to the whole apparatus." This captures the word mid-clipping, when *phone* was still informal and could mean either the handset or the full device.
- **Key Claims Supported**: 1895 status of *phone* as colloquial, semantic ambiguity (receiver vs. apparatus), the word in transition
- **Verification**: ✅ Verified — historical dictionary, available via Internet Archive
