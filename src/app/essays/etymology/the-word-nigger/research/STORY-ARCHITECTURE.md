## Narrative Outline

### Central Question
**What makes a word wound?**

The essay answers: Language doesn't just describe the world — it builds it. This word became infrastructure for dehumanization, embedded in law, commerce, entertainment, and daily speech. But at every stage, people fought back.

## Section-by-Section Breakdown

### Chapter 1: Content Warning
**Type**: Entry threshold  
**Layout**: Text-forward, minimal  
**Content**: Respectful acknowledgment of subject weight; language policy explanation; invitation to proceed  
**Animation**: Minimal fade-in only  
**Duration**: Brief — transition to hero

### Chapter 2: When Words Become Weapons (Prologue)
**Type**: Thesis statement  
**Layout**: Full-bleed with typography fragments  
**Content**: Central question introduced; language as infrastructure; preview of harm AND resistance  
**Animation**: Question emerges, fragments gather  
**Key Line**: "This is the story of how one word was built — and how it was fought."

### Chapter 3: The Neutral Root
**Type**: Etymology  
**Layout**: Document gallery / text-forward  
**Content**: Latin *niger* as color term; no racial meaning in classical usage; linguistic pathway  
**Animation**: Word transformation (NIGER → NEGRO → variants)  
**Visual**: Manuscript aesthetic, warm sepia

### Chapter 4: First English Traces
**Type**: Documentary evidence  
**Layout**: Document gallery  
**Content**: OED 1574 attestation; early spellings; emerging colonial context  
**Animation**: Dictionary entry emerges from darkness  
**Visual**: Archive aesthetic, library atmosphere

### Chapter 5: Language of the Trade
**Type**: Historical context  
**Layout**: Full-bleed (ship diagram) → document overlay  
**Content**: Atlantic slave trade routes; word traveled with enslaved people; law and commerce  
**Animation**: Map routes, document reveals  
**Visual**: Maritime, legal documents, high contrast  
**Images**: Brookes diagram (LOC, 1788)

### Chapter 6: Slavery as System
**Type**: Legal/resistance dual narrative  
**Layout**: Split narrative  
**Content**: Dred Scott decision; Frederick Douglass as counter-voice  
**Animation**: Legal text emerges, then resistance voice overlays  
**Figure**: Frederick Douglass — The Great Orator  
**Images**: Douglass daguerreotype (NPG), Dred Scott document  
**Visual**: Split treatment — law vs. resistance

### Chapter 7: Minstrelsy's Normalization ⚠️
**Type**: Cultural documentation  
**Layout**: Document gallery with critical framing  
**Content**: Thomas Rice, Jim Crow character, entertainment as weapon  
**Animation**: Theater curtain, sheet music at reduced scale, map of spread  
**Handling**: Documentary only, never celebratory, reduced scale  
**Images**: Sheet music covers (LOC), reduced scale

### Chapter 8: Reconstruction's Betrayal
**Type**: Historical transition  
**Layout**: Split narrative (hope → reversal)  
**Content**: Constitutional amendments, Black legislators, then Black Codes, federal withdrawal  
**Animation**: Door opens (hope), then closes (betrayal)  
**Images**: Amendment documents (NARA), Currier & Ives lithograph

### Chapter 9: Jim Crow's Reign ⚠️
**Type**: Documentary / resistance dual  
**Layout**: Full-bleed → portrait focus  
**Content**: Segregation signage, Plessy v. Ferguson, Ida B. Wells, W.E.B. Du Bois  
**Animation**: Signs emerge, then resistance figures appear with dignity  
**Figures**: Ida B. Wells, W.E.B. Du Bois  
**Images**: Wells (NPG), Du Bois (LOC), Jim Crow signage (documentary)  
**Handling**: Signage documentary, critical context required

### Chapter 10: The Dictionary Learns
**Type**: Lexicographic evolution  
**Layout**: Document gallery / timeline  
**Content**: Dictionary definitions evolve; labels intensify; euphemism emerges  
**Animation**: Definitions transform through time  
**Visual**: Library/archive atmosphere, dictionary pages floating

### Chapter 11: Civil Rights — Contesting Language
**Type**: Resistance voices  
**Layout**: Portrait focus  
**Content**: MLK, Baldwin, Marshall — counter-language of dignity  
**Animation**: Baldwin portrait emerges, quote types onto screen  
**Figures**: Martin Luther King Jr., James Baldwin, Thurgood Marshall  
**Images**: MLK at March (NARA), Baldwin (Allan Warren CC), Marshall (official)  
**Key Quote**: "You can only be destroyed by believing that you really are what the white world calls a nigger." — Baldwin

### Chapter 12: The Euphemism Era
**Type**: Media/broadcasting analysis  
**Layout**: Text-forward with document accents  
**Content**: Rise of "the N-word"; FCC standards; broadcasting norms  
**Animation**: Caption bar, dictionary entry for euphemism  
**Visual**: Broadcast aesthetic, caption bars

### Chapter 13: Reappropriation Debates
**Type**: Complex contemporary  
**Layout**: Split narrative (multiple perspectives)  
**Content**: Richard Pryor's arc, N.W.A., hip-hop, generational debates  
**Animation**: Two perspectives in tension, both honored  
**Figure**: Richard Pryor  
**Note**: Essay does NOT settle this debate — presents complexity

### Chapter 14: Today — The Open Wound
**Type**: Contemporary relevance  
**Layout**: Text-forward with contemporary visual accents  
**Content**: Randall Kennedy's book, ongoing education debates, live questions  
**Animation**: Multiple contexts emerge  
**Figure**: Randall Kennedy  
**Key Line**: "How do we name what wounds without amplifying the wound?"

### Chapter 15: Naming What Wounds
**Type**: Closing reflection  
**Layout**: Full-bleed, return to weighing scale  
**Content**: Return to thesis; resistance figures in gallery; the weighing continues  
**Animation**: Scale returns, figure gallery, quotes overlay, scale settles  
**Final Line**: "Understanding is where doing begins."

## Production Notes

### Critical Implementation Rules

1. **Language Policy**: Full term appears ONLY in primary source citations. Display typography uses "the N-word" or equivalent.

2. **Never Decorative**: The word is never used as design element or decorative typography.

3. **Balance Required**: Every section showing harm must also show resistance.

4. **Dignity Centered**: Resistance figures given visual prominence; never degraded.

5. **Documentary Framing**: Racist imagery (minstrelsy, signage) shown at reduced scale with critical context.
