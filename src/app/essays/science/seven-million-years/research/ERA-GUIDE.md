# Era Guide — Seven Million Years

> Historical period treatments for the visual essay
> Defines the mood, environment, key events, and visual character of each era

---

## Era 1: The Deep Divergence (~7–4.4 MYA)
**Geological Period:** Late Miocene → Early Pliocene
**Climate:** Warm but cooling; forests fragmenting into woodland-grassland mosaics
**Setting:** Central and East Africa — Chad, Kenya, Ethiopia

### Key Species
- Sahelanthropus tchadensis (~7 MYA)
- Orrorin tugenensis (~6 MYA)
- Ardipithecus kadabba (~5.8 MYA)
- Ardipithecus ramidus (~4.4 MYA)

### Environmental Context
The late Miocene saw a gradual drying of Africa. Dense tropical forests that once covered the continent began breaking apart into patches of woodland interspersed with open grassland. This fragmentation created a mosaic landscape — edges between forest and savanna — where the first hominins emerged. The Rift Valley system was actively forming, creating new microclimates and geographic barriers that isolated populations.

### Key Challenges
- Forest retreat forcing adaptation to more open habitats
- Predation pressure from large cats, hyenas, and raptors
- Competition with other apes for diminishing forest resources
- Need to travel between food patches across open ground (driving bipedalism?)

### Visual Character
- Dense green canopy giving way to dappled woodland
- Red-brown African soil, volcanic landscapes
- The Great Rift Valley forming — dramatic escarpments
- Animals: early elephants, three-toed horses, sabertooth cats

### Mood
Mystery and ambiguity. These earliest hominins are known from fragments — a skull here, a femur there. The fossil record is sparse and contested. This era should feel like peering through fog.

## Era 2: The Australopith Radiation (~4.4–2.0 MYA)
**Geological Period:** Pliocene
**Climate:** Continued cooling and drying; expansion of grasslands; cyclical wet-dry periods
**Setting:** East and South Africa — Ethiopia, Tanzania, Kenya, South Africa

### Key Species
- Australopithecus anamensis (~4.2–3.9 MYA)
- Australopithecus afarensis (~3.9–2.9 MYA)
- Kenyanthropus platyops (~3.5 MYA)
- Australopithecus africanus (~3.0–2.0 MYA)
- Australopithecus garhi (~2.5 MYA)
- Australopithecus sediba (~1.98 MYA)
- Paranthropus aethiopicus (~2.7–2.3 MYA)
- Paranthropus boisei (~2.3–1.2 MYA)
- Paranthropus robustus (~2.0–1.0 MYA)

### Environmental Context
The Pliocene was a time of increasing environmental variability. Ice ages began cycling in the Northern Hemisphere around 2.6 MYA (onset of Pleistocene glaciation), sending ripples of climate change across Africa. Grasslands expanded at the expense of forests. Lakes formed and dried. The landscape was a patchwork of habitats, and hominins diversified to fill multiple ecological niches — some becoming hard-food specialists (Paranthropus), others remaining generalists (Australopithecus/early Homo).

### Key Challenges
- Habitat fragmentation and unpredictability
- Dietary stress — need to exploit harder, lower-quality foods
- Predation (eagles preying on juvenile australopiths — Taung Child)
- Competition between multiple hominin species occupying the same landscapes

### Visual Character
- Open savanna with scattered acacia trees
- Volcanic ash layers (Laetoli footprints preserved in ash)
- Lake margins and river corridors
- Animals: early antelopes, baboons, crocodiles, large felids

### Mood
Proliferation and experiment. This is evolution's workshop — multiple body plans being tested simultaneously. The family tree is bushy, not linear. Some succeed, some fail. The tone should convey diversity and possibility.

## Era 3: The First Humans (~2.8–1.0 MYA)
**Geological Period:** Early Pleistocene
**Climate:** Onset of major glacial-interglacial cycles; increasingly variable and extreme
**Setting:** East Africa → Caucasus (Dmanisi) → East and Southeast Asia

### Key Species
- Homo habilis (~2.4–1.4 MYA)
- Homo rudolfensis (~2.0–1.5 MYA)
- Homo erectus (~1.9 MYA – ~110 kya)

### Environmental Context
The Pleistocene brought dramatic climate oscillations — ice ages advancing and retreating in roughly 41,000-year and later 100,000-year cycles. In Africa, these translated to alternating periods of extreme aridity and lush productivity. Richard Potts' "variability selection" hypothesis proposes that it was this unpredictability itself — not any single environment — that selected for the flexibility, intelligence, and adaptability that defines genus Homo.

### Key Challenges
- Extreme climate swings requiring behavioral flexibility
- Need for higher-quality diet (meat) to fuel growing brains
- Competition with other carnivores (hyenas, sabertooth cats) for prey
- Long-distance dispersal into unfamiliar environments (Dmanisi, Java)

### Key Innovations
- **Oldowan tools** (~2.6 MYA): Simple but effective flaked stone tools
- **Acheulean hand axes** (~1.76 MYA): First standardized, symmetrical tools — represent planning and aesthetics
- **Fire** (~1.0 MYA): Controlled fire use transforms diet, social behavior, protection from predators
- **Long-distance walking**: H. erectus body proportions (long legs, narrow pelvis) built for endurance

### Visual Character
- Wide-open grasslands with distant volcanic cones
- Olduvai Gorge — layered canyon walls revealing deep time
- Dmanisi — medieval fortress town above the hominin site
- Zhoukoudian caves — limestone chambers
- Stone tools scattered on ancient surfaces

### Mood
Momentum and expansion. Homo erectus is the breakout species — the first to leave Africa, the first to control fire, the first with a body built like ours. This era should feel like movement, like a species finding its stride.

## Era 4: The Great Diversification (~800–130 kya)
**Geological Period:** Middle Pleistocene
**Climate:** Intensifying 100,000-year glacial cycles; extreme cold in north, variable conditions in Africa
**Setting:** Africa, Europe, Asia — multiple continents simultaneously

### Key Species
- Homo heidelbergensis (~800–200 kya)
- Homo neanderthalensis (~400–40 kya) — emerging
- Denisovans (~300–30 kya)
- Homo naledi (~335–236 kya)
- Homo longi (~146 kya)
- Homo sapiens (~315 kya –) — emerging

### Environmental Context
The Middle Pleistocene is when the hominin story becomes truly global and complex. Multiple species occupy different parts of the Old World simultaneously. In Europe, proto-Neanderthals adapt to glacial conditions. In Africa, populations of archaic Homo are evolving toward sapiens — but not in a single location. In Asia, Denisovans occupy a vast but poorly understood range. And on the Indonesian island of Flores, a remarkable dwarfing event is underway.

### Key Challenges
- Extreme glacial cold in Europe (Neanderthal adaptation)
- Environmental fragmentation isolating populations
- Multiple hominin species competing for similar resources
- Need for more sophisticated hunting strategies (cooperative hunts, wooden spears)

### Key Innovations
- **Prepared-core technique (Levallois)**: Predetermining flake shape before striking — represents cognitive planning
- **Wooden spears** (Schöningen, ~500 kya): Balanced, javelin-like weapons
- **Symbolic behavior emerging**: Red ochre use, possible burial, body disposal
- **Fire mastery**: Regular, controlled hearths; cooking becomes standard

### Visual Character
- Ice sheets in the distance; tundra-steppe landscapes
- Sima de los Huesos — deep cave shaft with piled bones
- African rift lakes and highland plateaus
- Denisova Cave — Siberian mountainside

### Mood
Complexity and parallel evolution. Multiple human species alive at once, each going their own way. Some will converge, some will vanish. The world is getting crowded with different kinds of humans.

## Era 5: The Coexistence (~130–40 kya)
**Geological Period:** Late Pleistocene
**Climate:** Last Interglacial → Last Glacial Period; dramatic fluctuations
**Setting:** Africa, Europe, Asia, Indonesia, Philippines, Australia

### Key Species (coexisting)
- Homo sapiens (Africa → global)
- Homo neanderthalensis (Europe, western Asia)
- Denisovans (central/eastern Asia)
- Homo floresiensis (Flores, Indonesia)
- Homo luzonensis (Philippines)
- Homo naledi (South Africa — if still surviving)
- Late H. erectus (Java — if still surviving)

### Environmental Context
This is the most remarkable period in hominin history: at least 4–5 different human species alive simultaneously. Homo sapiens, having emerged in Africa, begins dispersing out of Africa ~65–50 kya. They encounter Neanderthals in the Levant and Europe, Denisovans in Asia, and island hominins in Southeast Asia. Within 30,000 years, all non-sapiens species disappear.

### Key Challenges
- Multiple human species encountering each other — what happened at these meetings?
- Interbreeding (Neanderthal DNA, Denisovan DNA in modern humans)
- Climate instability (Heinrich events, Dansgaard-Oeschger cycles)
- Volcanic events (Toba ~74 kya — debated bottleneck)

### Key Innovations
- **Upper Paleolithic revolution** (~50–40 kya): Blade technology, art, personal ornaments, musical instruments
- **Cave art** (Chauvet ~36 kya, Lascaux ~17 kya, El Castillo ~40 kya)
- **Long-distance trade networks**: Obsidian, shells traded over hundreds of kilometers
- **Watercraft**: Reaching Australia requires sea crossings of 60–90 km

### Visual Character
- Cave interiors lit by flickering torchlight
- Painted cave walls — horses, bison, handprints
- Tundra-steppe of Ice Age Europe — mammoth, woolly rhino
- Coastal landscapes — the route to Australia
- Gorham's Cave, Gibraltar — the last Neanderthal refugium

### Mood
Encounter and loss. This is where the story narrows from many to one. The mood should carry both wonder (the art, the creativity) and a sense of elegy for the species that didn't make it.

## Era 6: Alone (~40 kya – present)
**Geological Period:** Late Pleistocene → Holocene
**Climate:** Last Glacial Maximum (~26–19 kya) → warming → Holocene stability
**Setting:** Global — every continent except Antarctica

### Key Species
- Homo sapiens (sole surviving hominin)

### Environmental Context
After the extinction of the last Neanderthals (~40 kya) and the disappearance of the remaining archaic species, Homo sapiens is alone. The Last Glacial Maximum (~26–19 kya) pushes populations into refugia, but as the ice retreats, humans expand into every habitable corner of the planet. The Americas are colonized (~15 kya or earlier), and by the start of the Holocene (~11.7 kya), humans have reached virtually everywhere except Antarctica and the most remote Pacific islands.

### Key Themes
- Solitude — we are the only human species for the first time in 7 million years
- Genetic legacy — Neanderthal and Denisovan DNA still circulating in our genomes
- What made us different? (Social networks, language, cultural cumulation, luck)
- The price of success — megafauna extinction, environmental transformation

### Visual Character
- Global diversity of landscapes — tundra, desert, tropical forest, coast, mountain
- Modern humans in all their variation
- DNA helix motif — the genetic legacy of extinct species living on in us
- Museum cases — the bones we've found, the story we've reconstructed

### Mood
Reflection and responsibility. We won — but we're alone, and we carry the DNA of those who didn't make it. The tone should be contemplative, not triumphalist.
