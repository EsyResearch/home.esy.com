# TIMELINE — Turkana Boy: The First Modern Body

Two-scale timeline for the Turkana Boy essay. Part 1 covers the deep-time evolutionary and archaeological context. Part 2 covers discovery, excavation, and reinterpretation in modern scholarship.

---

## Part 1: Deep Time (1.8 Ma – 1.5 Ma)

### [1.8–1.77 Ma] — Earliest *Homo* appears outside Africa at Dmanisi
**Location**: Dmanisi, Georgia  
**Significance**: Early *Homo* populations are already moving beyond Africa by this time. Dmanisi provides the best comparison for the "walk out of Africa" argument surrounding Turkana Boy.  
**Section Reference**: 9 — The Walk  
**Source**: [S11] Lordkipanidze et al. 2007

---

### [1.76 Ma] — Earliest securely dated Acheulean in West Turkana
**Location**: Kokiselei, West Turkana, Kenya  
**Significance**: Large cutting tools appear in the same broader basin as KNM-WT 15000. This anchors the essay's technology section in the local landscape rather than treating Acheulean as abstract background.  
**Section Reference**: 8 — The Tools  
**Source**: [S10] Lepre et al. 2011

---

### [1.7–1.6 Ma] — Early *Homo erectus/ergaster* body plan stabilizes
**Location**: East Africa  
**Significance**: Fossils from this interval show a clear shift toward larger body size, longer lower limbs, and increased ranging ability. Turkana Boy becomes the most complete embodiment of this transition.  
**Section Reference**: 4 — The Body; 5 — The Legs  
**Source**: [S07] Antón 2003; [S08] Ruff & Walker 1993

---

### [~1.6 Ma] — KNM-WT 15000 lives near Lake Turkana
**Location**: Nariokotome, west side of Lake Turkana, Kenya  
**Significance**: A juvenile early *Homo* individual grows up in a hot open landscape with access to stone-tool traditions already established in the basin.  
**Section Reference**: 2–9  
**Source**: [S01] Brown et al. 1985; [S14] Smithsonian

---

### [~1.53 Ma] — Death of Turkana Boy
**Location**: Nariokotome, Kenya  
**Significance**: The individual dies before full adulthood, leaving a uniquely informative snapshot of early *Homo* growth, anatomy, and pathology. African Fossils dates the specimen to approximately 1.53 Ma.  
**Section Reference**: 1 — The Abscess; 2 — The Age  
**Source**: [S15] African Fossils; [S01] Brown et al. 1985

---

## Part 2: Discovery and Interpretation (1984 – 2026)

### [August 1984] — Kamoya Kimeu spots the first skull fragment
**Location**: Southern side of the seasonal Nariokotome River  
**Significance**: Kamoya Kimeu identifies a matchbox-sized skull fragment on an eroded slope. What begins as a routine field find becomes one of the most important fossil discoveries in paleoanthropology.  
**Section Reference**: 10 — The Finder  
**Source**: [S15] African Fossils; [S01] Brown et al. 1985

---

### [1984–1989] — Excavation seasons recover the skeleton
**Location**: Nariokotome III, West Turkana  
**Significance**: Multiple field seasons recover 108 bones, making KNM-WT 15000 the most complete early *Homo* skeleton ever found.  
**Section Reference**: 11 — The Excavation  
**Source**: [S02] Walker & Leakey 1993

---

### [1985] — Discovery announced in *Nature*
**Location**: Global scientific community  
**Significance**: Brown, Harris, Leakey, and Walker publish the original description, immediately establishing the fossil as a major benchmark for early human anatomy.  
**Section Reference**: 11 — The Excavation; 12 — The Inheritance  
**Source**: [S01] Brown et al. 1985

---

### [1993] — Definitive monograph published
**Location**: Springer-Verlag  
**Significance**: *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton* consolidates the anatomy, field context, and interpretive debates into a single reference work that still structures much later discussion.  
**Section Reference**: 2–12  
**Source**: [S02] Walker & Leakey 1993

---

### [1993] — Wheeler models early *Homo* thermoregulatory advantage
**Location**: Journal of Human Evolution  
**Significance**: A tall, linear body is modeled as a major heat-management advantage in open equatorial habitats, helping explain why Turkana Boy's proportions matter.  
**Section Reference**: 4 — The Body  
**Source**: [S09] Wheeler 1993

---

### [1999] — Speech debate intensifies around breathing control
**Location**: AJPA  
**Significance**: MacLarnon and Hewitt popularize the idea that thoracic innervation and breathing control place constraints on speech evolution, with KNM-WT 15000 becoming a key fossil in the discussion.  
**Section Reference**: 7 — The Voice  
**Source**: [S12] MacLarnon & Hewitt 1999

---

### [2002] — Stature-at-death estimates are revised downward
**Location**: *Human Evolution*  
**Significance**: Ohman and colleagues challenge the classic image of Turkana Boy as already exceptionally tall at death, showing how dependent the narrative is on chosen growth models.  
**Section Reference**: 2 — The Age; 3 — The Growth  
**Source**: [S06] Ohman et al. 2002

---

### [2003] — Antón synthesizes the natural history of *Homo erectus*
**Location**: Yearbook of Physical Anthropology  
**Significance**: Turkana Boy is placed within the larger story of *H. erectus* as a species of larger bodies, broader ranges, and ecological expansion.  
**Section Reference**: 4, 5, 9, 12  
**Source**: [S07] Antón 2003

---

### [2004] — Smith reframes the age problem
**Location**: AJPA  
**Significance**: Skeletal age and dental age are shown not to align neatly even in recent humans, weakening simplistic conclusions about Turkana Boy's exact maturity profile.  
**Section Reference**: 2 — The Age; 3 — The Growth  
**Source**: [S03] Smith 2004

---

### [2009] — Dean & Smith push the specimen younger by dental evidence
**Location**: Springer volume on the first humans  
**Significance**: Dental microstructure strengthens the argument for an age around 8–9 years and therefore a faster-than-modern-human developmental pattern.  
**Section Reference**: 2 — The Age; 3 — The Growth  
**Source**: [S04] Dean & Smith 2009

---

### [2011] — Acheulean origins in West Turkana pushed earlier
**Location**: *Nature*  
**Significance**: Lepre et al. place large cutting tools in the basin by 1.76 Ma, making Acheulean technology an even stronger contextual backdrop to Turkana Boy's species.  
**Section Reference**: 8 — The Tools  
**Source**: [S10] Lepre et al. 2011

---

### [2015] — Vertebral canal interpretation is challenged
**Location**: *Journal of Human Evolution*  
**Significance**: Meyer and Haeusler argue that the classic speech-limitation reading of KNM-WT 15000 may overstate what the vertebrae actually tell us.  
**Section Reference**: 7 — The Voice  
**Source**: [S13] Meyer & Haeusler 2015

---

### [2015] — Adult growth projections reassessed again
**Location**: *Journal of Human Evolution*  
**Significance**: Ruff and Burgess reopen the question of how tall and heavy KNM-WT 15000 would have become, showing that the specimen remains interpretively alive, not settled.  
**Section Reference**: 3 — The Growth; 12 — The Inheritance  
**Source**: [S05] Ruff & Burgess 2015

---

### [2026] — Turkana Boy remains the clearest body-plan bridge in the cluster
**Location**: Esy paleoanthropology series  
**Significance**: Within the cluster, Lucy shows early bipedal compromise, *Homo naledi* shows cognitive surprise, and Turkana Boy shows the emergence of the first clearly modern-ranging body.  
**Section Reference**: 12 — The Inheritance  
**Source**: Cluster synthesis based on [S01]–[S15]
