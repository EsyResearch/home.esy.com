# Key Figures & Persons — Turkana Boy

**Build**: opus-4-6 (Claude Opus 4.6)

---

## Kamoya Kimeu (1940–2022)

**Role**: Discoverer of KNM-WT 15000  
**Significance**: Kenya's most prolific fossil hunter. Self-taught paleontologist who discovered more hominin fossils than any other individual in history, including KNM-WT 15000 (Turkana Boy), KNM-ER 1813 (Homo habilis), and KNM-ER 3733 (Homo erectus). Worked with the Leakey family for over four decades. Found the first cranial fragment of Turkana Boy on August 22, 1984, at Nariokotome III on the west shore of Lake Turkana.  
**Quote context**: Kimeu rarely gave interviews but his discovery method — walking systematic transects, scanning eroded surfaces — is well documented in Leakey & Lewin 1992.  
**Image needs**: Portrait photograph if available; alternatively, the Nariokotome III site landscape.

## Richard Leakey (1944–2022)

**Role**: Director of the excavation; co-author of the definitive monograph  
**Significance**: Led the five-season excavation (1984–1988) that recovered 108 bone fragments of KNM-WT 15000. His popular writings (Origins Reconsidered, 1992) provide the narrative account of the discovery and its impact on understanding human evolution.  
**Image needs**: Photograph of Leakey at excavation site if available.

## Alan Walker (1938–2017)

**Role**: Senior anatomist; co-editor of the Nariokotome monograph  
**Significance**: Coordinated the technical analysis. His work with Ruff on body size and shape established the quantitative framework for understanding Turkana Boy's proportions.

## Holly Smith

**Role**: Dental development specialist  
**Significance**: Authored the pivotal chapter on physiological age (Smith 1993) that established the dental vs. skeletal age discrepancy. Her later work (2004) refined the age-at-death estimates to 7.6–8.8 years.

## Peter Wheeler

**Role**: Thermoregulation theorist  
**Significance**: Provided the mechanistic explanation for why the Turkana Boy body plan evolved. His modeling (1991, 1993) of heat loss, water economy, and body geometry in open equatorial environments remains the standard framework.

## Ann MacLarnon

**Role**: Vertebral canal analyst  
**Significance**: First to note the narrow thoracic vertebral canal in KNM-WT 15000 and hypothesize that it implied limited respiratory control for speech (MacLarnon 1993). Her interpretation, while later corrected, shaped two decades of debate about Homo erectus cognitive capacity.

## Bruce Latimer & James Ohman

**Role**: Pathology specialists  
**Significance**: Identified skeletal dysplasia in KNM-WT 15000 (Latimer & Ohman 2001), demonstrating that the narrow vertebral canal was a congenital anomaly rather than a species-typical trait. This reframed the speech debate entirely.

## Marc Meyer & Martin Haeusler

**Role**: Spinal cord evolution researchers  
**Significance**: Confirmed the dysplasia interpretation (Meyer & Haeusler 2015) and showed that other Homo erectus specimens have normal vertebral canal dimensions. Closed the loop on the speech-canal question for this specimen.

## David Lordkipanidze

**Role**: Director of Dmanisi excavations  
**Significance**: Led the team that recovered postcranial remains from Dmanisi, Georgia (1.77 Ma), showing that early Homo dispersed out of Africa before the full Turkana Boy body plan was established (Lordkipanidze et al. 2007).
