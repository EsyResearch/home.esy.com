## Table 2 — Age-at-Death Estimates (D2)

Backs **D2: Age Dispute** — the split-panel visualization comparing dental vs skeletal age assessments.

| Researcher(s) | Year | Method | Estimated Age | Key Reasoning | Citation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smith, B.H. | 1993 | Dental eruption (modern human standards) | ~11 years | Second molars erupting; compared to modern human eruption schedule (Smith 1993 in Walker & Leakey 1993) |
| Smith, B.H. | 1993 | Dental eruption (great ape standards) | ~7.6–8 years | Same eruption status compared against chimpanzee/gorilla schedules; implies faster maturation (Smith 1993) |
| Smith, B.H. | 2004 | Revised dental comparative analysis | ~8–9 years | Intermediate developmental schedule between apes and modern humans (Smith 2004) |
| Dean, M.C. et al. | 2001 | Perikymata counting (histological) | ~8 years | Enamel microstructure indicates faster tooth formation than modern humans; supports younger age (Dean et al. 2001) |
| Dean, M.C. & Smith, B.H. | 2009 | Integrated dental histology + eruption | ~8–8.8 years | Combined histological and eruption data; growth intermediate between apes and humans (Dean & Smith 2009) |
| Ruff, C.B. & Walker, A. | 1993 | Skeletal maturity (epiphyseal fusion) | ~11–12 years | Long bone lengths and epiphyseal fusion status compared to modern human standards (Ruff & Walker 1993) |
| Ruff, C.B. & Burgess, M.L. | 2015 | Revised skeletal growth assessment | ~11–13 years | Updated modern reference data; supports older skeletal age under human growth model (Ruff & Burgess 2015) |
| Antón, S.C. | 2003 | Review synthesis | ~8–11 years | Notes the discrepancy is a function of which developmental model is applied; does not resolve (Antón 2003) |

**Summary for D2**:
- **Dental age consensus**: ~7.6–9 years (faster-than-human maturation model)
- **Skeletal age consensus**: ~11–13 years (modern human growth model)
- **Working range for essay**: 8–11 years, presented as a genuine debate rather than a single number

**D2 implementation note**: Split panel — left shows dental eruption sequence with age markers; right shows skeletal maturity markers. The two timelines diverge visually to make the discrepancy legible.

**Wheeler's key finding**: A tall, narrow body form (like Turkana Boy's) reduces solar heat gain by ~30% compared to a shorter, wider body of equivalent mass in equatorial open environments, and requires ~30% less water to maintain thermal balance (Wheeler 1993).
