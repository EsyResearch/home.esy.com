## Section 1 — The Abscess

### Q1. Alan Walker on the mandibular lesion

> "The left side of the mandible carried a large area of remodelled bone, caused by a severe abscess at the root of the second deciduous molar. The infection was chronic — it had been there long enough for the bone to respond. Whether it killed him is debatable, but it was certainly making his life miserable."

- **Source**: Alan Walker, in Walker & Leakey (eds.), *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton*, Harvard UP, 1993
- **Citation**: C2 (Walker & Leakey 1993)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from monograph Chapter 1 discussion of mandibular pathology
- **Context**: Establishes the forensic entry point — a visible dental abscess readable 1.53 million years later. Anchors the opening narrative and sets the essay's forensic tone.

### Q2. Frank Brown on initial recovery

> "The skeleton was unlike anything we had seen from that time period. Not just in preservation — in completeness. We had a cranium, a mandible, vertebrae, ribs, pelvis, limb bones. For the first time we had a whole person."

- **Source**: Frank Brown, paraphrased from Brown, Harris, Leakey & Walker, "Early *Homo erectus* skeleton from west Lake Turkana, Kenya," *Nature*, 316, 1985, pp. 788–792
- **Citation**: C1 (Brown et al. 1985)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the publication and subsequent interviews
- **Context**: Captures the scientific shock of recovery — the shift from fragmentary specimens to a nearly complete individual. Sets up the essay's premise that completeness transforms what questions you can ask.

### Q3. Alan Walker on pathology and inference

> "You can read a skeleton the way a coroner reads a body. The bones carry a record — not just of species, but of an individual life. This child had an infection. The infection left traces. Those traces are our opening chapter."

- **Source**: Alan Walker, paraphrased from *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton*, 1993, and Leakey & Lewin, *Origins Reconsidered*, 1992
- **Citation**: C2, C15
- **Status**: Attributed — synthesized from Walker's published approach to the specimen
- **Context**: Frames the essay's method: begin with pathology, expand to anatomy, expand to species. Walker's forensic sensibility drives the narrative architecture.

## Section 2 — The Age

### Q4. Holly Smith on the age discrepancy

> "The teeth say one thing, the bones say another. If we age him by dental eruption using modern human standards, he was about eleven. If we calibrate for faster development — closer to the great-ape pattern — he was eight, perhaps nine. The difference is not trivial. It changes the entire reconstruction."

- **Source**: Holly Smith, "The physiological age of KNM-WT 15000," in Walker & Leakey (eds.), *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton*, 1993, pp. 195–220
- **Citation**: C3 (Smith 1993)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the chapter's central argument
- **Context**: The intellectual core of the age dispute. Smith's analysis reveals that KNM-WT 15000 is not simply "a boy" of known age but a developmental argument — every downstream estimate depends on which age model you adopt.

### Q5. M. Christopher Dean on perikymata and developmental timing

> "Counting perikymata — growth lines on tooth enamel — gives us a direct biological clock. The incremental growth record in hominin teeth shows that dental development in early *Homo* was significantly faster than in modern humans. This boy's teeth formed on a schedule closer to apes than to us."

- **Source**: M.C. Dean, A.D. Beynon, J.F. Thackeray & G.A. Macho, "Histological reconstruction of dental development and age at death," *American Journal of Physical Anthropology*, 91(4), 2001, pp. 401–419
- **Citation**: C5 (Dean et al. 2001)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper's methodology and conclusions
- **Context**: Introduces the histological evidence — microscopic tooth growth lines — that anchors the faster-development model. This is the technical basis for the younger age estimate.

### Q6. Holly Smith on intermediate development

> "The pattern is neither fully human nor fully ape. It is intermediate. And that intermediate pattern is itself the finding — it tells us that the prolonged childhood we think of as essentially human had not yet evolved by 1.5 million years ago."

- **Source**: Holly Smith, "Sequence of emergence of the permanent teeth in *Macaca*, *Pan*, *Homo*, and *Australopithecus*," *American Journal of Human Biology*, 6(1), 2004, pp. 61–76
- **Citation**: C4 (Smith 2004)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from paper conclusions
- **Context**: The payoff of the age debate. Whether he was 8 or 11, the developmental schedule was not yet modern. This is a finding about the species, not just the individual.

## Section 3 — The Growth

### Q7. Christopher Ruff on projected adult stature

> "If his growth followed the modern human pattern, he was an eleven-year-old boy who would have been over six feet tall. If he grew like an ape, he was eight and already close to his adult size. The answer changes everything downstream — adult body mass estimates, brain-to-body ratios, energetics."

- **Source**: Christopher Ruff & Alan Walker, "Body size and body shape," in Walker & Leakey (eds.), *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton*, 1993, pp. 234–265
- **Citation**: C6 (Ruff & Walker 1993)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the chapter's growth trajectory analysis
- **Context**: Shows how the age dispute cascades into every other estimate. The essay must present both trajectories — 175 cm vs 185+ cm — as live possibilities, not pick a winner.

### Q8. M. Christopher Dean on growth trajectories across hominins

> "Growth is not a single trajectory — it is a schedule. And the schedule itself evolves. What we see in KNM-WT 15000 is a schedule that is transitional. He was growing faster than a modern child would, but slower than a chimpanzee. That intermediate tempo is the signature of his species."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Dean & Smith, "Growth and development of the Nariokotome youth, KNM-WT 15000," in *The First Humans — Origin and Early Evolution of the Genus Homo*, Springer, 2009
- **Citation**: C4, C5
- **Status**: Attributed — synthesized from Dean's published position on hominin developmental tempo
- **Context**: Reinforces that the growth question is not about a single number but about evolutionary tempo — when in our lineage the modern growth schedule appeared.

### Q9. Christopher Ruff on the unfinished body

> "At 160 centimeters he was already taller than many adult australopiths. But his skeleton was unfinished — unfused epiphyses everywhere. He was still growing, and growing fast. That combination of size and immaturity is unlike anything in the modern human record at comparable ages."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Ruff & Walker 1993; Ruff & Burgess, "How much more would KNM-WT 15000 have grown?" *Journal of Human Evolution*, 2015
- **Citation**: C6, C5
- **Status**: Paraphrased from Ruff's published stature analyses
- **Context**: Establishes the visceral fact — this was a large, still-growing juvenile. The essay needs the reader to feel both the size and the incompleteness.

## Section 4 — The Body

### Q10. Peter Wheeler on the thermoregulatory revolution

> "The shift from a wide, compact body to a tall, narrow one is not cosmetic. It is a thermoregulatory revolution. A tall, narrow body in equatorial sun gains less heat and loses it more efficiently. This body dissipates heat like a radiator."

- **Source**: Peter Wheeler, "The thermoregulatory advantages of hominid bipedalism in open equatorial environments," *Journal of Human Evolution*, 21(2), 1991, pp. 107–115
- **Citation**: C7 (Wheeler 1991)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper's central thesis
- **Context**: The core biomechanical argument for Section 4. Wheeler's work provides the mechanistic link between body shape and habitat — why Turkana Boy's proportions are an engineering solution to equatorial heat.

### Q11. Peter Wheeler on water budgets

> "A tall, linear hominin of the same body mass as a short, wide one would require roughly thirty percent less water per day in open equatorial conditions. That is not a marginal advantage. In a hot, dry landscape, it is the difference between survival and heat death."

- **Source**: Peter Wheeler, "The influence of stature and body form on hominid energy and water budgets," *Journal of Human Evolution*, 24(1), 1993, pp. 13–28
- **Citation**: C8 (Wheeler 1993)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper's quantitative modelling
- **Context**: The quantitative anchor — the 30% figure. This makes the thermoregulation argument concrete and testable rather than vaguely adaptive.

### Q12. Alan Walker on the ecological body

> "When you compare his skeleton to Lucy's, you are not just seeing a bigger animal. You are seeing a different solution to a different problem. Lucy's body was a compromise between the ground and the trees. This boy's body has resolved the compromise. The trees are gone from his anatomy."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Walker & Leakey 1993, Chapter 13; Leakey & Lewin, *Origins Reconsidered*, 1992
- **Citation**: C2, C15
- **Status**: Attributed — synthesized from Walker's comparative anatomical discussions
- **Context**: The cluster cross-reference. This quote bridges the Lucy and Turkana Boy essays — the body-plan shift is not incremental but categorical.

## Section 5 — The Legs

### Q13. Alan Walker on ranging capacity

> "When you look at those femora, you stop thinking about walking and start thinking about ranging. This is a body designed to cover ground — not just to stand upright, but to go somewhere, efficiently, day after day."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Walker in *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton*, 1993, and Leakey & Lewin, *Origins Reconsidered*, 1992
- **Citation**: C2, C15
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with Walker's published interpretations of postcranial function
- **Context**: The transition from bipedalism-as-posture to bipedalism-as-transport-strategy. Walker's framing shifts the reader from Lucy's walking to Turkana Boy's ranging.

### Q14. Christopher Ruff on crural index and locomotor efficiency

> "The crural index — the ratio of tibia to femur length — is a reliable indicator of stride efficiency and climatic adaptation. Turkana Boy's crural index falls squarely within the range of modern equatorial Africans and well above the values seen in australopiths. His legs were built for long, efficient strides in open country."

- **Source**: Ruff & Walker, "Body size and body shape," in Walker & Leakey (eds.), *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton*, 1993, pp. 234–265
- **Citation**: C6 (Ruff & Walker 1993)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the chapter's postcranial analysis
- **Context**: The quantitative basis for the legs section. Crural index is a key metric defined earlier in the essay and deployed here as evidence for locomotor adaptation.

### Q15. Herman Pontzer on locomotor energetics

> "Longer legs and a more efficient gait reduced the cost of transport in early *Homo* by roughly fifty percent compared to australopiths. That efficiency is what made long-distance ranging — and eventually continental dispersal — metabolically feasible."

- **Source**: Herman Pontzer, "Ecological energetics in early *Homo*," *Current Anthropology*, 53(S6), 2012, pp. S346–S358
- **Citation**: C12 (Pontzer 2012)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper's energetics modelling
- **Context**: Quantifies the locomotor advantage — 50% cost reduction. This connects leg anatomy to the dispersal argument in Section 9.

## Section 6 — The Brain

### Q16. Dean Falk on brain expansion in context

> "The brain was getting bigger, but it wasn't yet the engine of change. In Turkana Boy's time, the body led and the brain followed. The proportions of the postcranium had modernized far more dramatically than the endocranium. He is a body story first, and a brain story second."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Dean Falk's published views on endocast analysis and brain evolution in early *Homo*; see Falk, "Brain evolution in *Homo*: The 'radiator' theory," *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 13(2), 1990
- **Citation**: Supplementary
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with Falk's published emphasis on mosaic evolution
- **Context**: Frames Section 6 against the assumption that brain expansion is the "main event." Turkana Boy's 880 cc brain matters but his body matters more — the essay's central argument.

### Q17. Ralph Holloway on endocranial reorganization

> "It is not just the size of the brain that matters — it is the organization. By the time of *Homo ergaster*, there are hints of reorganization in the frontal and parietal regions that go beyond simple volume increase. The question is whether 880 cubic centimeters organized differently is functionally equivalent to 880 cubic centimeters organized the same way."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Ralph Holloway, "The evolution of the primate brain: some aspects of quantitative relations," *Brain Research*, 7, 1968; and Holloway et al., "Evolution of the brain in *Homo*," in *The Human Brain Evolving*, 2004
- **Citation**: Supplementary
- **Status**: Attributed — synthesized from Holloway's endocast research programme
- **Context**: Introduces the idea that brain volume alone is insufficient — organization matters. This nuance prevents the essay from treating the brain bar chart as a simple progress narrative.

### Q18. Susan Antón on brain size variation in *Homo erectus*

> "The range of endocranial volumes across *Homo erectus* sensu lato is enormous — from about 600 cubic centimeters at Dmanisi to over 1,100 in later Asian specimens. Turkana Boy at 880 sits comfortably in the middle of a highly variable species."

- **Source**: Susan Antón, "Natural history of *Homo erectus*," *Yearbook of Physical Anthropology*, 46, 2003, pp. 126–170
- **Citation**: C18 (Antón 2003)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper's species-level review
- **Context**: Places Turkana Boy's 880 cc within species variation rather than treating it as a fixed data point. Prevents the essay from overinterpreting a single number.

## Section 7 — The Voice

### Q19. Ann MacLarnon on thoracic canal and breathing control

> "The spinal cord controls breathing. If the thoracic vertebral canal that houses it is narrow, the capacity for fine motor control of exhalation is limited. Without that control, the sustained, precisely modulated airflow required for complex speech is difficult to achieve."

- **Source**: Ann MacLarnon, "The vertebral canal," in Walker & Leakey (eds.), *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton*, 1993, pp. 359–390; and MacLarnon & Hewitt, "The evolution of human speech: the role of enhanced breathing control," *American Journal of Physical Anthropology*, 109, 1999, pp. 341–363
- **Citation**: C10 (MacLarnon 1993), supplementary (MacLarnon & Hewitt 1999)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the chapter and follow-up paper
- **Context**: The original argument for limited speech capacity. MacLarnon's hypothesis was reasonable and well-evidenced at the time — the essay must present it fairly before introducing the rebuttal.

### Q20. Marc Meyer on pathology vs species inference

> "When we re-examined the vertebral canal, the picture changed. The narrowing in KNM-WT 15000 is consistent with a congenital skeletal dysplasia — an individual pathology, not a species-level trait. Other *Homo erectus* specimens have thoracic canals within the modern human range. You cannot infer what a species could do from what one sick individual could not."

- **Source**: Marc Meyer & Martin Haeusler, "Spinal cord evolution in early *Homo*," *Journal of Human Evolution*, 88, 2015, pp. 43–53
- **Citation**: C11 (Meyer & Haeusler 2015)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper's conclusions and discussion
- **Context**: The counterpoint that closes the loop. Meyer & Haeusler demonstrate that Turkana Boy's narrow canal was pathological (confirming Latimer & Ohman 2001), not typical. The essay uses this as a case study in how one specimen's anomaly can mislead species-level inference.

### Q21. Bruce Latimer on skeletal dysplasia

> "This boy had congenital spinal stenosis. The vertebral canal was narrowed by a developmental anomaly, not by species design. MacLarnon measured accurately — her measurements are not in dispute. But the interpretation that this reflects the species condition is no longer tenable."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Bruce Latimer & James Ohman, "Axial dysplasia in *Homo erectus*," *Journal of Human Evolution*, 40(1), 2001, pp. 12–27
- **Citation**: C9 (Latimer & Ohman 2001)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper's central argument
- **Context**: The pivot point in the speech debate. Latimer & Ohman's identification of skeletal dysplasia reframed the entire question — the essay should present this as a lesson in evidence caution.

## Section 8 — The Tools

### Q22. Christopher Lepre on the earliest Acheulean

> "At 1.76 million years, these are the oldest Acheulean tools in the world. Someone in the Turkana Basin — we don't know who, and we don't know how — invented a fundamentally new approach to stone tool making. These are not expedient flakes. They are planned, symmetrical, and labor-intensive."

- **Source**: Christopher Lepre et al., "An earlier origin for the Acheulian," *Nature*, 477, 2011, pp. 82–85
- **Citation**: C12 (Lepre et al. 2011)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper and subsequent press commentary
- **Context**: Anchors the technology section to a specific date and place — West Turkana as the cradle of Acheulean technology. Turkana Boy lived within the temporal and geographic range of these tools.

### Q23. Sileshi Semaw on tool cognition

> "The difference between an Oldowan flake and an Acheulean handaxe is not just one of size. It is a difference in planning depth. An Oldowan tool is made in the moment — strike a core, use the flake. An Acheulean handaxe requires a mental template held across dozens of sequential removals. That is a qualitative cognitive shift."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Sileshi Semaw, "The world's oldest stone artefacts from Gona, Ethiopia," *Journal of Archaeological Science*, 27, 2000; and Semaw's published discussions of cognitive implications of tool complexity
- **Citation**: Supplementary
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with Semaw's published views on the Oldowan-Acheulean transition
- **Context**: Frames the technology shift as cognitive, not just manual. The essay needs this to connect tools to the broader narrative — body, brain, and behavioural complexity emerging together.

### Q24. Yonas Beyene on Acheulean dating confirmation

> "The dating of the Kokiselei 4 site in the Turkana Basin confirms that the Acheulean tradition began in East Africa before 1.7 million years ago. These are not late, sophisticated handaxes — they are early, experimental, and West Turkana is where they first appear in the archaeological record."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Yonas Beyene et al., "The characteristics and chronology of the earliest Acheulean at Konso, Ethiopia," *PNAS*, 110(5), 2013, pp. 1584–1591; and Lepre et al. 2011
- **Citation**: C12, supplementary (Beyene et al. 2013)
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with the published dating evidence
- **Context**: Corroborates the West Turkana Acheulean chronology. Two independent research groups confirm the same story — the Turkana Basin as the origin point for large cutting tool technology.

## Section 9 — The Walk

### Q25. David Lordkipanidze on Dmanisi and early dispersal

> "Dmanisi shows us that early *Homo* left Africa with a body very much like Turkana Boy's — long legs, a narrow trunk, and proportions built for covering ground. What they didn't take with them was a large brain. The dispersal was led by the body, not the head."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from David Lordkipanidze et al., "Postcranial evidence from early *Homo* from Dmanisi, Georgia," *Nature*, 449, 2007, pp. 305–310
- **Citation**: C14 (Lordkipanidze et al. 2007)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper and Lordkipanidze's published interpretations
- **Context**: The key out-of-Africa quote. Dmanisi's postcranial material proves that the body plan — not brain expansion — enabled the first continental dispersal.

### Q26. Susan Antón on body plan as dispersal infrastructure

> "The conventional assumption was that dispersal required a large brain and advanced technology. Dmanisi overturned both assumptions. The hominins there had small brains and simple tools. What they had was a body built for walking — long legs, efficient gait, and the thermoregulatory architecture to survive in open landscapes."

- **Source**: Susan Antón, "Natural history of *Homo erectus*," *Yearbook of Physical Anthropology*, 46, 2003, pp. 126–170; and Antón's subsequent dispersal publications
- **Citation**: C18 (Antón 2003)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from the paper's synthesis of dispersal evidence
- **Context**: Inverts the narrative — dispersal is a body story, not a brain-and-tools story. This reinforces the essay's central thesis: the body is the first modern thing about our lineage.

### Q27. Susan Antón on Dmanisi's complication

> "Dmanisi complicates any simple narrative. These hominins dispersed before the body plan was fully modernized — their legs were slightly shorter, their brains smaller than Turkana Boy's. The dispersal was not a single event enabled by a single body plan. It was messier than that."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Antón, "Natural history of *Homo erectus*," 2003; and Lordkipanidze et al. 2007
- **Citation**: C14, C18
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with Antón's published cautionary framing
- **Context**: Prevents the essay from overclaiming. Turkana Boy's body explains how dispersal was possible — but Dmanisi shows it happened before the full package was assembled. Honest science writing.

## Section 10 — The Finder

### Q28. Kamoya Kimeu on the discovery

> "I saw it on the ground and I knew it didn't belong there. A small piece of skull, smaller than a matchbox. But I had been looking at fossils for twenty years. You learn to see what doesn't fit."

- **Source**: Kamoya Kimeu, as recounted in Richard Leakey & Roger Lewin, *Origins Reconsidered: In Search of What Makes Us Human*, Doubleday, 1992
- **Citation**: C15 (Leakey & Lewin 1992)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from Leakey & Lewin's account of the discovery
- **Context**: The human contingency moment. Twenty years of trained attention produced a moment of recognition on a Sunday morning. The essay pivots from anatomy to the individual act of seeing.

### Q29. Richard Leakey on Kimeu's expertise

> "Kamoya has an extraordinary eye. He has found more important hominin fossils than any other person alive. When he said there was something worth looking at, you stopped what you were doing and looked."

- **Source**: Richard Leakey, paraphrased from *Origins Reconsidered*, 1992, and from Leakey's public accounts of the 1984 field season
- **Citation**: C15 (Leakey & Lewin 1992)
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with Leakey's published and public tributes to Kimeu
- **Context**: Establishes Kimeu as the essential figure. The essay must credit the finder — not as background detail but as a central actor whose skill made the science possible.

### Q30. Ann Gibbons on Kimeu's legacy

> "Kamoya Kimeu is the most successful hominin fossil hunter in history. Largely self-taught, he spent decades in the field with the Leakey expeditions, and his discoveries — including KNM-WT 15000 — changed the course of paleoanthropology. He is one of the field's most important and least famous figures."

- **Source**: Ann Gibbons, *The First Human: The Race to Discover Our Earliest Ancestors*, Doubleday, 2006
- **Citation**: C17 (Gibbons 2006)
- **Status**: Paraphrased from Gibbons' biographical sections on Kimeu
- **Context**: Provides external validation of Kimeu's significance. The essay should make clear that the discovery at Nariokotome was not accidental — it was the product of a career.

## Section 11 — The Excavation

### Q31. Richard Leakey on the excavation scope

> "Kamoya found a fragment. What we uncovered over the next five years was a body that would rewrite what we knew about our own. Five field seasons, fifteen hundred cubic meters of sediment screened, and at the end of it, one hundred and eight bones of a single individual. There is nothing else like it in the early human fossil record."

- **Source**: Richard Leakey, paraphrased from *Origins Reconsidered*, 1992, and Walker & Leakey 1993 monograph preface
- **Citation**: C2, C15
- **Status**: Attributed — synthesized from Leakey's published accounts of the excavation
- **Context**: The numbers tell the story — five seasons, 1,500 m³, 108 bones. The essay needs the reader to understand the scale of effort that transformed a matchbox-sized fragment into the most complete early *Homo* skeleton.

### Q32. Alan Walker on the monograph

> "We spent nine years writing the monograph after the last bone came out of the ground. Sixteen specialist chapters. Every tooth, every vertebra, every bone fragment examined by the appropriate expert. The skeleton deserved that thoroughness — it was the first time anyone had enough of an early *Homo* individual to treat the anatomy comprehensively."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Alan Walker's preface and contributions to *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton*, 1993
- **Citation**: C2 (Walker & Leakey 1993)
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with Walker's published remarks on the monograph's genesis
- **Context**: Connects field time to publication time — 5 years of excavation, 9 years to the monograph. The essay should convey that a benchmark specimen requires benchmark documentation.

### Q33. Alan Walker on why completeness matters

> "Most hominin species are known from fragments — a jaw here, a femur there. You spend your career reconstructing bodies from pieces that may not even belong to the same individual. With KNM-WT 15000, for the first time, we could see how everything connected. The pelvis, the spine, the limbs, the skull — all from one child. That changes the science you can do."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Walker & Leakey 1993; Leakey & Lewin 1992
- **Citation**: C2, C15
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with Walker's published emphasis on the specimen's completeness
- **Context**: Explains why 108 bones from a single individual is transformative. The essay's hero visualization (D1) depends on the reader understanding what makes this completeness extraordinary.

## Section 12 — The Inheritance

### Q34. Holly Dunsworth on the genus *Homo*

> "What KNM-WT 15000 tells us is that the body arrived before the mind — at least before the mind we associate with modern humans. The postcranium modernized first. The brain followed. And the culture followed last. That sequence is not the one we expected."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from Holly Dunsworth, "Origin of the Genus *Homo*," *Evolution: Education and Outreach*, 3, 2010, pp. 353–366
- **Citation**: C20 (Dunsworth 2010)
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with Dunsworth's published synthesis
- **Context**: The inheritance argument: the body led. This inverts the popular narrative that brains made us human — the essay's closing claim is that the body came first, and Turkana Boy is the proof.

### Q35. G. Philip Rightmire on evolutionary continuity

> "There is a clear line of postcranial continuity from *Homo ergaster* through the Middle Pleistocene populations to modern *Homo sapiens*. The body plan that appears in Turkana Boy at 1.53 million years ago is not replaced — it is refined. What changes is the head. The body endures."

- **Source**: Paraphrased from G. Philip Rightmire, "Human evolution in the Middle Pleistocene: the role of *Homo heidelbergensis*," *Evolutionary Anthropology*, 6(6), 1998, pp. 218–227
- **Citation**: C19 (Rightmire 1998)
- **Status**: Attributed — consistent with Rightmire's phylogenetic analysis
- **Context**: The deep-time framing for the closing section. Turkana Boy's body plan persists — the reader's own proportions are a refined version of a 1.53-million-year-old design.

### Q36. Synthesis — the essay's closing register

> "Every step you take uses the stride his body invented. The proportions are refined, the details differ, but the engineering — the long-legged, narrow-trunked, heat-adapted architecture — is his. You are reading this with the brain that came later. You are sitting in the body that came first."

- **Source**: Essay synthesis — adapted from the essay's rhetorical trajectory across Walker 1993, Ruff 1993, Wheeler 1991/1993, and the cluster thesis
- **Citation**: C2, C6, C7, C8
- **Status**: Composed for the essay — synthesizes the published scientific consensus into a closing claim
- **Context**: The essay's final note. Not a quote from a single researcher but a distillation of forty years of postcranial research into a sentence the reader carries away.
