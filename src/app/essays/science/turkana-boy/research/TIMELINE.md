# Timeline — Turkana Boy: The First Modern Body

---

## Table 1: Deep Time (6 Ma to Present)

Contextualizes KNM-WT 15000 within the full arc of hominin evolution. Dates reflect current consensus ranges; individual entries cite key specimens or sites.

| Date | Event | Key Specimen / Site | Significance for Essay |
|------|-------|---------------------|------------------------|
| ~6–7 Ma | *Sahelanthropus tchadensis* | TM 266-01-060-1 (Toumaï), Toros-Menalla, Chad | Earliest possible hominin; foramen magnum position debated. Sets the outer boundary of the hominin lineage. |
| ~5.8 Ma | *Orrorin tugenensis* | BAR 1002'00 femur, Tugen Hills, Kenya | Early bipedal indicators in the femoral morphology; contested. |
| ~4.4 Ma | *Ardipithecus ramidus* | ARA-VP-6/500 (Ardi), Aramis, Ethiopia | Facultative biped in a woodland habitat. Grasping hallux suggests significant arboreal component. Body plan far from modern. |
| ~3.9 Ma | *Australopithecus anamensis* | KNM-KP 29285, Kanapoi & Allia Bay, Kenya | Early obligate biped at Lake Turkana. Tibia shows committed bipedal weight-bearing. |
| **~3.2 Ma** | **Lucy (*A. afarensis*)** | **AL 288-1, Hadar, Ethiopia** | **Bipedal but short-legged, wide-pelved, and long-armed. The compromise body — cluster sibling essay anchor.** |
| ~3.0 Ma | *Australopithecus africanus* | Stw 573 (Little Foot), Sterkfontein, South Africa | Southern African australopith; more derived hand morphology. |
| ~2.8 Ma | Earliest *Homo* (mandible) | LD 350-1, Ledi-Geraru, Ethiopia | Jaw fragment with traits transitional between *Australopithecus* and *Homo*. |
| ~2.6 Ma | First stone tools (Oldowan) | Gona, Ethiopia | Earliest confirmed knapped stone tools. Predates clear association with genus *Homo*. |
| ~2.5 Ma | *Paranthropus aethiopicus* | KNM-WT 17000 (Black Skull), West Turkana | Robust lineage divergence; discovered by Kimeu. |
| ~2.0 Ma | *Homo habilis* sensu lato | OH 7, Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania | Small-bodied, still australopith-like postcranially. Brain ~510–687 cc. |
| **~1.9 Ma** | ***Homo ergaster/erectus* appears in Africa** | **KNM-ER 3733, East Turkana, Kenya** | **First tall, narrow-bodied hominin. The lineage that will produce Turkana Boy.** |
| **~1.77 Ma** | **Dmanisi hominins** | **D2700, D4167 postcranial, Dmanisi, Georgia** | **Earliest confirmed *Homo* outside Africa. Small-brained (~630 cc) but postcranially similar to Turkana Boy. Migration section anchor.** |
| **~1.76 Ma** | **Earliest Acheulean tools** | **Kokiselei 4, West Turkana, Kenya (Lepre et al. 2011)** | **Large cutting tools requiring bilateral symmetry and forward planning. Same basin as KNM-WT 15000.** |
| **~1.53 Ma** | **KNM-WT 15000 (Turkana Boy)** | **Nariokotome III, West Turkana, Kenya** | **THE SPECIMEN. Dies at 8–11 years, already 160 cm. 108 bones recovered. The most complete early *Homo* skeleton.** |
| ~1.0 Ma | *Homo erectus* in Java | Trinil 2, Sangiran, Java, Indonesia | Classic *H. erectus*. Dispersal to Southeast Asia well established by this date. |
| ~0.78 Ma | *Homo antecessor* | Atapuerca (Gran Dolina), Spain | Earliest European hominin with modern facial features. |
| ~0.6 Ma | *Homo heidelbergensis* | Bodo cranium, Ethiopia; Mauer mandible, Germany | Large-brained (~1,100–1,400 cc) middle Pleistocene hominin. |
| **~335–236 ka** | ***Homo naledi*** | **DH1 composite, Rising Star Cave, South Africa** | **Small-brained (~465–560 cc) but potentially complex behaviour. Cluster sibling essay anchor.** |
| ~300 ka | Earliest *Homo sapiens* | Jebel Irhoud, Morocco | Modern humans emerge in North/North-West Africa. |
| ~200 ka | Earliest *H. sapiens* in East Africa | Omo Kibish 1, Ethiopia | Anatomically modern form in the Turkana Boy's own region. |
| ~70–50 ka | Out-of-Africa dispersal of *H. sapiens* | Multiple sites | Modern humans disperse globally — using the same long-legged, heat-adapted body plan Turkana Boy drafted 1.5 Ma earlier. |

## Table 2: Discovery and Excavation Timeline

| Date | Event | Details | Source |
|------|-------|---------|--------|
| **August 23, 1984** | Kamoya Kimeu discovers cranial fragment | A single fragment of cranial vault bone, smaller than a matchbox, found on an eroded slope at Nariokotome III, west shore of Lake Turkana. Kimeu was walking a systematic surface survey. | Brown et al. 1985; Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| August–September 1984 | First excavation season | Richard Leakey's team begins systematic excavation. Multiple postcranial elements recovered alongside additional cranial fragments. The skeleton's extraordinary completeness becomes apparent. | Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| **1985** | Second season; formal description published | Additional skeletal elements recovered. Brown et al. publish the first formal description in *Nature* (vol. 316, pp. 788–792), establishing the specimen's taxonomic assignment to *Homo erectus* and reporting preliminary measurements. | Brown et al. 1985 |
| 1986 | Third season | Continued excavation. Pelvis and additional vertebral elements recovered. Excavation area expanded to ~28 m². | Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| 1987 | Fourth season | Further postcranial recovery. Total sediment screened approaches 1,500 m³. | Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| 1989 | Fifth and final season | Last elements recovered. Total: 108 bone fragments from a single individual — the most complete early *Homo* skeleton in the fossil record. | Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| **1993** | Walker & Leakey monograph published | *The Nariokotome Homo erectus Skeleton* (Harvard University Press). 16 specialist chapters covering anatomy, pathology, growth, geochronology, and ecology. The definitive technical reference. | Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| 1993 | Smith dental age analysis | B. Holly Smith publishes the dental developmental age assessment (7.6–8.8 years), establishing the age discrepancy with skeletal maturation estimates. | Smith 1993 (in Walker & Leakey) |
| 1993 | MacLarnon vertebral canal analysis | Ann MacLarnon reports the narrow thoracic vertebral canal, launching the speech/breathing debate. | MacLarnon 1993 (in Walker & Leakey) |
| 2001 | Latimer & Ohman pathology reanalysis | Identify skeletal dysplasia (congenital vertebral anomaly), suggesting the narrow spinal canal is pathological rather than species-typical. | Latimer & Ohman 2001 |
| 2004 | Smith developmental reassessment | Refines age-at-death estimates and growth models for early *Homo*. | Smith 2004 |
| 2009 | Dean & Smith growth synthesis | Major synthesis of growth and development data for KNM-WT 15000, integrating dental microstructure evidence. | Dean & Smith 2009 |
| 2015 | Ruff & Burgess growth projections | Most current adult growth trajectory projections using revised body-mass estimation methods. | Ruff & Burgess 2015 |
| 2015 | Meyer & Haeusler spinal cord reassessment | Confirm dysplasia interpretation; demonstrate other *H. erectus* specimens have normal vertebral canal dimensions. Effectively close the KNM-WT 15000–specific speech debate. | Meyer & Haeusler 2015 |

## Table 3: Key Measurements — KNM-WT 15000

All values from primary literature. Where multiple estimates exist, ranges are provided with source attribution.

| Measurement | Value | Notes | Source |
|-------------|-------|-------|--------|
| **Age at death (dental)** | 7.6–8.8 years | Based on dental eruption sequence and enamel microstructure. Indicates faster maturation than modern humans. | Smith 1993; Dean & Smith 2009 |
| **Age at death (skeletal)** | ~11–13 years | Based on epiphyseal fusion assessed against modern human standards. Higher estimate assumes modern-human-like growth tempo. | Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| **Stature at death** | ~160 cm (5′3″) | Already taller than many adult modern human females and adult *A. afarensis*. | Ruff & Walker 1993 |
| **Projected adult stature** | ~180–185 cm (5′11″–6′1″) | Range depends on growth model adopted. Lower if ape-like fast maturation (near adult size at death); higher if modern-human-like extended growth. | Ruff & Walker 1993; Ruff & Burgess 2015 |
| **Body mass at death** | ~48–50 kg | Estimated from femoral head diameter and other postcranial regressions. | Ruff & Walker 1993; Ruff 2010 |
| **Projected adult body mass** | ~63–68 kg | Depends on growth trajectory model. | Ruff & Burgess 2015 |
| **Endocranial volume (ECV)** | ~880 cc | Juvenile value. ~63% of modern human mean (~1,400 cc). Well within *Homo erectus* range (600–1,100 cc). | Holloway et al. 2004; Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| **Projected adult ECV** | ~900–910 cc | Based on ~15% postnatal brain growth expected after dental age of 8–9 years. | Antón 2003 |
| **Femoral length** | ~432 mm | Long relative to body and to australopith femora. | Ruff & Walker 1993 |
| **Tibial length** | ~380 mm | High tibia-to-femur ratio reflects tropical adaptation. | Ruff & Walker 1993 |
| **Crural index** (tibia/femur × 100) | ~88 | Matches modern tropical East Africans (~88–91). Significantly higher than Lucy (~85) and modern cold-adapted populations (~80). | Ruff & Walker 1993; Ruff 1994 |
| **Bi-iliac breadth** | ~228 mm | Narrow relative to stature (ratio ~0.15). Contrasts strongly with Lucy's wide, flaring pelvis (~0.19). | Ruff & Walker 1993 |
| **Bi-iliac / stature ratio** | ~0.15 | Linear, heat-adapted body form consistent with Allen's Rule for equatorial open-country organisms. | Ruff 1994 |
| **Humeral length** | ~297 mm | Shorter relative to femur than in australopiths, reflecting reduced upper-limb emphasis. | Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| **Humerofemoral index** (humerus/femur × 100) | ~69 | Low compared to australopiths (~85–95) and apes (~100+). Close to modern human range (~68–72). | Ruff & Walker 1993 |
| **Brachial index** (radius/humerus × 100) | ~78–80 (est.) | Tropical proportion; relatively long distal segments. | Holliday 2012 |
| **Thoracic vertebral canal** | Narrower than modern human mean | Originally interpreted as species-typical (MacLarnon 1993); now attributed to individual pathology (skeletal dysplasia). Other *H. erectus* specimens show normal dimensions. | MacLarnon 1993; Latimer & Ohman 2001; Meyer & Haeusler 2015 |
| **Skeletal completeness** | 108 fragments, ~40% of skeleton | Most complete early *Homo* skeleton ever found. Cranial, axial, and appendicular elements all represented. | Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| **Excavation area** | ~28 m² | Five seasons (1984–1989); ~1,500 m³ sediment screened. | Walker & Leakey 1993 |
| **Geological age of site** | ~1.53 Ma | Nariokotome III, Nachukui Formation, West Turkana, Kenya. Dated by ⁴⁰Ar/³⁹Ar and magnetostratigraphy. | Brown et al. 1985; McDougall et al. 2012 |
