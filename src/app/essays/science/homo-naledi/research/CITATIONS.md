## Discovery & Description

### 1. Berger et al. 2015 — Type Description

- **Authors:** Berger LR, Hawks J, de Ruiter DJ, Churchill SE, Schmid P, Delezene LK, Kivell TL, Garvin HM, Williams SA, DeSilva JM, Skinner MM, Musiba CM, Cameron N, Holliday TW, Harcourt-Smith WEH, Ackermann RR, Bastir M, Bogin B, Bolter D, Brophy J, Cofran ZD, Congdon KA, Deane AS, Dembo M, Drapeau M, Elliott MC, Feuerriegel EM, Garcia-Martinez D, Green DJ, Gurtov A, Irish JD, Kruger A, Laird MF, Marchi D, Meyer MR, Nalla S, Negash EW, Orr CM, Radovcic D, Schroeder L, Scott JE, Throckmorton Z, Tocheri MW, VanSickle C, Walker CS, Wei P, Zipfel B
- **Year:** 2015
- **Title:** "Homo naledi, a new species of the genus Homo from the Dinaledi Chamber, South Africa"
- **Journal:** eLife 4:e09560
- **DOI:** 10.7554/eLife.09560
- **Relevance:** Foundational paper. Describes H. naledi from over 1,550 fossil elements representing at least 15 individuals recovered from the Dinaledi Chamber. Establishes the mosaic morphology — small brain (~560 cc), curved fingers, but modern-like wrist, feet, and lower limbs — that makes naledi so difficult to place phylogenetically.
- **Verification:** VERIFIED
- **Tier:** 1

## Cave Geology & Taphonomy

### 4. Dirks et al. 2015 — Geological Context

- **Authors:** Dirks PHGM, Berger LR, Roberts EM, Kramers JD, Hawks J, Randolph-Quinney PS, Elliott M, Musiba CM, Churchill SE, de Ruiter DJ, Schmid P, Backwell LR, Belyanin GA, Boshoff P, Hunter KL, Holliday TW,"; Harcourt-Smith WEH, Ackermann RR, Kivell TL, Laird MF, Marchi D,"; Peixotto B,";"; Skinner MM, Williams SA, DeSilva JM, Zipfel B
- **Year:** 2015
- **Title:** "Geological and taphonomic context for the new hominin species Homo naledi from the Dinaledi Chamber, South Africa"
- **Journal:** eLife 4:e09561
- **DOI:** 10.7554/eLife.09561
- **Relevance:** Documents the geological setting of the Dinaledi Chamber: a nearly inaccessible space reached via a 12 m vertical chute ("the Chute") only 18 cm wide. Finds no evidence of water transport, carnivore activity, or mass-death events, supporting the hypothesis that bodies were deliberately deposited.
- **Verification:** VERIFIED
- **Tier:** 1

### 5. Val 2016 — Taphonomic Analysis

- **Authors:** Val A
- **Year:** 2016
- **Title:** "Deliberate body disposal by hominins in the Dinaledi Chamber, Cradle of Humankind, South Africa?"
- **Journal:** Journal of Human Evolution 96:145–148
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.jhevol.2016.02.004
- **Relevance:** Critical assessment of the deliberate-disposal hypothesis. Examines bone surface modifications, fragmentation patterns, and spatial distribution. Raises questions about whether taphonomic evidence alone can confirm intentional deposition versus other natural accumulation mechanisms.
- **Verification:** VERIFIED
- **Tier:** 1

### 6. Randolph-Quinney et al. 2016 — Taphonomy Response

- **Authors:** Randolph-Quinney PS, Backwell LR, Berger LR, Hawks J, Dirks PHGM, Roberts EM, Nhauro G, Kramers JD

## Anatomy

### 7. Kivell et al. 2015 — Hand Morphology

- **Authors:** Kivell TL, Deane AS, Tocheri MW, Orr CM, Schmid P, Hawks J, Berger LR, Churchill SE
- **Year:** 2015
- **Title:** "The hand of Homo naledi"
- **Journal:** Nature Communications 6:8431
- **DOI:** 10.1038/ncomms9431
- **Relevance:** Describes the H. naledi hand as a striking mosaic: curved, *Australopithecus*-like proximal phalanges (suggesting arboreal capability) paired with a human-like wrist and thumb architecture suited for tool use. Central to the essay's theme of evolutionary mosaicism.
- **Verification:** VERIFIED
- **Tier:** 1

### 8. Harcourt-Smith et al. 2015 — Foot Morphology

- **Journal:** Nature Communications 6:8432
- **DOI:** 10.1038/ncomms9432
- **Relevance:** The H. naledi foot is overwhelmingly modern in form — arched, with an adducted hallux and shortened toes — indicating committed bipedalism. The contrast with the primitive hand and shoulder underscores the mosaic body plan.

## Dating

### 17. Dirks et al. 2017 — Radiometric & Paleomagnetic Dating

- **Authors:** Dirks PHGM, Roberts EM, Hilbert-Wolf H, Kramers JD, Hawks J, Dosseto A, Duval M, Elliott M, Evans M, Grün R, Hellstrom J, Herries AIR, Joannes-Boyau R, Makhubela TV, Placzek CJ, Robbins J, Spandler C, Wiber J, Woodhead J, Berger LR
- **Year:** 2017
- **Title:** "The age of Homo naledi and associated sediments in the Rising Star Cave, South Africa"
- **Journal:** eLife 6:e24231
- **DOI:** 10.7554/eLife.24231
- **Relevance:** Dates H. naledi to 236,000–335,000 years ago using U-Th dating of flowstones, ESR and U-series dating of teeth, and paleomagnetic analysis of sediments. This unexpectedly young date means naledi overlapped with early *Homo sapiens* in Africa — the essay's central dramatic tension.
- **Verification:** VERIFIED
- **Tier:** 1

## Phylogenetics

### 18. Dembo et al. 2016 — Bayesian Phylogenetic Analysis

- **Authors:** Dembo M, Radovcic D, Garvin HM, Laird MF, Schroeder L, Scott JE, Brophy J, Ackermann RR, Musiba CM, de Ruiter DJ, Mooers AO, Collard M
- **Year:** 2016
- **Title:** "The evolutionary relationships and age of Homo naledi: An assessment using dated Bayesian phylogenetic methods"
- **Journal:** Journal of Human Evolution 97:17–26
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.jhevol.2016.04.008
- **Relevance:** Places H. naledi as a basal member of *Homo* using craniodental characters, potentially diverging near the origin of the genus (~2 Ma). The phylogenetic estimate of antiquity predicted an old date — making the later 236–335 ka result even more remarkable, since it implies long-term morphological stasis or ghost-lineage persistence.
- **Verification:** VERIFIED
- **Tier:** 1

### 20. Thackeray 2016 — Undiscovered Entrance Hypothesis

- **Authors:** Thackeray JF
- **Year:** 2016
- **Title:** "The possibility of lichen growth on bones of Homo naledi: Were they exposed to light?"
- **Journal:** South African Journal of Science 112(7/8):Art. #a0167
- **DOI:** 10.17159/sajs.2016/a0167
- **Relevance:** Proposes that an as-yet-undiscovered entrance to the Dinaledi Chamber may have existed, potentially explaining how bodies entered without deliberate disposal. If light reached the chamber, lichen traces on bone could provide evidence. A key counter-argument to the intentional-deposition hypothesis.

### 21. Berger et al. 2023a — Deliberate Burial

- **Authors:** Berger LR, Hawks J, Fuentes A,"; van der Made J,";"; de Ruiter DJ, Churchill SE,"; Dirks PHGM
- **Year:** 2023
- **Title:** "Evidence for deliberate burial of the dead by Homo naledi"
- **Journal:** eLife 12:RP89106
- **DOI:** 10.7554/eLife.89106
- **Relevance:** Claims that features in the Dinaledi Chamber sediments represent deliberate graves dug and filled by H. naledi — which would represent the oldest known burial by at least 100,000 years and the first by a non-*sapiens* hominin. Extremely controversial; critiqued for the strength of sedimentological evidence.

### 22. Berger et al. 2023b — Rock Engravings & Fire Use

- **Authors:** Berger LR, Hawks J, Fuentes A,"; de Ruiter DJ, Churchill SE, Dirks PHGM
- **Year:** 2023

- **Journal:** eLife 12:RP89102
- **DOI:** 10.7554/eLife.89102
- **Relevance:** Reports cross-hatched engravings on cave walls and evidence of fire use (soot deposits, burned bone) attributed to H. naledi.
