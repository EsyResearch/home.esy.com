# Key Figures — Homo naledi

## 1. Lee Berger

- **Affiliation:** University of the Witwatersrand, Johannesburg
- **Role:** Principal investigator. Led the Rising Star Expedition (2013–present). Orchestrated the discovery, excavation, analysis, and public communication of Homo naledi. Recruited the "underground astronauts" via Facebook and Twitter. Pushed for open-access publication and unprecedented speed of description. Later led the controversial 2023 claims of intentional burial and symbolic markings.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Berger, L.R. et al. (2015). *Homo naledi*, a new species of the genus Homo from the Dinaledi Chamber, South Africa. *eLife* 4:e09560.
  - Berger, L.R. et al. (2023). Evidence for deliberate burial in a cave by Homo naledi. *eLife* (preprint).
  - Berger, L.R. et al. (2010). *Australopithecus sediba*: A new species of Homo-like australopith from South Africa. *Science* 328(5975):195–204.
- **Why they matter:** Berger is the gravitational center of the naledi story. Every major claim — deliberate body disposal, the small brain paradox, fire use, engraving — originates from or passes through him. His approach broke paleoanthropology's culture of secrecy and slow publication, making him simultaneously celebrated and mistrusted. The essay cannot exist without him as both protagonist and lightning rod.

## 2. John Hawks

- **Affiliation:** University of Wisconsin-Madison
- **Role:** Co-lead scientist on the naledi project. Lead author on the Lesedi Chamber paper (2017). Principal voice on the dating results that placed naledi at 236,000–335,000 years ago, far younger than the morphology suggested. Advocates for open science and public engagement in paleoanthropology.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Hawks, J. et al. (2017). New fossil remains of Homo naledi from the Lesedi Chamber, South Africa. *eLife* 6:e24232.
  - Dirks, P.H.G.M. et al. (2017). The age of Homo naledi and associated sediments in the Rising Star Cave, South Africa. *eLife* 6:e24231.
- **Why they matter:** Hawks is the intellectual counterweight to Berger's showmanship — meticulous, articulate, deeply invested in the evolutionary implications. His blog and public writing make him the project's chief interpreter for non-specialist audiences. His framing of the dating shock is central to the essay's narrative arc.

## 3. Marina Elliott

- **Affiliation:** University of the Witwatersrand (PhD candidate at time of excavation, later postdoctoral researcher)
- **Role:** One of the original six "underground astronauts" — the team of slender early-career women scientists recruited to navigate the 18 cm (7-inch) chute into the Dinaledi Chamber. Became a key excavator and co-author on multiple naledi papers.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Co-author on Berger et al. (2015), Hawks et al. (2017), and numerous supplementary analyses.
- **Why they matter:** Elliott embodies the physical reality of the discovery — the claustrophobia, the danger, the bodily commitment required to retrieve the fossils. Her experience gives the essay its visceral, human dimension. She also represents the unconventional recruitment that made the project possible and controversial.

## 4. Paul Dirks

- **Affiliation:** James Cook University, Australia
- **Role:** Lead geologist on the Rising Star project. Mapped the cave system, analyzed the sedimentary context, and led the taphonomic analysis of how the bones arrived in the Dinaledi Chamber. Later led the dating study using six independent methods.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Dirks, P.H.G.M. et al. (2015). Geological and taphonomic context for the new hominin species Homo naledi from the Dinaledi Chamber, South Africa. *eLife* 4:e09561.
  - Dirks, P.H.G.M. et al. (2017). The age of Homo naledi and associated sediments in the Rising Star Cave, South Africa. *eLife* 6:e24231.
- **Why they matter:** Dirks's geological work is the foundation for the disposal hypothesis. His elimination of alternative explanations (water transport, predator accumulation, mass death event) is what forces the reader to confront the radical claim. Without his taphonomy, naledi is just bones; with it, naledi becomes a philosophical problem.

## 5. Tracy Kivell

- **Affiliation:** University of Kent, UK
- **Role:** Specialist in hand morphology. Described the Homo naledi hand, which showed a paradoxical combination: curved fingers suited for climbing alongside a long thumb and wrist architecture capable of tool use.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Kivell, T.L. et al. (2015). The hand of Homo naledi. *Nature Communications* 6:8431.
- **Why they matter:** Kivell's analysis of the hand is the clearest single illustration of naledi's mosaic anatomy — the feature that makes the species so disruptive to linear models of human evolution. The hand paradox is a natural visual anchor for the essay.

## 6. William Harcourt-Smith

- **Affiliation:** American Museum of Natural History / Lehman College, CUNY
- **Role:** Described the Homo naledi foot. Found it remarkably modern — nearly indistinguishable from Homo sapiens in many features — despite the primitive upper body.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Harcourt-Smith, W.E.H. et al. (2015). The foot of Homo naledi. *Nature Communications* 6:8432.
- **Why they matter:** The foot is the morphological counterpart to the hand paradox. A modern foot on a small-brained hominin walking the South African landscape 250,000 years ago is one of the essay's most visually and conceptually striking elements.

## 7. Heather Garvin

- **Affiliation:** Mercyhurst University, Pennsylvania
- **Role:** Estimated endocranial volume and contributed to body size analysis. Confirmed the small brain size (approximately 465–560 cc) relative to body size, placing naledi well below the traditional threshold for Homo.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Garvin, H.M. et al. (2017). Body size, brain size, and sexual dimorphism in Homo naledi from the Dinaledi Chamber. *Journal of Human Evolution* 111:119–138.
- **Why they matter:** Garvin's measurements provide the hard numbers behind the central paradox: can a brain this small belong to a species that disposes of its dead? Her data is the quantitative backbone of the essay's "what makes us human" question.

## 8. Aurore Val

- **Affiliation:** Independent researcher (previously Université de Toulouse)
- **Role:** Conducted independent taphonomic analysis of the Dinaledi assemblage. Published critical assessment questioning whether the evidence conclusively ruled out all non-deliberate accumulation mechanisms.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Val, A. (2016). Deliberate body disposal by hominins in the Dinaledi Chamber, Cradle of Humankind, South Africa? *Journal of Human Evolution* 96:145–148.
- **Why they matter:** Val represents the rigorous skepticism the disposal hypothesis must survive. Her work prevents the essay from becoming hagiography — she forces intellectual honesty about what we actually know versus what we infer.

## 9. María Martinón-Torres

- **Affiliation:** CENIEH (Centro Nacional de Investigación sobre la Evolución Humana), Burgos, Spain
- **Role:** Dental morphology specialist. Analyzed the naledi dentition, finding a mix of primitive and derived traits that complicated placement on the hominin family tree.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Martinón-Torres, M. (2016). On the misidentification of dental morphological traits in Homo naledi. *American Journal of Physical Anthropology* (commentary).
  - Contributing analysis in Berger et al. (2015).
- **Why they matter:** Teeth are the most common fossil evidence in paleoanthropology. Martinón-Torres's dental work connects naledi to the broader comparative framework of hominin evolution and grounds the species in the discipline's primary evidentiary tradition.

## 10. Francis Thackeray

- **Affiliation:** University of the Witwatersrand, Johannesburg
- **Role:** Senior paleoanthropologist who publicly questioned the disposal hypothesis. Raised the possibility that an undiscovered second entrance to the Dinaledi Chamber once existed, which would undermine the claim that bodies could only have been carried in deliberately.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Thackeray, J.F. (2016). The possibility of lichen growth on bones of Homo naledi: Were they deposited in a cave or in a rock shelter? *South African Journal of Science* 112(7/8).
- **Why they matter:** Thackeray is the most prominent "friendly critic" — a colleague at Berger's own institution who offered an alternative narrative. His counterargument is essential for the essay's intellectual balance.

## 11. Scott Williams

- **Affiliation:** New York University
- **Role:** Analyzed the vertebral column and trunk morphology of Homo naledi. Described the transition from a primitive, funnel-shaped upper trunk to a more human-like lower trunk.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Williams, S.A. et al. (2017). The vertebrae and ribs of Homo naledi. *Journal of Human Evolution* 104:136–154.
- **Why they matter:** The trunk bridges the hand and foot — it completes the picture of a creature whose body is a timeline of human evolution compressed into a single skeleton. Williams's work is visually important for any full-body reconstruction.

## 12. Mana Dembo

- **Affiliation:** Simon Fraser University, British Columbia
- **Role:** Led Bayesian phylogenetic analysis of Homo naledi's position in the hominin tree. Used statistical methods to evaluate competing hypotheses about where naledi fits relative to Homo erectus, Homo habilis, and australopiths.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Dembo, M. et al. (2016). The evolutionary relationships and age of Homo naledi: An assessment using dated Bayesian phylogenetic methods. *Journal of Human Evolution* 97:17–26.
- **Why they matter:** Dembo's statistical approach represents the modern toolkit of paleoanthropology — moving beyond visual comparison to probabilistic modeling. Her work is key to explaining why naledi's placement remains contested.

## 13. Rick Hunter

- **Affiliation:** Recreational caver (no academic affiliation)
- **Role:** Experienced caver who, along with Steven Tucker, first entered the Dinaledi Chamber on September 13, 2013. Hunter organized the caving expedition at Berger's request to survey the Rising Star cave system for fossils.
- **Key Publications:** N/A (acknowledged in Berger et al. 2015).
- **Why they matter:** Hunter represents the contingency of discovery — the fact that one of the most important fossil finds in decades happened because a paleoanthropologist asked cavers to poke around in unexplored passages. The essay's opening depends on this moment.

## 14. Steven Tucker

- **Affiliation:** Recreational caver (no academic affiliation)
- **Role:** The caver who physically spotted the first naledi fossils on the floor of the Dinaledi Chamber. Tucker squeezed through the "Superman's Crawl" and down the 12-meter chute, saw bone on the cave floor, and took the photographs that launched the Rising Star Expedition.
- **Key Publications:** N/A (acknowledged in Berger et al. 2015).
- **Why they matter:** Tucker's moment of recognition — seeing bone where no one expected it — is the essay's inciting incident. His photographs, sent to Berger, are the primary source for the entire story.

## 15. Ralph Holloway

- **Affiliation:** Columbia University, New York
- **Role:** Endocast specialist. Analyzed the internal braincase shape of Homo naledi, finding that despite its small volume, the naledi brain showed organizational features — particularly in the frontal lobe — more similar to Homo than to australopiths.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Holloway, R.L. et al. (2018). Endocast morphology of Homo naledi from the Dinaledi Chamber, South Africa. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 115(22):5738–5743.
- **Why they matter:** Holloway's endocast work is the strongest evidence that brain size alone does not determine cognitive capacity. This finding is the scientific foundation for the essay's central argument: that naledi challenges our definition of what it means to be human.

## 16. Tim White

- **Affiliation:** University of California, Berkeley
- **Role:** One of the most prominent critics of the naledi claims. Discoverer of Ardipithecus ramidus. Questioned the speed of publication, the disposal hypothesis, and the species designation. Represents the conservative establishment of paleoanthropology.
- **Key Publications:**
  - Public commentary and interviews (2015–2023); no formal rebuttal paper specifically on naledi, but extensive critical engagement in media and conferences.
- **Why they matter:** White is the voice of institutional skepticism — the senior figure who has seen spectacular claims come and go. His criticisms are not dismissible; they represent legitimate methodological concerns. The essay needs his perspective to avoid becoming a one-sided narrative.
