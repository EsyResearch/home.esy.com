# TIMELINE — Lucy: Before the Genus Homo

Two-scale chronological framework for the Lucy visual essay. Part 1 covers deep time (geological and evolutionary events from 5.0 to 2.0 Ma). Part 2 covers the modern history of discovery and scientific interpretation (1924–present).

---

## Part 1: Deep Time — The Pliocene World (5.0 Ma – 2.0 Ma)

Events are listed from oldest to most recent.

---

### [~5.0 Ma] — Opening of the Pliocene Epoch
**Date**: 5.333 Ma (Zanclean stage boundary)
**Location**: Global
**Significance**: The Pliocene begins with global temperatures 2–3°C warmer than pre-industrial levels and sea levels 15–25 m higher. This warm, relatively stable baseline is the world into which the australopiths radiate.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World
**Source**: [S13] Wynn et al. 2006, GSA Bulletin; Ravelo et al. 2004, Paleoceanography

---

### [~5.0–4.0 Ma] — East African Rift System Accelerates
**Date**: 5.0–4.0 Ma (continuous tectonic phase)
**Location**: East Africa — Afar Triangle, Ethiopian Rift, Gregory Rift (Kenya/Tanzania)
**Significance**: Accelerated rifting creates the topographic barriers (rift shoulders, volcanic highlands) that fragment East African habitats into a mosaic of microenvironments. The rift's subsiding basins accumulate the lacustrine and fluvial sediments that will preserve hominin fossils — including Lucy — millions of years later.
**Section Reference**: 1 — The Afar
**Source**: [S14] Wynn et al. 2006; WoldeGabriel et al. 2000, Nature

---

### [~4.4 Ma] — *Ardipithecus ramidus* at Aramis
**Date**: ~4.4 Ma
**Location**: Middle Awash, Ethiopia
**Significance**: The Aramis skeleton ("Ardi") represents an earlier stage of hominin locomotion — a facultative biped that still retained a grasping hallux. Ardi establishes that the last common ancestor did not resemble a modern chimpanzee, reframing the baseline from which Lucy's bipedalism evolved.
**Section Reference**: 4 — Built to Walk; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: [S16] Lovejoy 2009, Science; White et al. 2009, Science

---

### [~4.2–3.9 Ma] — *Australopithecus anamensis* Appears
**Date**: ~4.2–3.9 Ma
**Location**: Kanapoi, Allia Bay (Kenya); Middle Awash (Ethiopia)
**Significance**: *A. anamensis* is the earliest widely accepted australopith and the probable ancestor of *A. afarensis*. Its tibial morphology indicates bipedal locomotion, but its dentition and skull retain more primitive features. The anagenetic transition from *A. anamensis* to *A. afarensis* may have occurred ~3.9 Ma, though a 2019 *A. anamensis* cranium from Woranso-Mille complicates a simple linear model.
**Section Reference**: 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: [S17] Leakey et al. 1995, Nature; Haile-Selassie et al. 2019, Nature

---

### [~3.9 Ma] — *Australopithecus afarensis* First Appearance
**Date**: ~3.9 Ma (earliest securely dated *A. afarensis* specimens)
**Location**: Laetoli (Tanzania); Hadar and Woranso-Mille (Ethiopia)
**Significance**: *A. afarensis* appears in the fossil record and persists for roughly one million years — one of the longest-lived hominin species. Its geographic range spans at least 1,500 km from Tanzania to Ethiopia, indicating ecological versatility.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: [S01] Johanson & White 1979, Science; [S03] Kimbel & Delezene 2009, Journal of Human Evolution

---

### [~3.7–3.5 Ma] — Mid-Pliocene Warm Period Peak
**Date**: ~3.7–3.5 Ma (peak warmth phase)
**Location**: Global; African effects concentrated in East Africa
**Significance**: The mid-Pliocene warm period (MPWP) represents the last time Earth sustained temperatures comparable to near-future projections. In East Africa, warmer conditions support extensive woodland and gallery forest along watercourses. This warm, wooded baseline is the environment in which *A. afarensis* thrives — and it is the world that will later fragment as cooling begins.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World
**Source**: [S13] Wynn et al. 2006; Haywood et al. 2000, Journal of the Geological Society

---

### [3.66 Ma] — Laetoli Footprints
**Date**: 3.66 Ma (radiometrically dated tuff)
**Location**: Laetoli, Tanzania
**Significance**: Two parallel trackways in volcanic ash preserve the footprints of at least two bipedal hominins walking with a modern human-like heel-strike gait. Widely attributed to *A. afarensis*, they provide the earliest unambiguous evidence of habitual bipedalism in a natural context — complementing the skeletal evidence from Lucy's bones with behavioral evidence of walking.
**Section Reference**: 4 — Built to Walk
**Source**: [S08] Leakey & Hay 1979, Nature; Raichlen et al. 2010, PLoS ONE

---

### [~3.5–3.2 Ma] — *Kenyanthropus platyops* Range
**Date**: ~3.5–3.2 Ma
**Location**: West Turkana, Kenya
**Significance**: A flat-faced hominin contemporary with *A. afarensis*, *K. platyops* challenges the single-lineage model by demonstrating that at least two morphologically distinct hominin taxa coexisted in East Africa during Lucy's epoch. Its taxonomic validity is debated (some researchers attribute the flat face to taphonomic distortion), but if valid, it implies a bushier hominin tree than a simple *A. anamensis* → *A. afarensis* → *Homo* chain.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: [S10] Leakey et al. 2001, Nature

---

### [~3.5–3.3 Ma] — *Australopithecus deyiremeda* Range
**Date**: ~3.5–3.3 Ma
**Location**: Woranso-Mille, Afar Region, Ethiopia
**Significance**: Described in 2015, *A. deyiremeda* is a second contemporaneous taxon from the same geographic region as *A. afarensis*. Its robust mandible and smaller post-canine teeth distinguish it morphologically from *A. afarensis*, and its presence in the Afar means that multiple hominin species overlapped not just temporally but spatially. This further undermines simple linear models of human evolution.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: [S09] Haile-Selassie et al. 2015, Nature

---

### [~3.5–2.7 Ma] — Closure of the Central American Seaway Begins
**Date**: ~3.5–2.7 Ma (gradual shoaling; final closure ~2.7 Ma)
**Location**: Isthmus of Panama / Global ocean circulation effects
**Significance**: The progressive closure of the seaway between North and South America redirects warm Atlantic currents northward (intensifying the Gulf Stream), increases moisture transport to high northern latitudes, and ultimately triggers the buildup of Northern Hemisphere ice sheets. This is the distal tectonic cause of the cooling that will reshape African habitats and hominin evolution after ~3.0 Ma.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World
**Source**: [S13] Wynn et al. 2006; Haug & Tiedemann 1998, Nature

---

### [~3.4 Ma] — Earliest Possible Cut Marks on Bone (Dikika)
**Date**: ~3.4 Ma
**Location**: Dikika, Ethiopia
**Significance**: McPherron et al. (2010) reported cut-marked bovid bones from a Dikika context predating the earliest known stone tools by 800,000 years, attributing them to *A. afarensis*. The claim is highly contested — some researchers attribute the marks to trampling or crocodile tooth scratches. If valid, it would push the origin of tool use back to Lucy's species; if not, it remains a cautionary tale about equifinality in taphonomy.
**Section Reference**: 7 — What She Ate; 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: McPherron et al. 2010, Nature; Domínguez-Rodrigo et al. 2012, PNAS (critique)

---

### [~3.3 Ma] — Selam (DIK-1-1) Lives and Dies at Dikika
**Date**: ~3.3 Ma
**Location**: Dikika, Ethiopia (Sidi Hakoma Member)
**Significance**: Selam is a nearly complete skeleton of an approximately 3-year-old *A. afarensis* child — the most complete juvenile australopith ever found. Her hyoid bone (the first recovered from any early hominin) suggests an ape-like vocal tract. Her scapula resembles a gorilla's, supporting arboreal capability in young *A. afarensis*. She extends the species' known ontogenetic range and is often called "Lucy's baby," though she lived roughly 100,000 years earlier.
**Section Reference**: 5 — Or Built to Climb?; 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: [S07] Alemseged et al. 2006, Nature

---

### [~3.3 Ma] — Lomekwian Stone Tools at Lomekwi 3
**Date**: ~3.3 Ma
**Location**: West Turkana, Kenya
**Significance**: Harmand et al. (2015) reported stone tools predating the Oldowan by 700,000 years. If confirmed, these "Lomekwian" tools are contemporary with *A. afarensis* and *K. platyops*, implying that stone tool manufacture predates the genus *Homo*. The maker is unknown; attribution remains speculative.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: Harmand et al. 2015, Nature

---

### [~3.2 Ma] — Lucy (AL 288-1) Lives and Dies at Hadar
**Date**: ~3.18 Ma (dated by the KH-2 basaltic tuff and BKT-2 tuff bracketing)
**Location**: Hadar, Afar Region, Ethiopia (Kada Hadar Member of the Hadar Formation)
**Significance**: The individual who will become the most famous fossil in paleoanthropology dies at approximately 25–30 years of age on the floodplain of a meandering river system. She is roughly 107 cm tall, weighs approximately 29 kg, and is fully adult with fused epiphyses and erupted third molars. Her body is buried rapidly enough by fluvial sediment to preserve ~40% of her skeleton — an extraordinary recovery rate for any hominin fossil.
**Section Reference**: 2 — November 24, 1974; 3 — Forty Percent; 9 — The Fall
**Source**: [S01] Johanson & White 1979; [S02] Johanson & Edey 1981; [S04] Kappelman et al. 2016, Nature

---

### [~3.2 Ma] — The First Family (AL 333) Assemblage
**Date**: ~3.2 Ma (same stratigraphic interval as Lucy)
**Location**: Hadar, Ethiopia (locality AL 333)
**Significance**: At least 13 *A. afarensis* individuals — males, females, juveniles, and infants — died in a single depositional event at AL 333, less than 3 km from Lucy's locality. This catastrophic assemblage provides the best evidence for group structure in *A. afarensis*: the size range implies pronounced sexual dimorphism, and the mixed age profile suggests a social group rather than a mating aggregation. The cause of the mass death remains debated (flash flood is the most cited hypothesis).
**Section Reference**: 8 — Among Her Kind
**Source**: [S01] Johanson & White 1979; [S15] Reno et al. 2003, PNAS

---

### [~3.2 Ma] — Hadar Paleoenvironment at Lucy's Death
**Date**: ~3.18 Ma
**Location**: Hadar, Afar Region, Ethiopia
**Significance**: Paleosol analysis, fossil pollen, faunal assemblages (hippopotami, crocodiles, colobine monkeys, bovids, proboscideans), and sedimentary facies indicate that Hadar at 3.2 Ma was a mosaic landscape: closed woodland along watercourses grading into more open bushland and grassland away from rivers. This is not savanna — it is a heterogeneous habitat offering both arboreal refugia and open foraging ground.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World; 7 — What She Ate
**Source**: [S12] Reed 1997, Journal of Human Evolution; [S13] Wynn et al. 2006

---

### [~3.0 Ma] — Milankovitch Precessional Forcing Intensifies
**Date**: ~3.0 Ma (strengthening orbital influence on African monsoon)
**Location**: East and North Africa
**Significance**: Precession-driven (~21 kyr) cycles in Earth's orbital geometry amplify the African monsoon, creating alternating wet and dry phases across East Africa. These high-frequency oscillations superimpose on the longer-term cooling trend, producing environmental variability that may have driven hominin adaptive flexibility — or pushed specialized species toward extinction.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World
**Source**: [S13] Wynn et al. 2006; deMenocal 2004, Earth and Planetary Science Letters

---

### [~2.95–2.9 Ma] — *Australopithecus afarensis* Last Appearance
**Date**: ~2.95–2.9 Ma (youngest securely dated *A. afarensis* specimens from Hadar)
**Location**: Hadar, Ethiopia
**Significance**: *A. afarensis* disappears from the fossil record around 2.9 Ma after a ~1-million-year run. This disappearance coincides with the onset of significant cooling and habitat fragmentation in East Africa. Whether the species went extinct outright, evolved into a descendant species (potentially early *Homo* or *Paranthropus*), or persisted undiscovered elsewhere remains unresolved.
**Section Reference**: 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: [S03] Kimbel & Delezene 2009

---

### [~2.8 Ma] — LD 350-1 Mandible (Earliest *Homo*?)
**Date**: ~2.8 Ma
**Location**: Ledi-Geraru, Afar Region, Ethiopia
**Significance**: A partial mandible from Ledi-Geraru, just 30 km from Hadar, shows a mosaic of *Australopithecus*-like and *Homo*-like features. Villmoare et al. (2015) interpreted it as the earliest known member of genus *Homo*, pushing the origin of our genus back 400,000 years and placing it in the temporal and geographic neighborhood of late *A. afarensis*. If correct, this is the strongest evidence that *A. afarensis* (or a close relative) gave rise to *Homo*.
**Section Reference**: 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: Villmoare et al. 2015, Science

---

### [~2.7 Ma] — Onset of Northern Hemisphere Glaciation
**Date**: ~2.75–2.7 Ma (marine isotope stage M2 and subsequent intensification)
**Location**: Global; African effects include increased aridity and habitat fragmentation
**Significance**: The buildup of major ice sheets in the Northern Hemisphere marks a fundamental shift in Earth's climate system. In Africa, the consequences are indirect but transformative: overall cooling, reduced monsoon moisture, and the progressive replacement of woodland with open grassland and shrubland. This is the environmental inflection point around which multiple hominin species appear or disappear — the engine behind Vrba's turnover pulse hypothesis.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World
**Source**: [S11] Vrba 1985, South African Journal of Science; deMenocal 2004

---

### [~2.7–2.3 Ma] — *Paranthropus aethiopicus* Range
**Date**: ~2.7–2.3 Ma
**Location**: West Turkana, Kenya; Omo Valley, Ethiopia
**Significance**: The "Black Skull" (KNM-WT 17000) exemplifies the first appearance of the robust (megadont) lineage — massive chewing apparatus, sagittal crest, small brain — representing a radically different adaptive strategy from the one that would produce *Homo*. The robust lineage's appearance roughly coincides with the disappearance of *A. afarensis*, raising questions about whether environmental change drove both a speciation event (robust australopiths) and an extinction (*A. afarensis*).
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: Walker et al. 1986, Nature; [S11] Vrba 1985

---

### [~2.6 Ma] — Earliest Oldowan Stone Tools at Gona
**Date**: ~2.6 Ma (radiometrically dated)
**Location**: Gona, Afar Region, Ethiopia
**Significance**: The Gona tool assemblage is the earliest securely dated Oldowan industry. Found just 100 km from Hadar, these simple flaked cobbles appear shortly after the disappearance of *A. afarensis* and close to the first appearance of *Homo*. The maker is unknown — early *Homo*, late *Australopithecus garhi*, or another species could be responsible. The proximity to Hadar underscores the Afar's centrality to hominin evolution.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: Semaw et al. 1997, Nature; Semaw 2000, Journal of Archaeological Science

---

### [~2.5 Ma] — *Australopithecus garhi* at Bouri
**Date**: ~2.5 Ma
**Location**: Bouri, Middle Awash, Ethiopia
**Significance**: *A. garhi* was initially proposed as a possible link between *Australopithecus* and *Homo*, partly because cut-marked bones were found nearby. Its long femur-to-humerus ratio is more human-like than that of *A. afarensis*, but its brain size remains small. Its phylogenetic position is uncertain — it could be ancestral to *Homo*, a dead-end lineage, or part of the broader late-Australopithecus radiation.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: Asfaw et al. 1999, Science

---

### [~2.5–2.0 Ma] — Vrba's Turnover Pulse Window
**Date**: ~2.5–2.0 Ma
**Location**: Sub-Saharan Africa (most pronounced in East and southern Africa)
**Significance**: Elisabeth Vrba's turnover pulse hypothesis proposes that a sharp increase in species origination and extinction rates across African mammals — including hominins — was driven by the climatic cooling and aridification that peaked around 2.5 Ma. The simultaneous appearance of *Homo*, *Paranthropus*, and new bovid species and the disappearance of *A. afarensis* and woodland-adapted fauna support the hypothesis, though the timing is debated and alternative models (variability selection, habitat-specific responses) have been proposed.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World; 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: [S11] Vrba 1985, South African Journal of Science; Vrba 1995, in Vrba et al. (eds.)

---

### [~3.3–2.1 Ma] — *Australopithecus africanus* Range (South Africa)
**Date**: ~3.3–2.1 Ma
**Location**: Sterkfontein, Makapansgat, Taung (South Africa)
**Significance**: The southern African counterpart to *A. afarensis*. Originally described from the Taung Child in 1924, *A. africanus* shares many features with Lucy's species but exhibits a more globular braincase and less prognathic face. Whether the two species are sister taxa, ancestor-descendant, or independently derived from a common ancestor remains an active phylogenetic question. Their parallel existence across 1,500+ km of the African continent suggests that australopith diversification was widespread by the mid-Pliocene.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: Dart 1925, Nature; Clarke 2019, Journal of Human Evolution

---

### [~2.0 Ma] — End of the Pliocene / Onset of the Pleistocene
**Date**: 2.58 Ma (formal Pliocene–Pleistocene boundary, Gelasian stage); effective environmental shift ~2.0 Ma
**Location**: Global
**Significance**: By 2.0 Ma, Northern Hemisphere glacial cycles are well established, East African environments have shifted decidedly toward open grassland, *Homo* (including *H. habilis* and early *H. erectus*) is present, and the robust *Paranthropus* lineage is diversifying. The world Lucy inhabited has been transformed. The essay's deep-time window closes here.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: [S11] Vrba 1985; deMenocal 2004

## Part 2: Discovery & Science Timeline (1924 – Present)

Events are listed in chronological order.

---

### [1924] — Raymond Dart Discovers the Taung Child
**Date**: November 1924 (discovery); February 1925 (publication)
**Location**: Taung, South Africa (Buxton Limeworks quarry)
**Significance**: Dart receives a fossil skull and endocast of a juvenile primate from a lime quarry in South Africa. He identifies it as an intermediate form between apes and humans — the first australopith ever described. The discovery is met with decades of skepticism from the British paleoanthropological establishment, who favor Piltdown Man as the model for early human evolution. The Taung Child establishes southern Africa as hominin territory and introduces the genus *Australopithecus*.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries; 12 — Dinkinesh
**Source**: Dart 1925, Nature 115: 195–199

---

### [1925] — Dart Publishes *Australopithecus africanus* in Nature
**Date**: 7 February 1925
**Location**: Publication: London (Nature)
**Significance**: Dart's four-page paper names *Australopithecus africanus* and argues that the Taung Child's foramen magnum position indicates upright posture. The claim that a small-brained African ape could be ancestral to humans contradicts the reigning brain-first paradigm. It takes nearly three decades for the field to accept Dart's interpretation — a cautionary backdrop for understanding the more rapid (but still contentious) reception of Lucy fifty years later.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: Dart 1925, Nature

---

### [1959] — Mary Leakey Discovers *Paranthropus boisei* at Olduvai
**Date**: 17 July 1959
**Location**: Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania (FLK I)
**Significance**: "Zinj" — originally named *Zinjanthropus boisei*, now *Paranthropus boisei* — is the first major hominin fossil from East Africa. Its massive molars and robust cranium represent the hyper-specialized end of the australopith radiation. The discovery catalyzes international attention (and funding) for East African paleoanthropology, directly enabling the fieldwork programs that will lead to Lucy's discovery fifteen years later.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: Leakey 1959, Nature 184: 491–493

---

### [1973] — International Afar Research Expedition Begins at Hadar
**Date**: Autumn 1973 (first field season)
**Location**: Hadar, Afar Region, Ethiopia
**Significance**: Donald Johanson (Cleveland Museum of Natural History), Maurice Taieb (CNRS, France), and Yves Coppens (Collège de France) launch the International Afar Research Expedition (IARE) at Hadar, a site Taieb had identified as geologically promising. The first season yields hominin knee joint fragments (AL 129-1) that already indicate bipedalism. Hadar will prove to be one of the richest hominin fossil sites ever discovered.
**Section Reference**: 1 — The Afar; 2 — November 24, 1974
**Source**: [S02] Johanson & Edey 1981, *Lucy: The Beginnings of Humankind*

---

### [1974, November 24] — Johanson and Gray Discover AL 288-1 (Lucy)
**Date**: 24 November 1974
**Location**: Hadar, Ethiopia — locality AL 288, Kada Hadar Member
**Significance**: Donald Johanson and graduate student Tom Gray, surveying a gully at locality 288, spot a fragment of arm bone on the surface. Systematic collection over the next three weeks recovers approximately 40% of a single small-bodied adult hominin skeleton — an unprecedented level of completeness for any hominin fossil at the time. That evening, the camp celebrates while playing "Lucy in the Sky with Diamonds" on a tape player, and the specimen receives its informal name. In Amharic, she is named Dinkinesh: "you are marvellous."
**Section Reference**: 2 — November 24, 1974; 12 — Dinkinesh
**Source**: [S02] Johanson & Edey 1981; [S01] Johanson & White 1979

---

### [1975] — First Family (AL 333) Discovered at Hadar
**Date**: 1975 field season
**Location**: Hadar, Ethiopia — locality AL 333
**Significance**: Less than 3 km from Lucy's locality, the IARE team recovers remains of at least 13 *A. afarensis* individuals spanning a range of body sizes and ages, all deposited in a single geological event. The AL 333 assemblage remains the largest collection of associated *A. afarensis* individuals ever found and provides the primary evidence for assessing sexual dimorphism and group composition in the species.
**Section Reference**: 8 — Among Her Kind
**Source**: [S01] Johanson & White 1979; [S15] Reno et al. 2003, PNAS

---

### [1976] — Mary Leakey's Team Discovers Laetoli Footprints
**Date**: 1976 (initial discovery by Andrew Hill); major excavation 1978
**Location**: Laetoli, Tanzania
**Significance**: Footprints preserved in volcanic ash dated to 3.66 Ma demonstrate that bipedal hominins walked with a heel-strike gait nearly half a million years before Lucy. The prints are generally attributed to *A. afarensis*, the only known hominin species in East Africa at that date. Combined with Lucy's skeletal evidence, Laetoli transforms bipedalism from an anatomical inference to a documented behavior.
**Section Reference**: 4 — Built to Walk
**Source**: [S08] Leakey & Hay 1979, Nature; Raichlen et al. 2010, PLoS ONE

---

### [1978] — Johanson et al. Announce *A. afarensis* Material in Nature
**Date**: 1978 (preliminary announcement)
**Location**: Publication: London (Nature)
**Significance**: Johanson and colleagues publish the first formal scientific announcement of the Hadar hominin material, signaling that Lucy and the First Family represent something taxonomically new — not *A. africanus*, as had been initially assumed. The paper sets the stage for the formal naming the following year.
**Section Reference**: 3 — Forty Percent; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: Johanson et al. 1978, Nature

---

### [1979] — Johanson & White Formally Name *Australopithecus afarensis*
**Date**: 1979 (published in Kirtlandia 28: 1–14)
**Location**: Publication: Cleveland Museum of Natural History
**Significance**: Johanson and Tim White formally erect the species *Australopithecus afarensis*, designating the Laetoli mandible LH 4 as the type specimen and Lucy (AL 288-1) as the most complete referred specimen. The new species subsumes all Hadar and Laetoli hominins under a single taxon and is proposed as the ancestor of both *Homo* and the robust australopiths. The naming provokes immediate controversy — particularly from the Leakey family, who object to subsuming Laetoli material under a Hadar-based species name.
**Section Reference**: 11 — Where She Sits; 12 — Dinkinesh
**Source**: [S01] Johanson & White 1979, Science 203: 321–323 (announcement); Johanson et al. 1978, Kirtlandia 28: 1–14 (formal description)

---

### [1981] — *Lucy: The Beginnings of Humankind* Published
**Date**: 1981
**Location**: Publication: New York (Simon & Schuster)
**Significance**: Johanson and Maitland Edey's popular account of the discovery becomes a bestseller and transforms Lucy from a specimen number into a cultural icon. The book's vivid narrative of the discovery night — the tape player, the Beatles song, the camp celebration — becomes the defining origin story of modern paleoanthropology. It introduces millions of non-specialists to australopith science and establishes the template for "discovery narrative" popular science writing.
**Section Reference**: 2 — November 24, 1974; 12 — Dinkinesh
**Source**: [S02] Johanson & Edey 1981, *Lucy: The Beginnings of Humankind* (Simon & Schuster)

---

### [1983] — Stern & Susman Publish Arboreal Locomotion Hypothesis
**Date**: 1983
**Location**: Publication: American Journal of Physical Anthropology 60(3): 279–317
**Significance**: Jack Stern and Randall Susman argue that *A. afarensis* retained significant arboreal capability based on its curved manual and pedal phalanges, funnel-shaped thorax, cranially oriented glenoid fossa, and long forearms relative to body size. They propose Lucy was a "facultative biped" — obligately bipedal on the ground but regularly climbing trees for feeding and sleeping. This paper launches the bipedalism debate that will define *A. afarensis* research for the next four decades.
**Section Reference**: 5 — Or Built to Climb?
**Source**: [S05] Stern & Susman 1983, AJPA 60: 279–317

---

### [1985] — Vrba Publishes the Turnover Pulse Hypothesis
**Date**: 1985
**Location**: Publication: South African Journal of Science 81: 229–236
**Significance**: Elisabeth Vrba proposes that pulses of species origination and extinction across African mammals were driven by global climate shifts — particularly the cooling and aridification around 2.5 Ma. The hypothesis provides a powerful environmental framework for understanding why *A. afarensis* disappeared and why *Homo* and *Paranthropus* appeared in its wake. It remains one of the most influential (and debated) models linking climate to hominin evolution.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World; 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: [S11] Vrba 1985, South African Journal of Science 81: 229–236

---

### [1988] — Lovejoy Publishes Obligate Bipedal Model
**Date**: 1988
**Location**: Publication: Scientific American 259(5): 118–125
**Significance**: Owen Lovejoy argues that *A. afarensis* was an obligate biped with no significant arboreal component to its locomotor repertoire. He interprets the curved phalanges and other "arboreal" features as phylogenetic baggage — retained ancestral traits with no functional significance. Lovejoy's model proposes that bipedalism evolved in the context of male provisioning and pair bonding, not as a savanna adaptation. His position directly opposes Stern & Susman and anchors one pole of the bipedalism debate.
**Section Reference**: 4 — Built to Walk; 5 — Or Built to Climb?
**Source**: [S06] Lovejoy 1988, Scientific American; Lovejoy 1981, Science 211: 341–350

---

### [1997] — Reed Publishes Hadar Paleoecological Reconstruction
**Date**: 1997
**Location**: Publication: Journal of Human Evolution 32(4): 289–322
**Significance**: Kaye Reed's systematic analysis of the Hadar faunal assemblage establishes that Lucy's habitat was not open savanna but a complex mosaic of closed woodland, open bushland, and riparian forest. The ecomorphological analysis of bovid, primate, and carnivore communities provides the most detailed picture of the Hadar paleoenvironment and becomes a foundational reference for understanding what *A. afarensis* lived in — not what we assumed it lived in.
**Section Reference**: 6 — The Pliocene World
**Source**: [S12] Reed 1997, Journal of Human Evolution 32: 289–322

---

### [1999] — Sponheimer & Lee-Thorp: Isotopic Ecology of Australopithecus
**Date**: 1999
**Location**: Publication: Science 283(5400): 368–370
**Significance**: Matt Sponheimer and Julia Lee-Thorp's stable carbon isotope analysis of australopith teeth reveals that *A. africanus* consumed a significant proportion of C4 foods (grasses, sedges, or animals that ate them) — not just the C3 fruits and leaves expected from a woodland primate. This finding reshapes dietary models for all australopiths, including *A. afarensis*, and opens the door to the generalist-feeder hypothesis that will dominate subsequent dietary research.
**Section Reference**: 7 — What She Ate
**Source**: [S11a] Sponheimer & Lee-Thorp 1999, Science 283: 368–370

---

### [2000] — Selam (DIK-1-1) Discovered at Dikika
**Date**: 10 December 2000 (discovery); published 2006
**Location**: Dikika, Ethiopia
**Significance**: Zeresenay Alemseged discovers a remarkably complete infant *A. afarensis* skeleton eroding from sandstone at Dikika, across the Awash River from Hadar. The specimen — approximately 3 years old at death, dating to ~3.3 Ma — preserves the face, the near-complete skull, the hyoid bone, fingers, a foot, a kneecap, and much of the torso. It is the most complete australopith skeleton ever found and provides unique evidence on growth, development, and locomotor ontogeny in *A. afarensis*.
**Section Reference**: 5 — Or Built to Climb?; 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: [S07] Alemseged et al. 2006, Nature 443: 296–301

---

### [2001] — Leakey et al. Describe *Kenyanthropus platyops*
**Date**: 2001
**Location**: Publication: Nature 410: 433–440
**Significance**: Meave Leakey and colleagues describe a flat-faced hominin from West Turkana dated to ~3.5 Ma — contemporary with *A. afarensis*. The new genus and species challenges the prevailing view that *A. afarensis* was the sole hominin in East Africa during the mid-Pliocene. Its taxonomic validity is debated (Tim White argues the flat face is an artifact of post-mortem distortion), but even as a contested taxon, it has forced the field to consider a more complex, multi-lineage model of hominin diversity.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: [S10] Leakey et al. 2001, Nature 410: 433–440

---

### [2003] — Reno et al. Quantify Sexual Dimorphism in *A. afarensis*
**Date**: 2003
**Location**: Publication: PNAS 100(16): 9404–9409
**Significance**: Philip Reno and colleagues use template method resampling to compare *A. afarensis* skeletal size variation with modern humans and great apes. They argue that dimorphism in *A. afarensis* was moderate — comparable to modern humans, not gorillas — and therefore consistent with a pair-bonding rather than a harem-based mating system. The conclusion is contested (Gordon et al. 2008 argue for higher dimorphism), but Reno et al.'s methodology sets the standard for subsequent analyses.
**Section Reference**: 8 — Among Her Kind
**Source**: [S15] Reno et al. 2003, PNAS 100: 9404–9409

---

### [2006] — Alemseged et al. Publish Selam in Nature
**Date**: 20 September 2006
**Location**: Publication: Nature 443: 296–301
**Significance**: Six years after discovery (much of that time spent painstakingly extracting bone from sandstone matrix), Alemseged et al. formally describe DIK-1-1. The paper documents the hyoid bone (ape-like), the scapula (gorilla-like, suggesting climbing), the semicircular canals (intermediate between apes and humans), and the brain size (estimated adult equivalent of ~375 cc). The publication significantly expands the anatomical database for *A. afarensis* and provides the first direct evidence of development and growth patterns in the species.
**Section Reference**: 5 — Or Built to Climb?; 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: [S07] Alemseged et al. 2006, Nature 443: 296–301

---

### [2006] — Wynn et al. Publish Hadar Geological Context
**Date**: 2006
**Location**: Publication: GSA Bulletin 118(5–6): 515–529
**Significance**: Jonathan Wynn and colleagues provide the definitive geological framework for the Hadar Formation, dating key volcanic tuffs, characterizing sedimentary environments, and reconstructing the paleoclimate trajectory from ~3.4 to ~2.9 Ma. Their work establishes the stratigraphic context within which all Hadar fossils — including Lucy — are interpreted and documents the environmental shift from wetter to drier conditions across the *A. afarensis* temporal range.
**Section Reference**: 1 — The Afar; 6 — The Pliocene World
**Source**: [S13] Wynn et al. 2006, GSA Bulletin 118: 515–529

---

### [2007–2013] — Lucy US Exhibition Tour
**Date**: 2007 (opens at Houston Museum of Natural Science) through 2013
**Location**: Houston, TX; then traveling to Seattle, New York, Denver, and other US cities
**Significance**: For the first time, AL 288-1's original fossils leave Ethiopia for a multi-year international tour. The decision is controversial: Ethiopian and international scientists protest the risk to irreplaceable fossils, the Smithsonian and several major museums decline to host the exhibition, and critics argue that Ethiopia received inadequate compensation. Supporters counter that the tour brought unprecedented public attention and revenue. The episode crystallizes the tensions between scientific access, public education, national heritage, and fossil preservation.
**Section Reference**: 12 — Dinkinesh
**Source**: Gibbons 2006, Science 314: 574; [S02] Johanson & Edey 1981 (background)

---

### [2009] — Lovejoy Publishes Ardipithecus Bipedalism Model
**Date**: 2 October 2009
**Location**: Publication: Science 326(5949): 71–78 (part of the Ardipithecus special issue)
**Significance**: With the long-awaited publication of the *Ardipithecus ramidus* skeleton ("Ardi"), Lovejoy revises his bipedalism model. He argues that Ardi's primitive foot (with a divergent hallux) and pelvis show that bipedalism evolved not from a chimpanzee-like knuckle-walking ancestor but from a more generalized arboreal form. This reframes Lucy's bipedalism: rather than being a "modification of chimp locomotion," *A. afarensis* bipedalism is a further refinement of a mode already present in *Ardipithecus*, 1.2 million years earlier.
**Section Reference**: 4 — Built to Walk; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: [S16] Lovejoy 2009, Science 326: 71–78

---

### [2009] — Kimbel & Delezene Comprehensive *A. afarensis* Review
**Date**: 2009
**Location**: Publication: Journal of Human Evolution 57(3): 235–265
**Significance**: William Kimbel and Lucas Delezene publish the most comprehensive systematic review of *A. afarensis* since the species was named in 1979. The paper assesses thirty years of accumulated evidence on morphology, variation, taxonomy, and phylogenetic position. It reaffirms *A. afarensis* as a single, highly variable species (rejecting proposals to split it) and supports its position as a plausible stem ancestor for the *Homo*–*Paranthropus* clade.
**Section Reference**: 3 — Forty Percent; 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: [S03] Kimbel & Delezene 2009, Journal of Human Evolution 57: 235–265

---

### [2011] — Ward et al. Publish Fourth Metatarsal Evidence
**Date**: 10 February 2011
**Location**: Publication: Science 331(6018): 750–753
**Significance**: Carol Ward and colleagues describe a fourth metatarsal from Hadar showing the longitudinal and transverse arches characteristic of the modern human foot. This is direct evidence that *A. afarensis* had stiff, arched feet adapted for efficient bipedal push-off — contradicting models that proposed a more flexible, ape-like foot. The finding strengthens the "committed biped" interpretation and narrows the functional space for arboreal foot use.
**Section Reference**: 4 — Built to Walk
**Source**: [S18] Ward et al. 2011, Science 331: 750–753

---

### [2015] — Haile-Selassie Describes *Australopithecus deyiremeda*
**Date**: 27 May 2015
**Location**: Publication: Nature 521: 483–488
**Significance**: Yohannes Haile-Selassie and colleagues name a new species from Woranso-Mille, in the same Afar region as Hadar, dated to 3.5–3.3 Ma. *A. deyiremeda* (meaning "close relative" in Afar) has a more robust mandible and smaller cheek teeth than *A. afarensis*. Its existence in the same geographic region at the same time as *A. afarensis* provides the strongest evidence yet for hominin taxonomic diversity in the mid-Pliocene — multiple species, not a single lineage.
**Section Reference**: 10 — Her Contemporaries
**Source**: [S09] Haile-Selassie et al. 2015, Nature 521: 483–488

---

### [2016] — Kappelman et al. Publish Tree-Fall Death Hypothesis
**Date**: 29 August 2016
**Location**: Publication: Nature 537: 503–507
**Significance**: John Kappelman and colleagues use high-resolution CT scans of Lucy's bones to identify a pattern of perimortem compressive fractures — in the right humerus, left distal radius, left proximal femur, right ankle, left knee, and first sacral vertebra — consistent with injuries from a vertical deceleration event (i.e., a fall from height). They argue Lucy fell from a tall tree, struck the ground feet-first, and braced with her arms. If correct, the hypothesis implies that despite her bipedal adaptations, Lucy regularly climbed trees — and died doing so. The paper is immediately contested: Tim White and others argue the fractures are post-mortem taphonomic damage.
**Section Reference**: 9 — The Fall; 5 — Or Built to Climb?
**Source**: [S04] Kappelman et al. 2016, Nature 537: 503–507

---

### [~2019] — Ethiopian National Museum Permanent Lucy Exhibit
**Date**: Circa 2019 (renovated permanent exhibition)
**Location**: Ethiopian National Museum, Addis Ababa, Ethiopia
**Significance**: Following the US tour controversy and Lucy's return to Ethiopia, the National Museum installs a renovated permanent exhibition featuring AL 288-1 (a cast is on public display; the originals are in the museum's vault). The exhibit reframes Lucy within Ethiopian national heritage — emphasizing her Amharic name Dinkinesh and her significance to Ethiopian identity. She is simultaneously the most important specimen in paleoanthropology and a symbol of Ethiopian cultural patrimony.
**Section Reference**: 12 — Dinkinesh
**Source**: Ethiopian National Museum records; Gibbons 2006, Science (background)

---

### [2019] — Haile-Selassie et al. Describe *A. anamensis* Cranium (MRD)
**Date**: 28 August 2019
**Location**: Publication: Nature 573: 214–219
**Significance**: A nearly complete *A. anamensis* cranium from Woranso-Mille, dated to 3.8 Ma, shows that *A. anamensis* and *A. afarensis* overlapped temporally for at least 100,000 years. This challenges the longstanding anagenetic model (in which *A. anamensis* gradually transformed into *A. afarensis* without temporal overlap) and supports a branching (cladogenetic) speciation event. The finding further complicates *A. afarensis*'s evolutionary origins.
**Section Reference**: 11 — Where She Sits
**Source**: Haile-Selassie et al. 2019, Nature 573: 214–219
