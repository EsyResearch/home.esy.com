# Lucy: Before the Genus Homo — Era Treatment Guide

## The Pliocene Epoch: Overview

- Duration: 5.33–2.58 Ma
- Subdivisions: Zanclean (5.33–3.60 Ma), Piacenzian (3.60–2.58 Ma)
- Lucy lives in the early Piacenzian
- Global context: warmer than today, but cooling trend underway

## Climate at 3.2 Ma

- Temperature: globally ~2-3°C warmer than pre-industrial
- CO₂: estimated 350-450 ppm (similar to modern levels)
- Sea level: ~15-25m higher than present
- Northern Hemisphere: no permanent ice sheets yet (onset at ~2.7 Ma)
- Africa: more humid than today in the Sahel/East African region
- Milankovitch forcing: orbital cycles driving wet/dry oscillations on 21,000-41,000 year timescales
- The mid-Pliocene warm period (~3.3-3.0 Ma) represents the last time Earth was this warm before the anthropogenic era — important analogue for climate science

## East Africa at 3.2 Ma: The Afar Region

### Tectonic Setting

- East African Rift System actively spreading
- Afar Triple Junction where three rifts meet (Ethiopian Rift, Red Sea Rift, Gulf of Aden Rift)
- Active volcanism providing tuff deposits (critical for radiometric dating)
- Rift formation creating isolated basins with distinct microclimates

### Vegetation

- Mosaic landscape — NOT uniform savanna
- Closed canopy woodland near rivers and lakes (gallery forest)
- Open grassland on interfluves and floodplains
- Transitional bushland between the two
- C3 plants (trees, shrubs, forbs) and C4 plants (tropical grasses, sedges) co-occurring
- Isotopic evidence from paleosols shows mixed C3/C4 at Hadar
- More wooded than modern Afar (which is now desert/semi-desert)

### Water Systems

- Hadar area: lacustrine and fluvial environment
- Basal Member: deep lake phase
- Sidi Hakoma through Kada Hadar: river systems with seasonal flooding
- Lake-margin environments provided rich foraging grounds
- Seasonal variation: wet and dry seasons, but less extreme than modern East Africa

### Fauna at Hadar (~3.2 Ma)

**Megafauna:**
- Elephantids (Loxodonta/Elephas ancestors) — woodland browsers
- Hippopotami — indicator of permanent water
- Rhinocerotids — mixed feeders

**Bovids (antelopes):**
- Multiple species indicating mixed woodland-grassland
- Tragelaphus (kudu-like) — woodland indicator
- Alcelaphini (hartebeest-like) — grassland indicator
- Relative abundance of these tribes reveals vegetation balance

**Carnivores:**
- Dinofelis (large sabertooth-like felid) — potential predator of hominins
- Megantereon (sabertooth cat) — ambush predator
- Crocuta (hyena ancestors) — scavengers/predators
- Crocodylus — in river/lake systems
- These predators shaped hominin behavior: group living, vigilance, possibly tree-sleeping

**Suids (pigs):**
- Critical for biostratigraphic dating
- Notochoerus, Kolpochoerus species as index fossils

**Primates:**
- Theropithecus (gelada ancestors) — terrestrial
- Colobus-like primates — arboreal, woodland indicator
- These indicate available arboreal niches

### Predation Pressure

- Hominins were prey, not apex predators
- Evidence: hominin bones with carnivore tooth marks at various sites
- Implications for behavior: group vigilance, sleeping sites, possible tool-assisted defense (though pre-Oldowan)

## Lucy's Specific Environment

Based on Denen Dora Member sedimentology and faunal associations:

- Floodplain/marshy environment near a river or lake margin
- Mix of woodland and open ground
- Rich in food resources but also in predators
- Seasonal flooding would have structured movement patterns
- The fact that Lucy was found in fine-grained floodplain sediments suggests she died near water

## Climate Transition Context

- Lucy lived during a relatively stable warm period
- But the trend was toward cooling and drying
- Within ~500,000 years after Lucy, Northern Hemisphere glaciation begins (~2.7 Ma)
- Vrba's turnover pulse hypothesis: this climate shift drove speciation and extinction waves
- A. afarensis goes extinct by ~2.9 Ma — possibly linked to this drying trend
- The robust Paranthropus lineage and genus Homo both appear as the climate shifts
- Lucy's world was the end of an era — the last warm, wet East Africa before the ice ages reshuffled everything
