## [S01] Johanson & White 1979

**Citation**: Johanson, D.C. & White, T.D. (1979). A systematic assessment of early African hominids. *Science*, 203(4378), 321–330.
**DOI**: 10.1126/science.104384
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 2, 3, 11 — Original taxonomic description of *A. afarensis*; type specimen designation; morphological diagnosis placing Hadar and Laetoli hominins in a single species
**Key Data Points**:
- Formal naming of *Australopithecus afarensis* as a new species
- Designation of LH 4 (Laetoli mandible) as the type specimen; AL 288-1 as a key referred specimen
- Combined Hadar (Ethiopia) and Laetoli (Tanzania) material into one hypodigm
- Morphological diagnosis: primitive dental arcade, prognathic face, small cranial capacity
- Proposed *A. afarensis* as the most plesiomorphic (primitive) known hominin taxon
- Argued for a single-lineage model with *A. afarensis* ancestral to later hominins
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Science*; foundational taxonomic paper; universally cited in all subsequent *A. afarensis* literature

## [S04] Kappelman et al. 2016

**Citation**: Kappelman, J., Ketcham, R.A., Pearce, S., Todd, L., Akins, W., Colbert, M.W., Feseha, M., Maisano, J.A. & Witzel, A. (2016). Perimortem fractures in Lucy suggest mortality from fall out of tall tree. *Nature*, 537, 503–507.
**DOI**: 10.1038/nature19332
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 5, 9 — Perimortem fracture analysis; tree-fall death hypothesis; CT-scan evidence for compressive injuries; indirect evidence for arboreality
**Key Data Points**:
- High-resolution CT scanning of AL 288-1 revealed compressive fractures in multiple skeletal elements
- Right proximal humerus shows sharp, clean fracture with bone fragments preserved in situ — consistent with vertical deceleration impact
- Fracture pattern across distal radii, proximal femora, sacrum, and first rib consistent with a fall from significant height (~12+ meters)
- Proposed cause of death: impact with ground after falling from a tall tree, with organ damage
- Implies *A. afarensis* regularly occupied arboreal settings (sleeping, foraging, predator avoidance)
- Counter-argument acknowledged: taphonomic processes (sediment compaction, fossilization) can produce similar fracture patterns
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Nature*; accompanied by 3D-printable scan data; generated significant debate and media coverage

## [S05] Stern & Susman 1983

**Citation**: Stern, J.T. Jr. & Susman, R.L. (1983). The locomotor anatomy of *Australopithecus afarensis*. *American Journal of Physical Anthropology*, 60(3), 279–317.
**DOI**: 10.1002/ajpa.1330600302
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 4, 5 — The foundational arboreal-capability argument; detailed functional analysis of upper and lower limb morphology; the "facultative biped" model
**Key Data Points**:
- Curved manual and pedal phalanges in *A. afarensis* resemble those of extant apes that climb frequently
- Scapular morphology (cranially oriented glenoid fossa) suggests regular overhead arm use
- Funnel-shaped thorax more similar to apes than to modern humans
- Long arms relative to legs (high intermembral index) retained from arboreal ancestors
- Proposed the "facultative biped" model: *A. afarensis* was a habitual biped on the ground but retained significant climbing ability in the trees
- Directly opposed Lovejoy's obligate-bipedal interpretation
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *AJPA*; one of the two poles of the bipedalism/arboreality debate; 800+ citations

## [S06] Lovejoy 1981

**Citation**: Lovejoy, C.O. (1981). The origin of man. *Science*, 211(4480), 341–350.
**DOI**: 10.1126/science.211.4480.341
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 4, 8 — The obligate-bipedal model; provisioning hypothesis linking bipedalism to reproductive strategy; pair-bonding argument
**Key Data Points**:
- Identified five key characters distinguishing humans from other hominoids: large neocortex, bipedality, reduced anterior dentition, material culture, and unique reproductive behavior
- Argued that bipedalism evolved as a locomotor adaptation directly enhancing reproductive fitness, not as a feeding posture or dietary specialization
- Proposed the "provisioning model": male bipedal locomotion facilitated carrying food back to a mate and offspring, favoring monogamous pair-bonding
- Bipedalism freed the hands for carrying, creating a positive feedback loop with parental investment
- Dismissed arboreal retention as functionally insignificant: curved phalanges and other "primitive" traits were evolutionary baggage, not evidence of active climbing
- This paper established the theoretical framework that Stern & Susman (1983) challenged
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Science*; Lovejoy's most cited theoretical paper; foundational to the obligate-bipedal school

> **Note**: The G1 intake document references "Lovejoy 1988, Science." The 1988 publication was in *Scientific American* (259(5), 118–125), titled "Evolution of Human Walking." The 1981 *Science* paper is the primary research publication presenting the obligate-bipedal model; the 1988 *Scientific American* article is a popular-audience synthesis. This file uses the 1981 *Science* paper as the primary reference.

## [S07] Ward et al. 2011

**Citation**: Ward, C.V., Kimbel, W.H. & Johanson, D.C. (2011). Complete fourth metatarsal and arches in the foot of *Australopithecus afarensis*. *Science*, 331(6018), 750–753.
**DOI**: 10.1126/science.1201463
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 3, 4 — Direct evidence of longitudinal and transverse foot arches; key bipedalism anatomy; disproves flat-footed walking hypothesis
**Key Data Points**:
- AL 333-160: a complete fourth metatarsal from the Hadar Formation, dated ~3.2 Ma
- Metatarsal morphology demonstrates both transverse and longitudinal arches were present in the *A. afarensis* foot
- Arched feet are essential for efficient bipedal push-off during walking and running
- Torsion of the metatarsal head matches the pattern seen in modern humans, not in apes
- Refutes earlier suggestions that *A. afarensis* had flat, ape-like feet unsuited to obligate bipedalism
- Provides the strongest postcranial evidence that *A. afarensis* was committed to terrestrial bipedal locomotion
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Science*; reported with specimen photography; widely covered in paleoanthropology reviews

## [S08] Alemseged et al. 2006

**Citation**: Alemseged, Z., Spoor, F., Kimbel, W.H., Bobe, R., Geraads, D., Reed, D. & Wynn, J.G. (2006). A juvenile early hominin skeleton from Dikika, Ethiopia. *Nature*, 443, 296–301.
**DOI**: 10.1038/nature05047
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 3, 5, 10 — Selam (DIK-1-1) infant specimen; ontogenetic data for *A. afarensis*; hyoid bone morphology; scapular evidence for climbing
**Key Data Points**:
- DIK-1-1 ("Selam"): 3.3 Ma partial skeleton of a ~3-year-old female *A. afarensis* from Dikika, Ethiopia
- Most complete juvenile hominin skeleton from the Pliocene — includes cranium, mandible, hyoid, scapulae, ribs, vertebrae, fingers, foot, and knee
- Hyoid bone has African ape (not human) morphology — implications for vocalization capability
- Gorilla-like scapular morphology in the juvenile, suggesting retention of climbing adaptations
- Curved manual phalanges consistent with arboreal grasping capability
- Bipedal adaptations also present: humanlike femoral neck angle and knee morphology
- Nicknamed "Lucy's baby" though ~100,000 years older than AL 288-1
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Nature*; accompanied by companion geological context paper (Wynn et al. 2006 [S14]); extensively covered in subsequent literature

## [S09] Haile-Selassie et al. 2015

**Citation**: Haile-Selassie, Y., Gibert, L., Melillo, S.M., Ryan, T.M., Alene, M., Deino, A., Levin, N.E., Scott, G. & Saylor, B.Z. (2015). New species from Ethiopia further expands Middle Pliocene hominin diversity. *Nature*, 521, 483–488.
**DOI**: 10.1038/nature14448
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 10, 11 — Description of *A. deyiremeda*; challenges single-lineage models; evidence for contemporaneous hominin species diversity in the mid-Pliocene
**Key Data Points**:
- *Australopithecus deyiremeda* sp. nov., from 3.3–3.5 Ma deposits at Woranso-Mille, central Afar, Ethiopia
- Holotype: BRT-VP-3/1, a partial maxilla with associated mandible
- Distinguished from *A. afarensis* by anterior dental morphology, robust mandibular corpus, and forward-positioned zygomatic process
- Contemporaneous with *A. afarensis* (3.7–3.0 Ma) and *K. platyops* (3.5–3.2 Ma) — at least two, possibly three hominin species co-existed
- Undermines the single-species, single-lineage model for mid-Pliocene hominin evolution
- Species name means "close relative" in the Afar language
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Nature*; discovery widely discussed in paleoanthropology; some taxonomic debate ongoing (lumpers question whether it is distinct from *A. afarensis*)

## [S10] Leakey et al. 2001

**Citation**: Leakey, M.G., Spoor, F., Brown, F.H., Gathogo, P.N., Kiarie, C., Leakey, L.N. & McDougall, I. (2001). New hominin genus from eastern Africa shows diverse middle Pliocene lineages. *Nature*, 410, 433–440.
**DOI**: 10.1038/35068500
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 10, 11 — Description of *Kenyanthropus platyops*; flat-faced morphology distinct from *A. afarensis*; evidence for mid-Pliocene hominin diversity
**Key Data Points**:
- KNM-WT 40000: a 3.5 Ma cranium from Lomekwi, west of Lake Turkana, Kenya
- Assigned to a new genus and species: *Kenyanthropus platyops* ("flat-faced man of Kenya")
- Distinguished by flat face, small molars, and a unique combination of derived facial features with a primitive neurocranium
- Contemporaneous with *A. afarensis* — directly challenges the single-lineage model
- Some researchers (notably Tim White) have questioned whether distortion from matrix expansion artificially created the flat-faced appearance
- If valid, demonstrates that mid-Pliocene hominin diversity was greater than previously recognized
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Nature*; first new hominin genus described since *Ardipithecus*; taxonomic status remains debated

## [S11] Sponheimer & Lee-Thorp 1999

**Citation**: Sponheimer, M. & Lee-Thorp, J.A. (1999). Isotopic evidence for the diet of an early hominid, *Australopithecus africanus*. *Science*, 283(5400), 368–370.
**DOI**: 10.1126/science.283.5400.368
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 7, 10 — Stable carbon isotope methodology applied to australopith diet reconstruction; establishes the C3/C4 dietary framework used across the genus
**Key Data Points**:
- δ¹³C analysis of *A. africanus* tooth enamel from Makapansgat, South Africa
- Revealed a significant C4 component in the diet — not a pure frugivore/C3 browser as previously assumed
- C4 signal could indicate direct consumption of C4 grasses/sedges or consumption of animals that ate C4 plants
- Established the isotopic methodology subsequently applied to *A. afarensis* specimens
- Overturned the assumption that early hominins were dietary equivalents of modern chimpanzees
- Provided framework for the "generalist feeder" hypothesis applied to *A. afarensis* in later studies
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Science*; pioneering stable-isotope paleodietary study; launched a generation of isotopic work on hominin diets

## [S12] Reed 1997

**Citation**: Reed, K.E. (1997). Early hominid evolution and ecological change through the African Plio-Pleistocene. *Journal of Human Evolution*, 32(2–3), 289–322.
**DOI**: 10.1006/jhev.1996.0106
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 1, 6 — Paleoecological reconstruction of Hadar and other East African hominin sites; faunal community analysis; habitat characterization
**Key Data Points**:
- Systematic reconstruction of habitat structure at major hominin sites using mammalian community ecological analysis
- Hadar (3.4–2.9 Ma) characterized as mosaic habitat: closed woodland interspersed with open grassland and riparian gallery forest
- Faunal assemblage at Hadar includes aquatic taxa (crocodilians, hippopotamids), bovids (reduncins, tragelaphins), suids, elephantids, and diverse carnivorans
- Demonstrated that *A. afarensis* sites consistently associate with woodland or bushland rather than open grassland
- Ecological transition visible across the Plio-Pleistocene: gradual opening of habitats correlates with faunal turnover
- Provides the ecological framework for understanding *A. afarensis* as a woodland-edge species
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Journal of Human Evolution*; standard reference for Hadar paleoecology; widely cited in environmental reconstruction studies

## [S13] Vrba 1985

**Citation**: Vrba, E.S. (1985). Environment and evolution: alternative causes of the temporal distribution of evolutionary events. *South African Journal of Science*, 81, 229–236.
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 6, 11 — Turnover pulse hypothesis; climate-driven speciation and extinction; theoretical framework for linking Pliocene environmental change to hominin diversification
**Key Data Points**:
- Proposed the "turnover pulse hypothesis": major pulses of speciation and extinction in African mammals correlate with episodes of significant climatic and environmental change
- Key turnover events at ~2.8–2.5 Ma coincide with onset of Northern Hemisphere glaciation and increased East African aridity
- Predicted that hominin lineage diversification (emergence of *Homo*, *Paranthropus*) was driven by the same climatic forcing
- Used African bovid fossil record as the primary test dataset
- Contrasted with the "Red Queen" model of continuous background turnover
- Provides the theoretical framework for understanding why *A. afarensis* disappears from the record at ~3.0 Ma and is replaced by multiple descendant lineages
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *South African Journal of Science*; one of the most influential theoretical papers in African paleoecology; 1000+ citations

## [S15] Reno et al. 2003

**Citation**: Reno, P.L., Meindl, R.S., McCollum, M.A. & Lovejoy, C.O. (2003). Sexual dimorphism in *Australopithecus afarensis* was similar to that of modern humans. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 100(16), 9404–9409.
**DOI**: 10.1073/pnas.1133180100
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Section 8 — Quantitative analysis of sexual dimorphism in *A. afarensis*; challenges the extreme-dimorphism model; implications for mating system and social structure
**Key Data Points**:
- Used postcranial size ratios from AL 288-1 (Lucy) and AL 333 assemblage to estimate skeletal size dimorphism
- Employed template method with extensive bootstrap simulations to control for small sample sizes
- Concluded dimorphism was moderate — comparable to modern humans, not to gorillas or orangutans
- Contradicted earlier estimates of extreme dimorphism (e.g., McHenry 1991) that implied a gorilla-like polygynous mating system
- If correct, suggests *A. afarensis* practiced a social system more consistent with monogamy or mild polygyny
- This finding supports Lovejoy's [S06] provisioning model but is contested by researchers who favor larger dimorphism estimates
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *PNAS*; part of the Lovejoy group's systematic challenge to the extreme-dimorphism interpretation; significant ongoing debate

## [S16] Johanson et al. 1982

**Citation**: Johanson, D.C., Lovejoy, C.O., Kimbel, W.H., White, T.D., Ward, S.C., Bush, M.E., Latimer, B.M. & Coppens, Y. (1982). Morphology of the Pliocene partial hominid skeleton (A.L. 288-1) from the Hadar Formation, Ethiopia. *American Journal of Physical Anthropology*, 57(4), 403–451.
**DOI**: 10.1002/ajpa.1330570403
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 2, 3, 4 — The definitive morphological description of AL 288-1; bone-by-bone analysis; body proportions; stature and mass estimates
**Key Data Points**:
- Complete osteological inventory of AL 288-1: 47 of ~207 bones recovered (~40% of the skeleton by element, much higher by information content)
- Stature estimate: ~107 cm (3 ft 6 in); body mass: ~28 kg (62 lbs)
- Pelvis: short, broad iliac blade; wide sacrum; anteroposteriorly deep birth canal — all indicative of bipedalism
- Femur: high bicondylar angle (valgus knee), long femoral neck, small head — bipedal gait markers
- Humerus and ulna: relatively long compared to femur, high intermembral index (~85)
- Vertebral column: lumbar lordosis present, indicating habitual upright posture
- Determined to be adult female based on small body size, pelvic morphology, and dental wear
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *AJPA*; part of a landmark series of papers on Hadar hominids in the same journal issue; the primary morphological reference for AL 288-1

## [S17] Lovejoy 2009

**Citation**: Lovejoy, C.O. (2009). Reexamining human origins in light of *Ardipithecus ramidus*. *Science*, 326(5949), 74, 74e1–74e8.
**DOI**: 10.1126/science.1175834
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 4, 11 — Revised model for the origin of bipedalism incorporating *Ardipithecus*; reinterpretation of *A. afarensis* locomotion in light of a more primitive ancestor; implications for hominin phylogeny
**Key Data Points**:
- *Ardipithecus ramidus* (4.4 Ma) shows primitive bipedalism without knuckle-walking adaptations — bipedalism did not evolve from a chimpanzee-like ancestor
- The last common ancestor of humans and chimpanzees was neither a knuckle-walker nor a suspensory climber
- *A. afarensis* bipedalism was not the first step but a more derived stage in an already-established bipedal lineage
- Canine reduction in *Ar. ramidus* links to the provisioning/pair-bonding model proposed by Lovejoy in 1981 [S06]
- Reframes the arboreal debate: "primitive" features in *A. afarensis* may reflect ancestral retention, not active climbing behavior
- Part of the landmark *Science* special issue on *Ardipithecus* (11 papers, October 2009)
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Science* as part of the *Ardipithecus ramidus* special issue; synthesis paper by the leading proponent of the obligate-bipedal model

## [S18] White et al. 2009

**Citation**: White, T.D., Asfaw, B., Beyene, Y., Haile-Selassie, Y., Lovejoy, C.O., Suwa, G. & WoldeGabriel, G. (2009). *Ardipithecus ramidus* and the paleobiology of early hominids. *Science*, 326(5949), 64, 75–86.
**DOI**: 10.1126/science.1175802
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 4, 11 — Comprehensive description of *Ar. ramidus*; establishes the ancestral condition for bipedalism prior to *A. afarensis*; redefines the basal hominin adaptation
**Key Data Points**:
- ARA-VP-6/500 ("Ardi"): partial skeleton from 4.4 Ma deposits at Aramis, Middle Awash, Ethiopia
- Over 110 specimens recovered; skeleton includes much of the skull, hands, feet, pelvis, and limbs
- Endocranial capacity: 300–350 cc (comparable to a chimpanzee)
- Combined arboreal palmigrade clambering with a form of terrestrial bipedality more primitive than *A. afarensis*
- Lacked anatomical features of suspension, vertical climbing, or knuckle-walking
- Predominantly C3 (woodland) diet based on stable isotope and dental analysis
- Demonstrates that the last common ancestor of humans and African apes was radically different from any extant ape
- Establishes a baseline for interpreting the derived bipedal features of *A. afarensis*
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Science*; lead paper of the 11-paper *Ardipithecus* special issue; one of the most significant paleoanthropological publications of the 21st century

## [S19] Dart 1925

**Citation**: Dart, R.A. (1925). Australopithecus africanus: The man-ape of South Africa. *Nature*, 115, 195–199.
**DOI**: 10.1038/115195a0
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 10, 12 — Historical context; original description of the genus *Australopithecus*; the Taung Child; the first evidence for upright posture in a small-brained hominin
**Key Data Points**:
- Description of the Taung Child (Taung 1): a juvenile skull with natural endocast, discovered in a limestone quarry at Taung, South Africa
- Assigned to a new genus and species: *Australopithecus africanus* ("southern ape of Africa")
- Identified the foramen magnum position as evidence for upright posture — in a specimen with a small, ape-sized brain
- First proposal that bipedalism preceded brain expansion in human evolution
- Initially rejected by the British scientific establishment, who favored Piltdown Man (later shown to be a forgery)
- Vindicated decades later; the bipedalism-before-brains paradigm that Lucy conclusively confirmed was first articulated here
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Nature*; the founding document of australopith research; universally cited

## [S21] Ward 2002

**Citation**: Ward, C.V. (2002). Interpreting the posture and locomotion of *Australopithecus afarensis*: Where do we stand? *American Journal of Physical Anthropology*, 119(S35), 185–215.
**DOI**: 10.1002/ajpa.10185
**Tier**: 2
**Relevance**: Sections 4, 5 — Comprehensive review of the locomotion debate as of 2002; methodological framework for distinguishing adaptation from retained primitive morphology; synthesis of postcranial evidence
**Key Data Points**:
- Reviews the two principal disagreements: (1) interpretation of primitive characters and (2) whether *A. afarensis* was an obligate or facultative biped
- Proposes a methodological framework: derived traits enhancing a function → adaptations; retained primitive traits → null hypothesis of selective neutrality
- Lower limb morphology (valgus knee, arched foot, short toes, remodeled pelvis) represents derived adaptations for bipedal locomotion
- Upper limb morphology (long arms, curved phalanges, cranially oriented glenoid) represents retained primitive features — not necessarily evidence of active climbing
- Concludes that while *A. afarensis* was capable of climbing, its locomotor repertoire was fundamentally bipedal
- Provides a middle-ground synthesis that has influenced subsequent interpretations
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *AJPA* (Yearbook of Physical Anthropology); the most balanced review of the locomotion debate; 260+ citations

## [S22] Green & Alemseged 2012

**Citation**: Green, D.J. & Alemseged, Z. (2012). *Australopithecus afarensis* scapular ontogeny, function, and the role of climbing in human evolution. *Science*, 338(6106), 514–517.
**DOI**: 10.1126/science.1227123
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 5, 10 — Scapular morphology from the Selam juvenile; ontogenetic evidence for climbing adaptation; functional implications of ape-like shoulder development
**Key Data Points**:
- Analyzed both complete scapulae from DIK-1-1 ("Selam"), the juvenile *A. afarensis* from Dikika
- Scapulae display ape-like traits: cranially oriented glenoid fossa characteristic of species that engage in overhead arm use and climbing
- Critical finding: these ape-like traits remain stable from juvenile to adult in the fossil record, matching the developmental pattern of apes (not humans)
- In modern humans, scapular morphology changes significantly during development; in apes, it remains stable — *A. afarensis* follows the ape pattern
- Implies that climbing was a functionally significant behavior, not merely a retained primitive trait
- Suggests early human ancestors abandoned regular climbing behavior later in evolution than previously thought
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *Science*; based on the Selam specimen; directly contributes to the arboreality debate with ontogenetic evidence

## [S23] DeSilva 2009

**Citation**: DeSilva, J.M. (2009). Functional morphology of the ankle and the likelihood of climbing in early hominins. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 106(16), 6567–6572.
**DOI**: 10.1073/pnas.0900270106
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 4, 5 — Ankle joint morphology and climbing biomechanics; kinematic comparison with chimpanzee vertical climbing; constraints on hominin arboreality
**Key Data Points**:
- Kinematic study of wild chimpanzees at Kibale National Park: during vertical climbing, chimps achieve ~45° of ankle dorsiflexion and extreme foot inversion
- Modern humans achieve only ~15–20° of ankle dorsiflexion during walking
- Hominin ankle joints from 4.12–1.53 Ma (including *A. afarensis*) show derived morphology incompatible with chimpanzee-like vertical climbing
- The tibiotalar joint in *A. afarensis* restricts the range of dorsiflexion needed for ape-like trunk-vertical climbing
- Conclusion: early hominins could not have climbed trees in the manner of extant chimpanzees — if they climbed, they did so very differently
- Does not rule out all climbing; rather, constrains the biomechanical model of how *A. afarensis* interacted with arboreal substrates
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *PNAS*; combines field primatology with fossil functional morphology; important constraint on the arboreal debate

## [S24] Latimer & Lovejoy 1989

**Citation**: Latimer, B. & Lovejoy, C.O. (1989). The calcaneus of *Australopithecus afarensis* and its implications for the evolution of bipedality. *American Journal of Physical Anthropology*, 78(3), 369–386.
**DOI**: 10.1002/ajpa.1330780306
**Tier**: 1
**Relevance**: Sections 3, 4 — Functional analysis of the heel bone; evidence for human-like heel strike and push-off mechanics; supports obligate bipedalism
**Key Data Points**:
- Analyzed the AL 333-8 calcaneus from the Hadar Formation
- Calcaneal morphology is distinctly human-like: robust lateral plantar process, well-developed peroneal trochlea, and a posterior tuberosity oriented for bipedal heel strike
- Calcaneal tuber morphology indicates that the foot functioned as a rigid lever during push-off — essential for efficient bipedal gait
- These features are incompatible with habitual arboreal locomotion, which requires a more mobile, flexible foot
- Supports the interpretation that *A. afarensis* locomotion was fundamentally human-like from the ankle down
- Provides evidence from the foot skeleton that complements the knee (valgus angle) and pelvis arguments for obligate bipedalism
**Verification**: ✅ Published in *AJPA*; part of the Lovejoy group's systematic argument for obligate bipedalism; focused postcranial functional study
