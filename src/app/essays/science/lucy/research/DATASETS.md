- Femoral bicondylar angle of ~9° in Lucy is fully within the modern human range, indicating habitual bipedalism [S06].

### C₃/C₄ Reference Endpoints

| End-member | δ¹³C Enamel (‰) | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Pure C₃ consumer | −14.5 to −10.5 | Forest fruits, browse, dicot leaves |
| Pure C₄ consumer | −1.5 to +2.5 | Tropical grasses, sedges (or animals eating them) |
| 50/50 C₃:C₄ mix | −7.5 to −5.0 | Equal proportional intake |

### Notes

- δ¹³C values are measured on tooth enamel carbonate (VPDB scale). Enamel enrichment factor of ~+12‰ relative to diet is already accounted for in published values.
- *P. boisei*'s extreme C₄ signal is paradoxical given its large, flat molars — suggesting consumption of abrasive grasses or sedges rather than hard objects as once assumed (Cerling et al. 2011).
- *A. afarensis* shows more C₃ signal than later hominins, consistent with more wooded/mixed habitats in the Pliocene Hadar area [S12], [S14].

### Dimorphism Debate

| Study | *A. afarensis* Dimorphism Index | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| McHenry 1991 | ~1.52–1.58 | Gorilla-like extreme dimorphism; implies polygynous mating |
| [S15] Reno et al. 2003 | ~1.22–1.30 | Moderate dimorphism (human-like); AL 333 sample bias corrected |
| Gordon et al. 2008 | ~1.36 | Intermediate; above modern human, below gorilla |
| Plavcan et al. 2005 | ~1.40–1.55 | Significant dimorphism retained; canine dimorphism also considered |

### Notes

- The degree of sexual dimorphism in *A. afarensis* remains one of the most contested questions in paleoanthropology. Estimates depend heavily on whether small individuals (like Lucy) and large individuals (like AL 333 specimens) belong to the same species or represent two species.
- Reno et al. 2003 [S15] applied resampling methods to the AL 333 assemblage and argued dimorphism was human-like (~1.22), which would imply pair-bonding behavior. Critics (Plavcan et al. 2005) countered that the resampling underestimated true dimorphism.
- Gorilla (*G. gorilla*) included as a reference taxon for extreme dimorphism (DI ~2.36).

## D8 — Phylogenetic Position: Three Competing Models

**Section**: 11 (Where She Sits)

### Model 1: Stem Species (Traditional)
**Proponents**: Johanson, White, Kimbel
**Topology**:
```
A. anamensis → A. afarensis ─┬─→ Homo lineage (H. habilis → H. erectus → H. sapiens)
                              └─→ Paranthropus lineage (P. aethiopicus → P. boisei / P. robustus)
```
**Key argument**: A. afarensis occupies the right temporal position (~3.9–2.9 Ma) and has sufficiently primitive morphology to be ancestral to both lineages.
**Evidence**: Kimbel & Delezene 2009

### Model 2: Side Branch
**Proponents**: Some cladistic analyses (Strait & Grine 2004)
**Topology**:
```
A. anamensis ─┬─→ A. afarensis (terminal, no descendants)
              └─→ Unknown ancestor ─┬─→ Homo lineage
                                    └─→ Paranthropus lineage
```
**Key argument**: Some derived features in A. afarensis (e.g., mandibular morphology) are not found in early Homo, suggesting A. afarensis is a cousin, not an ancestor.
**Evidence**: Cladistic analyses that place A. africanus or an unknown taxon closer to Homo.

### Model 3: Bush / Diversity
**Proponents**: Haile-Selassie, Wood
**Topology**:
```
        ┌─ A. afarensis
        ├─ K. platyops
        ├─ A. deyiremeda
Multiple ├─ A. africanus
lineages ├─ A. garhi
        ├─ → Paranthropus
        └─ → Homo (ancestor uncertain)
```
**Key argument**: With 3+ species coexisting in East Africa at 3.5–3.0 Ma, hominin evolution was bushy, not ladder-like. Identifying a single stem species may be impossible.
**Evidence**: Haile-Selassie et al. 2015 (A. deyiremeda), Leakey et al. 2001 (K. platyops)

## D9 — Cause of Death Evidence: Fracture Inventory

**Section**: 9 (The Fall)

From Kappelman et al. 2016 (Nature):

| # | Bone | Fracture Type | Kappelman Interpretation | Counter-Argument |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Right proximal humerus | Compressive, 4-part | Outstretched arm impact during vertical fall | Post-mortem sediment compression |
| 2 | Left proximal humerus | Similar compressive | Bilateral pattern = simultaneous impact event | Taphonomic damage can be bilateral |
| 3 | Right distal radius | Greenstick-type | Wrist impact (Colles'-like fracture) | Common post-mortem break pattern |
| 4 | Left proximal femur | Femoral neck compression | Vertical deceleration impact | Weight of overlying sediment |
| 5 | First thoracic vertebra | Compression | Axial loading from vertical impact | Sediment compression common in vertebrae |
| 6 | Left os coxa | Superior pubic ramus fracture | High-energy pelvic impact | Extremely common in any fossil |
| 7 | Right scapula | Glenoid fracture | Shoulder-first impact | Scapulae are thin, break easily post-mortem |
| 8 | Sacrum | Multiple fractures | Vertical impact loading | Taphonomic |
| 9 | Multiple ribs | Fractures | Thoracic compression from fall | Ribs are fragile, almost always broken in fossils |

**Core debate**: Kappelman argues the fracture PATTERN (bilateral humeri, specific fracture types consistent with clinical fall-from-height literature) is diagnostic. Critics (notably Tim White) argue each individual fracture is readily explained by 3.2 million years of taphonomic processes, and that the pattern is coincidental or cherry-picked.
