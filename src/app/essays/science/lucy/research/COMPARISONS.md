# Species Comparisons: Lucy and Her Contemporaries

Research file for *Lucy: Before the Genus Homo* — supporting Section 10 ("Her Contemporaries") and visualizations D6 (Contemporary Species Map), D8 (Phylogenetic Position), and D12 (Species Trait Radar).

This document profiles every hominin species alive within approximately ±1 million years of *Australopithecus afarensis* (3.9–2.9 Ma), then provides structured pairwise analytical comparisons against the baseline species.

---

## 1. Australopithecus afarensis — The Baseline

**Temporal range**: ~3.9–2.9 Ma
**Geographic range**: East Africa — Ethiopia, Tanzania, Kenya
**Key sites**: Hadar (Ethiopia), Laetoli (Tanzania), Maka (Ethiopia), Fejej (Ethiopia), Woranso-Mille (Ethiopia)
**Key specimens**: AL 288-1 (Lucy), AL 333 (First Family), AL 444-2 (large male cranium), AL 129-1 (distal femur/proximal tibia — first evidence of valgus knee)

### Cranial and Dental

- **Cranial capacity**: ~380–530 cc (mean ~438 cc)
- **Cranial morphology**: low, flat cranium with prominent sagittal and nuchal crests in large males; compound temporal/nuchal crest in some specimens; strong postorbital constriction
- **Facial morphology**: marked subnasal prognathism; broad nasal aperture; no canine fossa
- **Dental arcade**: U-shaped to parabolic; moderate diastema between upper canine and lateral incisor
- **Molar area**: ~460–550 mm² (mandibular M1 area)
- **Enamel thickness**: moderate to thick (intermediate between *Pan* and *Paranthropus*)
- **Canine size**: reduced relative to apes but larger than later *Homo*; slight sectorial morphology retained in some specimens
- **Dental microwear**: mixed pattern — scratches and pits consistent with hard-object fallback foods and softer fruits/leaves

### Body Size and Proportions

- **Female stature**: ~105 cm (~3 ft 5 in) — based primarily on AL 288-1
- **Male stature**: ~151 cm (~4 ft 11 in) — based on AL 333 and AL 444-2 extrapolations
- **Female body mass**: ~29 kg (~64 lb)
- **Male body mass**: ~45 kg (~99 lb)
- **Sexual dimorphism index**: ~1.52 (male/female body mass ratio) — among the highest in the hominin record, approaching gorilla-level dimorphism (Reno et al. 2003 argue for lower estimates; Gordon et al. 2008 argue for higher)
- **Intermembral index**: ~88 (intermediate — shorter than apes, longer than modern humans)
- **Relative arm length**: longer than *Homo*, shorter than *Pan* — humerofemoral ratio suggests retained upper-body climbing adaptation

### Locomotion

- **Primary mode**: obligate biped (consensus)
- **Evidence for bipedalism**: valgus knee angle (~9°, within modern human range); bowl-shaped ilium with shortened, laterally flared blades; lumbar lordosis; anteriorly positioned foramen magnum; robust calcaneus; adducted hallux; longitudinal arch (Ward et al. 2011 — fourth metatarsal evidence)
- **Arboreal capability debate**: curved proximal hand and foot phalanges; cranially oriented glenoid fossa; long, ape-like scapular spine (Selam specimen); relatively long arms — Stern & Susman (1983) interpret as functional arboreal retention; Lovejoy (1988, 2005) interprets as phylogenetic baggage with no functional significance
- **Laetoli footprints (3.66 Ma)**: heel-strike, longitudinal arch, adducted hallux, push-off from first metatarsal head — essentially modern bipedal gait preserved in volcanic ash

### Diet

- **δ13C values**: range from approximately −12‰ to −1‰, indicating a mixed C3/C4 diet (Sponheimer & Lee-Thorp 1999; Wynn et al. 2013)
- **Interpretation**: generalist feeder exploiting both closed-canopy C3 resources (fruits, leaves, insects) and open-habitat C4 resources (grasses, sedges, or animals eating C4 plants)
- **Dental microwear**: complex, variable pattern across specimens — inconsistent with dietary specialization
- **Enamel thickness**: sufficient for hard-object processing but not as extreme as *Paranthropus* — consistent with fallback-food strategy rather than obligate hard-object feeding

### Tool Use

- No direct association with stone tools
- *A. afarensis* predates the earliest known Oldowan tools (~2.6 Ma at Gona) by at least 300,000 years
- The 3.3 Ma Lomekwian tools from Lomekwi 3 (Harmand et al. 2015) overlap temporally with late *A. afarensis* but are geographically associated with *Kenyanthropus platyops* territory — attribution to *A. afarensis* is speculative
- Cut marks on bones from Dikika (~3.39 Ma, McPherron et al. 2010) — if confirmed, would push tool use into *A. afarensis* range, but the marks remain contested (some argue trampling damage)

### Phylogenetic Position

- **Consensus view**: stem species ancestral to both the *Homo* lineage and the *Paranthropus* lineage — the "last common ancestor" model
- **Alternative views**: (1) side-branch with no direct descendants; (2) ancestral to *Homo* only, with *Paranthropus* derived from a separate lineage; (3) too variable to be a single species (but this view has lost support)
- **Complicated by**: *K. platyops* and *A. deyiremeda*, which — if valid species — imply that East Africa hosted multiple hominin lineages contemporaneously, undermining the single-lineage model that *A. afarensis* requires to be the universal ancestor

## 2. Australopithecus africanus

**Temporal range**: ~3.3–2.1 Ma (some estimates extend to 3.7 Ma based on Sterkfontein Member 2 dates, but these remain controversial)
**Geographic range**: South Africa
**Key sites**: Taung (Northwest Province), Sterkfontein (Gauteng), Makapansgat (Limpopo)
**Key specimens**: Taung Child (holotype — juvenile cranium with natural endocast, described by Raymond Dart in 1925), Sts 5 ("Mrs. Ples" — adult cranium), Sts 71 (cranium), StW 573 ("Little Foot" — near-complete skeleton, ~3.67 Ma if current dates are correct, though attribution to *A. africanus* vs. a distinct species is debated)

### Cranial and Dental

- **Cranial capacity**: ~420–510 cc (mean ~458 cc) — slightly larger mean than *A. afarensis*
- **Cranial morphology**: more globular than *A. afarensis*; less postorbital constriction; no sagittal crest in any known specimen; more flexed cranial base
- **Facial morphology**: less prognathic than *A. afarensis*; face is shorter and more orthognathic, particularly in the subnasal region
- **Dental arcade**: more parabolic than *A. afarensis*; reduced diastema
- **Molar area**: ~440–520 mm² (comparable to *A. afarensis* but with different proportions — relatively larger premolars)
- **Enamel thickness**: thick — comparable to or slightly thicker than *A. afarensis*
- **Anterior teeth**: relatively larger incisors and canines compared to *A. afarensis* in proportion to postcanine teeth

### Body Size and Proportions

- **Female stature**: ~115 cm
- **Male stature**: ~138 cm
- **Female body mass**: ~30 kg
- **Male body mass**: ~41 kg
- **Sexual dimorphism index**: ~1.35 (lower than *A. afarensis*)
- **Limb proportions**: similar intermembral index to *A. afarensis* but with relatively more robust forearms (suggested by StW 573 if correctly attributed)

### Locomotion

- Obligate biped with probable retained arboreal capability (same interpretive framework as *A. afarensis*)
- Inner ear morphology (semicircular canals) suggests a balance system intermediate between apes and humans, possibly indicating less derived bipedalism than previously assumed
- StW 573 ("Little Foot") preserves a divergent hallux — more ape-like than the Laetoli footprints, though attribution to *A. africanus* is uncertain

### Diet

- **δ13C values**: broader range than *A. afarensis*, with some specimens showing significant C4 intake
- **Interpretation**: also a generalist feeder, but possibly with a greater proportion of C4 resources in some populations — potentially more varied or seasonally variable diet
- **Dental microwear**: less pitting than *Paranthropus*, more scratches than *A. afarensis* in some studies — could indicate more tough-food processing

### Tool Use

- No confirmed tool manufacture
- Possible bone tools at Sterkfontein and Swartkrans (Backwell & d'Errico 2001), but these are associated with deposits that also contain *Paranthropus robustus* and early *Homo* — attribution ambiguous

### Phylogenetic Position

- Variously proposed as: (1) direct ancestor to *Homo* (Dart's original claim); (2) ancestor to *Paranthropus robustus* specifically (South African robust lineage); (3) evolutionary dead end; (4) sister taxon to *A. afarensis* with a shared ancestor
- The South African vs. East African geographic divide is significant — if *A. africanus* and *A. afarensis* were contemporaneous (which the 3.3 Ma dates for Sterkfontein Member 2 suggest), ancestor-descendant relationships become unlikely and a bush-like model of hominin evolution is favored

### Historical Significance

- The Taung Child (1925) was the first australopith ever described — Raymond Dart recognized it as intermediate between apes and humans decades before the paleoanthropological establishment accepted it. His 1925 *Nature* paper was initially dismissed by the British scientific community (who favored Piltdown Man). Vindication came in the 1940s–50s through Robert Broom's adult specimens from Sterkfontein.

## 3. Kenyanthropus platyops

**Temporal range**: ~3.5–3.2 Ma
**Geographic range**: Kenya — Lomekwi, west of Lake Turkana
**Key specimens**: KNM-WT 40000 (holotype — distorted cranium described by Meave Leakey, Fred Spoor, and colleagues in 2001)

### Cranial and Dental

- **Cranial capacity**: uncertain — the cranium is severely distorted by post-depositional matrix expansion, making reliable endocranial volume estimates impossible
- **Cranial morphology**: the defining feature is a remarkably flat face (reduced subnasal prognathism); tall, flat malar region; small ear openings
- **Dental**: small molars relative to other contemporary hominins; molar size closer to early *Homo* than to australopiths — a highly unusual combination for 3.5 Ma
- **Enamel**: uncertain due to poor preservation

### Body Size

- No postcranial material is securely attributed — all body size estimates are speculative
- If molar size correlates with body size (as in other hominins), *K. platyops* may have been smaller-bodied than *A. afarensis*

### Locomotion

- Unknown — no postcranial fossils

### Diet

- Small molars and thin-to-moderate enamel (if enamel estimates are correct) suggest a diet different from the hard-object fallback strategy of australopiths — possibly more frugivorous
- No isotopic data available

### Tool Use

- The 3.3 Ma Lomekwian stone tools from Lomekwi 3 (Harmand et al. 2015) were found in the same geographic region and temporal horizon — *K. platyops* is the most geographically proximate hominin, making it a candidate tool-maker, though direct association is unproven

### Controversy

- **Tim White's critique (2003)**: argued that KNM-WT 40000 is simply a distorted *A. afarensis* cranium; the "flat face" is an artifact of taphonomic crushing and matrix expansion. White pointed out that similar distortion could transform a prognathic face into a flat one.
- **Leakey's response**: the small molars, tall malar region, and combination of features cannot be produced by distortion of a typical *A. afarensis* specimen; the morphological differences are qualitative, not merely quantitative.
- **Current status**: most paleoanthropologists treat *K. platyops* as provisionally valid but await better-preserved material. The genus *Kenyanthropus* itself remains contested — some prefer *Australopithecus platyops*.

### Phylogenetic Position

- If valid, *K. platyops* is potentially ancestral to *Homo rudolfensis* (KNM-ER 1470), which shares the flat-faced morphology — Leakey et al. (2001) explicitly proposed this connection
- This would create a parallel lineage to *A. afarensis* → *Homo habilis/erectus*, challenging single-ancestor models

## 4. Australopithecus deyiremeda

**Temporal range**: ~3.5–3.3 Ma
**Geographic range**: Ethiopia — Woranso-Mille site, Afar region (only ~35 km north of Hadar)
**Key specimens**: BRT-VP-3/1 (holotype — partial maxilla with teeth), BRT-VP-3/14 (mandible fragment)
**Described by**: Yohannes Haile-Selassie et al. 2015, *Nature*

### Cranial and Dental

- **Cranial capacity**: unknown — no cranial vault material
- **Dental morphology**: the defining features are in the teeth and jaws — anterior teeth are smaller than in *A. afarensis*; mandible is more robust (thicker corpus); premolar roots show different configurations; enamel thickness appears greater than *A. afarensis*
- **Molar dimensions**: absolutely smaller than *A. afarensis* — unusual for a more robust mandible
- **Maxillary morphology**: the cheekbone root originates above the premolars rather than above the first molar as in *A. afarensis* — a diagnostic anatomical difference

### Body Size

- No postcranial material — body size estimates unavailable
- Mandibular robusticity could indicate a more powerfully built jaw musculature independent of overall body size

### Locomotion

- Unknown — no postcranial fossils

### Diet

- The combination of robust mandible, smaller teeth, and thick enamel suggests a mechanically demanding diet — possibly harder or tougher foods than *A. afarensis*
- However, without microwear or isotopic data, this remains anatomical inference

### Tool Use

- No associated tools or cut-marked bones

### Controversy

- **Validity debate**: some researchers argue the diagnostic features fall within the range of variation of *A. afarensis*, which is already an extremely sexually dimorphic and morphologically variable species. The mandibular and dental differences could represent population-level or individual variation rather than species-level distinction.
- **Haile-Selassie's argument**: the combination of features — robust mandible with small teeth, different cheekbone root position, distinct premolar root morphology — is outside the known range for *A. afarensis* across hundreds of specimens from multiple sites
- **Geographic implication**: if valid, *A. deyiremeda* lived just 35 km from Hadar, meaning two (or possibly three, with *K. platyops* in Kenya) hominin species coexisted in the same region during the mid-Pliocene — a degree of sympatry not previously recognized for this time period

### Phylogenetic Position

- Too poorly known for reliable cladistic placement
- If valid, either a sister species to *A. afarensis* (derived from a shared ancestor) or a member of an entirely separate lineage — resolution requires more fossil material

## 5. Australopithecus garhi

**Temporal range**: ~2.5 Ma
**Geographic range**: Ethiopia — Bouri, Middle Awash region
**Key specimens**: BOU-VP-12/130 (holotype — partial cranium with associated upper dentition), BOU-VP-12/1 (postcranial elements — femur, humerus, radius, ulna — found nearby but not securely associated with the cranium)
**Described by**: Berhane Asfaw, Tim White, and colleagues, 1999, *Science*

### Cranial and Dental

- **Cranial capacity**: ~450 cc — within the *A. afarensis* range, unexpanded despite being 700,000 years younger
- **Cranial morphology**: relatively prognathic face; moderate postorbital constriction; no sagittal crest
- **Dental morphology**: the unique diagnostic combination — enormous anterior teeth (incisors and canines larger than *A. afarensis*) combined with massive molars (exceeding most australopiths). This combination is unexpected: in most hominin lineages, anterior tooth reduction accompanies postcanine expansion (or vice versa)
- **Molar area**: ~600+ mm² (estimated — among the largest in the australopith record outside *Paranthropus*)
- **Enamel thickness**: thick

### Body Size

- **Femoral length**: the associated femur (BOU-VP-12/1) is long relative to the humerus — the humerofemoral ratio (~72) is closer to modern humans than to other australopiths, suggesting a body plan that had "modernized" its lower limb proportions while retaining a small brain
- **Estimated stature**: ~140–150 cm (but based on an insecure association)
- **Body mass**: uncertain, but femoral dimensions suggest ~30–35 kg

### Locomotion

- The long femur and humanlike humerofemoral ratio suggest committed bipedalism with reduced arboreal climbing compared to *A. afarensis*
- Forearm (radius) is long relative to the humerus, however — an ape-like upper-limb proportion that complicates the picture

### Diet

- The massive postcanine dentition and thick enamel suggest a diet that included hard or abrasive foods
- The enlarged anterior teeth might indicate significant incisal preparation (biting into hard-shelled fruits or tubers)
- No isotopic data published for the type specimen

### Tool Use and Butchery

- **Critical association**: cut-marked and percussion-marked bones were found at the same stratigraphic level as the hominin fossils at Bouri (de Heinzelin et al. 1999)
- These bones — antelope limb shafts with stone tool cut marks and hammerstone percussion marks — represent some of the earliest evidence for butchery behavior (~2.5 Ma), contemporaneous with the earliest Oldowan tools at Gona (~2.6 Ma)
- *A. garhi* is the only hominin at Bouri at 2.5 Ma, making it the prime candidate for the tool-maker — but direct association (tools found in the hand of a fossil) does not exist
- If *A. garhi* was butchering large mammals, this represents a major behavioral transition: tool-mediated meat acquisition by a species with an ape-sized brain

### Phylogenetic Position

- Asfaw et al. (1999) proposed *A. garhi* as a candidate ancestor for genus *Homo*, based on its temporal position (2.5 Ma — just before the earliest *Homo* at ~2.8 Ma), its geographic position (Ethiopia), and its modern limb proportions
- The small brain and large teeth, however, are not obviously transitional toward *Homo* — most *Homo* species have smaller teeth and larger brains
- Could alternatively represent a late-surviving australopith lineage with no descendants
- The poor fossil record at the critical 2.8–2.5 Ma boundary means *A. garhi*'s phylogenetic position remains highly uncertain

## 6. Paranthropus aethiopicus

**Temporal range**: ~2.7–2.3 Ma
**Geographic range**: East Africa — Kenya (West Turkana), Ethiopia (Omo, Shungura Formation)
**Key specimens**: KNM-WT 17000 ("Black Skull" — named for its dark manganese-stained coloring; a remarkably complete cranium), Omo 18-18 (mandible — the holotype, described earlier but less famous)
**Described by**: Originally Arambourg & Coppens 1968 (Omo mandible); Walker et al. 1986 (Black Skull)

### Cranial and Dental

- **Cranial capacity**: ~410 cc — smaller than the *A. afarensis* mean despite being ~500,000 years younger
- **Cranial morphology**: massive sagittal crest (the largest of any hominin, proportional to cranium size); enormous zygomatic arches flared widely for massive temporalis muscles; highly pneumatized mastoid region; dish-shaped face
- **Facial morphology**: surprisingly prognathic for a robust australopith — the Black Skull combines the extreme masticatory apparatus of *Paranthropus* with the prognathic facial profile of earlier australopiths, suggesting it is morphologically intermediate or primitive within the *Paranthropus* lineage
- **Dental**: megadont postcanine teeth — massive premolars and molars with very thick enamel; reduced anterior teeth (small incisors and canines)
- **Molar area**: ~700+ mm² — among the largest in the hominin record

### Body Size

- Postcranial material for *P. aethiopicus* is extremely scarce
- **Estimated body mass**: ~38–50 kg (extrapolated from cranial and dental dimensions and by comparison with the better-known *P. boisei*)
- **Sexual dimorphism**: poorly constrained, but cranial dimorphism appears substantial

### Locomotion

- Inferred obligate biped based on phylogenetic position and the few postcranial elements attributed to early *Paranthropus*
- No direct locomotor evidence from securely attributed *P. aethiopicus* postcranial material

### Diet

- **The megadont adaptation**: the entire cranial architecture — sagittal crest, flared zygomatics, massive molars, thick enamel, dish-shaped face — is interpreted as an adaptation for generating and withstanding extreme masticatory forces
- **Traditional interpretation**: hard-object feeding specialist (nuts, seeds, hard-shelled fruits, roots)
- **Revised interpretation**: microwear studies of the closely related *P. boisei* (Ungar et al. 2008) showed unexpectedly few pits and many scratches, suggesting tough foods (grasses, sedges) rather than hard foods. If this applies to *P. aethiopicus*, the megadont apparatus may be an adaptation for high-volume processing of low-quality tough vegetation rather than for cracking hard objects
- **δ13C**: heavily enriched in 13C — indicating substantial C4 dietary input (grasses and/or sedges), in sharp contrast to the more mixed *A. afarensis* signal

### Tool Use

- No confirmed tool association
- The massive craniodental specialization is itself a "tool" — morphological adaptation replaced (or preceded) technological solutions for dietary challenges

### Phylogenetic Position

- **Consensus**: basal member of the *Paranthropus* clade — ancestral to both *P. boisei* (East Africa, 2.3–1.2 Ma) and possibly *P. robustus* (South Africa, 2.0–1.2 Ma)
- **Relationship to *A. afarensis***: most cladistic analyses derive *P. aethiopicus* from an *A. afarensis*-like ancestor, making it a "grandchild" of Lucy's species — the lineage that traded dietary generalism for extreme specialization
- **Significance**: the Black Skull was a shock when discovered (1985) because it combined primitive (*A. afarensis*-like) and derived (*P. boisei*-like) features that complicated simple linear phylogenies. It demonstrated that the *Paranthropus* lineage retained some ancestral features while independently evolving extreme megadont specialization

### Extinction Context

- *Paranthropus aethiopicus* marks the beginning of a robust lineage that persisted for ~1.5 million years (until *P. boisei* disappearance at ~1.2 Ma)
- The dietary specialization strategy was initially successful — robust australopiths coexisted with early *Homo* for over a million years — but ultimately proved an evolutionary dead end when environmental instability intensified in the Pleistocene

## 7. Selam (DIK-1-1) — Juvenile A. afarensis

**Species**: *Australopithecus afarensis* (same species as Lucy)
**Age of specimen**: ~3.3 Ma (approximately 100,000 years older than Lucy)
**Age at death**: ~3 years old (based on dental development)
**Site**: Dikika, Ethiopia — across the Awash River from Hadar, approximately 4 km south of Lucy's discovery site
**Described by**: Zeresenay Alemseged et al. 2006, *Nature*

### What Was Recovered

- Nearly complete cranium with mandible
- Nearly complete hyoid bone (only the second known for any early hominin)
- Both scapulae (shoulder blades)
- Multiple cervical and thoracic vertebrae
- Ribs
- Partial fingers and a complete kneecap
- Nearly complete foot

This is the most complete juvenile hominin skeleton ever found for any species older than *Homo neanderthalensis*.

### Why Selam Matters for Understanding A. afarensis

**Growth and development**: Adult fossils reveal what *A. afarensis* was; Selam reveals how it *became* what it was. The juvenile skeleton provides data on growth rates, developmental trajectories, and ontogenetic changes impossible to recover from adult specimens.

**Brain growth**: Selam's cranial capacity (~275–330 cc at age 3) compared to adult *A. afarensis* values (~438 cc mean) suggests a brain growth trajectory more similar to chimpanzees than to modern humans — rapid early growth with limited postnatal expansion. This implies a shorter period of childhood dependency than in *Homo* and, by extension, less extended parental investment.

**Hyoid bone**: The hyoid is nearly identical to that of a juvenile gorilla — not to a human child. This suggests that *A. afarensis* vocalization was ape-like, not speech-like. The hyoid position and morphology imply an air sac system similar to that of African great apes, producing calls rather than articulate speech.

**Scapular morphology**: Both scapulae show a cranially oriented glenoid fossa — the shoulder socket faces upward, as in apes, not laterally as in modern humans. This is the strongest single piece of evidence for retained climbing behavior in *A. afarensis*. Green & Alemseged (2012) demonstrated that this orientation is present from birth in apes and develops postnatally in humans — finding it in a 3-year-old *A. afarensis* suggests it was genetically determined (not an activity-induced trait) and functionally significant for overhead climbing.

**Foot morphology**: The Dikika foot preserves evidence of a longitudinal arch and adducted hallux even in a juvenile, confirming that bipedal adaptations were present from very early in ontogeny — these were not features that developed only in adults through habitual use.

### Significance for the Essay

Selam is not a different species — she (likely female, based on developing dentition) is Lucy's species seen through the lens of development. Her skeleton resolves debates that adult fossils alone cannot: the scapula proves climbing anatomy was genetically specified, the hyoid proves the vocal apparatus was ape-like, and the foot proves bipedalism was innate. She also proves *A. afarensis* was present at Dikika at 3.3 Ma, extending the geographic and temporal sampling of the species.

## 8. The Laetoli Footprint Makers (~3.66 Ma)

**Date**: ~3.66 Ma (Pliocene) — preserved in Footprint Tuff at Laetoli Site G, Tanzania
**Attribution**: usually attributed to *Australopithecus afarensis*, the only hominin species known from Laetoli at that time — but this is circumstantial, not proven
**Discoverers**: Mary Leakey's team, 1978; footprints preserved in volcanic ash (tuff) from the Sadiman volcano
**Extent**: two parallel trackways (G-1 and G-2/G-3) spanning approximately 27 meters, with at least 70 individual footprints from 3 individuals

### Morphological Details

- **Heel strike**: clear impressions of calcaneal contact at the rear of the print
- **Longitudinal arch**: medial midfoot region is unloaded, indicating a functional arch
- **Adducted hallux**: the big toe is aligned with the other toes (not divergent as in apes)
- **Toe-off**: deepened impressions under the first metatarsal head, indicating push-off from the big toe
- **Stride length**: consistent with a habitual bipedal gait, not an occasional upright walk
- **Print depth**: relatively uniform, suggesting an upright posture with the center of gravity over the feet (not a bent-hip, bent-knee gait)
- **No hand impressions**: no knuckle prints or hand impressions accompany the footprints — these individuals were walking exclusively on two legs

### Gait Analysis

Raichlen et al. (2010) used experimental data from humans walking with different gait patterns to demonstrate that the Laetoli footprints are most consistent with fully extended-limb bipedalism — essentially modern human gait — rather than the bent-hip, bent-knee walking proposed by some researchers for *A. afarensis*. Crompton et al. (2012) reached similar conclusions using pressure distribution analysis.

### Attribution Debate

- *A. afarensis* is the only hominin known from Laetoli in the 3.6–3.7 Ma range, making it the default attribution
- However: (1) *K. platyops* is known from 3.5–3.2 Ma in Kenya — could an earlier population have extended to Tanzania?; (2) *A. deyiremeda* (3.5–3.3 Ma in Ethiopia) suggests multiple species were present in East Africa during this interval; (3) the Laetoli footprints show a fully modern gait that may exceed what *A. afarensis* postcranial anatomy would predict (though this is debated)
- New footprints discovered at Laetoli Site S (Masao et al. 2016) from a larger individual confirm bipedal locomotion and significant size variation among the track-makers — consistent with *A. afarensis* sexual dimorphism

### Significance for the Essay

The Laetoli footprints are the single most powerful piece of evidence for habitual bipedalism in the mid-Pliocene. They prove that by 3.66 Ma — hundreds of thousands of years before Lucy — some hominin was walking with an essentially modern gait. If the track-makers are *A. afarensis*, then Lucy's species was walking like us for at least 700,000 years. If they are a different species, then bipedalism was even more widespread than previously recognized. Either way, the footprints anchor the bipedalism-before-brains paradigm that Lucy's skeleton confirmed anatomically.

## Pairwise Analytical Comparisons

### A. afarensis vs. A. africanus

**Shared primitive features.** Both species are small-brained australopiths with cranial capacities in the 400–500 cc range, obligate bipedal locomotion combined with probable retained arboreal capability, generalist diets incorporating both C3 and C4 resources, and moderate-to-thick dental enamel. They share the fundamental australopith body plan: a small brain perched atop a bipedally adapted postcranium with relatively long arms. Both lack any confirmed association with stone tools. Their dental morphology is broadly similar — large postcanine teeth relative to *Homo*, reduced canines relative to apes, and thick enamel suitable for processing a varied diet.

**Derived differences.** *A. africanus* is more encephalized: its mean cranial capacity (~458 cc) slightly exceeds that of *A. afarensis* (~438 cc), and its brain-to-body-mass ratio is marginally higher because of slightly smaller male body size. The cranium is more globular and less prognathic, with a shorter face and a more flexed cranial base — features that anticipate the cranial shape of *Homo*. *A. afarensis* retains greater facial prognathism, stronger postorbital constriction, and occasional sagittal cresting in males. Dentally, the two species differ in proportions: *A. africanus* has relatively larger anterior teeth (incisors and canines) compared to its molars, while *A. afarensis* tends toward a more postcanine-dominant dentition. Sexual dimorphism is substantially greater in *A. afarensis* (~1.52 body mass ratio vs. ~1.35 for *A. africanus*), implying different social or mating structures.

**What this comparison reveals about A. afarensis.** Placing *A. afarensis* alongside *A. africanus* reveals it as the more anatomically primitive of the two — more prognathic, more dimorphic, with a less globular braincase. The geographic separation (East Africa vs. South Africa) raises the question of whether these represent a single widespread lineage that differentiated geographically or two independently derived lineages from a shared ancestor. If the ~3.3 Ma dates for early *A. africanus* (Sterkfontein Member 2) hold, the two species overlapped temporally for at least 400,000 years, making a simple ancestor-descendant model untenable and supporting a bushy model of early hominin diversity. For the essay, *A. africanus* is the mirror — it shows what a slightly different evolutionary trajectory looked like on the other side of the continent, developing a modestly larger brain and a less prognathic face while *A. afarensis* maintained its more primitive configuration.

---

### A. afarensis vs. K. platyops

**Shared primitive features.** Both are mid-Pliocene East African hominins with small brains (though cranial capacity is unconfirmed for *K. platyops*), and both predate any confirmed stone tool technology. If *K. platyops* was bipedal — which is assumed based on phylogenetic position but unconfirmed by postcranial evidence — both shared the fundamental adaptation of upright walking in an East African mosaic environment. Their temporal overlap (~3.5–3.2 Ma) is near-complete for the known range of *K. platyops*, meaning they were strict contemporaries.

**Derived differences.** The distinction between these species — if *K. platyops* is valid — is dramatic. *A. afarensis* has a markedly prognathic face with large, procumbent incisors; *K. platyops* has a flat face with reduced subnasal prognathism that is startlingly *Homo*-like for 3.5 Ma. *A. afarensis* has relatively large molars suited to a generalist or hard-object diet; *K. platyops* has surprisingly small molars that are more similar to those of early *Homo* than to contemporary australopiths. The combination of flat face plus small molars suggests an entirely different masticatory strategy — and potentially a different ecological niche — from *A. afarensis*.

**What this comparison reveals about A. afarensis.** If *Kenyanthropus platyops* is a valid taxon, then *A. afarensis* was not alone in mid-Pliocene East Africa. This matters enormously for the essay's phylogenetic discussion (Section 11): the "stem species" model requires *A. afarensis* to be the sole hominin ancestor at 3.5 Ma, the trunk from which all later branches emerge. A contemporaneous *K. platyops* — with its *Homo*-like flat face and small teeth — challenges that model by providing an alternative ancestor for at least part of the *Homo* clade. The comparison also highlights a key feature of *A. afarensis*: its prognathism. We tend to focus on what is derived in *A. afarensis* (bipedalism), but comparing it to *K. platyops* foregrounds what is primitive — the projecting ape-like face that *A. afarensis* retained even as it walked upright.

---

### A. afarensis vs. A. deyiremeda

**Shared primitive features.** These two species — if *A. deyiremeda* is valid — shared time, geography, and general adaptive grade. Both are mid-Pliocene Ethiopian australopiths from the Afar region, separated by only ~35 km. Both presumably practiced bipedal locomotion (though no postcranial material is known for *A. deyiremeda*). Both had thick dental enamel and relatively large postcanine dentition compared to *Homo*. Their temporal overlap at ~3.5–3.3 Ma means they were not merely contemporaneous but effectively sympatric — living in the same regional ecosystem at the same time.

**Derived differences.** *A. deyiremeda* is distinguished from *A. afarensis* by a combination of smaller anterior teeth, smaller overall molar dimensions, and a paradoxically more robust mandibular corpus. The cheekbone root originates above the premolars in *A. deyiremeda* versus above the first molar in *A. afarensis* — a structural difference in facial architecture that affects masticatory biomechanics. These differences, though seemingly subtle, fall outside the known range of variation across hundreds of *A. afarensis* specimens from multiple sites, according to Haile-Selassie et al. (2015). The counterargument is that *A. afarensis* is already one of the most morphologically variable hominin species known, and its extreme sexual dimorphism could encompass even greater dental and mandibular variation than currently documented.

**What this comparison reveals about A. afarensis.** The *A. deyiremeda* question is ultimately a question about the boundaries of *A. afarensis* itself. How much morphological variation can a single species contain? *A. afarensis* already spans enormous ranges in body size (29–45 kg), cranial capacity (380–530 cc), and craniofacial morphology (from small, gracile females to large, crested males). If *A. deyiremeda* is absorbed into *A. afarensis*, the species becomes even more variable — potentially the most morphologically diverse hominin outside of *Homo sapiens*. If *A. deyiremeda* stands, then some of that variation is actually interspecific diversity masquerading as intraspecific variation, and the paleoanthropological community has been lumping where it should have been splitting. For the essay, this is a vivid example of the lumper/splitter debate in action, with real stakes for how we understand mid-Pliocene hominin diversity.

---

### A. afarensis vs. A. garhi

**Shared primitive features.** Both are Ethiopian australopiths with small brains (~438–450 cc), thick dental enamel, and generalized bipedal locomotion. Both have prognathic faces with significant postcanine dentition. *A. garhi* is plausibly derived from *A. afarensis* or a closely related ancestor, and the two share the fundamental australopith bauplan — small brain, large teeth, bipedal postcranium. Geographically, both are from the Middle Awash/Afar region of Ethiopia, potentially sampling the same broad evolutionary lineage at different time points.

**Derived differences.** *A. garhi* (2.5 Ma) postdates the youngest *A. afarensis* (2.9 Ma) by roughly 400,000 years — a significant but bridgeable gap. The most striking difference is dental: *A. garhi* has both enormous anterior teeth and enormous molars, a combination not seen in *A. afarensis* or in any other hominin species. In *A. afarensis*, anterior teeth are moderately reduced (a step toward the *Homo* pattern of small front teeth); in *A. garhi*, they are expanded, producing a uniquely large-toothed overall dentition. Postcranially, *A. garhi*'s long femur and relatively human-like humerofemoral index suggest a body plan that had modernized below the neck while retaining a primitive cranium — the reverse of the brain-first model and an extension of the bipedalism-first pattern established by *A. afarensis*.

**What this comparison reveals about A. afarensis.** *A. garhi* offers a glimpse of what happens after *A. afarensis* — and the answer is not a simple linear progression. Rather than evolving toward *Homo* by reducing teeth and expanding brains, the *A. garhi* lineage expanded teeth in all dimensions while keeping the brain at ~450 cc. This underscores a critical point about *A. afarensis*: the trajectory that followed it was not inevitable. The adaptive generalism of *A. afarensis* could lead in multiple directions — toward the megadont specialization of *Paranthropus*, toward the large-toothed generalism of *A. garhi*, or toward the encephalization of *Homo*. *A. afarensis* sits at a phylogenetic crossroads, and *A. garhi* is one of the less-traveled paths leading away from it. The association of *A. garhi* with cut-marked bones at 2.5 Ma also raises the question of whether the behavioral potential for tool use existed in *A. afarensis* 700,000 years earlier — unexpressed or unpreserved.

---

### A. afarensis vs. P. aethiopicus

**Shared primitive features.** This comparison is the most phylogenetically informative because *P. aethiopicus* is widely regarded as derived from an *A. afarensis*-like ancestor. The Black Skull retains several features characteristic of *A. afarensis*: marked facial prognathism (unusual in later *Paranthropus*), a heart-shaped foramen magnum, and an overall cranial architecture that combines derived masticatory features with a primitive cranial vault shape. The shared prognathism is particularly telling — later robust australopiths (*P. boisei*, *P. robustus*) have orthognathic faces, so the prognathism in *P. aethiopicus* is a primitive retention from its *A. afarensis*-like ancestor. Both species are East African, and their temporal ranges nearly abut (*A. afarensis* ends ~2.9 Ma; *P. aethiopicus* begins ~2.7 Ma), consistent with an ancestor-descendant relationship with a brief gap.

**Derived differences.** The differences are extreme and represent a fundamentally different adaptive strategy. *P. aethiopicus* has a massive sagittal crest (absent or minimal in *A. afarensis* females and modest in males), enormously flared zygomatic arches, and postcanine megadontia (~700+ mm² molar area vs. ~460–550 mm² in *A. afarensis*). The entire cranial architecture is reorganized around generating maximum bite force — the temporalis and masseter muscles anchored to the sagittal crest and zygomatic arches would have produced forces far exceeding anything *A. afarensis* could generate. The anterior dentition is dramatically reduced (small incisors and canines), the opposite of the moderately sized anterior teeth in *A. afarensis*. The cranial capacity (~410 cc) is actually *smaller* than the *A. afarensis* mean, suggesting that the *Paranthropus* lineage invested in masticatory musculature at the expense of brain expansion. Isotopically, *P. aethiopicus* is heavily C4-enriched, indicating a dietary specialization on grasses and sedges that contrasts sharply with the mixed C3/C4 generalism of *A. afarensis*.

**What this comparison reveals about A. afarensis.** *Paranthropus aethiopicus* is the clearest demonstration of what *A. afarensis*'s generalist body plan could become under directional selection for dietary specialization. The contrast illuminates *A. afarensis*'s defining ecological character: it was a jack-of-all-trades. It could eat C3 and C4 foods, process hard and soft items, walk bipedally and probably climb trees. This flexibility was both its strength and — in evolutionary terms — its ambiguity. *P. aethiopicus* shows what happens when one axis of that flexibility is maximized: you get extraordinary morphological specialization that works brilliantly for 1.5 million years but leaves no descendants. The comparison also reveals the tempo of evolutionary change: in roughly 200,000–400,000 years (from the end of *A. afarensis* to *P. aethiopicus*), the hominin skull was reorganized so thoroughly that a sagittal crest, mega-molars, and flared zygomatics emerged — among the most dramatic cranial transformations in the hominin record. For the essay's D12 radar chart, this contrast will be visually striking: *A. afarensis*'s polygon will be moderate and balanced across all axes; *P. aethiopicus*'s will spike dramatically on molar area and crest development while collapsing on cranial capacity.

### D8: Phylogenetic Position (Cladogram Toggle)

Three competing phylogenies to toggle between:

1. **Stem-species model**: *A. afarensis* is the last common ancestor → branches to *Homo* lineage and *Paranthropus* lineage (via *P. aethiopicus*)
2. **Side-branch model**: *A. afarensis* is a sister taxon, not a direct ancestor; a separate (undiscovered) lineage gave rise to *Homo*; *K. platyops* may be on the *Homo* stem
3. **Bush model**: multiple contemporaneous lineages (*A. afarensis*, *K. platyops*, *A. deyiremeda*) with uncertain relationships; ancestry of *Homo* unresolved; *A. afarensis* is one of several candidates, not the default
