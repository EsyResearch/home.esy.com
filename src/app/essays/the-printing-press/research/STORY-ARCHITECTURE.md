## Story Brief

**Title**: The Printing Press: A Global Timeline History
**Subtitle**: From Tang Dynasty China to the Reformation — How Humanity Learned to Multiply Words

**Central Question**: Who invented the printing press — and why does that question have a more complicated answer than most people realize?

**Core Thesis**: The printing press wasn't invented once. It was invented multiple times, in different forms, across different civilizations. The story isn't about who "really" invented printing, but about how the same innovation has different impacts in different contexts.

**Target Audience**: General readers with a mix of backgrounds — some with vague memories from high school history, others with no prior knowledge. Educational storyteller tone.

**Read Time**: 22-25 minutes
**Section Count**: 16 sections across 4 chapters
**Scroll-Lock Sequences**: 5

### CHAPTER 1: Eastern Origins (868-1400)

**Duration**: 4 sections, ~5 minutes
**Visual Treatment**: Warm amber palette, aged scroll textures, Asian calligraphic elements
**Era Color**: #D4A574

#### Section 1.1: The Diamond Sutra
**Layout**: Split-screen (artifact left, text right)
**Content**: Establish the 868 CE scroll — 600 years before Gutenberg. Buddhist printing in Tang Dynasty. "The product of a mature printing industry."

**Key Quote**: "Reverently made for universal free distribution by Wang Jie on behalf of his two parents. May 11, 868."

#### Section 1.2: Bi Sheng — The First Movable Type
**Layout**: Quote-monument
**Content**: A commoner inventor around 1040 CE. Clay tiles, organized by rhyme-group. The concept Gutenberg would "invent" 400 years later.

**Key Quote**: "If one were to print only two or three copies, this method would be neither simple nor easy. But for printing hundreds or thousands of copies, it was marvelously quick." — Shen Kuo

#### Section 1.3: The 45,000 Problem (Scroll-Lock)
**Layout**: Sticky-scroll with comparison panels
**Duration**: ~45 seconds scroll

**Scroll-Lock Choreography**:
| Progress | Visual | Text |
|----------|--------|------|
| 0-20% | Chinese characters flood screen | "45,000+ unique characters" |
| 20-40% | Characters sort into massive type cases | "Each character requires its own type piece" |
| 40-60% | English alphabet appears | "European languages: 26 letters" |
| 60-80% | Side-by-side: massive Chinese case vs. compact European | — |
| 80-100% | Resolution | "The same innovation. Radically different economics." |

**Takeaway**: Movable type didn't revolutionize China because woodblock already worked well enough and the character system made movable type impractical.

#### Section 1.4: Korea's Metal Leap
**Layout**: Timeline (vertical)
**Content**: Korean craftsmen develop metal movable type (1230s). The Jikji (1377) — 78 years before Gutenberg Bible. UNESCO verification.

**Key Figures**: Jikji printers Seoksan and Daldam at Heungdeok Temple.

### CHAPTER 2: The Synthesis (1440-1460)

**Duration**: 4 sections, ~5 minutes
**Visual Treatment**: Deep burgundy palette, candlelit shadows, Gothic influences
**Era Color**: #8B1538

#### Section 2.1: The Goldsmith's Secret
**Layout**: Full-bleed (workshop atmosphere)
**Content**: Johannes Gutenberg in Mainz, working on "aventur und kunst." Secretive, borrowing money. What he created wasn't printing — that existed. It wasn't movable type — that existed too. What Gutenberg created was a *system*.

#### Section 2.2: The System
**Layout**: Comparison panels (three-part)
**Content**: Movable metal type + Oil-based ink + Wooden screw press = Revolution

**Key Point**: "Gutenberg served the same role for printing as James Watt did for the steam engine. Neither of the men were the original inventors of the concept, but they made improvements so radical that they made the technology transformational."

### CLOSING: The FAQ Answer & Sources

**Layout**: Standard
**Content**:

**Q: Who invented the printing press?**

**A**: Johannes Gutenberg of Mainz, Germany, around 1440 — but the full answer is more nuanced. Gutenberg didn't invent printing itself. Chinese woodblock printing dates to the 9th century, and Bi Sheng created ceramic movable type around 1040. Korea produced metal movable type by the 1230s.

What Gutenberg invented was a *system*: movable metal type + oil-based ink + wooden screw press. This synthesis made mass production of books economically viable in Europe for the first time.

So Gutenberg invented *the European printing press* — the specific technology that ignited the information revolution in the West.

**Connection to Now**: We're living through another information revolution. Understanding how the last one unfolded illuminates our present moment.
