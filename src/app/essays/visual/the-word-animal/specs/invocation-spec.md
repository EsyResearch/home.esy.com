# Layer 1: Strategic Foundation

## Project Title
**ANIMUS — The Living Word**
*How Seven Letters Named Every Creature That Breathes*

## Executive Brief

### Emotional Throughline
From the first breath to the last classification—the word "animal" contains 2,500 years of humanity's struggle to understand what makes living things alive. This is the story of a word that began as breath, became soul, transformed into moral category, crystallized into scientific kingdom, and now stands at the center of our most urgent questions about consciousness, rights, and our own nature.

### Stakes
The word "animal" is invisible today—we use it without thinking. But hidden in those seven letters is every major shift in how humans understood themselves and their fellow creatures: Greek philosophy, medieval theology, Renaissance observation, Enlightenment classification, Darwinian revolution, and modern molecular biology. To understand this word is to understand how we came to think about life itself.

### Transformation Promise
Readers will never use the word "animal" casually again. They will understand it as a living document of intellectual history—a word that carries ancient breath, medieval souls, scientific precision, and evolutionary kinship in every utterance.

## Visual Treatment Philosophy

### Medium: Photorealistic with Era-Evolving Typography
This essay uses archival photography, museum artifacts, historical portraits, and documentary imagery. No illustrations mixed with photography. Typography evolves through each era to reflect the character of its time.

### Era Treatments (5 Distinct Visual Modes)
1. **Ancient** (350 BCE - 500 CE): Terracotta, stone, bronze, weathered marble
2. **Medieval** (500 - 1500 CE): Vellum gold, manuscript vermillion, candlelit warmth
3. **Renaissance** (1500 - 1700): Sepia, burnt umber, chiaroscuro, naturalist observation
4. **Enlightenment** (1700 - 1859): Cream parchment, engraved plates, scientific precision
5. **Modern** (1859 - Present): Progressive color from Victorian sepia to full documentary

### Source Guidance
- Primary: Museum collections (Louvre, Met, British Library, National Portrait Gallery)
- Secondary: Biodiversity Heritage Library, Darwin Online, Wikimedia Commons
- Modern: Unsplash licensed photography for contemporary imagery
