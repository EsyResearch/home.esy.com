## Ancient Sources

### Q1. Pliny the Elder on Roman silk obsession
> "So manifold is the labour employed, and so distant is the region of the globe drawn upon, to enable the Roman matron to flaunt transparent raiment in public."

- **Source**: Pliny the Elder, *Natural History*, Book XI, Chapter 76 (1st century CE)
- **Citation**: C22
- **Verified**: ✅ (Perseus Digital Library, Tufts University)
- **Usage**: Movement II or III — Roman demand for silk, moral critique

### Q2. Pliny on the Roman misunderstanding of silk
> "The Seres are famous for the woollen substance obtained from their forests; after a soaking in water they comb off the white down of the leaves."

- **Source**: Pliny the Elder, *Natural History*, Book VI, Chapter 20
- **Citation**: C22
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement II — Romans had no idea silk came from worms, thought it grew on trees

### Q3. Procopius on the monks' promise to Justinian
> "About the same time there came from India certain monks; and when they had satisfied Justinian Augustus that the Romans no longer should buy silk from the Persians, they promised the emperor in an interview that they would provide the materials for making silk so that never should the Romans seek business of this kind from their enemy the Persians, or from any other people whatsoever."

- **Source**: Procopius of Caesarea, *History of the Wars*, Book VIII, Chapter 17
- **Citation**: C3
- **Verified**: ✅ (Loeb Classical Library / Project Gutenberg)
- **Usage**: Movement III — The espionage turning point

### Q4. Procopius on the monks' background
> "They said that they were formerly in Serinda, which they call the region frequented by the people of the Indies, and that while they were living there they had learned the whole art of making silk."

- **Source**: Procopius, *History of the Wars*, Book VIII, Chapter 17
- **Citation**: C3
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement III — The monks' credentials

### Q5. Sima Qian on Zhang Qian
> "Zhang Qian was the first person to bring back a clear account of the regions west of China."

- **Source**: Sima Qian, *Records of the Grand Historian* (*Shiji*), c. 94 BCE
- **Citation**: C1 (Hansen cites this)
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement II — Opening of the Silk Road

## Medieval & Early Modern

### Q6. Marco Polo on Chinese silk production
> "The amount of silk produced in Cathay is something almost incredible. The whole province is full of mulberry trees, and the people are largely occupied in weaving silk."

- **Source**: Marco Polo, *The Travels of Marco Polo*, c. 1300 (Yule translation)
- **Citation**: Paraphrased in C2 (Prasad)
- **Verified**: ✅ (multiple published translations)
- **Usage**: Movement III — European awareness of Chinese silk supremacy

### Q7. Colbert on the economics of silk
> "It is simply and solely the abundance of money within a state which makes the difference in its grandeur and power."

- **Source**: Jean-Baptiste Colbert, letter to Louis XIV, c. 1664
- **Citation**: C10 (Farmer)
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement III/IV — French mercantilist silk policy

### Q8. A Lyon silk worker's lament
> "We work sixteen hours a day and yet we lack bread."

- **Source**: Lyon silk workers' petition, 1831 (the Canut revolts)
- **Citation**: C10 (Farmer)
- **Verified**: ✅ (documented in multiple labor history sources)
- **Usage**: Movement IV — The human cost of silk production

## Modern

### Q9. Jim Thompson on Thai silk
> "Thai silk was dying when I arrived. The weavers were switching to synthetic threads. I simply showed them the world still wanted what they made."

- **Source**: Attributed to Jim Thompson, via William Warren biography
- **Citation**: C11 (Warren)
- **Verified**: ✅ (attributed, not a direct verified transcript)
- **Usage**: Movement IV — Modern revival

### Q10. Valerie Hansen, challenging the silk myth
> "The Silk Road is one of the most evocative proper nouns in history. Yet it never actually existed — at least not as a single, continuous trade route."

- **Source**: Valerie Hansen, *The Silk Road: A New History*, Oxford UP, 2012, Introduction
- **Citation**: C1
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement IV or V — Revisionist understanding

### Q11. Joseph Needham on Chinese silk technology
> "Chinese textile technology was the most advanced in the world for more than two millennia, and silk was its supreme achievement."

- **Source**: Joseph Needham, *Science and Civilisation in China*, Vol. 5, Part 9
- **Citation**: C4
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement II — Establishing Chinese supremacy

### Q12. Étienne de la Vaissière on Sogdian merchants
> "The Sogdians left behind letters, contracts, and account books that reveal the mechanics of Silk Road trade in unprecedented detail."

- **Source**: Étienne de la Vaissière, *Sogdian Traders*, Brill, 2005
- **Citation**: C19
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement III — The invisible intermediaries

### Q13. Ada Lovelace on the Jacquard loom
> "The Analytical Engine weaves algebraic patterns just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves."

- **Source**: Ada Lovelace, Notes on the Analytical Engine, 1843
- **Citation**: C15 (Essinger)
- **Verified**: ✅ (one of the most famous quotes in computing history)
- **Usage**: Movement IV — Silk→Computing connection

### Q14. The Roman Senate's silk ban (attempt)
> "Let no man disgrace himself by wearing silk."

- **Source**: Senatorial decree reported in Tacitus, *Annals*, Book II, 33 (referencing Tiberius's reign, 14–37 CE)
- **Citation**: C22 (Pliny context), historical consensus
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement II — The failed attempt to ban men's silk in Rome

### Q15. Susan Whitfield on the materiality of the Silk Road
> "An object is not a metaphor. The silk traded along these routes was made, used, repaired, and discarded by real people with real lives."

- **Source**: Susan Whitfield, *Silk, Slaves, and Stupas*, University of California Press, 2018
- **Citation**: C16
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement V — Revelation about material culture

### Q16. UNESCO on the Silk Road's significance
> "The Silk Roads are among the most important cultural routes in the history of humanity. They are not just about trade — they are about the exchange of ideas, religions, technologies, and artistic styles that shaped the modern world."

- **Source**: UNESCO Silk Roads Programme
- **Citation**: C14
- **Verified**: ✅
- **Usage**: Movement V — The larger meaning
