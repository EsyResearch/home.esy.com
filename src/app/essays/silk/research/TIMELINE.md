# Timeline — Silk

> Chronological events mapping for Layer 4 narrative beats.
> Source citations reference CITATIONS.md identifiers (C1–C22).

---

## Neolithic & Legendary Origins (6500 BCE – 2000 BCE)

| Date | Event | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|-------------|--------|
| c. 6500 BCE | Earliest silk protein residues found at Hemudu site, Zhejiang | Pushes back silk use by ~3,000 years from previous estimates | C5 |
| c. 3630 BCE | Silk fabric fragments found at Qianshanyang site, Zhejiang | Oldest known woven silk cloth | C4, C5 |
| c. 2700 BCE (legendary) | Empress Leizu discovers silk when a cocoon falls into her tea | Founding myth of sericulture; Leizu worshipped as "Silkworm Mother" (蚕母) | C4, C7 |
| c. 1600–1046 BCE | Shang Dynasty — silk used in ritual and burial | Oracle bone inscriptions contain characters for silk, silkworm, and mulberry | C4 |

## China's Silk Monopoly (1000 BCE – 200 BCE)

| Date | Event | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|-------------|--------|
| c. 1000 BCE | Zhou Dynasty codifies silk as currency and tribute | Silk becomes a unit of taxation and diplomatic exchange | C4 |
| 5th c. BCE | Silk mentioned in *Book of Songs* (*Shijing*) | Earliest literary references to silk cultivation and weaving | C4, C7 |
| c. 475–221 BCE | Warring States period — silk production scales up | Regional silk specializations emerge; sophisticated polychrome silks produced | C4 |
| Death penalty | Smuggling silkworms or mulberry seeds out of China | The most protected industrial secret in ancient history | C2, C4 |

## Opening of the Silk Road (138 BCE – 200 CE)

| Date | Event | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|-------------|--------|
| 138 BCE | Zhang Qian's first mission westward (captured, held 10 years) | First Chinese diplomatic contact with Central Asia; opens intelligence about the West | C1, C8 |
| 119 BCE | Zhang Qian's second mission — establishes trade routes | Formal beginning of sustained East-West trade | C1 |
| 1st c. BCE | Silk reaches Rome via Parthian intermediaries | Roman elites develop an insatiable demand; Senate attempts to ban men's silk wearing | C22 |
| 14–37 CE | Emperor Tiberius's attempt to ban silk for men | Failed morality legislation — silk demand was too strong | C22 |
| 79 CE | Pliny the Elder describes silk in *Natural History* | Romans still believe silk grows on trees — total misunderstanding of sericulture | C22 |
| c. 100 CE | Silk Road trade at peak volume under Roman-Han parallel prosperity | Annual Roman trade deficit to Asia estimated at 100 million sesterces — Pliny calls it a "drain" | C1, C22 |

## Sogdian Dominance & Spread (300 – 800 CE)

| Date | Event | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|-------------|--------|
| 4th–8th c. | Sogdian merchants dominate Silk Road trade | Iranian-speaking traders from Samarkand create the most sophisticated trade network in pre-modern history | C19, C16 |
| c. 552 CE | Two monks smuggle silkworm eggs to Constantinople in hollow bamboo | Breaks Chinese/Persian silk monopoly; launches Byzantine silk industry | C3 |
| 6th c. | Byzantine imperial silk workshops (gynaecea) established | Justinian I makes silk a state monopoly — only the emperor can wear Tyrian purple silk | C3, C2 |
| 7th–8th c. | Islamic conquest brings silk to Damascus, Baghdad, Córdoba | Islamic tiraz workshops produce distinctive patterned silks; "damask" named for Damascus | C6 |

## European Silk Wars (900 – 1800)

| Date | Event | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|-------------|--------|
| 12th c. | Norman conquest of Sicily captures Islamic silk workshops | Roger II establishes Palermo as a silk center, transferring Islamic techniques to Christian Europe | C2, C6 |
| 13th c. | Lucca becomes Italy's silk capital | Italian city-states begin competing for silk supremacy | C2 |
| c. 1300 | Marco Polo describes Chinese silk production | European awareness of the scale of Chinese sericulture | C2 |
| 1466 | Louis XI brings Italian silk workers to Lyon | Beginning of French silk industry | C10 |
| 1540 | François I grants Lyon silk monopoly | Lyon begins its rise to become the world silk capital | C10 |
| 1685 | Revocation of the Edict of Nantes | Huguenot silk workers flee France to England, Netherlands, Germany — spreading silk technology | C10, C2 |
| 1720 | Lyon silk industry employs 28,000+ workers | Peak of European hand-loom silk production | C10 |

## Industrial Revolution & Computing (1800 – 1900)

| Date | Event | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1804 | Joseph Marie Jacquard perfects the punch-card loom | Automates complex silk pattern weaving; the punch card will directly inspire Babbage and Lovelace | C15 |
| 1831 | First Canut revolt in Lyon | Silk workers rebel against starvation wages — "Work or die fighting!" | C10 |
| 1834 | Second Canut revolt | Brutally suppressed by the French army; marks birth of industrial labor movement | C10 |
| 1843 | Ada Lovelace writes: "The Analytical Engine weaves algebraic patterns just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves" | The silk-to-computing connection made explicit | C15 |
| 1855 | Silkworm disease (pébrine) devastates French silk industry | Louis Pasteur asked to investigate; his research helps found germ theory | C2 |
| 1869 | Suez Canal opens | Changes Silk Road dynamics — sea routes further marginalize overland trade | C1 |

## Modern Era (1900 – Present)

| Date | Event | Significance | Source |
|------|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1935 | DuPont patents nylon — "the synthetic silk" | Begins the decline of silk as a mass commodity | C2 |
| 1948 | Jim Thompson arrives in Bangkok, begins reviving Thai silk | Single-handedly creates a modern luxury silk brand from moribund cottage industry | C11 |
| 1967 | Jim Thompson vanishes in Malaysian Cameron Highlands | One of Asia's great unsolved mysteries — body never found | C11 |
| 2014 | Silk Roads inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage Site | International recognition of the routes' civilizational importance | C14 |
| 2023 | Global raw silk production: ~180,000 metric tons | China produces 80%, India 15%, rest shared by Uzbekistan, Thailand, Brazil | C18 |
| 2023 | Global silk market valued at ~$16.9 billion | Luxury fashion, medical sutures, and high-tech textiles drive modern demand | C17, C18 |

## Key Turning Points (for Movement Structure)

1. **~6500 BCE**: First silk use (archaeological) — *Ancestral*
2. **138 BCE**: Zhang Qian opens the Silk Road — *Ancestral → Collision transition*
3. **552 CE**: Monks smuggle silkworm eggs to Byzantium — *Collision climax*
4. **1804**: Jacquard loom invented — *Modern Echo opening*
5. **1948–1967**: Jim Thompson's Thai silk revival and disappearance — *Modern Echo*
6. **Present**: China still produces 80% of world silk — *Revelation*
