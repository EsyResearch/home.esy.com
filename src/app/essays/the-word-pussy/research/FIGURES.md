# Key Figures: Etymology of "Pussy"

## Lexicographers & Linguistic Pioneers

### **Samuel Johnson** — The Great Omitter
- English lexicographer (1709–1784)
- Author of *A Dictionary of the English Language* (1755)
- Notably **did not include** "pussy" in his dictionary
- Editorial choice reflects 18th-century attitudes toward vulgarity
- Included other body-related words (fart, piss) but drew lines
- His omission is itself historical evidence of the word's status
- *Quote*: [None directly about this word—his silence speaks]
- **Photograph**: Period portrait, frontispiece of dictionary

### **Philip Stubbes** — The First Documenter
- English pamphleteer (c. 1555–c. 1610)
- Wrote *The Anatomie of Abuses* (1583)
- Documented: "The word pussie is now used of a woman"
- First written evidence of word applied to females
- Puritan moralist documenting social language
- **Image**: Title page of *The Anatomie of Abuses*

### **Charles Cotton** — The Double-Meaning Master
- English poet (1630–1687)
- Wrote *Virgile Travestie* (1664)
- Line: "Aeneas, here's a Health to thee, To Pusse and to good company"
- Demonstrates explicit double meaning in 17th century
- Burlesque poet comfortable with wordplay
- **Image**: Period portrait or manuscript page

### **James Murray** — The OED Pioneer
- Scottish lexicographer (1837–1915)
- Primary editor of the *Oxford English Dictionary*
- Documented the word's etymology and usage history
- Brought scholarly rigor to recording "vulgar" terms
- OED entries trace the word's documented evolution
- **Photograph**: Victorian-era portrait with OED pages

## Linguistic Scholars (Modern Analysis)

### **Geoffrey Hughes** — Historian of English Swearing
- Author of *Swearing: A Social History of Foul Language* (1991)
- *Encyclopedia of Swearing* (2006)
- Documented the "euphemism treadmill" phenomenon
- Analyzed how taboo shapes language evolution
- Key insights on semantic shift and pejoration
- **Image**: Book covers as visual

### **Jesse Sheidlower** — The F Word Expert
- Lexicographer and editor
- Editor of *The F-Word* (comprehensive etymology)
- Former OED editor
- Expert on taboo language documentation
- Demonstrates scholarly approach to "forbidden" words
- **Image**: Contemporary photograph

## Historical Context Figures

### **Johannes Gutenberg** — The Technology Enabler
- German inventor (c. 1400–1468)
- Printing press enabled mass literacy
- Fixed spellings and spread standardized language
- His Blackletter type represents the medieval era
- Typography evolution parallels word evolution
- **Image**: Gutenberg portrait, 42-line Bible pages

### **Nicolas Jenson** — The Humanist Typographer
- French engraver (c. 1420–1480)
- Created first true Roman typeface (1470)
- His type represents the Renaissance shift
- Visual counterpart to language evolution
- Venice, the printing capital
- **Image**: Jenson type specimens
