> Used for species profiles, figure cards, comparison data

## Part I: The Hominid Species

### 1. Sahelanthropus tchadensis
- **Common Name:** "Toumaï" (Hope of Life)
- **Temporal Range:** ~7–6 MYA
- **Geographic Range:** Chad, Central Africa (Djurab Desert)
- **Key Specimen:** TM 266-01-060-1 (cranium)
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~320–380 cc
- **Height:** Unknown (no postcranial remains)
- **Body Mass:** Unknown
- **Key Features:** Small canines, anteriorly positioned foramen magnum (suggesting upright posture), massive brow ridges
- **Diet:** Unknown, likely omnivorous (mixed C3/C4 isotopes from associated fauna suggest woodland-grassland mosaic)
- **Tool Technology:** None known
- **Behavioral Evidence:** None beyond anatomy
- **Theoretical Status:** Contested — may be earliest hominin, may be ancestral to gorillas, or may be basal ape. Foramen magnum position is key debate point.
- **Cause of Decline:** Unknown (poor fossil record)
- **Discoverer:** Michel Brunet, 2001
- **Key Source:** Brunet et al. 2002

### 2. Orrorin tugenensis
- **Common Name:** "Original Man" / "Millennium Man"
- **Temporal Range:** ~6 MYA
- **Geographic Range:** Tugen Hills, Kenya
- **Key Specimen:** BAR 1002'00 (femoral fragment)
- **Cranial Capacity:** Unknown (no cranium found)
- **Height:** ~1.2 m (estimated from femur)
- **Body Mass:** ~35–50 kg (estimated)
- **Key Features:** Thick femoral cortex suggesting bipedalism, small teeth, curved finger bones suggesting tree climbing
- **Diet:** Frugivorous (dental morphology suggests fruit/soft food)
- **Tool Technology:** None known
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Bipedal locomotion combined with arboreal behavior
- **Theoretical Status:** Possible early hominin; some argue it's closer to the common ancestor
- **Cause of Decline:** Unknown
- **Discoverer:** Brigitte Senut & Martin Pickford, 2000
- **Key Source:** Senut et al. 2001

### 3. Ardipithecus ramidus
- **Common Name:** "Ardi"
- **Temporal Range:** ~4.4 MYA
- **Geographic Range:** Afar Depression, Ethiopia
- **Key Specimen:** ARA-VP-6/500 (~45% complete skeleton)
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~300–350 cc
- **Height:** ~1.2 m
- **Body Mass:** ~50 kg
- **Key Features:** Divergent great toe (grasping), bipedal pelvis but with arboreal adaptations, small canines (reduced sexual dimorphism), woodland habitat (not savanna)
- **Diet:** Omnivorous — fruit, leaves, possibly small animals
- **Tool Technology:** None known
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Facultative bipedalism (walked upright but still climbed trees); reduced male canines suggest less male-male competition than in chimps
- **Theoretical Status:** Strong candidate for early hominin; challenges the "savanna hypothesis" (lived in woodlands)
- **Cause of Decline:** Evolved into or was replaced by Australopithecus lineage
- **Discoverer:** Tim White, Gen Suwa, Berhane Asfaw, 1992 (published 2009)
- **Key Source:** White et al. 2009

### 4. Australopithecus afarensis
- **Common Name:** "Lucy" (most famous specimen)
- **Temporal Range:** ~3.9–2.9 MYA
- **Geographic Range:** East Africa (Ethiopia, Tanzania, Kenya)
- **Key Specimens:** AL 288-1 ("Lucy"), Laetoli footprints, "Selam" (DIK-1-1, juvenile)
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~375–500 cc
- **Height:** ~1.0–1.5 m (strong sexual dimorphism)
- **Body Mass:** ~25–64 kg (strong sexual dimorphism)
- **Key Features:** Obligate biped, human-like knee joint, small brain, prognathic face, long arms relative to legs
- **Diet:** Omnivorous — fruits, seeds, tubers, possibly some meat
- **Tool Technology:** Possibly Lomekwian (3.3 MYA tools could be A. afarensis or Kenyanthropus)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Bipedal terrestrial locomotion (Laetoli footprints), probable group living, strong sexual dimorphism suggests male competition
- **Theoretical Status:** Well-established species; likely ancestor (or close relative of ancestor) of genus Homo
- **Cause of Decline:** Climate-driven habitat change; may have given rise to multiple descendant lineages
- **Discoverer:** Donald Johanson & Tom Gray, 1974 (Lucy)
- **Key Source:** Johanson & White 1979

### 5. Australopithecus africanus
- **Common Name:** "Taung Child" (first specimen), "Mrs. Ples"
- **Temporal Range:** ~3.0–2.0 MYA
- **Geographic Range:** South Africa (Taung, Sterkfontein, Makapansgat)
- **Key Specimens:** Taung 1 (juvenile cranium), Sts 5 ("Mrs. Ples")
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~400–500 cc
- **Height:** ~1.15–1.40 m
- **Body Mass:** ~30–41 kg
- **Key Features:** Rounder cranium than A. afarensis, smaller teeth, more gracile build
- **Diet:** Varied diet including harder foods (C4 plants, possibly bone marrow)
- **Tool Technology:** Associated with bone tools (Swartkrans, debated)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Predation victims (eagle talon marks on Taung Child)
- **Theoretical Status:** Possible ancestor of Homo or Paranthropus; may be ancestral to A. sediba
- **Cause of Decline:** Habitat change, competition with Paranthropus and early Homo
- **Discoverer:** Raymond Dart, 1924
- **Key Source:** Dart 1925

### 6. Paranthropus boisei
- **Common Name:** "Nutcracker Man," "Zinj"
- **Temporal Range:** ~2.3–1.2 MYA
- **Geographic Range:** East Africa (Olduvai Gorge, Koobi Fora, Omo)
- **Key Specimens:** OH 5 ("Zinj"), KNM-ER 406
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~475–545 cc
- **Height:** ~1.24–1.37 m
- **Body Mass:** ~34–49 kg
- **Key Features:** Massive sagittal crest, enormous molars (4× human size), huge zygomatic arches for jaw muscles, "dish-shaped" face
- **Diet:** Contested — sagittal crest suggests hard foods, but isotope analysis indicates C4 grasses/sedges (not nuts)
- **Tool Technology:** None definitively associated (Oldowan tools at same sites likely made by Homo)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Specialized feeder, possibly spent significant time in wetland margins
- **Theoretical Status:** Evolutionary dead end — extreme dietary specialization
- **Cause of Decline:** Climate-driven habitat loss; dietary specialization made it vulnerable to environmental change; competition with more flexible Homo
- **Discoverer:** Mary Leakey, 1959 (originally named Zinjanthropus boisei)
- **Key Source:** Leakey 1959; Wood & Constantino 2007

### 7. Homo habilis
- **Common Name:** "Handy Man"
- **Temporal Range:** ~2.4–1.4 MYA
- **Geographic Range:** East and South Africa (Olduvai Gorge, Koobi Fora)
- **Key Specimens:** OH 7 (type specimen), KNM-ER 1813
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~510–687 cc
- **Height:** ~1.0–1.35 m
- **Body Mass:** ~32–37 kg
- **Key Features:** Larger brain than Australopithecus, smaller teeth, more rounded cranium, but still relatively small body with long arms
- **Diet:** Omnivorous with increased meat consumption (cut marks on bones)
- **Tool Technology:** Oldowan (Mode 1) — simple cores and flakes
- **Behavioral Evidence:** First confirmed stone tool maker in genus Homo, possibly earliest meat processing (cut marks on animal bones)
- **Theoretical Status:** Debated — some argue it should be in Australopithecus (small body, long arms); others maintain it as earliest Homo
- **Cause of Decline:** Evolved into H. erectus/ergaster, or was competed out by them
- **Discoverer:** Louis Leakey, Phillip Tobias, John Napier, 1964
- **Key Source:** Leakey et al. 1964

### 8. Homo erectus
- **Common Name:** "Upright Man," "Java Man," "Peking Man"
- **Temporal Range:** ~1.9 MYA – ~110 kya (possibly later in Java)
- **Geographic Range:** Africa, Georgia (Dmanisi), China, Java, possibly southern Europe
- **Key Specimens:** Trinil 2 ("Java Man"), Zhoukoudian ("Peking Man"), KNM-WT 15000 ("Turkana Boy"), Dmanisi skulls 1–5
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~546–1,250 cc (increases over time)
- **Height:** ~1.45–1.85 m
- **Body Mass:** ~40–68 kg
- **Key Features:** First hominin with modern body proportions (long legs, short arms, barrel chest), prominent brow ridges, low elongated cranium, reduced sexual dimorphism
- **Diet:** Omnivorous with significant meat component; cooking after fire mastery
- **Tool Technology:** Oldowan → Acheulean (hand axes, ~1.76 MYA); fire use (~1.0 MYA)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** First to leave Africa (~1.8 MYA), long-distance dispersal, possibly first seafarer (reaching Flores?), controlled fire use, prolonged childhood development
- **Theoretical Status:** Most successful hominin species (survived ~1.8 million years); ancestor of later Homo species
- **Cause of Decline:** In Africa, evolved into H. heidelbergensis/archaic Homo; in Asia, possibly survived until ~100 kya (Ngandong, Java) before extinction from climate change or sapiens competition
- **Discoverer:** Eugène Dubois, 1891 (Java Man)
- **Key Source:** Antón et al. 2014; Lordkipanidze et al. 2013

### 9. Homo heidelbergensis
- **Common Name:** "Heidelberg Man"
- **Temporal Range:** ~800–200 kya (broadly defined)
- **Geographic Range:** Africa, Europe, possibly western Asia
- **Key Specimens:** Mauer 1 (mandible, Germany), Bodo cranium (Ethiopia), Kabwe cranium (Zambia), Sima de los Huesos (Spain)
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~1,100–1,390 cc
- **Height:** ~1.60–1.80 m
- **Body Mass:** ~60–80 kg
- **Key Features:** Large brow ridge, large brain approaching modern size, robust build, wide nose
- **Diet:** Hunters of large game (evidence from Schöningen, Boxgrove)
- **Tool Technology:** Acheulean → early Middle Stone Age/Middle Paleolithic (prepared core techniques)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Constructed shelters, used wooden spears for hunting, possible symbolic behavior (red ochre, Sima de los Huesos body disposal)
- **Theoretical Status:** "Wastebasket taxon" — probably represents multiple species. European forms may be proto-Neanderthals; African forms may be proto-sapiens.
- **Cause of Decline:** Diverged into Neanderthals (in Europe) and Homo sapiens (in Africa)
- **Discoverer:** Otto Schoetensack, 1908
- **Key Source:** Rightmire 1998; Arsuaga et al. 2014

### 10. Homo neanderthalensis
- **Common Name:** "Neanderthal"
- **Temporal Range:** ~400–40 kya
- **Geographic Range:** Europe, western/central Asia (from Iberia to Altai Mountains)
- **Key Specimens:** Neanderthal 1 (Feldhofer Cave), La Chapelle-aux-Saints, Shanidar, Krapina, El Sidrón
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~1,200–1,740 cc (larger average than modern humans)
- **Height:** ~1.50–1.75 m
- **Body Mass:** ~64–82 kg
- **Key Features:** Robust build, barrel chest, large nose (cold adaptation), occipital bun, no chin, large brow ridges, wide pelvis
- **Diet:** Top predators — primarily large game hunters (horse, bison, mammoth); evidence of plant consumption, shellfish, cooking
- **Tool Technology:** Mousterian (Levallois prepared-core technique), some late Châtelperronian (possibly influenced by sapiens)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Buried dead (Shanidar "flower burial" debated), used pigments (manganese dioxide), made string, created cave art (Cueva de los Aviones, ~65 kya), cared for injured/elderly
- **Theoretical Status:** Our closest known relative; interbred with sapiens (1–4% of non-African human DNA is Neanderthal)
- **Cause of Decline:** Multiple factors debated: (1) Climate instability during late Pleistocene, (2) Competition with sapiens for resources, (3) Genetic swamping/absorption through interbreeding, (4) Smaller population sizes and lower genetic diversity, (5) Possible disease introduction by sapiens
- **Discoverer:** Johann Carl Fuhlrott, 1856
- **Key Source:** Green et al. 2010; Prüfer et al. 2014; Stringer 2012

### 11. Denisovans
- **Common Name:** "Denisovans"
- **Temporal Range:** ~300–30 kya (estimated from DNA)
- **Geographic Range:** Siberia, Tibet, possibly Southeast Asia (inferred from admixture in Melanesian/East Asian populations)
- **Key Specimens:** Denisova 3 (finger bone), Denisova 11 ("Denny" — Neanderthal-Denisovan hybrid), Xiahe mandible (Baishiya Karst Cave, Tibet)
- **Cranial Capacity:** Unknown for most; Xiahe mandible suggests large jaw
- **Height:** Unknown
- **Body Mass:** Unknown
- **Key Features:** Known primarily from DNA; Xiahe mandible is large and robust; Denisova 11 is first-generation Neanderthal-Denisovan hybrid
- **Diet:** Unknown
- **Tool Technology:** Upper Paleolithic-like artifacts at Denisova Cave (debated association)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Made jewelry (chlorite bracelet, debated), adapted to high-altitude hypoxia (EPAS1 gene passed to Tibetans)
- **Theoretical Status:** Sister group to Neanderthals; interbred with both sapiens and Neanderthals; possible "ghost population" contributing to multiple modern groups
- **Cause of Decline:** Unknown — possibly absorbed into sapiens populations through interbreeding
- **Discoverer:** DNA analysis by Svante Pääbo's team, 2010
- **Key Source:** Reich et al. 2010

### 12. Homo naledi
- **Common Name:** "Star Man" (from Rising Star Cave)
- **Temporal Range:** ~236–335 kya
- **Geographic Range:** South Africa (Rising Star Cave system, Cradle of Humankind)
- **Key Specimens:** DH1 (holotype cranium), 1,500+ specimens from 15+ individuals
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~465–560 cc (comparable to Australopithecus)
- **Height:** ~1.44–1.47 m
- **Body Mass:** ~40–56 kg
- **Key Features:** Small brain but human-like hands (capable of tool use), human-like feet, curved fingers (climbing), small teeth
- **Diet:** Unknown
- **Tool Technology:** None definitively associated (stone tools found in cave system, 2023 — debated)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Bodies deposited deep in cave system through narrow passages — possible deliberate body disposal (controversial); possible fire use in caves (2023 claims, debated)
- **Theoretical Status:** Enigmatic — archaic body plan surviving alongside modern humans; challenges assumption that large brains are prerequisite for complex behavior
- **Cause of Decline:** Unknown — may have been a relic population
- **Discoverer:** Lee Berger and team, 2013
- **Key Source:** Berger et al. 2015

### 13. Homo floresiensis
- **Common Name:** "The Hobbit"
- **Temporal Range:** ~100–60 kya
- **Geographic Range:** Flores, Indonesia (Liang Bua cave)
- **Key Specimens:** LB1 (partial skeleton)
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~380 cc
- **Height:** ~1.06 m
- **Body Mass:** ~25 kg
- **Key Features:** Extremely small body and brain, long feet relative to legs, no chin, prominent brow ridges
- **Diet:** Stegodon (dwarf elephant), fish, rats, birds
- **Tool Technology:** Sophisticated stone tools despite small brain (core-and-flake)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Cooperative hunting of Stegodon, fire use
- **Theoretical Status:** Island dwarfism from H. erectus ancestor (most accepted), or descended from earlier small-bodied hominin
- **Cause of Decline:** Volcanic eruption (~50 kya) or arrival of H. sapiens; Flores experienced major eruptions
- **Discoverer:** Mike Morwood and team, 2003
- **Key Source:** Brown et al. 2004

### 14. Homo luzonensis
- **Common Name:** None established
- **Temporal Range:** ~67–50 kya
- **Geographic Range:** Luzon, Philippines (Callao Cave)
- **Key Specimens:** CCH6 (foot bone), teeth, hand and foot bones
- **Cranial Capacity:** Unknown (no cranium)
- **Height:** Unknown (estimated small)
- **Body Mass:** Unknown (estimated small)
- **Key Features:** Curved toe phalanges (Australopithecus-like), premolars with 2–3 roots (primitive trait), small molars
- **Diet:** Unknown
- **Tool Technology:** None directly associated (stone tools found in region)
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Reached Luzon by water crossing (implies watercraft or rafting)
- **Theoretical Status:** Another island hominin with mosaic of primitive and derived traits; relationship to H. floresiensis debated
- **Cause of Decline:** Unknown — possibly sapiens arrival
- **Discoverer:** Florent Détroit and team, 2019
- **Key Source:** Détroit et al. 2019

### 15. Homo sapiens
- **Common Name:** "Modern Humans," "Wise Man"
- **Temporal Range:** ~315 kya – present
- **Geographic Range:** Originated in Africa; now global
- **Key Early Specimens:** Jebel Irhoud (Morocco, ~315 kya), Omo Kibish (Ethiopia, ~195 kya), Herto (Ethiopia, ~160 kya)
- **Cranial Capacity:** ~1,100–1,700 cc (average ~1,350 cc)
- **Height:** ~1.50–1.85 m (varies by population)
- **Body Mass:** ~50–80 kg (varies by population)
- **Key Features:** High rounded cranium, flat face, chin (mental eminence), gracile skeleton, reduced brow ridges
- **Diet:** Extreme generalist — virtually anything edible
- **Tool Technology:** Middle Stone Age → Upper Paleolithic → Neolithic → Industrial → Digital
- **Behavioral Evidence:** Language, symbolic thought, art, music, religion, long-distance trade, cumulative culture, domestication of plants and animals
- **Theoretical Status:** Only surviving hominin; displaced all other species through combination of ecological flexibility, social cooperation, and cultural innovation
- **Survival Factors:** See SYNTHESIS.md
- **Discoverer:** Carl Linnaeus, 1758 (taxonomic description)
- **Key Source:** Hublin et al. 2017; Stringer 2012; Tattersall 2012

## Part II: Key Scientists

### Michel Brunet (b. 1940)
- French paleoanthropologist who discovered Sahelanthropus tchadensis in Chad
- Pushed the hominin record back to ~7 MYA and out of East Africa

### Donald Johanson (b. 1943)
- American paleoanthropologist who discovered "Lucy" (A. afarensis) in 1974
- Named the skeleton after the Beatles' "Lucy in the Sky with Diamonds"

### The Leakey Family
- **Louis Leakey (1903–1972):** Discovered H. habilis at Olduvai Gorge
- **Mary Leakey (1913–1996):** Discovered Paranthropus boisei, Laetoli footprints
- **Richard Leakey (1944–2022):** Major East African fossil finds, conservation leader
- **Meave Leakey (b. 1942):** Discovered Kenyanthropus platyops

### Lee Berger (b. 1965)
- American-South African paleoanthropologist
- Discovered A. sediba (2008) and H. naledi (2013)
- Pioneer of "open science" in paleoanthropology

### Svante Pääbo (b. 1955)
- Swedish geneticist, Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine 2022
- Sequenced the Neanderthal and Denisovan genomes
- Founded the field of paleogenomics

### Tim White (b. 1950)
- American paleoanthropologist who described Ardipithecus ramidus
- Led the 15-year study of "Ardi" (published 2009)
