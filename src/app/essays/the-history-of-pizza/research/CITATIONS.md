## Key Facts Verification

### Ancient Precursors
- **Flatbreads**: Present in ancient Egypt, Greece, Rome
- **Focaccia-like breads**: Roman panis focacius
- **Not pizza yet**: Lacked defining tomato element until 16th century

### Etymology
- **997 CE**: First documented use of word "pizza" (Gaeta Codex)
- **Latin origin theories**: Possibly from "pinsa" (to pound/flatten)
- **Regional variations**: Different meanings across Italian regions

### Tomato Arrival
- **16th century**: Tomatoes arrive in Europe from Americas
- **Initially feared**: Considered poisonous by wealthy Europeans
- **Poor adoption first**: Peasants in Naples add tomatoes to flatbread

### Pizza Margherita Legend
- **1889**: Pizzaiolo Raffaele Esposito creates pizza for Queen Margherita
- **Tricolore design**: Red (tomato), white (mozzarella), green (basil)
- **Disputed history**: Some historians question the legend's accuracy
- **Cultural significance**: Story widely accepted in popular culture

### Traditional Neapolitan Specifications (AVPN)
- **Dough**: 00 flour, water, salt, yeast only
- **Fermentation**: 8-24 hours minimum
- **Hand-stretched**: 35cm diameter maximum, 3mm thick edge
- **Wood-fired oven**: 485°C (905°F)
- **Cooking time**: 60-90 seconds
- **Toppings**: San Marzano tomatoes, mozzarella di bufala or fior di latte

### American Pizza Evolution
- **1905**: Lombardi's opens in New York (first US pizzeria)
- **Italian immigrants**: Bring pizza traditions to America
- **Post-WWII boom**: Returning soldiers drive demand
- **Regional styles emerge**: New York, Chicago deep-dish, Detroit, California

### Global Statistics
- **Billions of pizzas**: Consumed annually worldwide
- **$145+ billion**: Global pizza market (2024 estimates)
- **350 slices per second**: Consumed in United States alone
- **October 26**: "National Pizza Day" in United States

### UNESCO Recognition (2017)
- **"Art of Neapolitan Pizzaiuolo"**: Listed as Intangible Cultural Heritage
- **Traditional knowledge**: Recognized pizza-making as cultural practice
- **Preservation**: Ensuring traditional techniques continue
